    </span><span class="bold italic">, Accepted 23rd June 2022</span></div><p class="bold italic">First published on 24th June 2022</p><hr><div class="abstract"><h2>Abstract</h2><p>The major difficulties for the development of flexible energy storage batteries lie in the scalable manufacture of high-performance flexible electrodes with bending tolerance. In the present study, large-scale CNT films are prepared by a continuous production method and used to fabricate a self-supported flexible high-capacity conversion anode with ultra-long cycling life by the <span class="italic">in situ</span> growth of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets tightly anchored on the CNTs. The CNTs produced <span class="italic">via</span> such a scalable method have interconnected porous channels, providing a large contact area between the active materials and electrolyte facilitating the electrochemical conversion reaction of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>. An ultra-high rate capability is achieved in terms of a capacity of 280 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at 20 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>. The interlaced construction of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets with CNTs and firm anchoring on the CNT film result in a remarkable ultra-long cyclability of the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>/CNT electrode with a capacity retention rate of 96% after 7500 cycles. A flexible full battery device is further established with the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>/CNT anode and Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>/CNT cathode with the sealed package of PDMS, exhibiting good cycling stability and mechanical durability under different bending states. The present work highlights a scalable flexible battery electrode material, and demonstrates its potential applications in flexible Na-ion batteries.</p></div><hr>
    
      
      <h2 id="sect265"><span class="a_heading">1. Introduction</span></h2>
      <span>Flexible electronic devices such as smart wearables, roll-up displays and implantable medical devices are increasingly demanded in recent years. As one of the key components, flexible, ultrathin and light weight energy storage devices are highly expected.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit1"><sup><span class="sup_ref">14</span></sup></a> Therefore, great efforts have been devoted to develop reliable and flexible electrode materials and battery systems with optimized structure, high energy density and good mechanical durability. Principally, battery materials have to be abundant facilitating large-scale applications, and thus Na-ion batteries (SIBs) are considered as the most favorable candidates.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit5"><sup><span class="sup_ref">59</span></sup></a> In particular, with recent breakthroughs in the production of first-generation SIBs by Contemporary Amperex Technology Co., Ltd (CATL), the flexible Na-ion based energy storage technology is gaining considerable attention to meet the demand of flexible electronic devices.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit10"><sup><span class="sup_ref">10,11</span></sup></a> However, there are still hindrances to overcome prior to manufacturing of the flexible SIBs. The conventional rechargeable battery electrodes are usually made by a slurry-casting process with a binder, conductive carbon and current collector. The active materials are easy to be delaminated during the mechanical deformation of the electrodes, which may generate particle isolation and severe deterioration of the battery performance. Meanwhile, the metal current collector is not flexible enough to meet the requirements of the batteries. Hence, it is preliminary to explore flexible battery materials which have outstanding performance at bending conditions as well as technology fundamentals for scalable production.</span>
      <p class="otherpara">Carbon-based substrate materials such as carbon nanotubes (CNTs),<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit12"><sup><span class="sup_ref">1214</span></sup></a> carbon textiles,<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit15"><sup><span class="sup_ref">1518</span></sup></a> graphene<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit19"><sup><span class="sup_ref">1921</span></sup></a><span class="italic">etc</span>. are better current collectors for flexible energy-storage apparatus with their superior electronic conductivity and high flexibility. Prominently, CNTs are the most promising candidate as they have light weight and low density, which are favorable to improve the energy density of the full batteries. More significantly, the intrinsic nature of CNT substrates facilitates the fabrication of free-standing electrodes, which largely improves the electrode flexibility.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit14"><sup><span class="sup_ref">14,2224</span></sup></a> With the active materials firmly anchored on the carbon-based current collectors, the electrodes can accommodate high deformable states and alleviate the stress concentration. CNTs are constantly present in diverse forms such as CNT fibers, CNT films and aligned CNT arrays,<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit14"><sup><span class="sup_ref">14,25,26</span></sup></a> among which CNT films are considered as the most favorable candidate because the film can maintain an integrated structure even after drastic bending or soaking, showing tremendous potential application in flexible energy storage devices. CNT fibers and films can be manufactured <span class="italic">via</span> wet or dry approaches,<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit27"><sup><span class="sup_ref">27,28</span></sup></a> while the chemical vapor deposition (CVD) dry method exhibits the most considerable yield.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit29"><sup><span class="sup_ref">29,30</span></sup></a> This provides scalable and continuous production fundamentals for flexible battery materials with outstanding toughness and bending properties.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">In the present study, large-scale CNT films are prepared by a continuous CVD production method and then used to fabricate a self-supported flexible high-capacity conversion anode by the <span class="italic">in situ</span> growth of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets tightly anchored on CNTs. Transition bimetallic nickelcobalt sulfides are naturally abundant, highly conductive, and of high Na-storage capacity <span class="italic">via</span> electrochemical conversion reactions, making them an ideal choice for high-capacity SIB anode materials.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit31"><sup><span class="sup_ref">3138</span></sup></a> The CNTs produced <span class="italic">via</span> such a scalable method have interconnected porous channels, providing a large electrode/electrolyte contact area and are highly beneficial for the electrochemical conversion reaction of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>. The NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>/CNT film flexible electrode demonstrates a remarkable ultra-long cyclability with a high-capacity retention rate of 96% after 7500 cycles. It is confirmed that the abundant pores of CNT films and the self-standing structure of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets combined with CNTs can effectively buffer the volume changes involved by conversion reactions and prohibit nanoparticle aggregations, leading to the significantly optimized cyclability of flexible conversion electrodes. Furthermore, a flexible full SIB device was packaged with the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>/CNT anode, Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>/CNT cathode and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) thin film. This flexible full SIB exhibits good cycling stability and mechanical durability under different bending states, demonstrating great potential in practical large-scale application for flexible energy-storage devices.</p>
    
    
      
      <h2 id="sect301"><span class="a_heading">2. Experimental</span></h2>
      
        
        <h3 id="sect304"><span class="b_heading">2.1. Preparation of the carbon nanotube film (CF)</span></h3>
        <span>The large-scale carbon nanotube film was fabricated by chemical vapor deposition (CVD) as reported in our previous work.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit39"><sup><span class="sup_ref">39</span></sup></a> The preparation process is schematically shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig1">Fig. 1a</a>. Ethanol (60.0 wt%) and acetone (37.5 wt%) solutions were used as the carbon source, while ferrocene (1.5 wt%) and thiophene (1.0 wt%) were used as the catalyst and activator, respectively. The homogeneously mixed solution containing these raw materials was firstly atomized in a horizontal quartz to form a continuous carbon nanotube aerogel at 1100 C, and then flowed out by a mixture of nitrogen and hydrogen. Finally, the aerogel was collected by a moving carrier to form carbon nanotube films (denoted as CF).</span>
        <br><div class="image_table"><table><tbody><tr><td colspan="3" class="imgHolder" id="imgfig1"><a href="/image/article/2022/NR/d2nr02232j/d2nr02232j-f1_hi-res.gif" title="Select to open image in new window" onclick="open(this.href, &quot;_blank&quot;, &quot;toolbar=1,scrollbars=yes,resizable=1&quot;); return false;"><img alt="image file: d2nr02232j-f1.tif" src="/image/article/2022/NR/d2nr02232j/d2nr02232j-f1.gif"></a></td></tr><tr><td class="pushTitleRight"> </td><td class="image_title"><b>Fig. 1 </b> <span id="fig1"><span class="graphic_title">(a) Schematic illustration of the fabrication process of the large-scale CNT film, (b) photograph of the prepared CNT film, (c) scheme for the preparation process of the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>-CNT film (NCS-CF) electrode, (d) photograph of prepared flexible NCS-CF at various mechanical deformations.</span></span></td><td class="pushTitleLeft"> </td></tr></tbody></table></div>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect314"><span class="b_heading">2.2. 
          <span class="italic">In situ</span> growth of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> on the CNT film</span></h3>
        <span>Firstly, the CNT film (CF) was pretreated by refluxing in hydrochloric acid solution (20 vol%) with polyvinylpyrrolidone (0.005 g mL<small><sup>1</sup></small>) at 80 C for 8 h. Typically, 0.5 mmol Ni(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>2</sub></small>6H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O, 1 mmol Co(NO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>2</sub></small>6H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O and 2.5 mmol urea were added in 70 mL of methanol with ultrasonic treatment and stirring. Then the CNT film (30 mm  50 mm) was placed in the above solution and transferred to a 100 mL hydrothermal reactor and kept at 180 C for 12 h. The precursor-CF was washed with distilled water and dried at 80 C for 8 h. Then the dried precursor-CF was placed in a 50 mL homogeneous Na<small><sub>2</sub></small>S9H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O aqueous solution (0.025 mol L<small><sup>1</sup></small>) under solvothermal conditions of 90 C for 24 h. The NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>/CF flexible film was finally obtained and denoted as NCS-CF. The amount of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> grown on the CNT films is about 0.5 mg cm<small><sup>2</sup></small>. The NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> powder was synthesized and collected by the same process without the presence of the CNT film.</span>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect338"><span class="b_heading">2.3. Structural characterization</span></h3>
        <span>The structures of the as-prepared NCS-CF and NCS powder were measured by X ray diffraction (XRD, D8-Advance, CuK). The morphology and EDS element mapping were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM, SU8010) and scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM, Talos F200S). X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, VG-Multilab-2000) was conducted fixing the C 1s hydrocarbon band at 284.6 eV as calibration. <span class="italic">Ex situ</span> measurements were performed using electrodes at charge/discharge states after rinsing with dimethoxyethane (DME) in an Ar-filled glove box (Mikrouna, H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O and O<small><sub>2</sub></small> &lt; 0.1 ppm).</span>
      
      
        
        <h3 id="sect345"><span class="b_heading">2.4. Electrochemical measurements</span></h3>
        <span>The prepared NCS-CF material was directly used as the electrode to evaluate its Na-storage performance in half-cell and full flexible cell devices, respectively. The NCS powder electrode was fabricated with 70 wt% active material, 20 wt% conductive carbon (Super P) and 10 wt% CMC binder using the CNT film as a current collector. 2032 coin-type half cells were assembled using the fabricated working electrode, a metal Na counter electrode, a Whatman separator, and an electrolyte of NaPF<small><sub>6</sub></small> (1.0 mol L<small><sup>1</sup></small>) dissolved in DME. Galvanostatic charge/discharge was done on the LAND cycler (WuhanKingnuo, China). Cyclic voltammetry was finished using the electrochemical workstation (CHI660e, ChenHua, China). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed on a workstation (Ivium-n-Stat instrument, Netherlands) with the AC voltage amplitude of 5 mV in the frequency range of 10<small><sup>5</sup></small>10<small><sup>2</sup></small> Hz.</span>
        <p class="otherpara">For the assembly of the flexible full cell device, Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small> was used as the cathode material, which was prepared <span class="italic">via</span> a freeze-drying method.<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit40"><sup><span class="sup_ref">40</span></sup></a> The Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small> flexible cathode (denoted as NVP-CF) was prepared with the active material, Super P and PVDF (weight ratio of 8<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">1<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">1) coating on the CNT film. For the full cell assemble process, NVP-CF and NCS-CF films were cut into 30  50 mm pieces and used as the cathode and anode, respectively. The Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small> electrode was pre-activated in 1 M NaClO<small><sub>4</sub></small>-PC/5%FEC electrolyte for 2 cycles, and assembled with the NCS-CF anode into full cells using electrolyte 1 M NaClO<small><sub>4</sub></small> in diglyme (DGM) and a Celgard separator. The capacity of the cathode is 1.2 times that of the anode. The flexible packaging materials used PDMS which was fabricated with the polydimethylsiloxane prepolymer and a curing agent (Sylgard 184, 10<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">1 by volume). The full cell was initially encapsulated with semi-cured PDMS and then completely cured and sealed under vacuum conditions. Galvanostatic charge/discharge cycling of the full cells was performed within 0.33.2 V and the specific capacity is shown with the anode active material mass.</p>
      
    
    
      
      <h2 id="sect370"><span class="a_heading">3. Results and discussion</span></h2>
      <span>The CNT films were manufactured by a scalable chemical vapor deposition method. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig1">Fig. 1a and b</a>, a continuous preparation of CNT films is achieved with a large area (over 50 cm in length) and uniform thickness. The preparation process of the NCS-CF flexible electrode is shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig1">Fig. 1c</a>. Firstly, NiCo LDH precursors were <span class="italic">in situ</span> grown on the CNT film by a solvothermal method, in which the nanocrystalline nanosheets were interlaced with CNTs. Then the NCS material was obtained by ion exchange of S<small><sup>2</sup></small>, which maintained the nanosheet structures and remained tightly anchored on the CNT films. Various mechanical deformations of NCS-CF like bending, rolling, twisting and even folding multiple times are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig1">Fig. 1d</a>, visually verifying the superior flexibility and good mechanical properties of the as-prepared NCS-CF film.</span>
      <p class="otherpara">
        <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2a</a> shows the XRD patterns of the CNT film, NCS-CF and NCS powder (see Fig. S1<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> for the NiCo LDH precursor). The diffraction peaks of the synthesized NiCo intermediate powder correspond to Ni<small><sub>0.33</sub></small>Co<small><sub>0.66</sub></small>(CO<small><sub>3</sub></small>)<small><sub>0.5</sub></small>(OH)0.11H<small><sub>2</sub></small>O (JCPDS no. 48-0083). The crystallinity of the sample decreased significantly after the sulfurizing treatment, and broad diffraction peaks appeared in NCS-CF and NCS powder, which are ascribed to the cubic NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> (JCPDS no. 20-0782). The morphology and microstructure of the as-prepared CNT film, NiCo LDH-CF and NCS-CF film were observed by SEM. As displayed in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2b and c</a>, the CNT film is composed of interlaced CNTs with plenty of pores. The SEM image (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2d</a>) of the cross section indicates the thickness of the film is about 3.2 m. <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2e and f</a> show the SEM images of the NiCo LDH/CNT film. It appears as thin nanosheets uniformly interlinked with CNTs and anchored on the films. After being sulfurized by ion exchange reactions (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2g and h</a>), no remarkable changes of the nanosheet structure were observed. The size of the single NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanocrystalline sheet is about 500 nm. The CNTs are tightly interlaced with the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets, which would greatly improve the conductivity and firmly fix the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets on the film. The microstructure of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> powder was also characterized by SEM (Fig. S2<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>), in which the NCS powder also exhibits well-structured microflowers (68 m in diameter) assembled by thin nanosheets. The atomic ratio of Ni<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">Co<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">S is about 1<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">1.9<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">:<img class="charmap" src="https://www.rsc.org/images/entities/char_2009.gif" alt="[thin space (1/6-em)]">3.6, which is close to the theoretical ratio of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>.</p>

      <p class="otherpara">The microstructure and element mapping of the NCS-CF film was further observed <span class="italic">via</span> TEM. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3a</a>, NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets are composed of uniform nanocrystals with the grain size of 1020 nm. More importantly, after the drastic ultrasonic dispersion treatment, these NCS nanosheets were still closely attached to the CNTs. It is demonstrated that the NCS nanosheets were tightly anchored on the CNTs, which may effectively prevent the active materials from separating from the CNT film during mechanical deformation or chargedischarge cycling, thus ensuring the structural stability of the NCS-CF electrode. HRTEM (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3b</a>) identifies crystal lattice fringes of the grains in the NCS nanosheet, corresponding to the lattice plane (311) (<span class="italic">d</span> = 0.283 nm) of the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> phase. <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3c</a> shows the element distributions of the NCS-CF sample. Considerably uniform distributions are clearly observed for Ni, Co and S, further confirming that the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets were tightly adhered to CNTs.</p>

      <p class="otherpara">XPS characterization was further performed to confirm the chemical states of the NCS-CF sample. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3d and e</a>, there are three groups of peaks in the Ni 2p and Co 2p spectra, which can be well assigned to 2p<small><sub>1/2</sub></small> and 2p<small><sub>3/2</sub></small> peaks, along with a couple of satellites. For the Ni 2p spectrum (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3d</a>), the two dominant peaks at 856.1 and 874.2 eV are assigned to 2p<small><sub>3/2</sub></small> and 2p<small><sub>1/2</sub></small> of Ni<small><sup>3+</sup></small>, and the peaks at 853.3 and 873.3 eV are attributed to Ni<small><sup>2+</sup></small>.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit33"><sup><span class="sup_ref">33,34</span></sup></a> In the Co 2p spectrum (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3e</a>), the peaks with binding energies of 778.7 and 793.7 eV correspond to 2p<small><sub>3/2</sub></small> and 2p<small><sub>1/2</sub></small> of Co<small><sup>3+</sup></small> and those at 781.1 and 797.1 eV are assigned to Co<small><sup>2+</sup></small>.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit34"><sup><span class="sup_ref">34,37,41</span></sup></a> The S 2p spectrum (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig3">Fig. 3f</a>) exhibits three fitting peaks. The S 2p<small><sub>1/2</sub></small> and 2p<small><sub>3/2</sub></small> peaks correspond to the binding energies of NiS and CoS bonds that appear at 161.7 and 163.0 eV, respectively, while the peak at 168.7 eV is attributed to the satellite peak.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit33"><sup><span class="sup_ref">3335</span></sup></a> The XPS results indicate that the NCS-CF sample contains Ni<small><sup>2+</sup></small>, Ni<small><sup>3+</sup></small>, Co<small><sup>2+</sup></small>, Co<small><sup>3+</sup></small> and S<small><sup>2</sup></small>, in good agreement with the reported NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit33"><sup><span class="sup_ref">3337</span></sup></a></p>
      <p class="otherpara">
        <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4</a> shows the electrochemical performance of the NCS-CF and NCS powder electrodes. As seen in the CV curves (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4a</a>), the NCS-CF electrode shows redox peaks at 1.4/2.0 and 1.0/1.6 V after the initial cycle, which correspond to the Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> intercalation/deintercalation and conversion reactions, respectively.<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit35"><sup><span class="sup_ref">3537</span></sup></a> The NCS powder electrode shows redox peaks with similar potentials but with lower reproducibility (Fig. S3a<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>). <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4b and c</a> respectively show the charge/discharge profiles and cycling performance of NCS-CF (see Fig. S3b<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> for the NCS powder electrode). The charge/discharge plateaus are in accordance with the redox peaks in the CV curves (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4a</a>). NCS-CF provides charge/discharge capacities of 500/693 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at the first cycle at 200 mA g<small><sup>1</sup></small>, and a reversible capacity of 428 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> was maintained after 200 cycles (81% capacity retention). The capacity of blank CNT films is determined to be 70 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> (Fig. S4<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>), while the contribution to the whole NCS-CF electrode is negligible for the light weight of CNTs. In sharp contrast, NCS powder experienced a fast capacity fading from 667 to 186 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> within 100 cycles (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4c</a>, 28% capacity retention). This comparison suggests the microflower morphology could not enhance the cycling stability, and anchoring the NCS nanosheets on the conductive CNT networks is a more efficient strategy to maintain the electrochemical activity.</p>

        <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4d</a> shows a comparison of the rate performance of the NCS-CF and NCS powder electrodes (see Fig. S5<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> for the charge/discharge curves). NCS-CF delivers reversible capacities of 420, 415, 400, 380, 360, 328, 305 and 280 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at the current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0, 10 and 20 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>, respectively. It is highlighted that 67% capacity could be retained at a current density as high as 20 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>, which is favorable for the prompt responsibility of high power energy storage. Moreover, the capacity recovers to the original level when the current density returns to 0.2 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>. On the other hand, the NCS powder electrode delivers only 15% capacity at 20 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>, and only 55% capacity could be maintained upon recovery of the current density. A comparison of the rate capability with reported results indicates the prominent advantages of the NCS-CF electrode at high current density (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4e</a>).<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit36"><sup><span class="sup_ref">3638,4247</span></sup></a> The impedance spectra demonstrate that NCS-CF shows much lower resistances than the NCS powder (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4f</a>). The compact composite of NCS nanosheets and highly conductive CNTs construct large domains favoring electrochemical reactions with fast kinetics, thus enhancing the rate capability. Such an electrode design also benefits the long-term cycling. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig4">Fig. 4g</a>, the NCS-CF electrode shows an extraordinarily high cycling stability at a high current density of 1 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>, retaining a reversible capacity of 454 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> (473 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at the initial cycle) after 7500 ultra-long cycles with an astonishing capacity retention rate of 96% as well as stable coulombic efficiency all through the cycling. However, the NCS powder electrode delivers an initial capacity of 460 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> and declines to 230 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> after 200 cycles with 50% capacity retention. This outstanding cycling stability of the NCS-CF electrode is attributed to the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets tightly intertwined with the CNT matrix, which effectively prevents active material aggregation and maintains the structure stability of the whole electrode during the ultra-long cycling.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The reaction mechanism and morphology evolution were elucidated <span class="italic">via ex situ</span> XRD, XPS, TEM and SEM characterization studies. As described in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5a</a>, upon the first discharge to 0.01 V (from state a to c), the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> peaks vanish while the Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> and Co<small><sup>0</sup></small> peaks appear at 45.5 and 53.5, demonstrating the structure conversion from NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> to Ni and Co metals. In the subsequent charge process to 3.0 V (from state c to e), the Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> and Co<small><sup>0</sup></small> peaks disappear and a series of tiny peaks arise at 27, 38 and 50, corresponding to the phases of Ni<small><sub>9</sub></small>S<small><sub>8</sub></small> (JCPDS no. 22-1193) and Co<small><sub>3</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> (JCPDS no. 02-1361).<a title="Select to navigate to references" href="#cit33"><sup><span class="sup_ref">33</span></sup></a> Further to the second full discharge state (point g), Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> and Co<small><sup>0</sup></small> peaks appear again. The XRD results above confirm the reversible conversion mechanism, and the reaction is mainly between Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small>/Co<small><sup>0</sup></small> metals and Ni<small><sub>9</sub></small>S<small><sub>8</sub></small>/Co<small><sub>3</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> after the first cycle. To further confirm the reaction mechanism, the NCS electrode at the fully discharged and charged state was observed by HRTEM. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5(b)</a>, the lattice fringes with a <span class="italic">d</span>-space of 0.17 and 0.20 nm appear at the fully discharged state, corresponding to the (200) and (111) planes of metallic Co or Ni, respectively. When the electrode was fully charged (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5c</a>), the lattice fringes of the Ni<small><sub>9</sub></small>S<small><sub>8</sub></small> (222) and Co<small><sub>3</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> (220) phase was observed. <span class="italic">Ex situ</span> XPS was also employed to confirm the reaction mechanism. As shown in the Ni 2p spectra (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5b</a>), the pristine Ni<small><sup>2+</sup></small> and Ni<small><sup>3+</sup></small> bands are located at 853.1, 872.2 eV and 855.6, 873.5 eV, respectively. At the discharge state of 0.01 V, the Ni<small><sup>2+</sup></small> and Ni<small><sup>3+</sup></small> bands decrease and new bands of Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> appear at 852.3 and 869.8 eV. At the charge state of 3.0 V, the Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> bands vanish and Ni<small><sup>2+</sup></small> and Ni<small><sup>3+</sup></small> bands reemerge. The Co 2p spectra shows a similar trend (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig5">Fig. 5c</a>). The Co<small><sup>2+</sup></small> (780.9 eV, 796.6 eV) and Co<small><sup>3+</sup></small> (778.6 eV, 793.6 eV) peaks decrease/increase and Co<small><sup>0</sup></small> (778.0 eV, 792.8 eV) peaks increase/decrease in the discharge/charge states. The XPS results are in good agreement with the XRD and HRTEM characterization, and demonstrate the conversion mechanism of the NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small>-CF electrode for the Na-storage reactions as follows:<a title="Select to navigate to reference" href="#cit34"><sup><span class="sup_ref">34,37</span></sup></a>At the first discharge,</p><table><tbody><tr><td class="leftEqn">&nbsp;</td></tr><tr><td class="eqn"><span id="eqn1"><span id="eqn1">NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> + 8Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> + 8e<small><sup></sup></small>  4Na<small><sub>2</sub></small>S + Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> + 2Co<small><sup>0</sup></small></span></span></td><td class="rightEqn">(1)</td></tr></tbody></table>At subsequent chargedischarge cycles,<table><tbody><tr><td class="leftEqn">&nbsp;</td></tr><tr><td class="eqn"><span id="eqn2"><span id="eqn2"><span class="italic">x</span>Ni<small><sup>0</sup></small> + Na<small><sub>2</sub></small>S  2Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> + Ni<small><sub><span class="italic">x</span></sub></small>S + 2e<small><sup></sup></small></span></span></td><td class="rightEqn">(2)</td></tr></tbody></table><table><tbody><tr><td class="leftEqn">&nbsp;</td></tr><tr><td class="eqn"><span id="eqn3"><span id="eqn3"><span class="italic">x</span>Co<small><sup>0</sup></small> + Na<small><sub>2</sub></small>S  2Na<small><sup>+</sup></small> + Co<small><sub><span class="italic">x</span></sub></small>S + 2e<small><sup></sup></small>.</span></span></td><td class="rightEqn">(3)</td></tr></tbody></table><p></p>

      <p class="otherpara">The cycled electrode was also observed by SEM to investigate the structure stability of the NCS-CF electrode (Fig. S6<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>). As seen in Fig. S6(a and b),<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> the microflower structure of the NCS powder electrode was unidentifiable after long cycling with serious structural changes as well as pulverization, thus causing the fast capacity fading. In contrast, as seen in Fig. S6(c and d),<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> the NCS-CF electrode after long cycling still maintains the original morphology as the pristine state (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig2">Fig. 2</a>), demonstrating the firm anchoring of <span class="italic">in situ</span> grown NCS nanosheets on the CNT films, which improves the durable cyclability of the NCS-CF electrode.</p>
      <p class="otherpara">The kinetic properties of NCS-CF electrode were further evaluated by CV tests and are shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig6">Fig. 6</a> (see Fig. S7<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> for the NCS powder electrode). <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig6">Fig. 6a</a> shows the CV profiles of the NCS-CF electrode at different scan rates and <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig6">Fig. 6b</a> shows the relationship between the peak current and scan rate (oxidation peak A and reduction peak C). The <span class="italic">b</span> value approaches about 1.0, indicating the electrode reaction is predominated by a surface-controlled process. <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig6">Fig. 6c</a> shows the pseudo capacitive contributions at various scan rates (the calculation details are provided in Fig. S7<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>). The pseudocapacitance increases with increasing scan rate, achieving 87% at 1.0 mV s<small><sup>1</sup></small> (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig6">Fig. 6d</a>). Comparatively, the NCS-CF electrode shows pseudocapacitive contributions much higher than the NCS powder electrode (Fig. S7<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>). This is owing to the larger specific surface area of NCS-CF, which facilitates the interface charge transfer reactions. This also explains the much better rate capability of NCS-CF than the NCS powder electrode.</p>

(c) Pseudocapacitive contributions of the NCS-CF and NCS powder electrodes (d) CV curves with pseudocapacitance of NCS-CF at 1.0 mV s<small><sup>1</sup></small>.</span></span></td><td class="pushTitleLeft"> </td></tr></tbody></table></div>
      <p class="otherpara">The flexible NVP-CF//NCS-CF full Na-ion battery was further fabricated using the NCS-CF anode and NVP-CF cathode (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig7">Fig. 7a</a>). The NVP-CF cathode uses Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small> as the active material (see Fig. S8 and S9<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> for the XRD pattern and SEM images) and the as-prepared CNT film as the current collector. The flexible full battery device was assembled using polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), to ensure the compact contact for the electrodes and air tightness for the battery. As seen in Fig. S10,<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> the battery is transparent and highly flexible, showing high tolerance for mechanical deformation. In order to confirm the compatibility of the NCS-CF anode and NVP-CF cathode with the electrolyte, the charge/discharge curves and cycling performance of the electrodes were tested in electrolyte of 1 M NaClO<small><sub>4</sub></small> in diglyme (DGM) (Fig. S11 and S12<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a>). <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig7">Fig. 7b</a> shows the CV curves of the NVP-CF and NCS-CF electrodes, in which sharp redox peaks are observed at 3.35/3.40 V and 0.95/1.55 V. Thus, the voltage window for the NVP-CF//NCS-CF full cell was set between 0.3 and 3.2 V. Fig. 7c<a title="Select to navigate to footnote" href="#fn1"></a> shows the charge/discharge curves of the full battery. Sloping charge/discharge plateaus are centered at 2.0 V, which are in good agreement with redox potential difference between the NVP-CF cathode and NCS-CF anode (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig7">Fig. 7b</a>). The discharge plateau is stable with cycling, and the charge plateau even decreases during the first 10 cycles, confirming the steady output voltage. The full battery exhibits a stable cycling (<a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig7">Fig. 7d</a>), providing a capacity of 253 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at the 50th cycle (based on the mass of NCS). Furthermore, the performance of the full cell device under operation at different bending states was also demonstrated. As shown in <a title="Select to navigate to figure" href="#imgfig7">Fig. 7e</a>, the single full battery could light 51 red LEDs (nominal voltage of 1.8 V) in parallel, and the brightness does not decrease on bending the device in different directions. This demonstrates the outstanding performance of the flexible NVP-CF//NCS-CF full Na-ion battery, and indicates the potential applications in flexible electronic devices.</p>

      <h2 id="sect614"><span class="a_heading">4. Conclusions</span></h2>
      <span>In summary, large-scale CNT films were prepared by a continuous CVD production method, and then used to fabricate a self-supported flexible electrode (NCS-CF) by the <span class="italic">in situ</span> growth of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets tightly anchored on a CNT film. The NCS-CF electrode shows superior flexibility and good mechanical properties withstanding bending, rolling, twisting and folding. As a Na-storage anode, NCS-CF has a large specific surface area facilitating interface charge transfer reactions, and thus exhibiting a superior rate capability, delivering a reversible capacity of 280 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at the current density as high as 20 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small>. The NCS-CF electrode retains 454 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at 1 A g<small><sup>1</sup></small> with a capacity retention rate of 96% over 7500 cycles, indicating that the interlaced construction of NiCo<small><sub>2</sub></small>S<small><sub>4</sub></small> nanosheets with CNTs effectively maintains the firm structure of the electrode during the electrochemical conversion process, guaranteeing an extraordinarily ultra-long cyclability. The flexible full Na-ion battery device based on the NCS-CF anode and Na<small><sub>3</sub></small>V<small><sub>2</sub></small>(PO<small><sub>4</sub></small>)<small><sub>3</sub></small>/CF cathode with the sealed PDMS package exhibits a stable cycling with a capacity of 253 mA h g<small><sup>1</sup></small> at the 50th cycle, as well as mechanical durability under different bending states. The present study certifies a high-performance scalable flexible battery electrode material, and shows its practical applications in flexible Na-ion batteries.</span>
