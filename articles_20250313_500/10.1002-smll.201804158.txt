Sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> kinetics property leads to large electrode polarization, resulting in poor rate and cyclic performances. Herein, an electrode of ultrasmall tin nanoparticles decorated in N, S codoped carbon monolith (TCM) with exceptional high‐rate capability and ultrastable cycling behavior for Na‐storage is reported. The resulted TCM electrode exhibits an extremely high retention of 96% initial charge capacity after 500 cycles at a current density of 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Significantly, when the current density is elevated to an ultrahigh rate of 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, a high reversible capacity of 228 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after the 2000th cycle is still maintained. More importantly, the stable and fast Na‐storage of TCM is investigated and understood by experimental characterizations and kinetics calculations, including interfacial ion/electron transport behavior, ion diffusion, and quantitative pseudocapacitive analysis. These investigations elucidate that the TCM shows improved ion/electron conductivity and enhanced interfacial kinetics. An entirely new perspective to deep insights into the fast ion/electron transport mechanisms revealed by interfacial kinetics of sodiation/desodiation, which contributes to the profound understanding for developing fast charging/discharging and long‐term stable electrodes in sodium‐ion batteries, is provided.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201804158-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Fast ion/electron transport</b> is realized by decorating ultrasmall tin nanoparticles on N, S codoped carbon monolith (TCM). The interfacial transport mechanism is elucidated by experimental characterizations and kinetics calculations. Excellent rate capability and ultrastable cycling performance of Na‐storage are mainly contributed by the fast interfacial kinetics and enhanced pseudocapacitive storage of TCM.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201804158:smll201804158-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201804158-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201804158-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll201804158-sec-0010"><label role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion battery (SIB), based on the relatively low cost and abundant sodium, has attracted significant attention as a potential alternative energy storage system to lithium‐ion battery (LIB). It is found that even lithium and sodium both belong to the alkali metal sequence, the success of commercial graphite anode in the LIBs can hardly be replicated for Na‐storage because of the different radius of the two elements.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0001"/> Hence, it is extremely urgent to find appropriate anode materials to realize satisfactory sodium storage performance.</p><p>Recently, some extensive research efforts focus on carbonaceous materials,<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0002"/> alloy reaction material,<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0003"/> metal oxides, and metal sulfides,<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0004"/> which have achieved some achievements in Na‐storage, indicating that there is a great prospect for the development of SIB electrodes. Among the aforementioned materials, benefiting from the environment‐benignity, highly natural abundance, and high theoretical sodium storage capacity, the tin anode is deemed as a promising electrode candidate for SIBs.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0005"/> In the recent researches, tin‐based anodes were fabricated as advanced SIB anodes with various nanostructure. Xu et al. reported porous Sn/C with initial charge capacity of 295 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 mA<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0006"/> Liu et al. also synthesized tin nanoparticles uniformly embedded in carbon spheres, showing a capacity of 415 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in SIBs.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007"/> Zhu et al. reported Sn@wood fiber composite with an initial capacity of 339 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and stable cycling performance of 400 cycles.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0008"/> Although much progress has been made in these researches, the high‐rate performance and long‐term cyclic ability of tin‐based anodes still need to be improved for SIBs applications. Based on this situation, it is necessary to study the storage mechanism of sodium batteries to guide and explore the development of electrode materials of advanced SIBs.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0009"/> Very lately, some advanced detection techniques have been used to study the mechanism of Na‐storage in electrode materials. However, most of these characterizations gather in the study of the lattice change and phase transition of the electrode materials in the sodiation/desodiation.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0010"/></p><p>Remarkably, the sodium charge storage performances are mainly limited by the transport of Na<sup>+</sup> and interfacial kinetics. It ought not to ignore that the formation of solid electrolyte interface (SEI) film and its electrochemical stability influence the ion diffusion rate and thus determine the cyclic stability and rate performance of charge storage.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0011"/> Nevertheless, the correlation between interfacial charge transport behavior and the resulted electrochemical properties of advanced SIB anode in the initial cycle has not been studied in depth until now.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0012"/> Therefore, it is very important to study such interfacial ion/electron transport properties of anode materials with high rate and ultrastable cycling abilities.</p><p>At the same time, previous reports have shown that surface control capacitive‐controlled pseudocapacitive charge storage is closely related to the electrode interfacial reaction kinetics, which has demonstrated superior fast charge/discharge and cycle stability characteristics.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0013"/> And it has been reported that the pseudocapacitive behavior contribution of the system could be significantly enhanced by introducing highly nanosized active materials particle (&lt;10 nm), resulting in boost charge storage and fast charge/discharge kinetics of the electrode material.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0014"/> In addition, 3D host materials with various porous structure have also been reported to be effective in preventing the aggregation of nanosized active particles and accommodating their volume changes.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007 #smll201804158-bib-0015"/> Moreover, the porous structure is also beneficial for shortening electrolyte penetration path, providing an easily accessible surface area, and enhanced electronic conductivity, thus further improving the Na‐storage abilities.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0016"/></p><p>In this work, we report an anode of ultrasmall tin nanoparticles (&lt;5 nm) uniformly decorated in N, S codoped carbon monolith (TCM) with high‐rate and ultrastable sodium storage performance. Benefiting from the 3D interconnected porous carbonaceous structural scaffold and uniformly decorated ultrasmall tin nanoparticles, the TCM electrode can deliver an extremely high retention of 96% after 500 cycles at a current density of 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. And at a high current density of 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the electrode also exhibits a high reversible capacity of 228 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles, demonstrating its superior cycle stability and rate capability.</p><p>More importantly, through the careful and intensive study of TCM, we systematically expound the correlation between ultrafast Na‐storage performances and interfacial kinetics via experimental characterizations and kinetics calculations, including interfacial ion/electron transport behavior, ion diffusion, and quantitative pseudocapacitive analysis. These profound understanding will have great guidance for the development of advanced sodium‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201804158-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>In a typical fabrication process, graphene oxide (GO) was coated with melamine formaldehyde (MF) resin to form a suspension. SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates were separately prepared through a hydrothermal process. The scanning electron microscope (SEM) image of the as‐obtained SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates is shown in Figure S1a (Supporting Information). The detailed fabrication process of GO@MF and SnS<sub>2</sub> is illustrated in the Experimental Section. Simply by drying the mixture of GO@MF and SnS<sub>2</sub> dispersed solution and then annealing at a carbonization temperature of 700 °C, the TCM was obtained (inset in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a). MF resin was used to act as not only a nitrogen source, but also a hard template to inhibit the restacking of carbon nanosheets, thus shaping a 3D interconnected porous structure during carbonization. It should be noted that the mixture amount of GO@MF and SnS<sub>2</sub> dispersed solution was carefully controlled with no excessive SnS<sub>2</sub>. The TCM precursor was pyrolyzed during the annealing process, triggering the in situ reduction of SnS<sub>2</sub> to Sn and S. Meanwhile, a small amount of S elements participates in the carbonization process and was doped into the carbon lattice, resulting in a tin nanoparticle decorated, N, S codoped carbon monolith. For comparison, carbon monolith (CM) was also fabricated through similar experimental procedure without the addition of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates. The SEM image of CM is shown in Figure S1b (Supporting Information). Besides, commercial pure tin nanoparticle (TNP) was examined for further performance comparison (Figure S1c, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smll201804158-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201804158:smll201804158-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201804158-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM images of TCM: A piece under a low magnification (insets are the optical images of the monolithic structure). b,c) STEM images of TCM. d) STEM image and the corresponding elemental mapping images of TCM. e–g) HRTEM images of TCM and the corresponding SAED pattern of TCM.</caption></figure><p>The SEM image under a low magnification shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"/>a confirms the monolithic 3D structure of as‐obtained TCM, indicating its good structural integrity. The wrinkled morphologies accompanied with interconnected carbon nanosheets and abundant pores are observed from the SEM images under higher magnifications in Figure S1d (Supporting Information), indicating a highly porous structure. Such unique 3D structure can evidently facilitate the rapid ion insertion and extraction, shorten the electrolyte penetration path, and provide the buffer space for volume expansion during cycling, which is favorable for enhanced sodium storage electrochemical performance. It should be noted that no large tin particles are observed from the SEM images. The scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) was applied to gain further insight into the microstructure of TCM. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"/>b,c, the ultrasmall tin nanoparticles with mean size of around 5 nm are uniformly distributed in the carbonaceous framework. Despite the similar SEM images of TCM and CM, the introduction of tin nanoparticles in TCM is affirmed by comparing its TEM image with that of CM (Figure S2, Supporting Information). The STEM image of TCM and the corresponding elemental mappings are shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"/>d that clearly confirmed the existence and uniform distribution of C, Sn, N, and S in the whole TCM structure. And the high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images further demonstrate that the tin nanoparticles with ultrasmall size (&lt;5 nm) uniformly decorated in carbonaceous structural scaffold (Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"/>e). Furthermore, from the corresponding selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of TCM in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"/>f, diffraction rings marked by the white arrow can be clearly observed, which corresponds to the (200), (220), (301), and (321) crystalline planes of tin.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0005 #smll201804158-bib-0017"/> The diffraction rings marked by the yellow arrow correspond to the porous carbon framework. Additionally, as demonstrated in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0001"/>g, the lattice fringes of tin nanoparticles exhibit an interplanar spacing of 2.91 Å, which corresponds to the (200) plane of tin nanoparticles.[<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007 #smll201804158-bib-0015" style="qv">7,15a</link>] And the interplanar spacing of carbon is measured to be ≈3.72 Å, corresponding to the expanded (002) plane of graphitic carbon.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0018"/></p><p>In order to investigate the crystalline structure of the as‐obtained TCM, X‐ray diffraction (XRD) was applied. As revealed in Figure S3 (Supporting Information), the broad peak detected at around 24° can be assigned to (002) plane of carbon content, indicating its amorphous nature.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0019"/> The broad peaks with weak intensities diffraction peaks can be indexed to the tetragonal tin (JCPDS Card No. 04‐0673), which is consistent with the results of SAED patterns, indicating the small crystalline size of tin.[<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007 #smll201804158-bib-0015" style="qv">7,15a</link>] <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a shows the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption analysis and the pore size distribution of TCM. The Brunauer−Emmett−Teller (BET) measurement surface is 167 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, and the distinct hysteresis loop at relative pressure <i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub> ranging from 0.4 to 1.0 indicates the presence of mesoporous. The pore size distribution curve of TCM was calculated from adsorption branch by the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) method, as shown in the inset of Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>a. The sizes of the majority of the mesopores fall within a relatively narrow range of 10–15 nm. The rich porous structure and suitable specific surface area can enhance the wettability of electrode and fast ion/electron transportation.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0020"/> As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>b, the content of tin in TCM was investigated by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) in O<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. The fast decrease of the curve before 150 °C is attributed to the evaporation of adsorbed water. After that, the curve go through a stable period until 410 °C, which is attributed to the simultaneous occurrence for formation of tin oxide (Sn + O<sub>2</sub>→SnO<sub>2</sub>) and carbon combustion (C + O<sub>2</sub>→CO<sub>2</sub>↑). When all tin is converted to tin oxide, the curve declines rapidly until 600 °C, which is attributed to the complete consumption combustion of carbon. Therefore, it could be calculated that the content of tin in TCM was ≈33.1%.</p><figure xml:id="smll201804158-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201804158:smll201804158-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201804158-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherm curves and the pore size distribution curve (inset) of TCM. b) TGA curves of TCM. c–f) XPS spectra of C 1s, N 1s, S 2p, and Sn 3d from TCM.</caption></figure><p>The chemical composition of TCM was characterized by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis. The three peaks exhibited in C 1s XPS spectra are attributed to CC, CN, and C(O)OR bonding, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>c). As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>d, the N 1s XPS spectra exhibit three dominated peaks at 398.6, 400.4, and 401.7 eV, corresponding to pyridinic nitrogen (N‐6), pyrrolic nitrogen (N‐5), and graphitic nitrogen (N‐Q), respectively. In Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>e, the XPS spectra of S 2p of TCM show two peaks at 163.6 and 164.9 eV, corresponding to S 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> peaks for the CSC covalent bond.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0019 #smll201804158-bib-0021"/> These results further confirm the successful codoping of N and S. Remarkably, according to previous reports, doping heteroatoms in carbon can enlarge interlayer distance, create defective sites, and enhance the electronic conductivity, thus facilitating the Na‐ion insertion/extraction and boosting sodium storage ability. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>f, two strong individual peaks at 486.9 and 495.3 eV are related to Sn 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Sn 3d<sub>3/2</sub>, respectively, indicating that at the surface tin nanoparticles are partially oxidized to SnO<sub>2</sub>.[<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0015" style="qv">15a</link>] Raman spectra of TCM and CM in Figure S4 (Supporting Information) both show two characteristic peaks of D‐band and G‐band at ≈1350 and 1580 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. It can be clearly observed that the TCM sample exhibits higher intensity ratio of D to G band (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) than that of CM sample, indicating a higher content of structural defects due to the decoration of tin and codoping of nitrogen and sulfur. This highly defective structure can provide more active sites for sodium storage, thus enhancing its reversible capacity.</p><p>To explore its sodium storage mechanism, cyclic voltammetry (CV) of TCM was also investigated. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a shows the initial three CV curves of TCM at a scanning rate of 0.05 mV s<sup>−1</sup> within the potential range of 0.005–3 V. During the first cathodic sweep, a broad irreversible peak from 1.0 to 1.5 V is observed for TCM, which disappears from the following cycles, corresponding to the formation of the SEI film.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007 #smll201804158-bib-0022"/> Similar observation is found in the CV curve of CM (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Compared with that of CM, an additional reduction peak at around 0.3 V is originated from the formation of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sn alloy in the first sodiation process.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007"/> In the anodic sweep, four well‐defined peaks at 0.23, 0.27, 0.58, and 0.82 V can be ascribed to the alloy formation of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>9</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, NaSn, and NaSn<sub>5</sub>, respectively.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0022 #smll201804158-bib-0023"/></p><figure xml:id="smll201804158-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201804158:smll201804158-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201804158-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV curves of TCM in the first three cycles at a scanning rate of 0.05 mV s<sup>−1</sup> within the potential range of 0.005–3 V. b) Charge–discharge curves of TCM electrode at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for the first three cycles. c) Rate capability of TCM at different current densities from 100 to 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. d,e) Long‐term cycle stability of TCM at high current densities of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for 1000 cycles, and 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for 2000 cycles. f,g) Cycling performance of TCM, CM, and TNP electrodes at current densities of 100 and 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The comparisons of electrochemical performances for the TCM electrodes with other reported results: h) the cyclic stability and i) the rate capability.</caption></figure><p>The sodium storage performance of the TCM anode was systematically evaluated by galvanostatic discharge–charge cycling in a voltage window of 0.005–3 V. Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0002"/>b shows the voltage profiles of TCM in the first three cycles at a current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. A relatively high initial reversible capacity of 455 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is delivered, corresponding to a Coulombic efficiency of 48%. The irreversible capacity loss in the first sodiation/desodiation cycle can be assigned to the formation of SEI film on the electrode surface.[<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0007 #smll201804158-bib-0015" style="qv">7,15a</link>] However, it should be noted that the Coulombic efficiency reached over 90% from the second cycle, demonstrating a high reversibility. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>c, the TCM anode was tested at increasing current densities from 100 to 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for five cycles at each rate to evaluate the rate capability. Impressively, TCM can deliver the reversible capacities of 372, 333, 307, 280, 250, and 230 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> obtained at 100, 200, 500, 1000, 2000, and 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The electrode shows minor drops upon increasing current density, and virtually no capacity decay is exhibited at each rate, demonstrating an excellent high‐rate stability. Besides, when the current density is abruptly returned to the origin value of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after high‐rate cycling, the reversible capacity of TCM anode is recovered to 344 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating its good tolerance of large charge/discharge currents.</p><p>The high‐rate and long‐term cycling property is an important parameter for the practical application of SIBs and was further examined for TCM electrodes. As revealed in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>d, the TCM electrode delivers a reversible capacity of 283 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. A relatively rapid charge capacity drop in the beginning of cycling was attributed to the inevitable electrolyte decomposition and the SEI formation in the initial cycles process.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0024"/> After 1000 cycles, a high reversible capacity of 250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is delivered. More impressively, upon further increasing the current density to 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>e, the TCM electrode still exhibits stable long‐term cycling performance. After 2000 cycles, the electrode is stabilized at a reversible capacity of 228 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of nearly 100%, demonstrating an exceptional high‐rate and long‐term stability. Meanwhile, the cycling performance of CM and TNP electrode is also investigated in SIBs. Compared with CM and TNP, the TCM exhibits a much better Na‐storage performance. Specifically, in the cycling performance comparisons of TCM, CM, and TNP electrodes are evaluated at different current densities as shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>f,g, TCM outperforms CM and TNP in terms of charge capacity throughout the testing period at two different current densities.</p><p>Furthermore, the electrochemical performance comparisons of TCM and other Sn/C composites in literature for SIBs are shown in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>h,i and Table S1 (Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>h demonstrates a comparison of capacity retention properties under same current densities.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0022 #smll201804158-bib-0023 #smll201804158-bib-0025"/> Compared to previous reports, the TCM electrode exhibits the most stable cycling performances with the capacity retention maintains over 90% in 200 cycles. In a comparison of the rate performance for TCM with other reported Sn/C composites in Figure <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0003"/>i,[<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0025 #smll201804158-bib-0008" style="qv">25a,c,d,8</link>] it can be obviously observed that the TCM electrode reveals the best capacity retention properties at high rates. Even at a high current density of 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, TCM can also deliver 50% capacity of that at low current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating its outstanding rate capability. The TCM electrode exhibits such outstanding high‐rate performance and long‐term cycling stability, which is mainly ascribed to the following three reasons: (a) 3D interconnected porous carbonaceous structure offers high integrity and durability electrolyte/electrode interface for the charge transfer reaction, which obviously enhance interfacial kinetics that facilitates ultrafast electron transfer and ion transport, as well as prevent pulverization and aggregation of ultrasmall tin nanoparticles; (b) the uniformly ultrasmall tin nanoparticles (&lt;5 nm) decorated in carbonaceous structure greatly reduce the interface resistance, facilitate the formation of homogeneous, robust, and high conductive SEI, shorten the ion diffusion path, and buffer the mechanical stress induced by the volume changes; (c) the introduction of nitrogen and sulfur in carbon framework can expand its interlayer distance, create more defect sites, and enhance the electronic conductivity.</p><p>In addition, the satisfactory cyclic stability and rate capability can be further supported by the structure retention ability. Figure S6 (Supporting Information) shows the STEM image of TCM electrode after 500 cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The electrode still preserves its original appearance compared to preceding STEM image before cycling, and the tin nanoparticles (white dots) still retain their ultrasmall sizes without severe agglomeration, demonstrating the high structural integrity and durability of TCM during electrochemical tests, which is highly beneficial to the sodium storage performance.</p><p>Interfacial transport behavior is of immense importance to the electrochemical performance of electrode materials.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0026"/> In order to gain further insight into the outstanding sodium storage stability and rate capability of TCM, through the combination of in situ electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (in situ EIS) and galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) to inspect the kinetics and interfacial phenomena of electrode during the first sodiation process, including characteristics of microinterface change between electrolyte and electrode, the formation mechanism of SEI films, charge transfer resistance, ionic diffusion, and so on.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0011 #smll201804158-bib-0027"/> As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201804158-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a,b, the in situ EIS curves are recorded for CM and TCM electrodes during the first discharge processes at a current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. During the initial discharge stage, <i>R</i><sub>2</sub> decreases rapidly, followed by a slow downward trend after the depth of discharge reaches middle stage and raises slightly at the last stage of discharge. Compared to CM electrode, TCM exhibits smaller charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>2</sub>) in the whole sodiation process, which is attributed to the enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> migration and faster electron transportation enabled by good ion/electron combination at electrochemical active sites and tin decoration, respectively, leading to an excellent interface with smaller electrochemical polarizations and concentration polarizations.<link href="#smll201804158-bib-0031"/> Therefore, TCM can achieve outstanding rate capability in SIBs. Specifically, the obtained TCM electrode delivers an outstanding capacity retention (96% maintained after 500th cycle, at a current density of 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), and an ultralong‐term stability (250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000th cycle, at a current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). Further analysis of ion diffusion shows that the ultrasmall tin nanoparticles (&lt;5 nm) play a crucial role to facilitate faster Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion. Meanwhile, kinetics analysis reveals a high pseudocapacitive contribution in TCM, which is highly beneficial to outstanding high‐rate capacity and cyclic stability. Melamine (99%), tin nanoparticles (99.9%), and formaldehyde aqueous solution (37 wt%) were purchased from Aladdin. Tin chloride pentahydrate (SnCl<sub>4</sub> · 5H<sub>2</sub>O) and thioacetamide (TAA) were purchased from Sigma Aldrich. All chemicals were used without further purification.</p><p><i>Synthesis of CM</i>: 3.3 mL formaldehyde aqueous solution (37 wt%) and 1.875 g melamine were dissolved into 30 mL deionized water, and then constant temperature oil was heated at 70 °C for ≈20 min. Until the mixture turns transparent, 20 mL GO solution (5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added into the solution, the precursor solution of CM was obtained. After that, the precursor solution of CM was heated at 98 °C for 3 h. Then the gray‐green product was obtained. The obtained product was centrifuged (8000 rpm, 8 min) for three times and washed with deionized water. After drying at 70 °C for 6 h, the product was then annealed in the tube furnace under the protection of Ar atmosphere to a carbonization temperature of 700 °C for 1 h with a ramping rate of 1 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. Finally, after cooling down naturally to room temperature, the CM was obtained.</p><p><i>Synthesis of SnS<sub>2</sub></i>: 1.0518 g SnCl<sub>4</sub> · 5H<sub>2</sub>O and 1.3524 g TAA were mixed in 30 mL deionized water and stirred for 20 min. Then the mixture was transferred into a 50 mL autoclave and hydrothermally treated at 160 °C for 24 h. The obtained product was collected by centrifugation (10 000 rpm, 20 min) and washed with deionized water three times. Finally, the SnS<sub>2</sub> solution was obtained.</p><p><i>Synthesis of TCM</i>: 5 mL precursor solution of CM and 0.2 mL SnS<sub>2</sub> solution were mixed, then dried at 70 °C for 7 h. The product was put into a tubular furnace heated to 700 °C under the Ar protective atmosphere for 1 h at the rate of 1 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. Finally, the TCM was obtained.</p><p><i>Materials Characterization</i>: The morphology of CM and TCM was observed by FESEM (SU8010, Hitachi) and TEM (FEI Tecnai G20, FEI NanoPorts). The BET surface area of TCM was measured using N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption analysis (Tristar II 3020, Micromeritics). XRD patterns were recorded by a powder XRD (D8 Advance, Bruker) with a Cu target (Kα, λ = 0.15406 nm). Surface elemental analysis was characterized by XPS (Escalab 250Xi, Thermo Fisher). Raman spectra were collected on a LabRAM HR Evolution Raman spectrometer (HORIBA Jobin Yvon). Density was measured by the automatic true density analyzer (UltraPYC 1200e, Quantachrome). TGA was performed on a TG/DTA instruments analyzer (7300, SII Nano). The loading of electrode active materials on Cu current collectors was ≈1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Pure sodium metal was used as the counter electrode and 0.8 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in EC:DEC (1:1) was used as the electrolyte. Whatman glass fiber (GF/A) was used as a separator. The cells were assembled in an argon‐filled glove box. Besides, the Na<sup>+</sup> full cell performances were tested by using TCM and NVP/C as anode and cathode, respectively. NVP/C was presodiated before assembly. Galvanostatic charge–discharge properties were tested on battery testing system (Land CT2001A, P. R. China), where the voltage range was from 0.005 to 3 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. CV and GITT were measured on an electrochemical workstation (VMP3, Bio‐Logic, France).</p><p><i>In Situ EIS</i>: In situ EIS measurements were measured for TCM and CM electrodes on an electrochemical workstation (VMP3, Bio‐Logic, France).
