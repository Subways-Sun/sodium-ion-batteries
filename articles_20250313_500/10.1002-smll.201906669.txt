type="main">Abstract</title><p>High‐temperature sodium ion batteries (SIBs) have drawn significant heed recently for large‐scale energy storage. Yet, conventional SIBs are in the depths of inferior charge/discharge efficiency and cyclability at elevated temperatures. Rational structure design is highly desirable. Hence, a 3D hierarchical flower architecture self‐assembled by carbon‐coated Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) nanosheets (NVP@C‐NS‐FL) is fabricated via a microwave‐assisted glycerol‐mediated hydrothermal reaction combined with a post heat‐treatment. The growth mechanism of NVP@C‐NS‐FL is systematically investigated, by forming a microspherical glycerol/polyglycerol‐NVP complex initially and then converting into flower‐like architecture during the subsequent annealing at a low temperature ramping rate. Benefiting from the integrated structure, fast Na<sup>+</sup> transportation, and highly effective heat transfer, the as‐obtained NVP@C‐NS‐FL exhibits an excellent high‐temperature SIB performance, e.g., 65 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (100 C) after 1000 cycles under 60 °C. When coupled with NaTi<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> anode, the full cell can still display superior power capability of 1.4 kW kg<sup>−1</sup> and long‐term cyclability (2000 cycles) under 60 °C.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201906669-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A 3D hierarchical flower architecture self‐assembled by carbon‐coated Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) nanosheets (NVP@C‐NS‐FL) is fabricated. Benefiting from the integrated structure, fast Na<sup>+</sup> transportation and highly effective heat transfer, the as‐obtained NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode exhibits an excellent high‐temperature electrochemical performance for sodium‐ion half/full batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201906669:smll201906669-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201906669-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201906669-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll201906669-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Recently, as the contradiction between increasing demand for energy storage and limited resource and high cost of lithium is becoming increasingly drastic, sodium ion batteries (SIBs) attract tremendous research interest as an alternative to lithium ion batteries (LIBs) in fields of electronic vehicles and grid energy storage in virtue of the abundance and environmental friendliness of sodium.<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0001"/> Nevertheless, due to the larger radius of Na<sup>+</sup> than Li<sup>+</sup>, SIBs are suffering from slower reaction kinetics, more serious volume expansion, and electrode materials pulverization problems during charging/discharging, resulting in low energy storage capacity and inferior cyclic stability, thus hindering their practical application. Besides, temperature‐dependent performance is also critical before SIBs can be put into commerce. For example, batteries working at high power would generate massive heat; batteries under field operation need to bear the summer climate. While a few research work on LIBs and supercapacitors has suggested that attributed to the structure degradation of the electrode/electrolyte interface caused by more intense electrolyte convection at temperature &gt;50 °C, low charge/discharge efficiency and poor cycling stability were usually occurred at high temperature,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0002"/> and thus it is urgent to exploit superior high‐temperature SIBs' electrode materials.</p><p>Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (abbreviated as NVP) with 3D open framework is a representative NASICON‐type cathode for SIBs, offering enough available space for sodium ion transport, high and stable redox potential at 3.4 V, and high theoretic energy density (394 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0003"/> Besides, owing to the strong PO bonding, NVP demonstrates an excellent thermal stability up to 450 °C,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0004"/> which can provide a good safety to operate the cell at high temperature (e.g., 60 °C). However, despite of these attractive features, NVP suffers from intrinsically sluggish electrochemical reaction kinetics, leading to inferior sodium storage performance in practice. On the other hand, to develop high‐temperature SIBs, it is also necessary to rapidly remove heat generated by some exothermic reactions from the interior of the battery. Otherwise, the internal temperature of the battery will elevate, which may trigger some deleterious reactions or even catastrophic explosion. Thus, heat dissipation is another challenge in NVP as cathode material for high‐temperature and high‐power SIBs.</p><p>Rational design of a hierarchical NVP architecture is an effective strategy to circumvent the above two issues. Low‐dimensional structures (e.g., nanoparticles, nanorods, nanowires, nanosheets, etc.) possess unique advantages of short Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion length and large exposed surface facilitating electrolyte penetration and fluxing. Nevertheless, these nanostructures with high surface energy are easy to agglomerate during repeated charge–discharge processes. To suppress this drawback, 3D hierarchical architectures built by nanoblocks are highly desirable, which can play dual functions of both microstructures and nanostructures, and thus enhance the long‐term cycling stability. Besides, the porous spaces in the robust 3D NVP/C nanostructures are very beneficial for withstanding the shock of electrolytes and maintain good stability of high‐temperature electrochemical performance. Specifically, a 3D hierarchical flower architecture self‐assembled by carbon coated NVP nanosheets (abbreviated as NVP@C‐NS‐FL), as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a, harboring configuration similar to the sharp of heat sinks utilized in electronic devices, refrigeration, light emitting diodes lamp, etc. (Figure S1, Supporting Information),<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0005"/> is expected to provide a superior heat transfer performance owing to the large surface area.</p><figure xml:id="smll201906669-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201906669:smll201906669-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201906669-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of superiority of NVP@C‐NS‐FL for fast sodium storage performance at elevated temperature. b–h) The phase and morphological characterization of as‐obtained NVP@C‐NS‐FL: (b) XRD pattern (inset: Raman spectrum), (c–e) FESEM images, (f) TEM images, (g) HRTEM image, and (h) TEM‐EDX elemental mapping of C, Na, V, and P of an individual flower.</caption></figure><p>Up to now, various hierarchical NVP architectures including NVP nanoparticles coated on inter‐connected carbon fibrous network,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0006"/> hierarchical nanoflakes‐assembled NVP@C microspheres,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0007"/> and hollow NVP@C spheres,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0008"/> have been fabricated under the assistance of templates or surfactants. Yet, it remains challenging to develop a facile, environmentally friendly, controllable method for synthesis of NVP@C‐NS‐FL, where new technology is highly desirable. Microwave (MW), which heats materials through dielectric loss instead of conventional heat convection, bears certain special heating features like fast, volumetric, and selective heating. As such, it is emerging as a promising new strategy for controllable synthesis of advanced nanomaterials,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0009"/> either due to its thermal or nonthermal effect.</p><p>In this work, we demonstrated a microwave‐assisted surfactant‐free synthesis of NVP@C‐NS‐FL. The reaction system only contained NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> and NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub> as the precursor, glycerol and water as the mixed solvent. Under microwave irradiation, well‐suspended precipitation of NVP‐glycerol complex was obtained. After annealing, NVP@C‐NS‐FL was yielded which exhibited superior high‐temperature sodium storage performance when tested as cathode material in SIBs due to the fast Na<sup>+</sup> transportation and highly effective heat transfer.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201906669-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smll201906669-sec-0030"><title type="main">Characterization of NVP@C‐NS‐FL</title><p>Glycerol is emerging as a green and popular solvent in the synthesis of nanomaterials due to its nontoxicity, high boiling point, and abundance in the nature. Here, glycerol is used as solvent, carbon source, and morphology‐directing agent for the synthesis of NVP@C‐NS‐FL under the assistance of microwave. First, mixture of NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>, and glycerol solution was exposed to microwave hydrothermal treatment, yielding well‐suspended gray‐green flocculent precipitation which was then freeze‐dried and annealed. Characteristic peaks of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS card NO. 62–0345) can be seen from the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">1b</link>), with no detectable peaks from other impurity phases, indicating that pure well crystallized NVP was obtained. Raman spectrum (inset of Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">1b</link>) shows an intensity ratio of D and G bands (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) of 1.01 and a 2D band located at ≈2750 cm<sup>−1</sup>, indicating the existence of carbon material. The carbon layer should be ascribed to the pyrolysis of glycerol. The overall carbon content in the NVP@C‐NS‐FL was estimated to be 13% by hot concentrated acid dissolution method. Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">1c</link>–<link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">e</link> displays the field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) images of the as‐obtained sample. It is clear that homogenously‐sized, well‐dispersed flower‐like nanostructures assembled from ultrathin 2D flakes are obtained. Corresponding transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images at low magnification in Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">1f</link> show that the flowers are about 300 nm in diameter and the building block nanosheets are about 10 nm thick, much thinner than previously reported NVP flakes (≈50–80 nm).<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0007 #smll201906669-bib-0010" style="qv">7a,10</link> From high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) observation, a porous amorphous carbon layer encased NVP with evident lattice spacing of 0.37 nm is observed (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">1g</link>). The elemental mapping (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0001">1h</link>) results show that the 3D flower consists of homogeneous distribution of Na, V, P, and C, suggesting that NVP@C‐NS‐FL was indeed obtained. Figure S2 of the Supporting Information exhibits FESEM image of the residual carbon obtained after acid treatment. As can be seen, flower‐like carbon skeleton is well maintained, suggesting the robustness of the as‐synthesized 3D hierarchical nanostructure. Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) results of the NVP@C‐NS‐FL product (Figure S3, Supporting Information) indicate the existence of large amount of micropores centered at 2.5 nm and mesopores centered at 28 nm, together contributing to a specific surface area of 119 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201906669-sec-0040"><title type="main">Formation Mechanism of NVP@C‐NS‐FL</title><p>To understand the mechanisms involved in the synthesis of NVP@C‐NS‐FL, parameters of microwave hydrothermal reaction and annealing were systematically studied. First of all, the component of the flocculent precipitation (inset of <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a) harvested after microwave hydrothermal treatment was examined. As can be seen, XRD pattern of the product (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2a</link>) shows a characteristic feature of amorphous materials. In X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2b</link>), strong peaks of Na, P, V, O, and C elements are observed. Specifically, the peaks at 516.6 and 524.0 eV (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2c</link>) are well matched with V 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and V 2p<sub>1/2</sub> in V<sup>4+</sup>,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0011"/> suggesting that V<sup>5+</sup> in NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub> has been reduced into V<sup>4+</sup>. Its Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra shows characteristic peaks for OH stretching at 3377 cm<sup>−1</sup>, CO stretching at 1111 and 1041 cm<sup>−1</sup>, and CH<sub>2</sub> stretching at 2939 and 2885 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S4, Supporting Information).<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0012"/> As such, it is probably that amorphous NVP‐glycerol/polyglycerol complex was formed during hydrothermal process (designated as MW‐precursor). FESEM image illustrates that the morphology of the complex was 400 nm spheres composed of many small particles (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2d</link>). Hence, it can be speculated that NVP phase crystallization and flower structure growth were occurred in subsequent annealing process. XRD patterns of the MW‐precursor at different heating temperatures (Figure S5, Supporting Information) indicate that NVP started to crystalize at 700 °C, and completely ripened at 750 °C for 8 h. The morphology evolution during this process is shown in Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2e</link>–<link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">i</link>. When the temperature reached 150 °C, aggregated spheres remained; at 250 °C, nanoflakes began to appear; continuous heating (450 and 650 °C) resulted in more and more nanoflake‐assembled flowers and thoroughly converted to well‐dispersed flower‐like nanostructures at 750 °C, confirming that the growth of NVP@C‐NS‐FL was greatly driven by the sintering. Additionally, it is worth mentioning here that the temperature ramping rate is also a critical factor. As can be seen from Figure S6 of the Supporting Information, when the heating was set at a high rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, bulk NVP with yellow‐green color was obtained, indicating there was little carbon in the final product. This was confirmed by FTIR results (Figure S7, Supporting Information), which demonstrate much weaker CO stretching and OH stretching peaks for the product annealed at 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> compared to 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. This indicated severe carbon‐resource loss via evaporation when the temperature rose too fast, which probably was due to a lack of enough time to decompose the MW‐precursor containing glycerol/polyglycerol at relative low temperature (e.g., &lt;190 °C).</p><figure xml:id="smll201906669-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201906669:smll201906669-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201906669-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD pattern and b,c) XPS spectra of the MW‐precursor. FESEM images of d) MW‐precursor and annealed intermediates (heating rate: 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>) obtained at the temperatures of e) 150 °C, f) 250 °C, g) 450 °C, h) 650 °C, and i) 750 °C. j) Schematic illustrating the synthesizing mechanisms for NVP@C‐NS‐FL.</caption></figure><p>Besides, key determinants for the formation of the MW‐precursor were also investigated. Although the microwave hydrothermal time did not have significant effect on the morphology of the final product (Figure S8, Supporting Information), the raw materials and their concentrations were crucial. When either of glycerol, NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub> (NVO) or NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> (NHP) was absent, solution without any solid product was obtained (Figure S9, Supporting Information), implying that glycerol can chelate the ions of H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>, VO<sub>3</sub><sup>−</sup>, etc., only under the specific ionic condition. Moreover, as shown in Figure S10 of the Supporting Information, with decreasing concentrations of NHP, NVO, and/or glycerol, blue gels with bulky morphology were produced, rather than gray‐green precipitation precursor shown in Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2a</link>. After annealing, irregular NVP/C particles were achieved. On the other hand, MW heating can provide sufficient energy for nucleation and growth.<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0013"/> By comparison, conventional hydrothermal reaction suffered a failure in enabling such a nucleation and thus yielded similar blue gel as in the case with lower reaction concentrations (Figure S11a, Supporting Information). The corresponding annealed (2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, 750 °C for 8 h) product was also irregular particles (Figure S11b, Supporting Information).</p><p>Based on the above results, the growing mechanism of the NVP@C‐NS‐FL is schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0002">2j</link>. With the assistance of microwave heating, the precursor composed of spherical [glycerol(polyglycerol)···VO<sup>2+</sup>H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>Na<sup>+</sup>] clusters is formed at an appropriate concentration, where glycerol can act as the reducing and chelating agent. Subsequent annealing involves processes of: i) evaporation and decomposition of glycerol/polyglycerol, driving the formation of nanosheet building blocks at low temperature‐ramping rate; and ii) carbonization and crystallization at relatively elevated temperature.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201906669-sec-0050"><title type="main">High‐Temperature Performance (Half‐Cell) of NVP@C‐NS‐FL Cathode</title><p>The electrochemical performance of the as‐synthesized NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode was initially evaluated in a coin‐type half‐cell configuration. The operating cell temperature was controlled at 30 and 60 °C. Galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles for the first cycle at a current density of 0.2 C (note that 1 C here equals 133 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for cathode application) are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a,<link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">b</link>. Flat voltage plateau at ≈3.4 V is associated to the V<sup>3+</sup>/V<sup>4+</sup> redox, i.e., extraction/insertion of two sodium per unit formula of NVP via Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> ↔ NaV<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>. Discharge capacities of 116.6 and 117.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with Coulombic efficiencies of ≈100% were achieved at 30 and 60 °C, respectively, which are very nearly equivalent to the theoretical value. Here, only the mass of NVP was involved when calculating the specific capacity owing to that the capacity contributed from the amorphous carbon was negligible in the voltage window of 2.5–3.8 V.<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0006" style="qv">6a</link> With a careful observation, it is found that the potential hysteresis at 60 °C (0.06 V) was extremely lower than that at 30 °C (0.1 V), indicating that Na<sup>+</sup> removal and uptake kinetics at higher temperature of 60 °C was more favorable. Moreover, unattenuated cycling performance was also demonstrated at both 30 and 60 °C (insets of Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">3a</link>,<link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">b</link>), implying the superior reversibility and structural stability of NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode, even at higher temperature.</p><figure xml:id="smll201906669-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201906669:smll201906669-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201906669-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of as‐synthesized NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode at 30 and 60 °C. a,b) Charge/discharge profiles at 0.2 C (insets: corresponding cycling performance at 0.2 C), c) rate performance (inset: EIS curves and the corresponding fitted equivalent circuit, where <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> = charge transfer resistance, <i>W</i> = Warburg impedance, <i>R</i><sub>b</sub> = bulk resistance, and CPE = constant phase element), d) discharge profiles at different rates under 60 °C, and long‐term cycling performance at rates of e) 50 C and f) 100 C.</caption></figure><p>Furthermore, our NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode can also demonstrate an excellently ultrafast charging/discharging capability at higher temperature, opening new opportunity for power batteries. Its rate capability is illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">3c</link>. At 60 °C, discharge capacities of 113, 109, 103, 99, 93, 88, 83, and 77 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> were obtained at rates of 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30, 50, and 80 C, respectively, and even at an ultrahigh rate of 100 C (equivalent to a full discharge in 36 s), a reversible capacity of 74 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> was still achieved, which are significantly higher than the corresponding capacities at 30 °C (e.g., showing only 71, 59, and 49 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50, 80, and 100 C, respectively). It is because that, as revealed by electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) and the corresponding fitted equivalent circuit (inset of Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">3c</link>), the charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) was dramatically decreased from 64 Ω at 30 °C to 18 Ω at 60 °C. Such rate performance (60 °C) is also superior to most other NVP‐based cathodes (e.g., 49 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 C for N‐doped carbon‐wrapped NVP,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0014"/> 78 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 30 C for porous Mn‐doped NVP,<link href="#smll201906669-bib-0015"/> etc.). Additionally, the operating voltage plateaus (60 °C) at various rates are clearly visible (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">3d</link>), indicating a relatively low polarization. Meanwhile, excellent long‐term cycling performances with almost no capacity fading were demonstrated at high rates of 50 and 100 C (Figure <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">3e</link>,<link href="#smll201906669-fig-0003">f</link>), e.g., delivering 65 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (100 C) after 1000 cycles at 60 °C, suggesting strong structural stability at higher temperature. Detailed performance comparison in Table S1 of the Supporting Information further confirms the outstanding electrochemical properties for our NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode.</p><p>To understand superior high‐temperature performance of the NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode, its structural stability and kinetic behavior were systematically investigated. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201906669-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a (bottom panel) shows in situ XRD patterns of the NVP@C‐NS‐FL cathode measured after 5 cycles at 60 °C. environmentally friendly method has been explored for controllable fabrication of NVP@C nanosheets assembled flower‐like architecture under the mediation of glycerol with the assistance of microwave‐enhanced nucleation. The synthesizing mechanism was systematically investigated. Benefiting from the unique nanostructure, the as‐prepared product delivered a superior rate and stability performance at higher temperature when evaluated as cathode materials in sodium ion batteries. Moreover, when coupled with NaTi<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> anode, the full cell demonstrated both high‐power (1.4 kW kg<sup>−1</sup>) and high‐energy densities (62 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>) at 60 °C, as well as long‐term cyclability.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201906669-sec-0080" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Key R&amp;D Research Program of China (Grant No. 2018YFB0905400), National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 61801314, 61731013, 51622210, 51872277, 51802007, 21606003, 51972067, 51802044, 51925207, and U1910210), the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (Nos.
