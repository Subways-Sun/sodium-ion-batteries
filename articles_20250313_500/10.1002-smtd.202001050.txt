Herein, a 3D Ag@C natrophilic substrate prepared by a facile thermal evaporation deposition method, which can be employed as a much safer “anode‐free” SMA, is reported. Initially, there is no bulk sodium on the Ag@C substrate in the assembled SIBs. Upon charging, sodium will be uniformly deposited onto the Ag@C substrate and afterwards functions as a real SMA, thus inheriting the intrinsic merits of SMA and enhancing safety simultaneously. While cycling, the as‐synthesized substrate demonstrates superior sodium plating/stripping cycling stability at 1, 2 and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. Theoretical simulations reveal that Na ions prefer to bind with Ag and form a Na‐Ag network, thus clearly revealing uniform sodium deposition on the Ag@C substrate. More importantly, a full battery based on Ag@C and Prussian white with impressive Coulomb efficiency (CE), high rate capability (from 0.1 C to 5 C) and long‐term cycling life is illustrated for the first time, thus making Ag@C feasible for the establishment of “anode‐free” SIBs with reduced cost, high gravimetric/volumetric energy density and enhanced safety.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smtd202001050-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A 3D Ag@C substrate, featuring facile electrons and sodium ions accessibility, enables stable Na ion deposition as uncovered by theoretical simulations and experimental analyses. xml:id="smtd202001050-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>By virtue of enticing theoretical capacity (1165 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), desirable redox potential (–2.71 V vs SHE) and materials abundance, sodium metal anode (SMA)<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> would substantially boost energy density and feasibility of conventional sodium ion batteries (SIBs).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, SMA also plays as an indispensable component for many novel Na‐S/Se/Te,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na‐P,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na‐O<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na‐CO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Na‐Br/I systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For these reasons, SMA chemistry is currently an important research area for building advanced sodium ion batteries (SIBs).</p><p>Albeit with these promising merits, daunting challenges including cycling lifetime and safety still plague practical adoption of the rechargeable SMA.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Particularly, heterogeneous Na‐ion deposition during repeated plating/stripping often leads to morphological and chemical instability manifested as dendritic Na growth, large volume expansion, poor Columbic efficiency (<i>CE</i>), incomplete solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) films and ultimately premature battery failure. Ever worse, sodium metal reacts detrimentally with flammable ester‐based electrolytes, suggesting that SMA is prone to catch fire or explode under harsh conditions. In short, safety concern is a major hurdle for the wide applications of the SMA. Thus far, a wealth of rational coping approaches have been pursued to achieve long cycling stability and enhanced operation safety, such as fabricating anode surface coating,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> innovating Na‐ion plating substrate,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesizing liquid Na metal alloy,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> regulating local molecular structure of electrolyte,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> generating robust artificial SEI, and constructing potential solid‐state electrolyte (SSE).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> While these approaches can indeed improve the cycling life and alleviate the issue of dendrite formation, the fact that the SMA itself being a bulk piece of sodium is still the origin of safety concern.</p><p>In fact, similar problem also exists for lithium metal anode (LMA) although the safety concern is not as severe as that in SMA. Louli et al. have recently proposed a new “anode‐free” approach to address this issue for lithium ion battery.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this design, they directly use a copper foil as anode without preloading any lithium metal leading to the following advantages: i) the absence of bulk lithium in the anode can significantly decrease weight and volume of the cell, thereby enabling much higher gravimetric and volumetric energy density; ii) upon initial charging process of Li on the copper foil, it works as a real LMA, hence inheriting intrinsic merits of LMA while alleviating safety concern of using bulk lithium metal. Inspired by this innovative approach, we hereby develop an “anode‐free” SMA to address the stability and safety issues of conventional SMA. However, as there is no bulk sodium in the “anode‐free” SMA, the loss of sodium metal to SEI formation has to be supplied from the cathode. This implies that if the SEI is unstable upon cycling, there will be a continuous consumption of sodium leading to short cycling life and low <i>CE</i>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On the other hand, rough and non‐uniform deposition of metal ion on the anode side will intensify the inhomogeneous distribution of the electrical field and thus drive dendrite formation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0001 #smtd202001050-bib-0015"><sup>1b,c,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, in order to implement a high performance “anode‐free” SMA, one pre‐requisite has to be achieved: Uniform sodium deposition on the anode substrate.</p><p>In this study, we use a carbon cloth as the substrate for the “anode‐free” SMA. We found that uniformity of sodium deposition can be substantially enhanced by coating a thin layer of silver on the carbon cloth. This Ag@C “anode‐free” SMA demonstrates an ultra‐stable cycling and high <i>CE</i> at 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> over 500 cycles. Comprehensive density functional theory (DFT), ab‐initial molecular dynamics (AIMD) simulation and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) analysis clearly reveal that the presence of Ag assists smooth plating/stripping of Na ions. By pairing the Ag@C “anode‐free” SMA with a Prussian white cathode, a sodium metal full battery shows a high initial capacity of 133/116 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1/0.5 C rate, along with a reasonable capacity retention of ≈56% over 800 cycles at 0.5 C rate. This work paves an effective avenue in taming the structural and surface properties of anode hosts toward superior anode‐free sodium metal batteries (SMB).</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0030"><title type="main">Surface and Microstructure Characterizations</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a,b</link> shows schematic illustration of Na ion plating behavior on a natrophilic and a sodium repellent substrate. On the natrophilic substrate, sodium will have a high nucleation rate and like to give a uniform sodium layer upon plating (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0001">1a</link>). On the contrary, the lower nucleation rate on the sodium repellent substrate will result in uneven sodium plating and islands (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0001">1b</link>). Upon application of a voltage, the uniform sodium layer and the sodium islands would lead respectively to a uniform and an uneven electric field distribution (right hand column of Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0001">1a,b</link>, as determined with the COMSOL Mutiphysics simulation). The uneven electric field on the sodium repellent substrate would cause preferential sodium disposition on the islands, which eventually bring about dendrite formation and thus is highly unfavorable for battery applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smtd202001050-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202001050:smtd202001050-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202001050-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) Na ion deposition behavior and finite element simulation results of potential distribution on a natrophilic and a sodium repellent substrate.</caption></figure><p>In consideration of these key factors, 3D natrophilic Ag@C substrate was fabricated via a facile thermal evaporation deposition method as visualized in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>. Morphology and microstructure of the pristine carbon cloth and its silver coated layer were studied with SEM. As shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0002">2b</link> and Figure S1a, Supporting Information 3D carbon cloth is woven with smooth carbon nanofibers, which provides open channels for Na ions and electrons accessibility. After silver deposition, the carbon cloth is decorated with a high density of silver nanoparticles (AgNPs) with uniform distribution (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0002">2c</link>; Figure S1b, Supporting Information). EDX mapping of the pristine carbon cloth and silver coated carbon cloth are shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0002">2e</link> and Figure S1c, Supporting Information respectively. X‐ray diffraction (XRD) peaks from face‐centered cubic silver can be clearly observed on the silver‐coated sample (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0002">2d</link>). This natrophilic 3D Ag@C substrate is largely expected to smooth Na ion plating/stripping, improve redox kinetics, and electrode chemistry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0010"><sup>10a,b,d</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smtd202001050-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202001050:smtd202001050-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202001050-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic diagram showing deposition of silver on a carbon cloth. b,c) SEM images of the carbon cloth and the Ag@C substrate. d) XRD patterns of the pristine carbon cloth and the Ag@C substrate. e) Elemental mapping images of the as‐synthesized Ag@C substrate.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0040"><title type="main">Electrochemical Analysis of the Na Ion Deposition</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link> and Figure S2, Supporting Information summarize the deposition voltages profiles and galvanostatic cycling lives of the Na||Ag@C, Na||C half‐cells with 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate (EC/PC) containing 5 wt% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC). It is reasonably concluded that the large part of capacity is delivered by Na plating/stripping rather than other intercalation reaction in the Ag@C and carbon cloth matrix (Figure S2a–d, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Upon cycling, the almost overlapping voltages curves (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0003">3a–c</link>) indicate stable Na ion plating/stripping for the first three cycles. Notably, only a slight increase of voltage hysteresis appears for Na ion deposition at the current densities ranging from 1 to 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. Long‐term cycling measurements of these cells were then undertaken to assess the capability and stability retention. As shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0003">3d–f</link>, these cells demonstrate excellent Na ion plating/stripping stability at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> after 100 cycles, respectively. Additionally, an extended 500 deep cycling profiles is presented in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0003">3g</link>, which exhibit no obvious potential shifts and more promising Na ion deposition kinetics among various strategies (Table S1, Supporting Information). In stark comparison, as shown in Figure S3a–d, Supporting Information, pristine carbon cloth substrate shows much poorer Na ion plating/stripping stability. For one thing, lager voltage hysteresis occurred for Na ion deposition at the current densities ranging from 1 to 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure S3a, Supporting Information). As the current density increased to 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, it is reasonable to conclude that cells become unworkable (Figure S3a, Supporting Information). Figure S3b–d, Supporting Information also shows severe potentials fluctuations at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> over 80 cycles. All these analyses confirm that the AgNPs decoration on the carbon cloth is beneficial for stable Na ion plating/stripping.</p><figure xml:id="smtd202001050-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202001050:smtd202001050-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202001050-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) Voltage profiles of the Na plating/stripping performance of the 3D Ag@C at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. d–f) Galvanostatic cycling performance of Na||Ag@C cells over 100 cycles at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. g) Long‐term cycling performance of Na||Ag@C cells over 500 cycles at 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>.</caption></figure><p>In support of above statement, we then investigated the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and <i>CE</i> of Na ion plating/stripping. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a–c</link> shows EIS curves of the Ag@C substrate at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) after 1, 10, 50, and 100 cycles. It is obvious that there exits only a limited change in the EIS profiles for these cells. <i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub> and <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> of the equivalent circuit were determined by fitting the corresponding EIS curves and listed in Table S2, Supporting Information. The most important observation is that after the AgNPs deposition, both <i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub> and <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> are much smaller and much more stable upon cycling. These suggest that cells with the Ag@C substrate own better interface stability and charge transfer dynamics. By contrast, cells with pristine carbon cloth substrate demonstrate inferior stability (Figure S4–c and Table S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="smtd202001050-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202001050:smtd202001050-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202001050-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) Electrochemical impedance of Ag@C substrate after 1, 10, and 100 cycles at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. d) <i>CE</i> of Na ion deposition on the Ag@C substrate at 1, 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> over 100 cycles and 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> over 500 cycles.</caption></figure><p><i>CE</i> is another quantifiable indicator accounting for the reversible Na ion deposition process. Intriguingly, the Na||Ag@C cell shows a gradual increase of <i>CE</i>s from ≈96% to ≈99% in just five cycles (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0004">4d</link>), which may be tentatively ascribed to the building of complete SEI. Upon subsequent cycles, Na||Ag@C cell preserves high <i>CE</i> at 1, 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> over 100 cycles and 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> over 500 cycles (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0004">4d</link>), again attesting to the remarkable stability of the Na deposition on the Ag@C substrate in ester‐based electrolyte. On the contrary, cells with pristine carbon cloth show severe erratic fluctuations of <i>CE</i> (Figure S4d–f, Supporting Information), which can be attributed to the low‐quality SEI and sodium dendrite growth upon cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0001 #smtd202001050-bib-0008 #smtd202001050-bib-0018"><sup>1b,8b,18</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0050"><title type="main">Sodium‐Ion Plating/Stripping Mechanism Analyses</title><p>For clarifying the stability of Na plating/stripping with Ag@C substrate, it is instructive to explore Na deposition kinetics. We initially study affinity between Na atoms and Ag@C substrate via DFT simulation. Specifically, as shown in Figure S5a,b, Supporting Information, (100), (110), and (111) crystallographic surface of Ag (grey color) and Na (golden color) metal were first constructed. Optimized binding energies of a Na ad‐atom (green color) on (111), (110), and (100) surfaces of Ag are −1.68, −1.72, and −1.53 eV respectively, while corresponding values on Na surfaces are only −0.62, −0.68, and −0.81 eV (Figure S5c, Supporting Information). These clearly show that Na atoms prefer to bind with Ag particles and the Ag@C substrate can thus provide natrophilic surfaces to enable uniform and stable sodium plating. On account of above modeling results, a matching Ag/Na interface is established to further illustrate Na ion plating dynamics via AIMD calculation. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link> shows the four snapshots (0, 5, 10, and 20 ps) with 216 Ag and 64 Na atoms. Upon heating at 400 K, it is evident that the Na atoms progressively diffuse into the silver surface, forming a Na–Ag network at the surface of silver. After 20 ps, it is found that almost all Na ions were inclined to spread upon the surface of silver (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0005">5a</link>) without forming any Na–Ag alloy since all the typical diffraction peaks belong to cubic silver and cubic sodium metal (Figure S6, Supporting Information), thus greatly benefiting Na ion redox kinetics and eliminating morphological instability. In parallel, DFT calculations (Figure S7, Supporting Information) show that the bind energy of a Na atom on a C (001) surface is only −0.52 eV, which is considerably smaller than the Na–Na binding energies (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Meanwhile, AIMD results (Figure S8, Supporting Information) indicate that Na atoms tend to move away from the carbon surface, rather than creating a mixed Na–C layer.</p><figure xml:id="smtd202001050-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202001050:smtd202001050-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202001050-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) AIMD simulation results of Na‐ion deposition kinetics on the Ag surface after 0, 5, 10, and 20 ps at 400 K. b–d) SEM images of the cycled Ag@C at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> after 100 cycles.</caption></figure><p>With the above understanding, we investigate morphological evolution of the Ag@C and pristine carbon cloth substrates upon extended Na plating/stripping cycling. As shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0005">5b–d</link>, after 100 cycles at 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>, the Ag@C substrates depict similar smooth morphologies with no apparent agglomeration of large sodium particles. Elemental Na, C, Ag, Na, O, and F mapping images of cycled Ag@C anode at 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure S9, Supporting Information) further affirm above results. This morphology features result in smooth electric field distributions, stable reaction interface, and reversible Na atoms plating/stripping. For comparison, SEM and elemental mapping images of the cycled pristine carbon cloth are shown in Figures S10 and S11, Supporting Information. It can be observed that sodium particles of hundreds micron sizes were randomly dispersed on the carbon cloth substrates, and these agglomerates may further catalyze sodium dendrite growth and morphological deterioration, hence contributing to the cells’ failure. High‐resolution X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra of C 1s, Na 1s, Ag 3d, and F 1s for cells (after 100 cycles) with the Ag@C and the pristine carbon cloth substrates at a current density of 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> are shown in Figure S12, Supporting Information. It is clear that both the Na 1s (1073.8 eV) and the F 1s (686.4 eV) spectra (Figure S12b–f, Supporting Information) demonstrated strong NaF signals for both substrates, which can be attributed to the effective reduction of FEC. Importantly, NaF protective layer is considered to be thermodynamically stable against Na metal and avoid continuously unwanted side reactions which sacrifice Na ion redox kinetics. Composition of the C 1s spectrum is rather complicated, consisting of CC (282.1 eV), CO (287.1 eV), ROCH<sub>2</sub>ONa (288.8 eV), Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> (290.8 eV) and CFx (292.3 eV) bonds (Figure S12a,d, Supporting Information). These C‐containing products can be stemmed from decompositions of PC and/or FEC. Two well‐separated peaks located at 367.7 and 373.9 eV correspond to the binding energies of Ag 3d<sub>3/2</sub> and Ag 3d<sub>5/2</sub>, respectively (Figure S12c, Supporting Information). Additionally, peak locations and distance are quiet distinct from pure metallic Ag, possibly attributing to the Na–Ag interaction. All these interfacial chemistry analyses agree well with the DFT results, confirming that the non‐planar natrophilic Ag@C substrate can markedly facilitate Na ion deposition dynamics, interface, and morphological stabilities.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0060"><title type="main">Anode‐Free Sodium‐Metal Full Cells</title><p>To confirm the advantages of the Ag@C substrate as “anode‐free” SMA, we prepare a full battery by paring with a Prussian white (PW) cathode. PW, featuring ease of preparation, high specific capacity, and acceptable Na ion mobility (Figure S13, Supporting Information), was viewed as an ideal cathode for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Detailed microstructure characterizations of the as‐synthesized PW are shown in Figures S14 and S15, Supporting Information. Rietveld refinement XRD results (Figure S14a, Supporting Information) suggest that the well‐crystallized PW possesses the monoclinic structure with P<sub>21/n</sub> space group (<i>a</i> = 10.1127 Å, <i>b</i> = 7.3826 Å, <i>c</i> = 6.9711 Å, α = γ = 90°, β = 90.6°). Three characteristic Raman peaks (Figure S14b, Supporting Information) lied in 2073.8, 2114.2, and 2161.9 cm<sup>−1</sup> were assigned to the Fe<sup>II</sup>‐CN‐Mn<sup>II</sup>, Fe<sup>II</sup>‐CN‐Mn<sup>III</sup>, and Fe<sup>III</sup>‐CN‐Mn<sup>II</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0019"><sup>19b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A typical SEM image (Figure S14c, Supporting Information) clearly shows that as‐synthesized PW owns the typical cubic shape with dimensions of 100–200 nm, as testified by the uniform distribution of Mn, Fe, K, C, and N presented in the elemental mapping images (Figure S15, Supporting Information).</p><p>Afterward, “anode‐free” sodium metal full batteries were fabricated to carry out performance and electrochemical analysis. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link> shows schematic constructions of anode‐free SMB, where active Na<sup>+</sup> ions are totally accommodated in the PW cathode. In Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0006">6b</link>, d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> curve exhibits two conspicuous peaks, which can be ascribed to Fe<sup>II</sup>/ Fe<sup>III</sup> and Mn<sup>II</sup>/ Mn<sup>III</sup> redox. As shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0006">6c</link>, nearly overlapping voltage‐capacity profiles for the first 3 cycles of the full cell demonstrate remarkable electrochemical reversibility and stability. Meanwhile, the cell is able to deliver an initial specific charge/discharge capacity of 136/128 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C, realizing an acceptable initial <i>CE</i> of 94.1%. Moreover, at 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, and 5 C rates (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0006">6d</link>; Figure S16, Supporting Information), the cell yields highly reversible capacities, respectively, of 110, 104, 95, 75, and 52 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. After recovering back to the 0.1 C rate, the cells still retain a considerable capacity of 122 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating the appreciable rate performance of this “anode free” SMB. More importantly, the cells cycled at 0.5 C rate show good cycling stability with a capacity retention of ≈56% after 800 cycles, corresponding to a relative slow capacity decay of 0.055% per cycle (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0006">6e</link>). Although these cells exhibit relative inferior capacity retention than those with pure Na metal anode in the initial 250 cycles (≈0.0055% loss per cycle), PW||Na cells suddenly lost its functionality after 250 cycles (Figure S17, Supporting Information). It can therefore be concluded that anode properties are the main reason for these full cells’ capacity degradation and failure. Compared with the Ag@C substrate, we also analyzed electrochemical performance of an “anode‐free” SMB metal cell with a pristine carbon cloth substrate upon the same testing conditions. It was worth noting that the d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> profile of the full cell with a carbon cloth substrate show larger voltage polarization, indicating inferior interfacial charge‐transfer kinetics (Figure S18a, Supporting Information). For the first three voltage profiles, we found a distinct decrease of capacity from 127 to 121 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S18b, Supporting Information). Even worse, the capacity values reduces rapidly from 108 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C to 5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 C rate (Figure S18c, Supporting Information), suggesting much inferior Na ion redox dynamics without the silver treatment. Apart from poor rate capability, the cell with the pristine carbon cloth cycled at 0.5 C rate also shows low capacity retention of ≈42% over 200 cycles, giving rise to a marked capacity decay of 0.29% per cycle (Figure S18d, Supporting Information). Finally, an Ag@C electrode sheet with ≈1 cm diameter in its fully charged state (i.e., sodium loaded) and a small piece (size 0.6 cm × 0.3 cm × 0.1 cm) of sodium metal were put into water to assess the safety as shown in the supported video. It was clearly that sodium metal reacts vigorously with water, while no obvious reaction can be observed on the sodium loaded Ag@C substrate. This suggests that the present design does deliver a much safer anode system comparing to using a real SMA. All these encouraging electrochemical analyses strengthen the great potential of Ag@C substrate for construction of the advanced “anode‐free” SMB.</p><figure xml:id="smtd202001050-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:23669608:media:smtd202001050:smtd202001050-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smtd202001050-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustrations of anode‐free sodium metal battery configurations. Electrochemical assessment of the anode‐free sodium metal full batteries paired Prussian white cathode with the Ag@C substrate: b) d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> curve; c) voltage profiles; d) rate capability at 2.0–4.2 V; e) long‐term cycling performance at 0. 5 C within 2–4.2 V.</caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0070"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In conclusion, we demonstrate a 3D Ag@C natrophilic substrate that is fabricated via a facile thermal evaporation deposition process. The natrophilic Ag provides abundant active sites for Na ion deposition, enabling uniform Na ion plating/stripping and much improved electrochemical stability. During cycling, cells with Ag@C configuration yield a superior cycling life and high <i>CE</i> at 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> over 500 cycles. DFT and AIMD analyses intrinsically unveil that Na ions prefer to adsorb on the Ag surface and dynamically form a Na–Ag network, thus effectively facilitating Na ion redox kinetics and morphological stability. More importantly, paired directly with a PW cathode, the “anode‐free” sodium metal full batteries show excellent rate capability at various cycling rates (0.1 to 5 C rates), and good long‐term cyclability at 0.5 C over 800 cycles, thus making our design a feasible “anode‐free” SMB.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0080"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Ag@C Substrate Fabrication</title><p>Typically, a piece of carbon cloth (≈50 cm<sup>−2</sup>) was thoroughly immersed into acetone followed by ultra‐sonication for 2 h. Afterward, the cleaned carbon cloth was dried at 70 °C in vacuum for 12 h. Next, 300 mg Ag particles were placed in a crucible and the carbon cloth was fixed just above the Ag source. Finally, Ag@C composites were successfully synthesized via thermal evaporation deposition for 10 min in a vacuum of 10<sup>−6</sup> pa. Surface area and average weight of the obtained carbon cloth electrodes were ≈1 cm<sup>2</sup> and 10.2 mg respectively. Average weight of the fabricated Ag@C electrodes (≈1 cm<sup>2</sup>) was 15.3 mg.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Prussian White Fabrication</title><p>0.001 mol MnSO<sub>4</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O and 0.01 mol Na<sub>3</sub>C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>O<sub>7</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O were first dissolved into 50 mL distilled water via vigorous stirring for 2 h to form transparent solution A. 0.001 mol K<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O was then added into 50 mL DI water to form clear solution B. Subsequently, solution B was drop‐wise added into solution A under vigorous stirring for 12 h. The obtained white products were completely washed with DI water and ethanol several times, and then finally dried under vacuum at 80 °C for 12 h.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>Detailed microstructure of the Ag@C substrate and the PW nanocubes were characterized by a QUATTRO S scanning electron microscope. Crystal structure of Ag@C, C, and PW were determined with a X‐ray diffractometer using Cu‐Kα radiation. XPS (VG ESCALAB 220i‐XL) were undertaken to determine chemical compositions and bonding features of the cycled carbon cloth and Ag@C electrodes. Raman (RENISHAW) measurements were carried out to analyze CN vibration mode with a 532 nm laser source. To better understand Na ion deposition morphological stability, cycled carbon cloth, and Ag@C electrodes against sodium metal at different current density were also checked with SEM.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemistry Measurements</title><p>Ag@C||Na and C||Na half cells were assembled to assess Na ion plating/stripping stability. Voltage profiles and long‐term cycling performances of these half cells were conducted with a MACCOR battery cycler at a current density of 1, 2, and 3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. Subsequently, anode‐free SMB were fabricated by pairing with a PW cathode and an Ag@C or a pristine carbon cloth substrate. Cyclic voltammetry tests were carried out with a Zahner electrochemical workstation (voltage range of 2–4.2 V, 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> scan rate) to study the redox reversibility. Rate capability and long‐term cycling life were assessed from 0.1 to 5 C rates. EIS measurements were conducted with an impedance analyzer (Zennium pro) with the frequency ranging from 1 MHz to 100 mHz.</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0150" numbered="no"><title type="main">Theoretical Simulations</title><p>DFT simulations were first performed to define binding characteristic between Na and Ag or pristine carbon. As to Ag@C substrate, specifically, three different supercells containing (111)/(64 Ag atoms), (110)/(48 Ag atoms), and (001)/(40 Ag atoms) crystal plane of silver were constructed, only one Na atom was placed on each top of these three crystal surface and the vacuum layer was 10 Å. For carbon, a supercell containing 392 C atoms and one Na atom was designed. Optimal Na‐ion adsorption sites and the corresponding binding energies were analyzed in the framework of DFT with projector augmented plane‐wave method as implemented in Vienna ab Initio Simulation Package.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The exchange−correlation interaction between itinerant electrons was described by the Perdew−Burke−Ernzerhof (PBE) functional. Long‐range van der Waals interactions between Na‐ion and Ag or C substrate were handled by semi‐empirical London dispersion corrections of Grimme et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> All calculations were performed with a cut‐off energy of 700 eV to guarantee complete structure and energy convergence, and the Monkhorst−Pack k‐point mesh was 6 × 6 × 1. AIMD simulations were then conducted to illustrate the Na‐ion deposition kinetics upon the Ag surface (10 layers, 96 Na atoms, and 216 Ag atoms in total as shown in Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0005">5a</link>) and the C surface (10  layers, 64 Na atoms, and 294 Ag atoms in total as shown in Figure S7, Supporting Information). All the AIMD simulations were performed with Gaussians and Plane Wave method embedded in the CP2K software package at single Γ point with an energy cutoff of 300 Ry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smtd202001050-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> PBE and DFT‐D3 were utilized to cope with exchange−correlation and relative weak van der Waals interactions. Each configurations were relaxed for 20 ps with a time step of 1 fs, and the equilibrium temperature was 400 K in the canonical ensemble (NVT) controlled by Nose−Hoover thermostat. Potential distributions of natrophilic/Na and sodium repellent substrate/Na (Figure <link href="#smtd202001050-fig-0001">1</link>) were determined by finite element simulation (COMSOL Multiphysics) via solving Poisson equation.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0160" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the City University of Hong Kong (CityU Internal Funds for External Grant Schemes Project No. 9678157) and Hong Kong Innovation and Technology Commission (Project No. ITS/219/19).</p></section><section xml:id="smtd202001050-sec-0170" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smtd202001050-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smtd202001050-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smtd202001050-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>X.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>A.</givenNames> <familyName>Wang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>X.</givenNames> <familyName>Liu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Luo</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Acc.
