However, low electrical conductivity and poor Na<sup>+</sup> ion transport in TiO<sub>2</sub> limit its practical applications. Here, substantially boosted Na<sup>+</sup> ion transport and charge transfer kinetics are demonstrated by constructing a near‐ideal non‐rectifying titanium carbonitride/nitrogen‐doped TiO<sub>2</sub> (TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub>) heterostructure. Owing to the fast plasma effects and metastable hybrid phases, the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> is epitaxially grown on TiO<sub>2</sub>. Energy band engineering at the interface induces high electron densities and a strong built‐in electric field, which lowers the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier by a factor of 1.7. As a result, the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode exhibits excellent electrochemical performance. The reversible specific capacities at rates of 0.1 and 10 C reach 312.3 and 173.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. After 600 cycles of charge and discharge at 10 C, the capacity retention rate is 98.7%. This work discovers an effective non‐equilibrium plasma‐enabled process to construct heterointerfaces that can enhance Na<sup>+</sup> ion transport and provides generic guidelines for the design of heterostructures for a broader range of energy storage, separation, and other devices that rely on controlled ionic transport.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202200694-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Plasma discharge enables fast construction of near‐ideal band engineering of TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> heterostructure, which induces an extraordinary electron concentration at the heterointerface, thereby substantially improving the conductivity of TiO<sub>2</sub> and accelerating the diffusion kinetics of sodium ions. The resulting negative electrode of sodium‐ion battery exhibits outstanding performance. This inspires a feasible method for fabricating advanced energy storage materials.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202200694:smll202200694-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202200694-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202200694-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>LIBs and SIBs are among the most promising types of clean and renewable energy storage systems. However, the scarce lithium resources are insufficient for the large‐scale use of energy storage systems in the future.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Since sodium is extremely abundant in the earth, has low cost, and is environment friendly, SIBs are expected to be an alternative to LIBs in large‐scale energy storage. However, with a larger radius than Li<sup>+</sup> ions, the insertion and extraction of Na<sup>+</sup> ions in the electrode material are more difficult. Therefore, proper choice and design of an electrode material which can effectively host and transport Na<sup>+</sup> ions, while presenting a stable structure and excellent rate performance is the key to the practical development and application of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0001 #smll202200694-bib-0002"><sup>1b,2</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Anatase titanium dioxide (TiO<sub>2</sub>) is one of the most promising and environment‐friendly LIB and SIB anode materials, with a theoretical specific capacity of 335 mAh g<sup>−</sup><sup>1</sup>, low cost, and notable cycle stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the major shortcomings of TiO<sub>2</sub> are the semiconductor characteristics with the large bandgap (3.2 eV) and the high ion diffusion barrier.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Downsizing the material, designing nanostructures,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> introducing advanced conductive agents,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> heteroatom doping,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and introducing oxygen vacancies or trivalent titanium<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been explored to improve the TiO<sub>2</sub> electrical conductivity. Attempts to speed up the ion transport include designing fast ion channel by ordered nanostructures,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> heteroatom doping,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0007 #smll202200694-bib-0010"><sup>7d–f,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and introducing intentional defects.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0008 #smll202200694-bib-0009 #smll202200694-bib-0011"><sup>8a,9e,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recently, increased attention has been paid to application of advanced heterostructures in energy devices including batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0004 #smll202200694-bib-0010 #smll202200694-bib-0012"><sup>4b,10b,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These efforts aspire to use self‐generated (built‐in) electric fields at the heterointerface to accelerate the transport of Na+ or Li+ and reduce diffusion barrier.</p><p>Although a built‐in electric field is ubiquitous at the interface of various heterostructures, it may not always play a role in accelerating ion transport, for the following reasons. First, the direction of the electric field should point to the active material which hosts Na<sup>+</sup> ions, otherwise the electric field may instead block the Na<sup>+</sup> from moving in the right direction. This requires electrons to accumulate rather than deplete on the active material side of the interface. Second, the strength of the electric field should be large enough to significantly reduce the diffusion barrier. Since TiO<sub>2</sub> has a large electrostatic dielectric constant (≈40), the screening effect will greatly lower the internal electric field. Third, the interfacial density of defect states must be low enough, otherwise a higher contact barrier may be formed. These critical elements of constructing such heterogeneous structures for enhanced ion transport have hardly been discussed, let alone quantitatively, in the past research.</p><p>Here, we address this issue by developing the needed non‐rectifying titanium carbonitride/nitrogen–doped titanium dioxide heterostructure (TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub>) which satisfies the above requirements. The heterostructure was produced by a “many birds with one stone” urea plasma treatment of commercially available anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> in one step within only 5 min. The electron concentration at the heterointerface is estimated to be 1.46 × 10<sup>20</sup> cm<sup>−3</sup>, and the electric field strength reaches 1 × 10<sup>7</sup> V cm<sup>−1</sup>, which is large enough to significantly lower the diffusion barrier. The scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) images reveal that TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> is epitaxially grown on the TiO<sub>2</sub>, with a nearly flawless interface. Plasma treatment also realized the N‐doping of TiO<sub>2</sub> and improved its conductivity. The resulting TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> heterostructure exhibits the reversible specific capacity of 312.3 and 173.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a rate of 0.1 and 10 C, respectively. After 600 cycles of charge and discharge at 10 C, the capacity retention rate is 98.7%, and the coulombic efficiency is 100%. Further analysis confirmed that the sodium ion diffusion barrier was reduced by a factor of 1.7. Our study not only reveals the specific mechanism of diffusion barrier reduction by heterostructures, but also provides practical guidelines for the design and synthesis of sodium‐storage heterostructures based on TiO<sub>2</sub> and other materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>By appropriately choosing a low‐work‐function semiconductor or metal contacting TiO<sub>2</sub> one can generate a high concentration of electrons accumulating at the TiO<sub>2</sub> side of the heterointerface. The 2D electron gas (2DEG)–like electron accumulation can generate a strong electrostatic field near the interface. By calculating the change of Gibbs free energy, it is found that the forward proceeding of the reversible reaction TiO<sub>2</sub>(s) + 3C(s)‐TiC(s) + 2CO(g) at low pressure (10<sup>−3</sup>–1 bar) requires a temperature over 1000 °C.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0006"><sup>6a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The coexistence of TiC and TiO<sub>2</sub> in a low‐temperature system indicates that the reversible reaction is frozen in a metastable state. In this case, a metastable hybrid‐phase system is formed, that is, the total Gibbs free energy of the system is at a local minimum rather than at the lowest possible minimum. This is due to the essentially non‐equilibrium characteristics of the plasma, and is also the result of the conductive TiC phase absorbing microwaves and becoming a hot spot to accelerate the local reaction process. In our past studies, the ability of plasmas to induce metastable hybrid phases has also been observed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the plasma accelerated other reaction kinetics. The size of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles becomes more non‐uniform after the plasma processing, suggesting the occurrence of Ostwald ripening. Specifically, Ti and O atoms are likely to be removed from the surface of the nanoparticles by the plasma exposure, and then redeposit on the larger nanoparticles at the expense of the smaller ones, as illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0001">1K</link>. The Ostwald ripening of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles has been reported in wet chemical synthesis, but it took as long as tens of hours to complete,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which is in sharp contrast to the plasma‐driven process that occurs on a time scale of minutes. The formation of TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> also validates the effects of Ostwald ripening—some of the Ti radicals react with carbon and nitrogen‐containing free radicals in the plasma to form TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>. Moreover, we speculate that Ostwald ripening plays a crucial role in the incorporation of N atoms into TiO<sub>2</sub>. Indeed, the plasma exposure may increase the surface energy by peeling off the reconstructed surfaces of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, thereby lowering the energy barrier for the N atom entry.</p><p>The crystal structures and phases of the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode are analyzed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD), as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>. It should be noted that the popular procedure proposed by Dunn et al. cannot be applied appropriately here, since significant peak shifts are observed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On the other hand, the method adopted from ref. <sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0026" style="unformatted"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> works well; indeed, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202200694-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information, the oxidation <i>i</i><sub>cap</sub> of both TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub> present clear linear dependency on <i>v</i>.</p><figure xml:id="smll202200694-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202200694:smll202200694-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202200694-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical study confirms the enhanced kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ion migration. C) Differentiation of Faradaic current <i>i</i><sub>diff</sub> and capacitive current <i>i</i><sub>cap</sub>. D) Linear fitting of Faradaic current versus square root of scan rate <i>v</i> of the two electrodes.</caption></figure><p>The kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ion transport in electrodes can be investigated by analyzing the variations of <i>i</i><sub>diff</sub>. Linearly fitting the oxidation <i>i</i><sub>diff</sub> versus <i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>, the Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion coefficient <i>D</i> can be compared, as suggested by Equation (<link href="#smll202200694-disp-0002">2</link>) (<i>i</i><sub>diff</sub> ∝ <i>D</i><sup>1/2</sup><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>). The resulting slopes corresponding to the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> (1.59) and the TiO<sub>2</sub> (0.68) electrodes convey that the diffusion coefficient of the former is 5.5 (=(1.59/0.68)<sup>2</sup>) times that of the latter (Figure <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0005">5D</link>). The hopping diffusion in solids is quantified with a diffusion coefficient<displayedItem xml:id="smll202200694-disp-0004" type="mathematics"><label>4</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:smll202200694:smll202200694-math-0006" wiley:location="graphic/smll202200694-math-0006.png"><mrow><mtable><mtr><mtd><mrow><mi>D</mi><mtext> </mtext><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>D</mi><mn>0</mn></msub><mtext> </mtext><mtext>exp</mtext><mrow><mfenced><mrow><mo>−</mo><mfrac><msub><mi>E</mi><mi>A</mi></msub><mrow><msub><mi>k</mi><mi mathvariant="normal">B</mi></msub><mi>T</mi></mrow></mfrac></mrow></mfenced></mrow></mrow></mtd></mtr></mtable></mrow></math></displayedItem>where <i>D</i><sub>0</sub> is a pre‐exponential factor, <i>E</i><sub>A</sub> is the apparent activation energy, and <i>k</i><sub>B</sub> is the Boltzmann constant. Therefore, a 5.5‐fold increase in the diffusion coefficient indicates a 1.7‐fold decrease in <i>E</i><sub>A</sub>.</p><p>Now we can discuss how the built‐in electric field at the heterointerface affects the transport of Na<sup>+</sup> ions. In the reported hopping diffusion studies, Na<sup>+</sup> moved along the c‐axis of a bulk anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> unit cell by a distance of 1/2 <i>c</i> = 0.48 nm (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0006"><b>6</b>A</link>), experiencing a hopping barrier of 0.56 eV (Figure <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0006">6B</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202200694-bib-0004"><sup>4b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As mentioned above, the electric field strength at the heterointerface is as high as 1 V nm<sup>−1</sup>, which will cause the hopping barrier to tilt substantially. The electric field reduces the barrier by 0.24 eV [=1/2 × (0.48 nm) × (1 V nm<sup>−1</sup>)], and the tilted barrier height is 0.32 eV (Figure <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0006">6C</link>). Therefore, the diffusion barrier at the heterointerface is 1.75 (=5.6 eV/3.2 eV) times lower than that of bulk TiO<sub>2</sub>, which is almost identical to the experimental result. Additionally, the charge transfer resistance [diameter of the semicircle of the Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) Nyquist plot] of the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> is much smaller than the pristine anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll202200694-fig-0006">6D</link>), confirming that the charge transfer kinetics has indeed been improved by the heterointerface and the nitrogen doping. The higher slope of the oblique portion in the Nyquist plot in the low‐frequency range corresponding to the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode compared to the TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode evidences the faster diffusion in the heterostructure.</p><figure xml:id="smll202200694-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202200694:smll202200694-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202200694-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Mechanism of electric field‐related reduction of the Na<sup>+</sup> ion hopping barrier. The inset is a unit cell of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub>. First, the active part of the heterostructure must be electron accumulating rather than depleting, otherwise it will repel cations instead of attracting, thus substantially impeding ion transport in the host electrode material. Second, to achieve an electric field that can significantly reduce the diffusion barrier, the concentration of the accumulated electrons must be high enough. For example, trying to make a degenerate layer at the active side of the heterogeneous interface is a feasible way to achieve this requirement. Finally, to ensure the energy band engineering of the interface, it is necessary to construct a high‐quality, ideally flawless interface. Otherwise, the effects of unknown interface features may become difficult to predict.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, this work has addressed low electrical conductivity and large ion transport barrier of TiO<sub>2</sub> SIB negative electrode by a “three birds with one stone” plasma approach. Specifically, a high‐quality, high‐performance non‐rectifying TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> heterostructure was successfully prepared by a one‐step fast plasma processing of TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode. The high electron concentration and strong electrostatic fields have thus been induced at the heterointerface, which lowered the Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion barrier by a factor of 1.7. As a result, the TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SIB anode exhibited high reversible specific capacities of 312.3 and 173.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 0.1 and 10 C, respectively. The specific capacity at 10 C can retain 98.7% after 600 charge‐discharge cycles. The plasma‐induced fast Ostwald ripening of TiO<sub>2</sub> is responsible for the formation of TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub>. A set of generic guidelines is proposed to design the heterostructures for energy storage electrodes. Our work reveals the mechanism of the diffusion barrier impacted by the interfacial electric field, also provides practical insights for the design of next‐generation heterostructure‐based energy storage materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Synthesis</title><p>The “many birds with one stone” synthesis of TiC<i><sub>x</sub></i>N<sub>1–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/N‐TiO<sub>2</sub> heterostructure was completed by a one‐step urea plasma treatment within only 5 min. The “many‐birds” of the plasma “stone” include the simultaneous epitaxial growth of the heterostructure, interface energy‐band engineering, and N‐doping. The preparation of the anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode followed the established process. The slurry for electrodes were prepared with anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> nanopowder (0.2 g, Aladdin, purity &gt; 99.8%, 25 nm), acetylene black (0.025 g), and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF, 0.025 g) by vigorous stirring in N‐methylpyrrolidone (NMP, 1 mL). The slurry was coated on copper foil and vacuum dried at 100 °C for 12 h to obtain TiO<sub>2</sub> electrodes.</p><p>The realization of the epitaxial growth of heterostructure, interface energy‐band engineering, and N‐doping relied on the non‐equilibrium character of the plasma, as well as the proprietary design of the plasma reactor and the process. The as‐obtained TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode was placed in a quartz reactor coupled with a rectangular microwave waveguide. After the reactor was evacuated, 10 standard cubic centimeters per minute (sccm) of argon were flown in. The urea was heated to 120 °C to make it sublime upstream of the TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode. The power of the microwave source was turned up to 500 W to ignite the plasma, and then H<sub>2</sub> (100 sccm) gas was introduced. The temperature in the reactor was about 500 °C as measured by an infrared thermometer. The plasma process was maintained for 5 min, then the sample was cooled in Ar flow after turning off the plasma.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The crystallographic phases of the samples were investigated with XRD (Bruker D8 Advance A25) using Cu Ka radiation with a scan rate of 4° min<sup>−1</sup>. The structure and morphology of the nanomaterials were examined by TEM, HRTEM (TEM, JEOL JEM‐2100f). STEM‐HAADF was conducted on a Themis Z (FEI, Titan Z G3, 300 kV). XPS was carried out on a Thermo Scientific Escalab 250Xi XPS.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrodes were assembled into CR2016 coin cells with the metal Na as the counter and reference electrode, glass fiber (Whatman GF/D, 19 mm in diameter) as the separator, and NaClO<sub>4</sub> 1 <sc>m</sc> in a mix of ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate (volume ratio of 1:1) with 5 vol.% fluoroethylene carbonate as the electrolyte. The mass of the material loading on an electrode was obtained by weighing <i>N</i> electrodes, subtracting the mass of <i>N</i> pieces of current collectors, and then divided by <i>N</i>. The mass of active material on an electrode = the material loading on an electrode × 0.8. The specific capacity was the measured capacity divided by mass of active material on an electrode. EIS (frequency ≈10<sup>−3</sup>–10<sup>6</sup> Hz) and CV (scan rate 0.2–20 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) were performed on a CHI660E electrochemistry workstation. Charge‐discharge tests were carried out in a voltage range of 0.01 to 3 V versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup> with a LAND battery tester (Wuhan, China).</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202200694-sec-0100" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>Q.C. and X.L. contributed equally to this work.
