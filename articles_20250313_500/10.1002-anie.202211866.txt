Whereas high energy/power densities and stabilities for DIBs are limited by cathodes. To overcome these barriers, we have designed a novel fluoridized‐polyaniline‐H<sup>+</sup>/carbon nanotubes (FPHC) as cathode for high‐efficiency PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> storage. F<sup>−</sup> in PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> is easy to form covalent bond with H on −NH− in FPHC, so that PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> can stably coordinate with FPHC, showing a symmetrical structure. FPHC cathode shows a highly reversible capacity of 73 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles, which provides a solid base for the advanced sodium dual‐ion batteries (SDIBs) (310 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>/7720 W kg<sup>−1</sup>). Besides, the relative pouch‐type SDIB can drive a vacuum cleaner model with an electric machine. This work may shed light on an up‐and‐coming strategy of robust cathodes for SDIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Conjugated polymers with extended π‐conjugation can get electrochemical performance improved by introducing functional groups. A proof‐of‐concept PANI‐H<sup>+</sup>/CNT organic composite cathode via fluoridized treatment provides a capacity of 73 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> for PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> storage, which may facilitate sodium dual‐ion batteries with long cycle life and stable energy/power outputs.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202211866:anie202211866-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202211866-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202211866-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Increasing energy demands put forward higher requirements for energy storage systems nowadays.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> It is essential to exploit the new‐type energy storage devices with controllable cost and ideal performance.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> At present, sodium‐based dual‐ion batteries (SDIBs) have been widely studied because of the low cost and abundant natural resources of sodium (Na). However, the development of SDIBs with high capacity and energy density is limited by the shortage of appropriate cathode materials. Most anions have larger sizes and lower ion diffusion rates than alkali metal ions, resulting in inadequate electrochemical properties. Graphite is usually used as the cathode material for dual ion batteries (DIBs) while its lack of active sites leads to an insufficient capacity (usually less than 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), which seriously limits the practical applications of DIBs. In addition, solvents in the electrolyte are co‐inserted into the graphite layer of the electrode due to anion‐solvent interactions (e.g., (Solv)<sub><i>y</i></sub>PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>), and this process can cause volume expansion/contraction of more than 136 %, further giving rise to graphite exfoliation and poor electrochemical performance.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0010"><sup>[3]</sup></link> For a SDIB system, the poor performance of an electrode severely restricts the overall energy/power outputs. Therefore, investigating new cathode is an effective approach to improve the performance of SDIBs.</p><p>With extended π‐conjugation and tunable properties, conjugated polymers can obtain refined electrochemical performance by modifying their organic structures, introducing diverse functional groups or redox moieties.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link> From the point of doping, more organic chains in doping states, more “charge islands” would be generated to overlap and delocalize in the polymers, which can further enhance the electrical conductivity of conjugated polymers.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0012"><sup>[5]</sup></link> In comparison with inorganic materials, conjugated polymers have better flexibility and larger free space compared to alleviate the volume expansion during charging/discharging and inhibit serious structural changes of electrode structure to a certain extent.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0015"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Polyaniline (PANI), as a typical conducting conjugated polymer, has been studied extensively in fields of energy storage with a high theoretical capacity of 294 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which can meet requirements of high‐performance for anions storage.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0016"><sup>[7]</sup></link> However, the existence of irreversible reactions caused by different oxidation states of PANI bring down the actual capacity below half of theoretical value.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0017"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Diverse polymerization conditions, such as the kinds of dopants, protonation degrees, pH values of solutions, etc. can change properties of PANI. While the electrochemical characteristics of PANI can be affected by the isomerism, density of structural defects and morphology of polymerized chains.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0018"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Importantly, protonation, which is reflected on the oxidation‐state adjustments of nitrogen atoms on the conjugated backbones of PANI, can expose more active sites and benefit to acquire relatively high capacity.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0019"><sup>[10]</sup></link> However, the effect of this strategy for PANI cathode is still limited and needs to be further improved.</p><p>Fluorine (F) can effectively regulate the photoelectric properties of conjugated polymers, thanks to its electron‐withdrawing, low van der Waals radius and low polarizability features. It is also a kind of available resonance donating species which can reduce the gap of conducting polymers.<link href="#anie202211866-bib-0022"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Different from the past, we have first succeeded in introducing fluorine element in protonated PANI/CNT composite (FPHC) to stabilize properties of PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> storage. The HOMO–LUMO gap can be reduced after the introduction of F element, implying better electrical conductivity. Reversible and stable covalent bonds between PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> and FPHC arise from F in PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> with H on −NH− in FPHC, and the whole optimized PF<sub>6</sub>‐FPHC molecular structure tends to be symmetric, which guarantees the cycling stability of FPHC under high current densities. When FPHC cathode coupled with nanoparticle‐like hard carbon (NLHC) anode, the SDIB can deliver high energy/power densities (310 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>/7720 W kg<sup>−1</sup>) and cycle steadily up to 5 000 times at a high rate of 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Likewise, the relative pouch‐type SDIB was assembled to further substantiate its preview of feasible application, which can drive a manufactured electrical vacuum cleaner model with a small motor (3 V, 1 W), indicating the adequate energy/power output of designed SDIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202211866-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202211866-fig-0001">1</link>a, FPHC cathode is adopted in a novel SDIB different from the past, to obtain high energy and power densities and excellent reversible performance. TEM images after 100 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> are depicted in Figure S22, in which FPHC is in good condition.</p><p>We established six monomer units to verify the converged properties with long‐chained polymer. From the Figure <link href="#anie202211866-fig-0003">3</link>a, the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO)‐energy values are calculated to be 3.99 and −0.89 eV for FPHC and PHC, respectively. Reduction potential in organic molecules is correlated with LUMO energy to a certain extent. Thus, it is effective to introduce the component with strong electron‐withdrawing to reduce the LUMO energy and stabilize the working voltage. LUMO is usually represents the ability to trap an electron, which implies that FPHC cathode with lower LUMO energy can more easily combined with PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>, revealing an accelerated process. Meanwhile, the introduction of F reduces the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy values from −3.56 to −4.81 eV. The reduction of HOMO–LUMO energy gap promotes rapid transfer of electrons between FPHC and PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>. Then, a relatively high conductivity of 0.162 S cm<sup>−1</sup> is obtained from FPHC by four‐point probe approach, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202211866-fig-0003">3</link>b. In contrast, the other three structures with higher energy values reveal the deficient stabilities. FPHC possess narrower HOMO–LUMO gap, after coordinating with PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>, the FPHC‐PF<sub>6</sub> tends to exhibits a symmetric structure, which is beneficial to the cycling stability at high current densities. As expected, the FPHC cathode can deliver a high specific capacity of 115 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, while a stable reversible capacity of 73 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> retained after 2000 cycles at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Low‐cost FPHC//NLHC with good performance and durability are reflected in high energy/power densities (310 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>/7720 W kg<sup>−1</sup>) and a robust CE ≈100 % after 5000 cycles under a high rate. With proper adjustment, FPHC based pouch‐type SDIB can meet electricity demand of an electrical motor (3 V, 1 W) for the electrical vacuum cleaner model. In summary, this research presents an avenue to design excellent SDIB cathodes, and we hope experimental results in this work would inspire more attraction and endeavour to delve into PANI‐based SDIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202211866-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National
