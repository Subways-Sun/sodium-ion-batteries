                  NASCION-type Na4MnV(PO4)3/C was synthesized through a sol–gel method. Two Na+ ions can reversibly (de)intercalation from/into the unit structure, with a reversible capacity of 106.7 mAh/g. The charge–discharge curves show a voltage slope at 3.4 V, and a plateau at 3.6 V. To elucidate the sodium storage mechanisms, the structure evolution and electron transfer are demonstrated using in-situ X-ray diffraction and ex-situ X-ray absorption spectroscopy. It is found that at different stage of the electrochemical process, it undergoes different phase reaction process with different redox couples. A single-phase reaction occurs when the first sodium-ion extracted from Na4MnV(PO4)3 with a V3+/V4+ redox, while a two-phase reaction takes place when the second sodium-ion extracted with a Mn2+/Mn3+ redox. Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique, GITT, indicates the single-phase reaction process shows a faster kinetic compared to the two-phase reaction process. These findings between the kinetics, chemical and structural evolution provide new insight into the sodium storage mechanisms of NASICON-type cathode, and further the understanding of other materials for sodium-ion batteries.

            <ce:abstract class="author" id="ab010" view="all"><ce:abstract-sec id="as010" view="all">
                  <ce:simple-para id="sp0010" view="all">NASCION-type Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C was synthesized through a <ce:italic>sol</ce:italic>–<ce:italic>gel</ce:italic> method. Two Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> ions can reversibly (de)intercalation from/into the unit structure, with a reversible capacity of 106.7 mAh/g. The charge–discharge curves show a voltage slope at 3.4 V, and a plateau at 3.6 V. To elucidate the sodium storage mechanisms, the structure evolution and electron transfer are demonstrated using <ce:italic>in-situ</ce:italic> X-ray diffraction and <ce:italic>ex-situ</ce:italic> X-ray absorption spectroscopy. It is found that at different stage of the electrochemical process, it undergoes different phase reaction process with different redox couples. A single-phase reaction occurs when the first sodium-ion extracted from Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> with a V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/V<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup> redox, while a two-phase reaction takes place when the second sodium-ion extracted with a Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup>/Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> redox. Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique, GITT, indicates the single-phase reaction process shows a faster kinetic compared to the two-phase reaction process. These findings between the kinetics, chemical and structural evolution provide new insight into the sodium storage mechanisms of NASICON-type cathode, and further the understanding of other materials for sodium-ion batteries.</ce:simple-para>

                  <ce:label>1</ce:label>
                  <ce:section-title id="st025">Introduction</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="p0030" view="all">Large scale energy storage has been urgently required for the use of grid application in the regulation of intermittent renewable sources (e.g., wind or solar energy) <ce:cross-refs refid="b0005 b0010" id="r0005">[1,2]</ce:cross-refs>. Benefiting from the natural abundance and low cost of sodium resources, sodium-ion batteries are suggested as a promising candidate <ce:cross-refs refid="b0015 b0020" id="r0010">[3,4]</ce:cross-refs>. Numerous materials, such as layered oxides <ce:cross-refs refid="b0025 b0030 b0035" id="r0015">[5–7]</ce:cross-refs>, Prussian blue analogues <ce:cross-refs refid="b0040 b0045" id="r0020">[8,9]</ce:cross-refs>, and polyanion compounds <ce:cross-refs refid="b0050 b0055 b0060" id="r0025">[10–12]</ce:cross-refs> 
                     <ce:italic>etc</ce:italic>. have been studied as candidate cathode materials for high performance SIBs, because of their high capacity, long cycle life and low cost <ce:cross-refs refid="b0065 b0070 b0075" id="r0030">[13–15]</ce:cross-refs>. However, the layered oxides with 2D sodium diffusion path usually surfer from the issues of irreversible phase transition and storage instability <ce:cross-ref refid="b0080" id="c0045">[16]</ce:cross-ref>. Prussian blue analogues, which has open frameworks and porous channels for Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> fast migration, surfer from the challenges of safety, toxicity, and environmental issues <ce:cross-ref refid="b0085" id="c0050">[17]</ce:cross-ref>, however the current bottleneck in the industrialization of Prussian blue compounds lies in the removal of crystal water <ce:cross-refs refid="b0090 b0095" id="r0035">[18,19]</ce:cross-refs>. The sodium super ionic conductor (NASICON) -structured polyanionic phosphates have been considered as promising cathodes for SIBs, for their 3-dimensional open channels, significant thermal stability, and competitive energy density <ce:cross-refs refid="b0100 b0105" id="r0040">[20,21]</ce:cross-refs>.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0035" view="all">Rhombohedral-type NASICON structure of Na<ce:inf loc="post">x</ce:inf>M<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> (<ce:italic>R-3c</ce:italic> space group) (0 ≤ x ≤ 4) (M = transition metal) is constituted of a three-dimensional framework built up by M<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> units, in which the octahedral MO<ce:inf loc="post">6</ce:inf> interlinks via the corners with tetrahedral PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf> 
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="b0105" id="c0055">[21]</ce:cross-ref>. Two different interstitial sites are available for sodium ions in the structure: Na1 with sixfold coordination and Na2 with eightfold coordination. These sites are arranged in an alternating way along the conducting channels. Each NASICON structural unit can accommodate four sodium ions, wherein one is located at Na1 sites and the other three are located at Na2 sites. Introducing different transition metal ions will tune the operation potential with different electrochemical redox couples <ce:cross-refs refid="b0100 b0110 b0115" id="r0045">[20,22,23]</ce:cross-refs>. For charge balance, the unit cell can accommodate 0 ∼ 4 sodium ions in the Na1 and Na2 sites <ce:cross-refs refid="b0120 b0125 b0130" id="r0050">[24–26]</ce:cross-refs>. Zhou <ce:italic>et al.</ce:italic> have introduced bivalence transition metal ions into the NASICON structure, and prepared Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> and Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>NiV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>, in which the Na1 and Na2 sites are fully occupied by sodium ions <ce:cross-ref refid="b0130" id="c0060">[26]</ce:cross-ref>. The introduction of Mn will decrease the cost and increase the working voltage for sodium-ion batteries. Hu et al. showed that in the Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> samples, two sodium-ions can reversibly (de)intercalation from/into the structure, with two voltage plateaus (slope) located at 3.6 and 3.4 V <ce:cross-ref refid="b0135" id="c0065">[27]</ce:cross-ref>. The average working voltage is about 0.2 V higher than that of Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> with an approximate capacity. It is a promising cathode material for sodium-ion battery.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0040" view="all">In this manuscript, Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C was synthesized through a simple sol–gel method. Two sodium-ions are reversibly (de)intercalate from the structure. It shows a high reversible capacity of 106.7 mAh/g, a high initial coulombic efficiency (approximately 96 %), and a good cycling performance (a capacity retention of 87 % after 100 cycles). The electron transfer and crystal structural evolution during the electrochemical process were investigated by <ce:italic>ex-situ</ce:italic> synchrotron X-ray absorption near edge structure, <ce:italic>in-situ</ce:italic> X-ray diffraction, and galvanostatic intermittent titration technique. The relationship between the kinetics, chemical and structural evolution would provide new insight into the sodium storage mechanisms of NASICON-type cathode, and further the understanding of other materials for sodium-ion batteries.</ce:para>
               </ce:section>

                  <ce:section id="s0015" view="all">
                     <ce:label>2.1</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st035">Sample preparation</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0045" view="all">The starting precursors of sodium carbonate (99.5 %, Alfa Aesar), manganese carbonate (99.9 %, Alfa Aesar), ammonium vanadium oxide (99 %, Alfa Aesar), citric acid monohydrate (AR, SCR) and phosphoric acid (85 wt% in H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O, 99.99 %, Sigma-Aldrich) were dissolved in deionized water in the molar ratio 4: 1: 1: 3: 2 (Na: Mn: V: P: C<ce:inf loc="post">6</ce:inf>H<ce:inf loc="post">8</ce:inf>O<ce:inf loc="post">7</ce:inf>·H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O) and stirred for 1 h, followed by an evaporation of solvent. Subsequently, the as-prepared powders were annealed at 800 °C for 3 h under hydrogen argon atmosphere (Ar: H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> = 96: 4) to obtain the Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C samples.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="s0020" view="all">
                     <ce:label>2.2</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st040">Material characterization</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0050" view="all">X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were measured using a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer equipped with a Cu K<ce:inf loc="post">α</ce:inf> radiation source. The powder patterns were refined using TOPAS software. The pair distribution function (PDF) data were obtained on a PANalytical Empyrean outfitted with Ag K<ce:inf loc="post">α</ce:inf> radiation. The sample morphology and composition characterizations were carried out with a scanning electron microscope (SEM, Hitachi S4800) with an energy dispersive X-ray spectrometer (EDS). The carbon content was determined by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA/DSC 3+, METTLER TOLEDO). The high-resolution transmission electron microscopy measurements were performed with a transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEM-2100F, JEOL).</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="s0025" view="all">
                     <ce:label>2.3</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st045">Electrochemical measurements</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0055" view="all">The electrochemical properties of the samples were investigated with CR2032 coin cells. The working electrodes were prepared by casting a slurry of active material, acetylene black, and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) binder in a 70: 20: 10 wt ratio with N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) as solvent coated onto an aluminum current collector. The coin cells were assembled in an argon-filled glove box with the cathode, dried at 100 °C under vacuum for 10 h, a pure sodium anode, a Whatman glass fiber separator, and electrolyte of 1 M NaClO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf> dissolved in PC: EC: FEC (47.5: 47.5: 5). Galvanostatic charge–discharge tests were carried out on a LANHE CT2001A battery test system from 2.5 to 3.8 V at a rate of 0.1C. The rate performance of the electrode was measured on a MACCOR 4200 battery test system. In the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT), the cells were charged at a current of 127 μA for 15 min followed by open circuit relaxation for 12 h.</ce:para>

                        <ce:italic>Ex-situ</ce:italic> synchrotron X-ray absorption spectroscopy</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0060" view="all">The electronic states of Mn and V during the charge and discharge were probed by X-ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) techniques. The XAS data at the Mn and V K-edge of the samples were recorded in transmission mode at beam line BL11B of the Shanghai Synchrotron Radiation Facility (SSRF). The station was operated with a Si (1<ce:hsp sp="0.12"/>1<ce:hsp sp="0.12"/>1) double crystal monochromator. The photon energy was calibrated with the first inflection point of Mn and V <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> in corresponding metal foil. MnCO<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> and Li<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> were selected as references. Analysis of the XAS spectra was carried out using program code of IFEFFIT<ce:cross-ref refid="b0140" id="c0070">[28]</ce:cross-ref>.</ce:para>

                  <ce:section id="s0045" view="all">
                     <ce:label>3.1</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st065">Material characterization of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0070" view="all">The SEM image in <ce:cross-ref refid="s0080" id="c0080">
                           <ce:bold>Fig. S1</ce:bold>
                        </ce:cross-ref> shows that the obtained Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C particles are irregular-shaped in several micrometer size. EDS mapping in <ce:cross-ref refid="s0080" id="c0085">
                           <ce:bold>Fig. S2</ce:bold>
                        </ce:cross-ref> confirm the homogeneous distribution of sodium, manganese, vanadium, phosphorus, and carbon elements in the Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C samples. TEM image in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0005" id="c0090">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:float-anchor refid="f0005"/>
                        <ce:bold>a</ce:bold> verifies the presence of a uniform amorphous carbon layer on the surface of the Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C particles, with a thickness of approximately 10–20 nm. The amount of carbon was estimated to be 9.88 wt% from the weight loss in the TGA, shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="s0080" id="c0095">

                        </ce:cross-ref>. The presence of carbon and the good connection of carbon and Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> are suggested to improve the electrical conductivity of the transition metal phosphates<ce:cross-ref refid="b0145" id="c0100">[29]</ce:cross-ref>. HR-TEM images show that lattice fringes with interplanar distances of 0.435 nm belongs to the (1<ce:hsp sp="0.12"/>0<ce:hsp sp="0.12"/>4) planes of the rhombohedral structure are clearly seen, indicating an ordered single-phase crystal structure.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0075" view="all">The crystal structure of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C was further characterized by XRD, with the result shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0005" id="c0105">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>b.</ce:bold> The diffraction pattern clearly shows a pure single phase, and no crystalline impurities are observed. No peaks belong to carbon are observed, indicating that the carbon coating layer is amorphous. All the diffraction peaks indicate that the obtained Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> crystallizes in the rhombohedral NASICON structure with a space group of R-3c <ce:cross-ref refid="b0130" id="c0110">[26]</ce:cross-ref>. Rietveld analysis shows the lattice parameters for the Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C particles are a = 8.9016(18) Å and c = 21.360(4) Å. The detailed refined results are listed in <ce:cross-ref refid="s0080" id="c0115">
                           <ce:bold>Table S1</ce:bold>
                        </ce:cross-ref>.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0080" view="all">While Bragg diffraction provides information about the average structure, to further explore the crystal structure of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C, the X-ray pair distribution function (PDF) analysis was introduced to gain local structural insight<ce:cross-refs refid="b0150 b0155" id="r0055">[30,31]</ce:cross-refs>. The peaks in PDF correspond to various atom − atom distances in the structure. The PDF curve of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C is plotted in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0005" id="c0120">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>c</ce:bold> (1.4–2.6 Å), showing a series of sharp peaks, each of which corresponds to an interatomic distance. The peaks at 2.10 Å correspond to the TM–O (TM = Mn and V) bond. The different binding energies of Mn, V and O result in two atoms occupying the same site having different bond lengths of Mn-O and V-O, resulting a clear asymmetry in the curve. <ce:cross-ref refid="f0005" id="c0125">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>d</ce:bold> shows the refinements of the PDF data within a r-range of 1–––20.0 Å. The data is in good agreement with the calculated values in short-range and long-range.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0085" view="all">The oxidation state of Mn and V were investigated via the Mn and V <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> XANES spectra, with the comparison of Mn <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> for MnCO<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> and V <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> for Li<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>, as shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0005" id="c0130">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>e and 1f</ce:bold>. The Mn <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> XANES of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C shows a cross-point of the spectra of MnO and MnCO<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> at the energy position at 0.9 of the main absorption height, which indicates a Mn oxidation state of Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> 
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="b0160" id="c0135">[32]</ce:cross-ref>. The absorption edges of V <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> is at energy similar to that of Li<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>, suggesting the oxidation state of V is V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> 
                        <ce:cross-refs refid="b0165 b0170 b0175" id="r0060">[33–35]</ce:cross-refs>.</ce:para>

                  <ce:section id="s0050" view="all">
                     <ce:label>3.2</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st070">Electrochemical properties of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0090" view="all">2032-type coin cells were fabricated to measure the electrochemical properties of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C samples. <ce:cross-ref refid="f0010" id="c0140">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:float-anchor refid="f0010"/>
                        <ce:bold>a</ce:bold> shows the galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of the selected cycles in the voltage window of 2.5–3.8 V at a rate of 0.1C (1C = 110 mA g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>). The typical charge–discharge curve can be roughly divided into a voltage slope at average 3.4 V (range from 3.3 V to 3.5 V), and a voltage plateau at 3.6 V, which suggests two different structural and chemical process. The insert dQ/dV curves of the first cycle further demonstrate that two redox couples are located at approximately 3.4 and 3.6 V. Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C shows a charge capacity of 111.3 mAh/g, and a discharge capacity is 106.7 mAh/g, with a coulombic efficiency of 95.9 %. The observed capacity is close to the theoretical value for two sodium extraction from Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>. The energy density of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C is calculated to be up to 368 Wh kg<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>. As shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0010" id="c0145">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>b</ce:bold>, Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C displays a good cyclic stability. After 100 cycles, reversible capacity is maintained at 93.2 mAh/g, which is 87 % of the original reversible capacity. A good long cycling performance is attributed to the uniform carbon coating and the 3-dimensional open channels of the NASICON structure. The rate capability is measured at different current rates, shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0010" id="c0150">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>c</ce:bold>. The reversible capacities are 105.1, 101.1, 88.2, 76.2, 60.8 and 39.8 mAh/g at current rates of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2 and 5C, respectively. It can be seen form the charge–discharge curve in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0010" id="c0155">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>c</ce:bold>, with the increased rate, the capacity contributed from the higher plateau decrease, and the plateau converts to a voltage slope. While, the capacity originates from the lower voltage slope does not decrease much, only showing an increased voltage hysteresis. The results indicate the different electrochemical kinetics during the two sodium deintercalation process. The voltage slope process may have a faster kinetics than the voltage plateau process.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0095" view="all">To further investigate the thermodynamic and kinetic properties upon electrochemical sodium (de)intercalation, which control the electrochemical performance of the materials, GITT measurements were employed. The quasi-equilibrium redox potentials at different electrochemical states are shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0010" id="c0160">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>d</ce:bold>. During the first sodium deintercalation, the quasi-OCV profile exhibits sloping voltage profiles indicating a single-phase reaction, while during the second sodium deintercalation, it exhibits a flat voltage plateau of 3.6 V indicating a two-phase reaction. The structure change mechanism will be further investigated by <ce:italic>in-situ</ce:italic> XRD. The overpotentials during the cycle are quite small. While there are still some differences of the overpotentials in different process. In the voltage sloping at ∼ 3.4 V, the charge/discharge polarization of the cell is only ∼ 5–10 mV. While, in the voltage plateau ∼ 3.6 V, the charge/discharge polarization is ∼ 20–40 mV. The results further indicated that the two plateau (slope) undergoes different electrochemical process with different kinetics. Ceder et al. have suggested that single-phase reaction pathway is kinetically favorable, as it allows the systems to bypass the nucleation and growth limitations in LiFePO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf> case <ce:cross-ref refid="b0180" id="c0165">[36]</ce:cross-ref>.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="s0055" view="all">
                     <ce:label>3.3</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st075">Redox mechanism investigated by <ce:italic>ex-situ</ce:italic> XAS</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0100" view="all">The redox reaction of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C during the first cycle were investigated by <ce:italic>ex-situ</ce:italic> XAS. The normalized XANES of <ce:italic>K</ce:italic>-edges for V and Mn are shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0015" id="c0170">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:float-anchor refid="f0015"/>
                        <ce:bold>a and 3b</ce:bold>. On charging stage, during the voltage slope at ∼ 3.4 V, the V <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> shifts towards higher energy position, indicating the increase of the average oxidation; while the Mn <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> remains unchanged. It indicates that the charge compensation mainly occurs on the oxidizing of V from V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> to V<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup>. At the voltage plateau at ∼ 3.6 V, the V <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> does not shift, while the Mn <ce:italic>K-edge</ce:italic> moves to a higher energy position: the Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> ions are oxidized to Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>, and the V<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup> ions do not change. On discharge, it is reversibly reduced from Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> to Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> and V<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup> to V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>, shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0015" id="c0175">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>. Extended X-ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) was measured to determine the local structure changes of V and Mn in Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C <ce:cross-ref refid="b0150" id="c0180">[30]</ce:cross-ref>. The <ce:italic>k</ce:italic>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">3</ce:sup> weighted Fourier transformed (FT)-EXAFS spectra during the initial cycle are presented in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0015" id="c0185">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>c and 3d</ce:bold>. During the initial cycle, it displays a bond length change solely on V during the slope process, and a bond length change solely on Mn during the plateau process. The bond length change for the first coordination environment is in agreement with the bond change during the redox of V and Mn.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
                  <ce:section id="s0060" view="all">
                     <ce:label>3.4</ce:label>
                     <ce:section-title id="st080">Structure evolution investigated by <ce:italic>in-situ</ce:italic> XRD</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0105" view="all">
                        <ce:italic>In-situ</ce:italic> XRD measurements were taken to monitor the structure evolution of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C during the initial cycle. The XRD patterns along with the initial cycle curve are shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0020" id="c0190">Fig. 4</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:float-anchor refid="f0020"/>
                        <ce:bold>a</ce:bold>. The invariant peaks at 38.4 and 44.7° belong to Al foil, used as current collector and X-ray window. It is clear seen that, during the voltage slope at ∼ 3.4 V, there is only a continuous diffraction peaks shifting, without any new peaks. It indicates that, during this process, Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C undergoes a single-phase reaction, which usually called solid solution reaction. While, several new peaks emerge and grow at the ∼ 3.6 V plateau, indicating a two-phase reaction. The second phase grows at the expense of the pristine one, and becomes a single phase at the end of charge. Considering the charge capacity, the final product can be denoted as Na<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>. Lattice parameter evolution calculated from the <ce:italic>in-situ</ce:italic> XRD patterns is shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0020" id="c0195">Fig. 4</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>b</ce:bold>. On subsequent discharge, the phase transition processes are reversible. The structure of the material completely transforms back to the original Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> phase at the end of discharge. The lattice parameters shift back continuously to their original values. <ce:italic>Ex-situ</ce:italic> XRD measurements were performed on the half-charge state (Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>) and charge state (Na<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>) samples. Rietveld refinements results are shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="f0020" id="c0200">Fig. 4</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:bold>c</ce:bold>. Rietveld refinement of the Na<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> sample was performed R-3c structure with 0.5Na in Na1 sites and 0.5Na in Na2 sites <ce:cross-refs refid="b0110 b0185" id="r0065">[22,37]</ce:cross-refs>. The crystal parameters of the Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> and Na<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> are summarized in <ce:cross-ref refid="s0080" id="c0205">
                           <ce:bold>Table S2</ce:bold>
                        </ce:cross-ref>.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0110" view="all">Herein, the sodium storage mechanisms of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C during the electrochemical process can be described in the following. It undergoes a single-phase reaction and a two-phase reaction. During the voltage slope at ∼ 3.4 V, V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> ions are oxidized to V<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup>, and the electrode undergoes a single-phase reaction with a faster kinetics due to the smaller lattice mismatch. During the voltage plateau at ∼ 3.6 V, Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> ions are oxidized to Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>, during which, it shows a slow kinetics limited by the nucleation and growth of secondary phase in the two-phase reaction.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0115" view="all">There have been many discussions on the reaction mechanism of NASICON-type materials. The sodium storage mechanisms of NASICON materials are either a two-phase reaction or a single-phase reaction. Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> exhibits a typical two-phase reaction process with a flat plateau at ∼ 3.4 V <ce:cross-refs refid="b0190 b0195" id="r0070">[38,39]</ce:cross-refs>. Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>VCr(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> undergoes a one-phase reaction during the sodium deintercalation<ce:cross-ref refid="b0200" id="c0210">[40]</ce:cross-ref>. Na<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>TiV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> undergoes a two-phase reaction during the desodiation, and a single-phase reaction during the sodiation<ce:cross-refs refid="b0205 b0210" id="r0075">[41,42]</ce:cross-refs>. There are a few studies reporting the coexistence of single-phase and two-phase reaction during sodium extraction in polyanionic phosphate electrodes<ce:cross-ref refid="b0215" id="c0215">[43]</ce:cross-ref>. Yamada et al. have verified the coexistence of single-phase and two-phase reaction during lithium extraction in LiFe<ce:inf loc="post">0.4</ce:inf>Mn<ce:inf loc="post">0.6</ce:inf>PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf> combing XAS and XRD studies<ce:cross-refs refid="b0220 b0225" id="r0080">[44,45]</ce:cross-refs>. The Fe<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/Fe<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> redox reaction proceeds in a single-phase reaction, and Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> redox reaction undergoes a two-phase reaction. The origin of the coexistence of single-phase and two-phase reaction in the NASICON-type polyanionic phosphate need to be further understand.</ce:para>
                  </ce:section>
               </ce:section>
               <ce:section id="s0065" view="all">
                  <ce:label>4</ce:label>
                  <ce:section-title id="st085">Conclusions</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="p0120" view="all">A NASICON-type Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C compound was synthesized through a <ce:italic>sol</ce:italic>–<ce:italic>gel</ce:italic> method and performed as a high-performance cathode material for SIBs. It shows a high capacity, good rate capability, and long cycle life. The (de)intercalation process revealed two parts, with a voltage slope and a voltage plateau. The chemical and structural evolution were investigated by <ce:italic>ex-situ</ce:italic> synchrotron XAS and <ce:italic>in-situ</ce:italic> XRD analysis. The electrochemical cycling of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C undergoes a single-phase reaction with V<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup>/V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> redox at the slope around 3.4 V and a two-phase reaction with Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/Mn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> redox at the plateau at 3.6 V, respectively. GITT indicates that the single-phase process shows a faster kinetics comparing to the two-phase reaction, since no nucleation and growth is required.</ce:para>
               </ce:section>
