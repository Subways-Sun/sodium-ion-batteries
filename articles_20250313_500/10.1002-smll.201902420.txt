type="main">Abstract</title><p>Potassium manganese hexacyanoferrate (KMHCF) is a low‐cost Prussian blue analogue (PBA) having a rigid and open framework that can accommodate large alkali ions. Herein, the synthesis of KMHCF and its application as a high‐performance cathode in sodium‐ion batteries (NIBs) is reported. High‐quality KMHCF with low amounts of crystal water and defects and with homogeneous microstructure is obtained by controlling the nucleation and grain growth by using a high‐concentration citrate solution as a precipitation medium. The obtained KMHCF exhibits superior cycling and rate performance as a NIB cathode, showing 80% capacity retention after 1000 cycles at 1 C and a high capacity of 95 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C. Unlike conventional single‐cation batteries, the hybrid NIB with KMHCF as cathode and Na as anode in Na‐ion electrolyte displays three reversible plateaus that involve stepwise insertion/extraction of both K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> in the PBA framework. In later cycling, the K<sup>+</sup>–Na<sup>+</sup> cointercalated phase is partially converted into a cubic sodium manganese hexacyanoferrate (NaMHCF) phase due to the increasing replacement of Na<sup>+</sup> for K<sup>+</sup>.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201902420-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>A K<sub>2</sub>MnFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> Prussian blue analogue with low amount of crystal water and defects, and uniform size distribution</b> can be obtained by precipitation in a citrate medium. This material shows high stability and rate capability as a hybrid sodium‐ion battery cathode, with both Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> being reversible cations in the monoclinic framework.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902420:smll201902420-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902420-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201902420-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="smll201902420-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Replacement of Li by Na in large‐scale stationary rechargeable batteries is of great interest for lowering fabrication cost because of the wider availability of Na versus Li.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0001"/> However, the replacement requires the identification of a suitable Na<sup>+</sup> insertion compound as the cathode. Since Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) has a larger ionic radius than Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å), the 3D mobility of Na<sup>+</sup> is limited in an oxide host having a closely packed structure. Therefore, layered oxides, polyanion framework oxides, and Prussian blue analogues (PBAs) have been explored as possible cathode materials.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0002"/> Open‐framework PBAs with formula A<i><sub>x</sub></i>M[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<i><sub>y</sub></i>·<i>z</i>H<sub>2</sub>O (0 &lt; <i>x</i> &lt; 2; 0 &lt; <i>y</i> ≤ 1, z &lt; 2, A = alkaline metal, M = transition metal) have a large interstitial space that can accommodate Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> with a high diffusion coefficient of 10<sup>−9</sup> to 10<sup>−8</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> and small lattice strain upon Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction, which makes PBAs attractive Na<sup>+</sup> cathodes.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0003"/></p><p>Among the various PBAs with different M, sodium manganese hexacyanoferrate Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MnFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (NaMHCF) has drawn particular attention owing to similar FeC<sub>6</sub> and MnN<sub>6</sub> redox energies, giving a large voltage and capacity in non‐aqueous electrolytes, which is beneficial to obtain high energy density for a sodium‐ion battery (NIB).<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0003" style="qv">3c</link> NaMHCF has three different structures when precipitated under different conditions. Generally, room‐temperature precipitation leads to a cubic phase with low interstitial Na<sup>+</sup> (<i>x</i> &lt; 1.5) and high water content (&gt;12 wt%), and precipitating at elevated temperature can increase the Na<sup>+</sup> filling (1.5 &lt; <i>x</i> &lt; 1.8) and reduce the interstitial water and Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub><sup>4−</sup> vacancies, resulting in a monoclinic structure that is slightly distorted (β = 92.1°) from cubic.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0003 #smll201902420-bib-0004" style="qv">3i,4</link> However, the remaining water in monoclinic NaMHCF is still too high (&gt;10 wt%), which is unfavorable for the cycling stability of NIBs. Rhombohedral NaMHCF is the only phase that contains an acceptable level of water (&lt;5 wt%), but the synthesis usually requires a long dehydration process in high vacuum or/and at high temperature.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0004" style="qv">4e,f</link></p><p>Filled with bigger K<sup>+</sup> (1.38 Å), potassium manganese hexacyanoferrate (KMHCF) is reported to have only one monoclinic structure similar to the monoclinic NaMHCF, but it has smaller lattice parameters and distortion (β = 90.06°).<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0005"/> This material was first studied as a cathode material for potassium‐ion batteries (KIBs).<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0005 #smll201902420-bib-0006"/> Recently, KMHCF has also been demonstrated to be an appealing cathode for NIBs giving a capacity of 120–130 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>,<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0007"/> which is comparable to most reported cubic and monoclinic NaMHCF. Compared with NaMHCF, KMHCF has two attractive merits as an electrode material. First, the bigger K<sup>+</sup> can better fit the large interstitial voids of the MHCF framework, endowing KMHCF with a higher structural stability. Second, as‐precipitated KMHCF usually contains less crystal water than NaMHCF.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0005 #smll201902420-bib-0006 #smll201902420-bib-0007"/> Therefore, using KMHCF as cathode may help to minimize water‐related side effects in non‐aqueous NIBs. However, since the nucleation of KMHCF is much faster than NaMHCF, as‐precipitated KMHCF powders are too fine in morphology (low crystallinity) and contain large amounts of Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub><sup>4−</sup> vacancies (7–15 at% vs Mn), which adversely affects the cathode performance.</p><p>Though KMHCF has been reported as a cathode in NIBs, the cation‐transfer mechanism of such a hybrid NIB is elusive because of the existence of two cation species. There is no doubt that the K<sup>+</sup> in KMHCF will be extracted upon the first charge, but which cation will be inserted upon discharge and take part in the following electrochemical reactions is still an open question. It was empirically inferred that Na<sup>+</sup> will simply replace the K<sup>+</sup> after the first charge resulting in a conversion from KMHCF to NaMHCF. However, no direct evidence has been provided in the literature. On the other hand, a reversed phase conversion from NaMHCF to KMHCF was reported when coupling the NaMHCF NIB with a Na‐K alloy anode that can offer K<sup>+</sup>, suggesting that the KMHCF phase is electrochemically more stable.</p><p>Herein, we report the synthesis of high‐quality KMHCF materials with regulated particle size, water, and defects, and explore its application in the hybrid NIB. A concentrated K/Na‐citrate solution was employed to chelate the Mn<sup>2+</sup> in order to slow down the nucleation of KMHCF and promote homogeneous and near‐stoichiometric crystal growth. The KMHCF electrode shows excellent cycling stability in the hybrid NIB, yielding 100% and 80% capacity retention after 200 and 1000 cycles, respectively, at a current density of 120 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (1 C). The rate capability of the electrode is superior too, being able to deliver a capacity of 95 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C. In addition, we find a distinct plateau evolution in the early cycling of the hybrid NIB, which has not been found in any single‐cation NIB or KIB. Through ex‐situ compositional and structural characterizations, it is revealed that both K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> are involved in reversible insertion/extraction in the PBA framework, forming a K<sup>+</sup>–Na<sup>+</sup> cointercalated phase as an intermediate cathode. With increasing Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and K<sup>+</sup> loss, a cubic NaMHCF phase is produced.</p><p>The KMHCF powders precipitated under different conditions show the same phase structure from their XRD patterns (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a), which can be well‐indexed into high‐purity monoclinic K<sub>2</sub>MnFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (space group: <i>p</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>n</i>).<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0005 #smll201902420-bib-0006"/> Scanning electron microscopic (SEM) images show the sample precipitated in chloride solution at room temperature (KMHCF1) is comprised of ultrafine nanometer‐scale primary grains that, however, can easily aggregate into 10–100 µm secondary particles (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0001"/>b,c). In contrast, the powders precipitated in the citrate solution (KMHCF2) exhibit much improved morphology with very homogenous size distribution (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0001"/>d–e). The secondary particles of KMHCF2 have an average size of ≈300 nm and are composed of several primary grains of ≈50 nm as indicated by transmission electron microscopic (TEM) images (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0001"/>h–i). Increasing the temperature of the citrate solution (KMHCF3) results in well‐crystallized, cubic‐shaped primary grains that show no aggregation at all (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0001"/>f,g). Compared with the nanometer‐sized primary grains of KMHCF1 and KMHCF2, the grain size of KMHCF3 is significantly increased to 2–3 µm, which is consistent with its narrower and stronger diffraction peaks. Due to increased grain size, the color of the powders turns from dark grey (KMHCF1) to light grey (KMHCF2) and white (KMHCF3) (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA, <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a) and inductively coupled plasma (ICP; Table S1, Supporting Information) analysis give the compositions K<sub>1.50</sub>Na<sub>0.02</sub>Mn(Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>)<sub>0.87</sub>·0.73H<sub>2</sub>O, K<sub>1.80</sub> Na<sub>0.05</sub>Mn(Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>)<sub>0.94</sub>·0.49H<sub>2</sub>O and K<sub>1.99</sub>Na<sub>0.01</sub>Mn(Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>)<sub>1.01</sub>·0.07H<sub>2</sub>O for KMHCF1, KMHCF2 and KMHCF3, with 7.5, 4.7, and 0.7 wt% of crystal water, respectively. Although both K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> are present in the solution, the precipitates contain little Na element because the larger K<sup>+</sup> is more adaptable to the interstitial spaces of the MnFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> framework. The powders were further characterized by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), showing very similar spectra for all samples (Figure S2, Supporting Information). The fitting results of Mn 2p and Fe 2p spectra (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0002"/>b,c) prove that the valence of Fe and Mn are both 2. Comparing the composition of the materials, we can see that employing the citrate solution has brought in two benefits: i) the filling ratio of interstitial K<sup>+</sup> is increased, and ii) the number of Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub><sup>4−</sup> vacancies is reduced. In other words, the total defects including the interstitial defects and the coordination defects, can be easily regulated by adjusting the precipitation condition. More importantly, lower defects in the PBA structure leads to a significant reduction of the crystal water as well as much improved crystallinity. In particular, the sample precipitated in high‐concentration citrate solution and at high temperature shows a unique single crystal morphology with a stoichiometry very close to the ideal water‐free K<sub>2</sub>MnFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> which has not been reported so far. We believe the high‐concentration citrate ligand that has stronger chelating ability than Cl<sup>−</sup> is able to slow down the release of Mn<sup>2+</sup> leading to more homogeneous nucleation and grain growth for KMHCF. On this basis, increasing the solution temperature further promotes the diffusion of K<sup>+</sup> and Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub><sup>4−</sup> and thus yields an extra‐low amount of water and defects.</p><figure xml:id="smll201902420-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902420:smll201902420-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902420-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of the KMHCF powders. SEM images of b,c) KMHCF1, d,e) KMHCF2, f,g) KMHCF3, and h,i) TEM images of KMHCF2.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="smll201902420-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902420:smll201902420-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902420-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) TG curves of the KMHCF powders. b) Mn 2p and c) Fe 2p XPS spectra of KMHCF2 powder.</caption></figure><p>KMHCF powders were mixed in a well‐dispersed suspension of carbon nanotubes/nanofibers (CNTs/CNFs) in isopropanol and then vacuum‐filtrated to produce a free‐standing and binder‐free cathode. The electrochemical performances were evaluated in Na half‐cells by using a NaClO<sub>4</sub>‐based electrolyte. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a,b, the KMHCF1 and KMHCF2 electrodes exhibit very clear charge/discharge plateaus within the potential range of 3.3–3.7 V versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup>. However, such plateaus are not observable in the galvonostatic profiles of the KMHCF3 electrode (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>c). The discharge capacity of KMHCF1 first increases to a maximum of 107 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 10 cycles and then decreases continuously to 88 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (82% retention) after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>d). In comparison, the KMHCF2 electrode exhibits a higher specific capacity (≈125 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) and shows no degradation during the first 200 cycles (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>e). Upon further cycling, the stability of KMHCF2 is still impressive, giving a capacity of 101 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles, which corresponds to 80% capacity retention. The cross‐linked conductive network of the KMHCF@CNTs/CNFs composite electrode (Figure S3, Supporting Information) also leads to a high rate capability of KMHCF2, delivering 95 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> capacity at 20 C, which is much higher than the capacity of 46 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for KMHCF1 (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>g). As for the KMHCF3 electrode, only 10 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> initial capacity is delivered which hardly changes during 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>d). After a long‐term cycling, this electrode can be fully activated, showing similar charge/discharge plateaus to KMHCF1 and KMHCF2 (Figure S4, Supporting Information) and a maximum capacity of 68 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 1600 cycles at 1 C, or of 130 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 250 cycles at 0.2 C (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>f). We find that the cubic‐shaped micrometer‐sized grains of KMHCF3 is well‐preserved after an ultralong cycling (Figure S5, Supporting Information) rather than being cracked like traditional layered cathode materials due to phase changes,<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0008"/> which testifies to the high structural stability of the PBA framework. The different cycling behaviors of the three electrodes demonstrate a strong influence of material properties on the electrochemical performances. We believe the faster degradation of KMHCF1 is ascribed to its poorer crystallinity and higher water content. The severe particle aggregation of KMHCF1 also leads to a lower specific capacity due to less utilization of active materials as well as a higher polarization than KMHCF2 which can be identified from the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) curves (Figure S6, Supporting Information). On the other hand, even though KMHCF3 shows the best crystal quality and is almost water‐free, its grain size is too large to enable sufficient kinetics for cation transport, which results in an ultralow initial capacity even at a lower rate. With synergetic improvements in morphology, stoichiometry and water content, KMHCF2 delivers the highest specific capacity among all electrodes and displays an outstanding cycling stability even superior to the water‐ removed NaMHCF reported earlier.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0004" style="qv">4f</link> The homogeneous morphology of KMHCF2 also reliefs the impedance level of the battery as compared to the agglomerated KMHCF1 (Figure S7, Supporting Information), thus leading to superior kinetics and rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="smll201902420-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902420:smll201902420-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902420-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Charge/discharge profiles of a) KMHCF1, b) KMHCF2 and c) KMHCF3 at 1 C. d) Cycling performances of the KMHCF electrodes. Long‐term cycling stability of e) KMHCF2 and f) KMHCF3. g) Rate capability of KMHCF1 and KMHCF2.</caption></figure><p>The aforementioned hybrid NIB differs from conventional NIBs in its cathode which provides only K<sup>+</sup>, while the Na<sup>+</sup> comes from the electrolyte or/and anode. We notice that the galvonostatic profiles of KMHCF1 and KMHCF2 electrodes undergo obvious changes during the early cycles. In particular, the profiles of KMHCF2 are found to evolve from two longer plateaus into three shorter ones within ≈20 cycles, which remain quite stable during continuous cycling (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0003"/>b). Even after 1000 cycles (Figure S8, Supporting Information), the charge/discharge profiles show very stable and reversible plateaus with very little change of the potentials. The cyclic voltammograms (CV) in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a reveal the same electrochemical behavior, showing three reversible peaks after the electrode is stabilized. The high‐potential oxidation peak (O3, 3.76 V) of the pristine electrode, which should be attributed to K<sup>+</sup> extraction, remains at the same potential after stabilization but shows decreased intensity, so does the reduction peak (R3, 3.57 V). The other two peaks are formed at 3.68/3.43 V (O2/R2) and 3.55/3.28 V (O1/R1), the latter of which has a more significant intensity. The unchanged positions of O3/R3 suggest that K<sup>+</sup> may have taken part in the insertion reaction during the discharge, and its contribution in later cycles seems to be partially replaced by another reaction that has a lower redox potential (O1/R1). This reaction can be inferred as Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction according to the fact that the discharge potential of NaMHCF‐based NIBs is ≈200 mV lower than that of the KMHCF‐based KIBs.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0004 #smll201902420-bib-0005 #smll201902420-bib-0006" style="qv">4a‐c,5,6</link> The less noticeable reactions taking place in the intermediate range (O2/R2) may indicate a transition involving the reactions of both cations.</p><figure xml:id="smll201902420-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902420:smll201902420-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902420-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV curves of the pristine and stabilized (30 galvonostatic cycles at 1 C) KMHCF2 electrode at 0.02 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) XRD patterns and c) EDS spectra of KMHCF2 electrodes before and after cycling (normalized with respect to Mn peak).</caption></figure><p>To verify this inference, KMHCF2 electrodes after 1, 30, and 200 cycles were disassembled for structural and compositional analyses. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0004"/>b, the electrode after the first discharge exhibits the same XRD peaks as the pristine electrode, which is very similar to the observations in KMHCF‐based KIBs where no Na<sup>+</sup> is involved.<link href="#smll201902420-bib-0005 #smll201902420-bib-0006"/> Energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS, Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0004"/>c; Table S2, Supporting Information) results prove that both K and Na are present in this electrode. The atomic ratio of K is evidently greater than Na (Na/K = 0.40), which may indicate that K<sup>+</sup> is the major intercalation cation during the initial discharge while Na<sup>+</sup> is a minor one. The Na/K ratio is remarkably increased to 1.58 after 30 cycles, which further reaches 2.11 after 200 cycles, suggesting increased participation of Na<sup>+</sup> in the following reactions. The electrode after 30 cycles exhibits basically the same XRD pattern as the monoclinic KMHCF, but a small amount of a secondary phase with lower diffraction angles is observed, which can be identified as a cubic NaMHCF phase. After 200 cycles, this secondary phase becomes more significant while the original KMHCF phase is still preserved. For more information, TEM observation was performed on an individual particle of active materials before cycling and after 30 cycles (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a,b). Compared to the pristine KMHCF, the cycled material shows the same lattice spacing of 5.1 Å, which belongs to the (200) plane of monoclinic KMHCF. Selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of the cycled material contains some additional details, but the main diffraction spots are consistent with those of the pristine powder. Both XRD and TEM results suggest that the basic monoclinic KMHCF structure can be well retained after the hybrid battery is cycled for a certain time. From the EDS mapping images (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0005"/>c,d), we observe a homogeneous and self‐consistent distribution of all elements. Compared to the pristine material, nearly 50% K is substituted by Na in the cycled material, while their overall stoichiometry in total metals is almost the same (Figure <link href="#smll201902420-fig-0005"/>e,f). Combining these results, we believe that the electrochemical cycling of the hybrid NIB involves a progressive Na<sup>+</sup>‐K<sup>+</sup> exchange in the PBA framework that may last for hundreds of cycles. In the very beginning, K<sup>+</sup> returns to the cathode prior to Na<sup>+</sup> due to its lower reaction barrier allowing a higher discharge potential. As the cycling continues, the high concentration of Na<sup>+</sup> and irreversible loss of K<sup>+</sup> promote more Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation into the PBA, forming a K<sup>+</sup>–Na<sup>+</sup> cointercalated phase (KNaMHCF), which has inherited the crystal structure of the pristine PBA due to the large space of the interstitial voids and the preintercalated larger K<sup>+</sup> that favors the formation of the monoclinic structure. This intermediate KNaMHCF phase is able to offer and accept both K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> of specific ratios for reversible energy storage as illustrated in Scheme S1 in the Supporting Information. The KNaMHCF phase is believed to exist for a long time since the corresponding XRD peaks are quite strong even after 200 cycles. However, as more and more K<sup>+</sup> is substituted by Na<sup>+</sup>, some KNaMHCF phase is transformed into cubic NaMHCF phase. Later on, the two PBA phases coexist in the cathode with a varying ratio at different cycling stages acting as a hybrid cathode. The reason of the superior cycling stability of KMHCF2 over the water‐removed NaMHCF can be attributed to the preinsertion of K<sup>+</sup> during the discharge, yielding a more stable KNaMHCF phase instead of directly forming the NaMHCF phase whose structure is more vulnerable. Although the absolute amount of K<sup>+</sup> is far less than that of Na<sup>+</sup> in the electrolyte, the stabilizing effect of K<sup>+</sup> on the PBA structure during long‐term cycling is significant since K<sup>+</sup> can better fit the interstitial voids of the PBA framework than Na<sup>+</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="smll201902420-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902420:smll201902420-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902420-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>TEM observations and EDS mapping results of a,c,e) pristine and b,d,f) cycled active materials of KMHCF2 (30 cycles at 1 C).</caption></figure><p>In summary, we have reported an effective method to regulate the chemical precipitation of KMHCF for improving its electrochemical performances. By slowing down the nucleation and grain growth, the amount of defects in KMHCF, including interstitial voids and Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub><sup>4−</sup> vacancies, is simultaneously reduced, leading to a reduced amount of crystal water and improved crystallinity. The electrode using ≈300 nm (secondary particle) KMHCF powders displays superior cycling stability and rate capability as a cathode of NIBs. The mechanism of the hybrid NIB is understood as a competing insertion/extraction of K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> in the PBA framework due to the lower energy barrier of K<sup>+</sup> insertion and the much higher concentration of Na<sup>+</sup>. The active material in the hybrid NIB is found to experience stepwise phase changes from the monoclinic KMHCF to a KNaMHCF phase with the same structure, and then partially to the cubic NaMHCF phase.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201902420-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>A.J.Z. and Z.M.X. contributed equally to this work.
