However, MoS<sub>2</sub> presents a disparate electrochemical performance in the ether‐based and ester‐based electrolyte with unclear mechanism. Herein, tiny MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets embedded in nitrogen/sulfur‐codoped carbon (MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC) networks are designed and fabricated through an uncomplicated solvothermal method. Thanks to the ether‐based electrolyte, the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC shows a unique capacity growth in the original stage of cycling. But in the ester‐based electrolyte, MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC shows a usual capacity decay. The increasing capacity puts down to the gradual transformation from MoS<sub>2</sub> to MoS<sub>3</sub> with the structure reconstruction. Based on the above mechanism, MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC demonstrates an excellent recyclability and the specific capacity keeps around 286 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles with an ultralow capacity fading rate of only 0.0034% per cycle. In addition, a MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC‖Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> full cell with ether‐based electrolyte is assembled and demonstrates a capacity of 71 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, suggesting the potential application of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC. Here the electrochemical conversion mechanism of MoS<sub>2</sub> is revealed in the ether‐based electrolyte and significance of the electrolyte design on the promoting Na ion storage behavior is highlighted.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202208291-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Tiny MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets embedded in nitrogen/sulfur‐codoped carbon (MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC) networks are designed and fabricated via an uncomplicated solvothermal method. xml:id="smll202208291-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Renewable energy technology can effectively reduce carbon emissions and assistance achieve carbon neutrality as soon as possible and battery energy storage technology is the key to make up for the intermittent shortcomings of renewable energy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0001 #smll202208291-bib-0002 #smll202208291-bib-0003 #smll202208291-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) become one of the extremely hopeful alternative chemical power sources for large scale energy storage system on account of the cheap and abundant sources of sodium.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0003 #smll202208291-bib-0005"><sup>3,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the large radius (10.2 Å) is not conducive to the rapid transfer and storage of sodium ion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0006 #smll202208291-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, the most studied carbon material, although inexpensive, has a layer spacing of only 0.335 nm, which is very unfavorable for Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is urgent to breakthrough electrode materials to further meliorate the recyclability and rate properties of SIBs to meet the increasing demand.</p><p>Molybdenum sulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>) with high theoretical capacity is particularly attractive among various anode materials for SIBs because of weak van der Waals forces and unique SMoS bond.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0009 #smll202208291-bib-0010"><sup>9,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The interlayer van der Waals relative force promises the easily expanded interlayer spacing, leading to unblocked Na ion transport channel and storage sites.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0009 #smll202208291-bib-0011"><sup>9,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, SMoS bond in MoS<sub>2</sub> results in the smaller polarizability and the phase transition reaction of products from semiconducting phase to metallic phase during discharge process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the practice application of MoS<sub>2</sub> is hindered by its low rate properties and poor cycling stability, which is ascribed to the unsatisfied electron conductivity and volume expansion under repeatedly sodiation/desodiation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0010 #smll202208291-bib-0013 #smll202208291-bib-0014"><sup>10,13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To overcome the above obstacles, intensive exploration has focused on manufacturing nano/microstructured hybrid materials, consisting of ultrasmall MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets uniformly anchored/confined in highly conductive carbon matrices such as graphene and heteroatom‐doped carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0015 #smll202208291-bib-0016 #smll202208291-bib-0017"><sup>15–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This strategy can shorten Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathway, ease volume fluctuations and effectively prevent aggregation, therefore enhancing Na<sup>+</sup> transportation kinetics and mechanical integrity. Many micro/nanostructured MoS<sub>2</sub>@Carbon composites have been built as SIB anode, such as MoS<sub>2</sub>/C‐multiwall carbon nanotube,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> E‐MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon fibers,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> G@MoS<sub>2</sub>‐C nanosheets.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Modulation of suitable electrolyte has been deemed as another efficacious tactic to improve the performance of SIBs and achieved some fine results. The mechanism of improving the performance of sodium storage with ether‐based electrolyte mainly falls into the following three categories: First, Na<sup>+</sup> can be inserted into the layers together with the ether‐based solvent to expand the layer spacing of the electrode material to store more sodium and improve the pseudocapacitance behavior. For instance, Kang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> uses the most familiar natural graphite as the anode material of SIB matched with ether‐based electrolyte obtained a splendid sodium storage capability (150 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 2500 cycles), which precedes the capability in the traditional ester electrolyte. Second, the electrolyte has a significant effect on solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer. It is easy to constitute a thin, uniform, secure and compact SEI film in the ether‐based electrolyte, which is beneficial to provide a high Coulombic efficiency (CE) and outstanding cycling stability. For instance, Yang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesis a series of carbon materials as SIB anode, taking reduced graphene oxide as the typical representative to show the initial CE of 74.6% and the capacity retention of 75.2% after 1000 cycles. Finally, the electrolyte influences the microtopography and crystalline phase evolution of electrode materials. Zhang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> takes unique TiO<sub>2</sub> as the research object and finds that TiO<sub>2</sub> can be completely converted into Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>(TiO<sub>2</sub>) in diglyme‐based electrolyte. While in ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate based electrolyte, even if the discharge to 0.01 V some TiO<sub>2</sub> is still not completely converted. In addition, some crucial electrochemical phenomena remain unclear, such as the gradual increase in the specific capacity of MoS<sub>2</sub> material at the beginning of the cycle when coupled with ether‐based electrolyte.</p><p>Herein, we report unique MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets embedded in nitrogen/sulfur‐codoped carbon (MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC) network composites as high‐performance SIB anode and investigate the activation mechanism of capacity rising in the early stage of the cycle. Specifically, few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> with expanded layer spacing embedded in N/S‐codoped carbon is built via a facile solvothermal combined with annealing treatment. The MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC displays a high capacities of 292 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> under 30 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a capacity of 286 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The remarkable sodium storage property is ascribed to the high‐conductive carbon network, abundant mesopore, and well‐knit structural stability. Additionally, we also reveal the mechanism of the gradually increasing capacity during initial sodiation/desodiation process. That is the gradual conversion of MoS<sub>2</sub> to MoS<sub>3</sub> and the reconfiguration of the microstructure of the electrode material. This work demonstrates the electrochemical conversion mechanism of MoS<sub>2</sub> in the ether‐based electrolyte and highlights importance of the electrolyte design on the promoting Na ion storage behavior.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussions</title><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0030"><title type="main">Morphology and Structure Characterizations of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC Composites</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> manifests the synthesis strategy of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC hybrid network. First, Mo<sup>6+</sup> ions, which surrounded by the surfactant polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), react with thioacetamide (TAA) to form MoS<sub>2</sub>@PVP network precursor in the facile hydrothermal progress. The PVP effectively prevents the aggregation of MoS<sub>2</sub> and serves as carbon source precursor. After annealing in the N<sub>2</sub>, PVP transforms into the carbon network framework with S and N doping, in which the few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> are embed. The S and N doping promise the high conductivity of the carbon network, providing a fast electron transport highway.</p><figure xml:id="smll202208291-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202208291:smll202208291-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202208291-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the preparation, b) SEM image, c,d) TEM images, e) HRTEM image, f) SAED pattern, and g) elemental mapping images of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites.</caption></figure><p>The morphologies of the as‐built MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC hybrids were characterized via scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis. The MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites show connective nanoparticles with the size of 20–30 nm (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1b</link>). In contrast, the commercial MoS<sub>2</sub> presents stacked chunk in the range of 3–5 µm (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S1a</link>, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1c</link> further confirms the interconnected networks of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC. The single primary MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC nanoparticle is constructed by numerous lamellar MoS<sub>2</sub> and NSC substrate. The MoS<sub>2</sub> is embedded in the NSC network, which promises the electron fast transport. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image illustrates that the MoS<sub>2</sub> is mainly one to three layers (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1d</link>) with expanded interlayer spacing such as 0.76 and 0.85 nm, which is larger than theoretical value of MoS<sub>2</sub> (0.62 nm) (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1e</link>). The enlarged interlayer spacing can be ascribed to the introduction the surfactant PVP, which prevents the aggregation between adjacent layers during the growth of MoS<sub>2</sub> crystals. The selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern displaying a series of diffuse rings combined with its radius indicates that the as‐prepared MoS<sub>2</sub> is polycrystalline (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1f</link>). In addition, the energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectrometer elemental mapping reveals that the MoS<sub>2</sub> and heteroatom nitrogen are uniformly distributed into the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC network (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1g</link>).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0040"><title type="main">Phase Composition and Surface Chemical State Analysis of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC Composites</title><p>The crystal structure of as‐made MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites was further examined by X‐ray diffraction (XRD). The XRD pattern shows that all diffraction peaks can be perfectly matched with the 2H phase MoS<sub>2</sub> (pair distribution function# 75‐1539), showing the successful synthesis of MoS<sub>2</sub> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). Moreover, it is worth noting that the very weaker (002) characteristic peak of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC nanonetwork than MoS<sub>2</sub> bulk (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S1b</link>, Supporting Information) indicates that it has a few‐layered structure features,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0023 #smll202208291-bib-0024"><sup>23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which echoes the TEM test results (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0001">1d,e</link>). The surface chemical components and elemental valence stats of the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC network and commercial MoS<sub>2</sub> bulk are identified via X‐ray photoelectron spectra (XPS) analysis (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002">2b–f</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). Their characteristic diffraction peaks appear at the same locations and in similar shapes as shown in the survey XPS spectra (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S2a</link>, Supporting Information). For the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC nanocomposites, the pair of strong peaks at 229.4 and 232.5 eV are attributed to Mo 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Mo 3d<sub>3/2</sub> of tetravalent molybdenum in MoS<sub>2</sub>, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002">2b</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0025 #smll202208291-bib-0026"><sup>25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, the two remaining peaks at 226.6 and 235.6 eV can boil down to S 2s and hexavalent molybdenum, respectively, which is the same as the commercial MoS<sub>2</sub> peak position (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S2b</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0025 #smll202208291-bib-0026"><sup>25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For S 2p spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002">2c</link>), the two distinct peaks located at 162.2 and 163.4 eV can be assigned to S 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of negative divalent sulfur in MoS<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0026 #smll202208291-bib-0027"><sup>26,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> respectively, which is also consistent with commercial MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S2c</link>, Supporting Information). Moreover, there also have two additional weak peaks at 164.5 and 161.2 eV related to SC bonds compared with commercial MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S2c</link>, Supporting Information), indicating that some sulfur is doped into the carbon matrix.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0028 #smll202208291-bib-0029"><sup>28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For the C 1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002">2d</link>), the peaks at 284.6, 285.2, 286, 287, and 288.7 eV can be ascribed to CC/CC, CS/CSMo, CN, CO, and OCO, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The presence of CN and CS bonds indicates that heteroatom nitrogen and sulfur are also doped into the carbon matrix.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The N species doped on carbon can be further subdivided into pyridinic N at 398.8 eV and quaternary N at 401.4 eV through the N 1s fine spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002">2e</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0032 #smll202208291-bib-0033"><sup>32,33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Additionally, the high peak at 395.3 eV is categorized as Mo 3p. The sp<sup>2</sup> hybridized pyridinic N and graphitic N boost conductivity of the carbon network.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The diffraction peaks at 531.6 and 533.8 eV can be classified into CO and CO respectively, which is the result of a few amount of adsorbed oxygen on the sample surface (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0002">2f</link>).</p><figure xml:id="smll202208291-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202208291:smll202208291-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202208291-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD pattern and fine XPS spectra of b) Mo 3d, c) S 2p, d) C 1s, e) N 1s, and f) O 1s of the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites.</caption></figure><p>The weight content of carbon in the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites was determined by thermogravimetric analysis. The MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC network undergoes two transformation processes of carbon to CO<sub>2</sub> and MoS<sub>2</sub> to MoO<sub>3</sub> under air atmosphere.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0017 #smll202208291-bib-0035"><sup>17,35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The weight losses of the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites is 35.2%, and the carbon content is calculated to be 27.9 wt% (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). The specific surface area and pore structure of the as‐built MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites were analyzed by nitrogen adsorption technique. The MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC network exhibits a type IV isotherm curve, indicating its own mesoporous structure. In addition, a large Brunauer–Emmett–Teller specific surface area of 64.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> is also exhibited, which means that the electrode and the electrolyte have sufficient active contact area (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S4a</link>, Supporting Information). The σ<sub>ω</sub> value is 2223 in ester‐based electrolyte, manifesting that slow Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion ability in the ester‐based electrolyte (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S9b</link>, Supporting Information). In addition, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) is another important means to evaluate the diffusion rate of Na‐ions in electrode materials. Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S10</link> in the Supporting Information shows the GITT curves obtained at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a duration of 30 min, followed by 120 min’ relaxation. During relaxation, the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode exhibits smaller voltage fluctuations in the ether electrolyte than in the ester electrolyte, indicating a faster ion transport rate in the ether electrolyte (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S10a</link>, Supporting Information). The fast Na<sup>+</sup> transport ability means that the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC has excellent rate properties in ether‐based electrolytes.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0070"><title type="main">Analysis of the Morphology and Composition of the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC Electrode after Cycling</title><p>To better elucidate the reason underlying the phenomenon that the specific capacity of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode in ether‐based electrolyte increased first and then stabilized, the morphology and chemistry of electrode material are characterized after cycling, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>. After 1200 cycles at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the morphology of electrode material in the fully charged state varies greatly compared with the uncycled state as the TEM result illustrated in Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5a</link>. The microtopography of the electrode material is reconstructed from a microsized network to a smaller nanosheets with several nanometers (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5a</link>). In HRTEM Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5b</link>, some lattice fringes can be observed on the nanosheet, and the interlayer spacing is 0.23 nm, which identify with the (110) interplanar spacing of metallic Mo (joint committee on powder diffraction standards (JCPDS) No. 42‐1120). SAED pattern manifests the amorphous feature of the electrode in charging to 3 V state after cycling (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5b</link>). This may be caused by the formation of amorphous MoS<sub>3</sub> and the presence of a small amount of molybdenum elemental, which is not oxidized. In addition, only the diffraction peaks of copper and some noise peaks of instruments can be observed in the XRD pattern after cycling, which further indicates the amorphous properties of electrode products (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information). In the state of full discharge, the sheets are obvious pulverized into smaller debris (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5d</link>). In addition, the crystal face spacing is 2.3 Å and is consistent with the (220) crystal plane spacing of Na<sub>2</sub>S (JCPDS No. 03‐0933), which can be observed in HRTEM image with discharge to 0.01 V (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5e</link>) suggesting the generation of Na<sub>2</sub>S.</p><figure xml:id="smll202208291-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202208291:smll202208291-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202208291-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,d) TEM images, b,e) HRTEM images and selected area electron diffraction (SAED) images, c,f) high‐resolution XPS spectra of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode after 1200 cycles with ether‐based electrolyte at a,b) full‐charge stage and d,e) full‐discharge stage, and g) schematic illustration of the morphology and composition evolution of the electrode before and after cycling.</caption></figure><p>The variation of the valence state of the electrode material after cycling is further characterized by high‐resolution XPS analysis. Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5c</link> shows the fine XPS spectra of Mo 3d with the electrodes in full charge/discharge state. In the fully charged state, the two obvious diffraction peaks at 235.5 and 232.4 eV can be ascribed to Mo 3d<sub>3/2</sub> and Mo 3d<sub>5/2</sub> of Mo<sup>6+</sup>, respectively. It is worth noting that no Mo<sup>4+</sup> diffraction peak was found, indicating that the electrode material has been transformed from MoS<sub>2</sub> to MoS<sub>3</sub> after 1200 cycles. While the discharge to 0.01 V, the diffraction peak of Mo<sup>6+</sup> disappeared and changed into metallic Mo. In the fully charged state, existing two pairs of peaks in the XPS spectra of S 2p (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5f</link>). The double diffraction peaks at 163 and 161.6 eV put down to 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and 2p<sub>3/2</sub> in S<sup>2−</sup>, respectively. Furthermore, another unique pair of diffraction peaks appeared at 170.1 and 168.8 eV, which can be attributed to partial oxidation of S<sup>2−</sup> to the high state of S<sup>6+</sup> during the charging process. This may be due to some chemical reaction between sulfur and electrolysis during the charging process to generate functional groups such as <inlineGraphic href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202208291:smll202208291-gra-0002" alt="InlineGraphics" location="graphic/smll202208291-gra-0002.png"/>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202208291-bib-0017 #smll202208291-bib-0021 #smll202208291-bib-0042 #smll202208291-bib-0051"><sup>17,21,42,51</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, under full charge state, no organic functional groups containing S<sup>6+</sup> were observed in the Fourier transform ioncyclotron resonance spectra of the electrolyte, which may be caused by the small content of S<sup>6+</sup> and its close adsorption on the surface of the electrode material (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information). When full discharge to 0.01 V, the doublet diffraction peaks of S<sup>6+</sup> disappear again, similar to the case of Mo 3d of Mo<sup>6+</sup>, indicating that this reaction is highly reversible.</p><p>Based on the above analysis results, the morphology and components evolution process of the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC after repeated discharge/charge can be obtained, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0005">5g</link>. The morphology and composition of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode transformed greatly in the sodiation and desodiation reactions. In terms of morphology, the initial microsized network is gradually pulverized and reconstructed to assemble the nanosized network. On the other hand, the composition has converted from pristine MoS<sub>2</sub> into MoS<sub>3</sub>. Fortunately, these two variations are beneficial and positive to improve the performance of sodium storage. Nanoscale sheets can provide a short ion transport distance, which is conducive to rapid ion diffusion, thus obtaining excellent rate property. MoS<sub>3</sub> can provide a higher number of transferred electrons than MoS<sub>2</sub>, thus providing a higher capacity.</p><p>To further confirm whether the effect is mainly from the solvent or the solute, the electrochemical performance of the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode in another ester‐based electrolyte was tested as a comparison, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S13</link> in the Supporting Information. The electrolyte contains the same solvent and solute as the ester‐based and ether‐based electrolytes used above, respectively. The MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrodes exhibit a similar conventional capacity attenuation trend but do not show the capacity growth phenomenon in the presence of matching ether‐based electrolytes. The MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode has a residual capacity of 393 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 140 cycles at a low rate of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a small initial Coulombic efficiency of 62.1% (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S12a,b,S13a,b</link>, Supporting Information). The specific capacity decays slowly from 377 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the 2nd cycle to 248 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the 900th cycle at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to a capacity attenuation rate of 0.038% each cycle (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S13c</link> Supporting Information). In addition, there is no new platform but a similar shape in the GCD curves under different cycles, which is quite different from the phenomenon in ether electrolytes (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S13d</link>, Supporting Information). Therefore, we can conclude that the solvent in the electrolyte has a dominant effect on the electrochemical performance of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0080"><title type="main">Feasibility Analysis of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC in Practical Application</title><p>To further verify the practical application of the as‐made MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC hybrid and ether‐based electrolyte, sodium‐ion full cell (SIFC) was assembled with MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC anode and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode. NVP shows outstanding cyclic stability, such as a reversible capacity remaining 101/97 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 120/400 cycles at 0.2/0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-supitem-0001">S14</link>, Supporting Information). Then, according to the capacity matching rules, Q<sup>+</sup>m<sup>+</sup> = Q<sup>−</sup>m<sup>−</sup>, the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC and NVP electrodes were assembled into the full cell for electrochemical performance testing. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link> shows the discharging process of SIFC. In the CV curve of NVP cathode, there is a pair of obvious redox peaks at 3.53/3.15 V, corresponding to the reversible deintercalation/intercalation of Na‐ion during charge/discharge process (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006">6b</link>). This corresponds to the high charge/discharge platforms of 3.4/3.3 V in Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006">6c</link>. For the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC anode, the CV curve has no obvious peak (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006">6b</link>) and the corresponding GCD curve is a sloping shape (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006">6c</link>). Therefore, the operation window of the NVP‖MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC SIFC is selected to be 0.5–3.6 V. Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006">6e</link> displays the GCD profile of NVP‖MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC SIFC at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The high discharge platform (3.35 V) and wide discharge window indicate that NVP‖MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC SIFC has bright practical application potential. The reversible specific capacity of NVP‖MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC SIFC is maintained at 71 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> undergoes 90 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202208291-fig-0006">6f</link>).</p><figure xml:id="smll202208291-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202208291:smll202208291-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202208291-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the SIFC for discharging process, b) CV curves of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> at 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, c) GCD profiles of MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, d) the photo of lighting LED powered by two SIFC, e) GCD profile of SIFC, and f) cycle property of SIFC (the capacity calculation is based on the total active materials mass of anode and cathode).</caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0090"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>In summary, few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>@nitrogen/sulfur‐codoped carbon network (MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC) was constructed via a simple solvothermal combined calcined treatment process and employed as anode materials for SIBs. Few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> with enlarged interlayer spacing increases the active area and material utilization, reduces the ion transport resistance, and improves the kinetics of chemical reactions. The porous 3D intertwined NSC network backbone effectively enhances the conductivity, strengthens the mechanical structural stability, confines the few‐layer MoS<sub>2</sub> in the intrinsic region, and effectively prevents its structural aggregation and collapse during repeated (dis)charge processes. Benefiting from these advances, the MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC composites deliver eminent cycling stability (286 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and outstanding rate performance (292 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 30 A g<sup>−1</sup>). Importantly, we have revealed the activation mechanism by which MoS<sub>2</sub>@NSC electrode specific capacity increases slowly at the beginning of the cycle when coupled with ether‐based electrolyte. That is, the composition is converted from MoS<sub>2</sub> to MoS<sub>3</sub> while the morphology/structure is optimized and reconstructed with a smaller size.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0100"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0110" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0120" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202208291-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials</title><p>MoO<sub>2</sub>(acac)<sub>2</sub> was purchased from Shanghai Aladdin Biochemial Technology Co., Ltd.
