Designing a 3D confinement scaffold is a promising strategy for constructing dendrite‐free sodium‐metal anodes; however, cycling stability at a large current density (&gt;10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) is still difficult to realize. Herein, the design of new lightweight and fibrous hydroxylated Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> (h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>) MXene based scaffolds with stepped sodiophilic gradient structure (h‐M‐SSG) is reported, and its thickness can be controlled (80−250 µm). The sodiophilic gradient structure (adjusted by h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>) can effectively induce sodium ions to preferentially deposit at the bottom of the scaffold, thus inhibiting dendrite growth. h‐M‐SSG/Na‐based symmetrical batteries exhibit a low polarization voltage and long cycling life at a high current density (40 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) and a high cut‐off capacity (40 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>). Moreover, a Na–O<sub>2</sub> battery with an h‐M‐SSG/Na anode exhibits a low potential gap of 0.137 V after 45 cycles at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and 1000 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. This deposition‐regulation strategy would inspire the design of 3D scaffolds for high‐performance sodium‐metal‐anode‐based batteries.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202106565-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A deposition‐regulation strategy for Na scaffold is reported to induce sodium ions to preferentially deposit at the bottom of anode. xml:id="adma202106565-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Energy storage systems with high energy density and low‐cost are of primary concern in the development of electric vehicles and grid‐scale storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0001 #adma202106565-bib-0002 #adma202106565-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium possesses a high theoretical specific capacity of 1160 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and abundant earth reserves, which is expected to be a promising anode material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0004 #adma202106565-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, as with Li‐metal anodes, Na‐metal anodes also raise serious safety concerns resulting from the uncontrolled dendrite formation and infinite relative dimension change during Na plating/stripping, which have plagued the practical application of Na‐metal‐based batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0006 #adma202106565-bib-0007 #adma202106565-bib-0008"><sup>6–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To circumvent the above problems, some efforts have been made by researchers in the past few decades.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0009 #adma202106565-bib-0010 #adma202106565-bib-0011"><sup>9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, additives of liquid electrolyte are conducive to the formation of stable solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer resulting in inhibiting Na dendrite growth.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0012 #adma202106565-bib-0013"><sup>12,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Engineering artificial protection layers are also prominent alternatives to stabilize the Na/electrolyte surface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0014 #adma202106565-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high reactivity of Na metal has been suppressed in these studies, it still suffers from large inner stress caused by huge volume change. For instance, a reversible areal capacity of 1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> requires plating/stripping an 8.9 μm‐thick Na metal.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The enormous volume change of Na anode generates huge internal stress fluctuations and thus tremendous engineering challenges in the cell. Recently, the strategy of 3D conductive scaffolds is developed for suppressing Na dendrite formation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0016 #adma202106565-bib-0017 #adma202106565-bib-0018 #adma202106565-bib-0019 #adma202106565-bib-0020 #adma202106565-bib-0021"><sup>16–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These nanostructures scaffolds can effectively reduce the local current density for inhibiting the Na dendrite growth, and provide sufficient room to accommodate the huge volume change during plating/stripping at the same time.</p><p>A series of new sodiophilic scaffolds are constantly being designed and synthesized to reduce the Na nucleation barrier. However, excellent cycling stability of scaffold‐based Na anodes under ultrahigh charging/discharging capacity (&gt;5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) and current density (&gt;10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) is still difficult to realize. The uncontrolled growth of dendrites under high current density is the primary cause for the above dilemma. It is worth noting that the location of Na dendrites in the anode has an important effect on the safety performance of batteries, which decides directly the affordability of high current density for the scaffold. This is because the dendrites closest to the electrolyte membrane side are the leading cause of short circuits, rather than those at the bottom of the anode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The scaffold with lithiophilic gradients structure can reduce the probability of dendrite‐induced short circuits, and improve the cycling stability under high current density, which have been proved in the lithium anode battery.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0022 #adma202106565-bib-0023"><sup>22,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> If we can regulate the location of Na deposition in the scaffold, it may improve the cycling stability of the Na‐metal anode under high capacity at large current density. Very recently, we have proposed that the hydroxylated Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> MXene (h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>)‐based scaffold can induce homogeneous nucleation of Na due to the features of sodiophilic, fast Na<sup>+</sup>/electron transport kinetics and good thermal conductivity and mechanical properties, which is deemed as an ideal scaffold.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the cycling stability of this scaffold at large current density (&gt;10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) is still hard to achieve.</p><p>Herein, we design a series of new lightweight h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐based scaffolds with stepped sodiophilic gradient structure (h‐M‐SSG), whose content of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> gradually increases from top (closest to electrolyte membrane side) to bottom (anode current collector side). The high sodiophilic h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> can induce sodium‐ion deposition preferentially at the bottom of anode. Its thickness is controlled and ranges from 80 to 250 µm. Symmetric batteries based on h‐M‐SSG/Na electrodes exhibit a low polarization voltage and excellent cycling stability at high current density (up to 40 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) with a high cut‐off capacity (40 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>). The cycling life of Na–O<sub>2</sub> batteries with pure Na metal, CNTs/Na, h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs/Na, and h‐M‐SSG/Na electrodes are 5, 15, 24, and 45 cycles at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (equivalent to 0.14 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) and 1000 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (equivalent to 0.14 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>), respectively. The Na–O<sub>2</sub> battery based on h‐M‐SSG/Na exhibits a low potential gap of 0.137 V after 45 cycles. This deposition‐regulation strategy maybe inspires the design of 3D scaffolds for high‐performance sodium‐metal anodes.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106565-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The preparation procedure of h‐M‐SSG is illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> and Supporting Information. After 88 h of alkalization process, the 2D layered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> with micrometer size (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S1a</link>, Supporting Information) transformed into the intermediate product a‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> formed by stacking nanoparticle units. The nanoparticles on the surface of a‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> self‐assembled into fibers (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S1b</link>, Supporting Information). As reaction time is prolonged to 156 h, all nanoparticle units of a‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> have completed self‐assembly to form 1D fibrous and hydroxylated Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> (h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>), as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>. Its tube diameter is 30–40 nm, and the internal lattice spacing is further increased (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0002">2b</link>). The solutions with weight mass ratio of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> to CNTs (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0002">2c</link>) of 0:1, 0.3:1, and 1.0:1, are prepared through ultrasonic mixing, and abbreviated as CNTs, 0.3‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs (or h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs) and 1.0‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, respectively. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of 0.3‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) and 1.0‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs solution (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0002">2d,e</link>) indicated that the h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> closely intertwined with CNTs, which would contribute to well mechanical property and tenacity of h‐M‐SSG resulting in a benign 3D conductive network. The h‐M‐SSG scaffold is prepared by vacuum filtration in the order of 1.0‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, 0.3‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, and CNTs solutions, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0001">1</link>. The sodiophilic h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> content of h‐M‐SSG gradually increased from top to bottom.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106565-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106565:adma202106565-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106565-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The Schematic illustration of synthesis of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> and h‐M‐SSG.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="adma202106565-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106565:adma202106565-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106565-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>, and b,c) TEM images of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> (b) and CNTs (c). d) SEM and e) TEM images of 1.0‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs. f) TEM image and TEM‐EDX element mapping (Ti, O, and F) of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>. g) XPS spectra of of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>. h,i) Corresponding high‐resolution XPS spectra of O1s (h) and F1s (i).</caption></figure><p>As we can see from TEM–energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (TEM–EDX) elemental mappings (Figure 2f; Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information), O and F elements are uniformly distributed throughout h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>, which play a key role in inducing Na homogeneous deposition. Meanwhile, the increasement of lattice spacing of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> conduces to the adsorption of K<sup>+</sup> during the alkalization process, and resulted in the increasing of K element content. It is worth noting that the high sodiophilic peculiarity of h‐M‐SSG stems from the rich content of O, F, and K function groups of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0002">2g</link>, Figures <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S4</link> and <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S5</link> in the Supporting Information. Among them, the O and F functional groups possess relatively large binding energies with Na atom, which can effectively reduce nucleation energy, and form the sodiophilic sites. The corresponding high X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra of O1s and F1s are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0002">2h,i</link>, respectively. From these XPS spectra, we can know that most of O and F functional groups are bonded with Ti of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>. As for the K functional group, it could be alloyed with Na, which further improves the sodiophilic property of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>The different thicknesses of the h‐M‐SSG scaffolds from 80 to 250 µm are easily prepared by adjusting the volume of various h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs solutions during the preparation, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S6</link> in the Supporting Information. The various thickness scaffolds can be applied to meet the requirements of different application scenarios. To get more clearly gradient structure in the characterization of SEM–EDX, we choose the thickness about 250 µm as a research object. In addition, the thickness of h‐M‐SSG scaffold is in direct relation to the thickness of Na composite anode. Therefore, the thicker h‐M‐SSG/Na composite anode is more suitable for the following cycling experiment of ultrahigh charging/discharging areal capacity, such as 1–40 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. The prepared h‐M‐SSG keeps perfect without any cracks or deformation after several times repeated folding operation (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>), indicating that it possesses satisfied flexibility. This trait is inherited on h‐M‐SSG/Na composite anode. In Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3b</link>, the prepared h‐M‐SSG film with the thickness about 250 μm has a relatively loose porous structure (101.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S7</link> in the Supporting Information), which is conducive to increase sodium loading capacity and reduce the local current density during cycling to lengthen the Sand's time for inhibiting dendrites growth. In order to verify the stepped gradient structure of h‐M‐SSG, it is characterized by EDS‐mapping. It is worth noting that Ti element comes from h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>, while C element comes from h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> and CNTs. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3c</link>, the structure of h‐M‐SSG can clearly be divided into three layers based on Ti content, indicating that the sodiophilic h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> content stepped increases from top to bottom. This viewpoint is also further verified by the distribution graph of C content (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3d</link>) and related element line scanning (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). Among them, the presence of sporadic Ti element in the top layer of h‐M‐SSG may be caused by slight interference between different solution layers during the preparation process.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106565-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106565:adma202106565-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106565-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Flexibility of h‐M‐SSG and h‐M‐SSG/Na. b) SEM and c,d) EDS‐mapping (Ti and C) of h‐M‐SSG. e) Illustration of Na deposition on h‐M‐SSG and corresponding optical photographs of the top and bottom of h‐M‐SSG before and after Na plating. f–i) Cross‐sectional SEM images of h‐M‐SSG after Na deposition with 10.0 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>, and related partially enlarged detail.</caption></figure><p>The different capacity‐limited protocols are conducted to plate Na on the h‐M‐SSG scaffolds to explore the deposition behavior. The corresponding schematic illustration about bottom‐up deposition and related optical photograph are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3e</link>. The optical photograph of the bottom of h‐M‐SSG exhibits obvious metallic luster (silvery white) after Na deposition with the capacity of 10 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>, whereas the top surface does not, which obvious demonstrates that the sodiophilic gradient structure of h‐M‐SSG could induce Na deposition preferentially at the bottom of anode. This conclusion is further confirmed by the cross‐sectional SEM images of h‐M‐SSG after Na deposition, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3f</link>. After 1 h of Na deposition at the current density of 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, Na filled the whole of h‐M‐SSG, but it could be clearly divided into three layers based on the compactness of Na. The Na layer at the bottom of the h‐M‐SSG scaffold is the most compact and thickest layer, which almost forms a smooth plane without any voids (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3g</link>). The Na layer at the middle of the h‐M‐SSG scaffold is relatively compact. Amounts of microsize sodium particles are stacked crossing each other, and exhibiting a rough granular surface (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3h</link>). The Na layer at the top of h‐M‐SSG scaffold is relatively loose and porous, and still keeps bits of fibrous CNTs (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0003">3i</link>). Therefore, it can be inferred that the deposition behavior of Na on the h‐M‐SSG scaffold is preferentially deposition on the bottom, and then gradually deposit to the middle and top at the condition of the bottom is filled tightly. The cross‐sectional SEM of h‐M‐SSG with different Na deposition capacity‐limited of 20, 30, and 40 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>, also verified above bottom‐up deposition behavior, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-supitem-0001">S9</link> in the Supporting Information. This is caused by the fact that the rich content of sodiophilic h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> at the bottom.</p><p>The sodium deposition behaviors with the capacity of 10 mAh cm<sup>–2</sup> on h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs (0.3‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs) and CNTs scaffolds, are also studied as a control group. As we can see from the cross‐sectional SEM images of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a,b</link>) after Na deposition, Na filled the whole of scaffold. The obtained h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs/Na could be clearly divided into two layers based on the compactness of Na. The upper of Na layer is compact and thick, and the bottom of Na layer is relatively loose. There is no similar bottom‐up deposition sequence as h‐M‐SSG for h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs scaffold. Therefore, the sodium deposition is uniform in the early middle stage, while it will be preferentially inducing Na deposition at the upper layer of scaffold in the latter stage. The metallic luster (silvery white) of top surface of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs scaffold after Na deposition, further verified this viewpoint (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0004">4c</link>). From the cross‐sectional SEM images of CNTs (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0004">4d,e</link>), we can see that Na will preferentially deposit at the surface layer of the scaffold resulting in amounts of bulges and dendrites. This is due to the poor sodiophilic property of CNTs. The metallic luster of surface of CNTs after Na deposition also verified this point (Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0004">4f</link>). In conclusion, we have demonstrated that the bottom‐up deposition sequence of h‐M‐SSG only stems from its stepped sodiophilic gradient structure.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106565-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106565:adma202106565-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106565-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Illustration of Na deposition on h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs. b) Cross‐sectional SEM images of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs after Na deposition with 10.0 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. c) The corresponding optical photographs of the top and bottom of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs before and after Na plating. d) The illustration of the Na deposition on CNTs. e) Cross‐sectional SEM images of CNTs after Na deposition with 10.0 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. f) Corresponding optical photographs of the top and bottom of the CNTs before and after Na plating.</caption></figure><p>The first Na plating behaviors on CNTs, h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, and h‐M‐SSG scaffolds at high current density of 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, are investigated by in situ XRD. The most stable structures of a single Na atom absorbed on h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> surface groups (h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O, h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>F) and CNTs and corresponding binding energies are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106565-fig-0005">5g</link>. In our next research work, we plan to make use of the synergistic effect of h‐M‐SSG/Na and quasi‐solid electrolyte to resolve above problem and improve discharge capacity.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106565-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We have designed a series of new lightweight 3D scaffolds with sodiophilic gradient structure, h‐M‐SSG, which could effectively induce sodium deposition preferentially at the bottom of anode. This sodiophilic gradient structure significantly improve the affordability of the scaffold for high current density and capacity. Its thickness is controlled and ranges from 80 to 250 µm. The symmetrical batteries based on h‐M‐SSG/Na exhibit a low polarization voltage and long cycling life at a high current density (up to 40 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) and capacity (up to 40 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>). Furthermore, the Na–O<sub>2</sub> battery based on h‐M‐SSG/Na exhibits a low potential gap is 0.137 V after 45 cycles at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (equivalent to 0.14 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) and 1000 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (equivalent to 0.14 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>), and the capacity and rate performance are not obviously weakened. This deposition‐regulation strategy maybe inspires the design of 3D scaffolds for high‐performance sodium‐metal anodes.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106565-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="adma202106565-sec-0050" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, h‐M‐SSG Scaffolds</title><p>The alkalization reaction time by KOH was prolonged from 108 to 156 h, beyond that the preparation strategy procedure of h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> is similar to the previous work.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106565-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The mass ratio of solutions for h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub> to CNTs is 0:1, 0.3:1, and 1.0:1, are prepared through ultrasonic mixing by Ultrasonic Homogenizer, and are abbreviated as CNTs, 0.3‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, and 1.0‐h‐Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>/CNTs, respectively. Therefore, the electrolyte was kept unchanged in the whole experiments, thus ensuring that the electrolyte has the same effect on the Na deposition process. DFT analysis were performed here for qualitatively comparing the interaction between different substrates and Na.
