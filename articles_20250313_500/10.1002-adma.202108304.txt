type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) show practical applications in large‐scale energy storage systems. But, their power density is limited by the sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion into the cathode and anode materials. Herein, the authors demonstrate a prototype of ultrahigh power SIB, consisting of the high‐rate Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode, graphite‐type mesocarbon microbeads (MCMB) anode, and Na<sup>+</sup>‐diglyme electrolyte. It is found that the overpotential of the NVP cathode obeys the Ohmic rule. Thus, the as‐synthesized NVP@C@carbon nanotubes (CNTs) cathode with the high conductive CNTs networks displays high electronic conductivity, reducing the overpotential and charge transfer resistances and leading to the remarkable rate capability over 1000C. For the MCMB anode, the initial [Na‐diglyme]<sup>+</sup> co‐intercalation step is pseudocapacitive dominated, and then the expanded graphite's layers ensure the subsequent fast ions diffusion. The rapid (de)intercalation kinetics in between the cathode and anode are well‐matched. Thus, the assembled MCMB|1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme|NVP@C@CNTs full‐cell SIB delivers the energy density of 88 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at the high power density of ≈10 kW kg<sup>−1</sup>. Even at the ultrahigh power density of 23 kW kg<sup>−1</sup>, an energy density of 58 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> is obtained. The encouraging results of the full cell will promote the development of high‐power SIB for large‐scale applications in the future.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202108304-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A prototype of ultrahigh power sodium ion battery (SIB), mesocarbon microbeads|Na<sup>+</sup>‐diglyme|Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, is assembled. The as‐synthesized Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C@CNTs cathode and mesocarbon microbeads anode display the well‐matched rate capability. xml:id="adma202108304-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The large‐scale electrochemical energy storage (EES) systems can integrate sustainable but intermittent energy (e.g., solar and wind), which increase the applications of green energy and reduce the carbon emission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0001 #adma202108304-bib-0002 #adma202108304-bib-0003 #adma202108304-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared with lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), the sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are promising for large‐scale EES applications since the abundant, worldwide distribution, and low‐cost of the sodium resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0005 #adma202108304-bib-0006 #adma202108304-bib-0007 #adma202108304-bib-0008 #adma202108304-bib-0009"><sup>5–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Owing to the larger size of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å), the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into the cathode and anode material is sluggish, leading to unsatisfied rate capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, it is usually accompanied by structural collapses during repeated Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and extraction, resulting in poor cycling stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0009 #adma202108304-bib-0010 #adma202108304-bib-0011 #adma202108304-bib-0012"><sup>9–12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For practical grid storage systems, the EES systems are static.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this case, except for the energy density, the rate capability, power delivering, and lifespan are important as well.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0014 #adma202108304-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, realizing the SIBs with the considerable energy density (higher than the lead–acid battery of 30–50 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>) and high power density (&gt;1 kW kg<sup>−1</sup>) shows promising applications in the start–stop system and energy recoveries for vehicles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0013 #adma202108304-bib-0014"><sup>13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> But, up to now, the ultrahigh power density and long‐life SIBs still remain largely unexplored.</p><p>The high‐power performance of SIBs relies highly on the cathode, anode, and electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0006 #adma202108304-bib-0011 #adma202108304-bib-0013"><sup>6,11,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> One key factor is the kinetics matching between the positive and negative sides. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) is the Na<sup>+</sup> super ionic conductor (NASICON), offering rapid and 3D Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathways with the diffusivities of 10<sup>−9</sup> to 10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, which are three to five orders of magnitude higher than those of layered or tunnel SIB cathodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0016 #adma202108304-bib-0017 #adma202108304-bib-0018"><sup>16–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> But, its high‐rate delivering ability is limited by the low electric conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0019 #adma202108304-bib-0020"><sup>19,20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The NVP requires highly electric conductive modifications (e.g., carbon coating, carbon nanotubes [CNTs], graphene and their composites) to enhance the overall electron transports.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0016 #adma202108304-bib-0017 #adma202108304-bib-0019 #adma202108304-bib-0020 #adma202108304-bib-0021"><sup>16,17,19–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The design of the NVP/conductive networks with the bicontinuous electronic and ionic transports is able to overcome the drawback of NVP cathode and achieve a high‐rate capability over 200C (<i>n</i>C means the completion of <i>n</i> times of charge/discharge cycles in 1 h).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0016 #adma202108304-bib-0019"><sup>16,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, it remains unclear the insightful relationships between the overpotential, conductivity, diffusivity, and rate capability of the NVP cathode, especially at those rates over 200C.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Utilization of the ultrahigh rate NVP cathode to assemble the SIBs with the power density over 10 kW kg<sup>−1</sup> has seldom been reported.</p><p>For the anode material of the SIBs, recently, it is found the solvated [Na‐diglyme]<sup>+</sup> can co‐intercalate into the crystalline graphite anode with fast diffusivity and high reversibility.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0006 #adma202108304-bib-0022 #adma202108304-bib-0023 #adma202108304-bib-0024 #adma202108304-bib-0025 #adma202108304-bib-0026 #adma202108304-bib-0027"><sup>6,22–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Applying such electrochemical behavior for the graphite anode it is able to assemble the high power SIBs with low material and manufacturing costs. Herein, we report the ultrahigh power SIB (<b>Scheme</b> <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0006"><b>1</b></link>), consisting of the NVP@C@CNTs cathode, mesocarbon microbeads (MCMB, a type of graphite material) anode, and 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme as electrolyte. The operation of this SIB follows the “rocking‐chair” mechanism. During the charging process, Na<sup>+</sup> ions are extracted from the NVP cathode and solvated with the solvent molecular, then the [Na‐diglyme]<sup>+</sup> ions co‐intercalate into the MCMB anode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The discharging process is reversed. The as‐synthesized NVP@C@CNTs composites with high electric conductive transport networks ensure rapid reaction kinetics, low internal and charge transfer resistances, and small overpotentials, leading to the rate capability over 1000 C. The MCMB is highly graphitized material, realizing rapid [Na‐diglyme]<sup>+</sup> co‐intercalation within several seconds. The ultrafast kinetics between the cathode and anode are well‐matched, resulting in the assembled full‐cell SIB delivers the energy density of 88 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at the ultrahigh power density of ≈10 kW kg<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="adma202108304-fig-0006"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202108304:adma202108304-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202108304-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Operation mechanism of the MCMB|1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> diglyme|NVP full‐cell SIB. The CNTs are hydrophobic and hard to be homogeneously dispersed in the aqueous solution. To obtain the well dispersion of CNTs, the CNTs were first dispersed in ethylene glycol (EG) through strong ultrasonication then they were mixed with the NVP precursor solution (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). After being dried and annealed in the flowing nitrogen atmosphere, the NVP@C@CNTs were obtained. The NVP@C were synthesized without adding the CNTs during the synthesized processes. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>) of the NVP@C@CNTs and NVP@C are well indexed to the rhombohedral phase (JCPDS No. 53‐0018), indicating the high purity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0017 #adma202108304-bib-0019"><sup>17,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The carbon content of the NVP@C@CNTs and NVP@C is 4.31 and 9.28 wt% respectively, according to the thermogravimetric analysis (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). Thus, the CNTs contents in NVP@C@CNTs is 4.97 wt%. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of NVP@C@CNTs (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001">1b</link>) shows the NVP particles are well embedded in the CNTs frameworks. The particle sizes of NVP are 100–300 nm. While, the NVP@C shows self‐aggregated morphologies (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001">1c</link>). The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001">1d</link>) shows the well‐separated NVP particles in the CNTs networks. The high‐resolution (HR)TEM image of NVP@C@CNTs (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001">1e</link>) shows the well crystalline NVP grains. The distinct lattice fringe of <i>d</i>‐spacing of 0.44 nm corresponds to the (210) plane.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The NVP particles are coated with the amorphous carbon layer of ≈4 nm, and they are well connected to CNTs. While the NVP@C shows the amorphous carbon coating layers (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001">1f</link>). The conductivity of the NVP@C@CNTs and NVP@C is 1144 and 1.7 mS cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. For better comparing the electrochemical performance, the NVP@C were ball‐milled with 5 wt% of CNTs (named as NVP@C/BM‐CNTs, Figure <link href="#adma202108304-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information), and its conductivity is 140 mS cm<sup>−1</sup>, lower than that of NVP@C@CNTs.</p><figure xml:id="adma202108304-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202108304:adma202108304-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202108304-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of the NVP@C@CNTs and NVP@C. SEM images of NVP@C@CNTs (b) and NVP@C (c), respectively. TEM (d) and HRTEM (e) images of NVP@C@CNTs, respectively. f) TEM image of NVP@C.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202108304-sec-0040"><title type="main">Sodium‐Ion Storage Performance of the NVP Cathode</title><p>The electrochemical performance of the NVP cathodes was measured using half‐cell with metallic sodium as the counter and reference electrode. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> shows the galvanostatic charge and discharge curves of the NVP cathodes at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the potential window of 2.5–4.3 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. The NVP@C@CNTs and NVP@C/BM‐CNTs show the specific capacity of 117 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, close to the theoretical capacity of 118 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The flat charge and discharge plateaus at ≈3.4 V are from the reversible two‐phase transitions between Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and NaV<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The NVP@C@CNTs cathode shows a small overpotential (Δ<i>E</i><sub>Q/2</sub>) of 23 mV, lower than the 35 mV for NVP@C/BM‐CNTs (inset of Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0001">1a</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108304-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Rate performance was measured at the specific currents ranging from 0.1 to 80 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202108304-fig-0002">2b</link>). The NVP@C@CNTs cathode delivers the specific capacity of 112, 87, and 73 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1, 10, and 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, higher than those of NVP@C/BM‐CNTs (102, 61, and 47 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). It is proved that the overpotential of the NVP cathode obeys the Ohmic rule. For the NVP@C@CNTs cathode, the NVP particles with a carbon coating layer are well connected to the conductive CNTs networks, showing a much enlarged electronic conductivity, lower overpotential and <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>, and thus an enhanced rate capability than those of the NVP@C/BM‐CNTs. Through kinetics analysis, the charge storage of the NVP@C@CNTs cathode is based on the two‐phase transition reaction and it is a diffusion‐controlled process, even at the high sweep rate of 100 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Benefitting from the high diffusivity and electronic conductivity, the NVP@C@CNTs cathode displays a remarkable rate capability over 1000C. For the MCMB anode, the [Na‐diglyme]<sup>+</sup> co‐intercalation process is highly reversible and rapid. The initial pseudocapacitive co‐intercalation step expands the graphite's layers for subsequent fast ion diffusion. The MCMB anode shows excellent rate capability, which well matches the reaction kinetics with the NVP@C@CNTs cathode.
