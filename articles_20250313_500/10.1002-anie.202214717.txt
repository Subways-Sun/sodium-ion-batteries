Herein, sodium diphenyl ketone (Na‐DK) is applied to realize dual‐function presodiation for HC anodes. It compensates the irreversible Na uptake at the oxygen‐containing functional groups and reacts with carbon defects of five/seven‐membered rings for quasi‐metallic sodium in HC. The as‐formed sodium induces robust NaF‐rich SEI on HC in 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme, favoring the interfacial reaction kinetics and stable Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction. This renders the presodiated HC (pHC) with high ICE of ≈100 % and capacity retention of 82.4 % after 6800 cycles. It is demonstrated to couple with Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathodes in full cells to show high capacity retention of ≈100 % after 700 cycles. This work provides in‐depth understanding of chemical presodiation and a new strategy for highly stable sodium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Sodium diphenyl ketone is applied as a reducing reagent to compensate irreversible Na uptake at oxygen‐containing functional groups and react with defective carbon for quasi‐metallic Na to initiate robust SEI. It leads to ICE of 99.2 % and capacity retention of ≈100 % for 700 cycles in the full cells of presodiated hard carbon//Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202214717:anie202214717-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202214717-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="anie202214717-ntgp-0004"><note xml:id="anie202214717-note-1001"><label>+</label><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie202214717-sec-0001"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as a promising cost‐effective technology for large‐scale electric energy storage and smart electric grid due to abundant sodium resources.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> The electrochemical performance is governed by the electrode materials and their interfacial structures facing electrolytes. A lot of efforts have been made to improve the cathode stability upon Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/extraction.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Of equal importance, the anodes are confronted with breakage and reformation of solid electrolyte interphases (SEI), which are derived from reductive electrolyte decomposition, and this results in low initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) and even Na dendrites.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0007"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Constructing stable interphases at anodes is essential for reversible Na storage and safety concerns.</p><p>Hard carbon (HC) is intensively investigated as anode materials due to its low cost, large specific capacity, and proper redox potentials against Na.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0010"><sup>[4]</sup></link> It is composed of curved, randomly oriented, and defective carbon layers with five/seven‐membered carbon rings and surface oxygen‐containing functional groups, such as hydroxyl and carboxyl groups.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0014"><sup>[5]</sup></link> The Na uptake in the graphitic domains and their constructed pores contributes to the reversible capacity of HC anodes.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0018"><sup>[6]</sup></link> However, the sodiation of carbon defects and oxygen‐containing functional groups is irreversible, as well as its contribution for the SEI formation. This results in low ICE, cycle degradation, and safety concerns of HC anodes, which encourages modulation of interfacial chemistry of HC anodes for stable sodium storage.</p><p>Microstructure manipulation and surface modification are proved to be effective to improve the ICE of HC anodes.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0021"><sup>[7]</sup></link> It is reported that ethanol can function as a pore‐forming agent to reduce surface area and defect level of HC, leading to alleviated irreversible Na loss.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0019"><sup>[6a]</sup></link> Surface coating on HC with Al<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> or TiO<sub>2</sub> is applied to reduce the electrode/electrolyte side reactions to promote its interfacial stability.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0022"><sup>[7a]</sup></link> These can suppress irreversible Na uptake in HC, but has little contribution to sodium loss in the SEI formation. Electrochemical presodiation can efficiently offset the initial Na loss in HC anodes, but is usually limited by the unstable SEI and infeasibility for mass production.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0023"><sup>[7b]</sup></link> Sodium naphthaline solution has been employed to chemically reduce or/and compensate functional groups on HC anode with improved ICE of 87 %, however, its cycle stability is limited by the inhomogeneous and unstable SEI.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0024"><sup>[7c]</sup></link> These urge effective presodiation chemistry to mitigate the irreversible Na uptake at HC anodes.</p><p>Herein, sodium diphenyl ketone (Na‐DK) is employed to react with the oxygen‐containing functional groups and carbon defects of the five/seven‐membered rings to compensate the irreversible Na uptake and produce quasi‐metallic sodium in HC. The chemically formed sodium leads to spontaneous formation of NaF‐rich SEI for the presodiated HC (pHC) in an electrolyte of 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme (G2). High ICE of 99.2 % for pHC is achieved with excellent cycle stability and rate capability. The full cell of pHC//Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> is demonstrated to show long cycle stability of 700 cycles, highlighting the significance of dual‐function chemical presodiation of HC.</p><p>Presodiation of HC is conducted via a chemically stepwise process, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>a. Na‐DK is firstly obtained with Na reacting with diphenyl ketone (DK) in dimethyl ether (DME), which is a mild process. Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) is applied for the detection of unpaired electrons on Na‐DK in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>b. It exhibits a sharp Dysonian peak at <i>g=</i>2.008, indicating electron transfer from Na to DK due to its strong electron affinity. The produced DK⋅<sup>−</sup> radicals exhibit similar EPR signal intensity after aging for 0, 1, 5, and 10 days, implying its excellent stability in Na‐DK/DME solution, as shown in Figure S1. This confirms the remarkable reducibility of Na‐DK, which is verified by its redox peaks at ≈0.55 V in the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curve in Figure S2. HC is then immersed into the Na‐DK solution of 0.5 mol L<sup>−1</sup> for presodiation, which is later washed with DME and dried under vacuum. The energy‐dispersive spectrometry (EDS) mappings recorded in a high‐angle annular dark field (HAADF) of transmission electron microscopy (TEM) suggest uniform distributions of Na, C, and O in pHC after presodiation in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>c and Figure S3.</p><figure xml:id="anie202214717-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202214717:anie202214717-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202214717-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Schematic presodiation. b) EPR spectrum of Na‐DK in DME. c) TEM image and EDS mappings of pHC. d) FTIR spectra of HC and pHC. e) Na 1s XPS spectra of HC and pHC. f) XRD contour map of pHC with presodiation. g) Low‐frequency Raman spectra of HC and pHC.</p></caption></figure><p>Fourier transform infrared spectra (FTIR) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) of pHC and HC are executed to confirm the reaction between Na‐DK and HC. The typical band of O−C=O (1630 cm<sup>−1</sup>) of carboxyl groups in HC is split into the asymmetric (1610 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and symmetric stretching (1474 cm<sup>−1</sup>) of −COONa in pHC, and the bending vibration of −OH (1374 cm<sup>−1</sup>) is eliminated in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>d and Figure S4. The Na 1s XPS spectrum of pHC exhibits a deconvoluted peak at 1070.6 eV in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>e, which is assigned to the Na−O−C species.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0026"><sup>[8]</sup></link> The surface groups and active surface area of HC and pHC are quantitatively revealed by temperature‐programmed desorption (TPD) in Figure S5 and S6. Two pronounced peaks at 320 and 570 °C correspond to the decomposition of hydroxyl, and the peaks at 750 and 900 °C are ascribed to the pyrolysis of carboxyl and carbonyl, respectively, in Figure S5a and 5c. The hydroxyl and carboxyl amounts are calculated from the integrated areas and decrease from 0.048 to 0 mmol g<sup>−1</sup> and 0.032 to 0.007 mmol g<sup>−1</sup> after presodiation, respectively, in accordance with FTIR in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>d and CO<sub>2</sub> desorption in Figure S5b and S5d. The active surface area of pHC for chemisorbed oxygen in Figure S6 is 0.752 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, against 5.934 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> of HC. This suggests that the surface oxygenated functionalities are transformed to the sodium counterparts in pHC. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of pHC and HC is 1.4 and 2.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, in Figure S7. It indicates that the presodiation has negligible influence on the BET surface area of HC. Its contour map of X‐ray diffraction (XRD) is displayed in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>f. The (002) peak is negatively shifted from 23.3° to 22.1° with addition of Na‐DK solution from 0 to 40 μL mg<sub>HC</sub><sup>−1</sup>, implying intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into carbon layers. These indicate that Na‐DK reacts with the functional groups of carboxy and hydroxyl in HC for Na compensation and facilitates the Na intercalation.</p><p>Raman spectroscopy is conducted to investigate Na uptake at defective sites by monitoring the defect degree of pHC and HC in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0001">1</link>g. The two peaks at 273.7 and 429.5 cm<sup>−1</sup> in the low‐frequency region are the signature of curvature‐related structures originated from the five/seven‐membered carbon rings of HC.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0015"><sup>[5a]</sup></link> They become absent in pHC to indicate that Na<sup>+</sup> prefers to adsorbing on the five/seven‐membered carbon rings, rather than the six‐membered carbon rings. The defects of HC are evaluated in Figure S8, where the bands in the range of 1000–1800 cm<sup>−1</sup> are deconvoluted to disordered graphene lattice (D4), defective graphene structure (D1), short‐range sp<sup>3</sup> carbon vibration (D3), and stretching vibration of C−C in planes (G), respectively, by analyzing the integral area ratio of D4 or D1 to the whole area.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0016"><sup>[5b]</sup></link> The defect degree of pHC is decreased to 32.2 % from 58.6 % of HC, indicating that dangling bonds and vacant atom sites are compensated in pHC. This agrees with the reduced <i>I<sub>D1</sub>/I<sub>G</sub></i> ratio in Figure S9, from 1.078 of HC to 0.985 of pHC. It gradually decreases from 1.006 at open circuit potential (OCP) to 0.928 at 0.01 V, and then recovered to 0.987 at 1.5 V in the initial cycle, denoting compensation of the irreversible defects of HC by Na‐DK.</p><p>Solid‐state <sup>23</sup>Na nuclear magnetic resonance (SS NMR) is applied for the compensated Na in pHC in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>a. The two typical peaks at 540 and 858 ppm correspond to the Knight shift of quasi‐metallic sodium, which is attributed to the strong interaction of unpaired electrons and nuclear spins, and a sharp peak at 0 ppm is stemmed from Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0027"><sup>[9]</sup></link> This indicates that the Na species, like Na<sup>+</sup> and quasi‐metallic sodium, is formed after presodiation. Similarly, the two deconvoluted peaks at 1070.6 and 1071.6 eV in Figure S10 are assigned to the surface Na−O−C and quasi‐metallic Na, respectively.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0026"><sup>[8]</sup></link> The pHC anode reacts with ethanol containing 1 % phenolphthalein to show color change from transparent to pink in Figure S11 and S12. The product is analyzed to be sodium ethoxide by <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectra in Figure S13, together with H<sub>2</sub> by gas chromatography (GC) in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>b. The content of quasi‐metallic sodium in pHC is calculated to be 3.24×10<sup>−5</sup> mol g<sub>pHC</sub><sup>−1</sup>, based on the released H<sub>2</sub> of 12 084 ppm. These indicate that the quasi‐metallic sodium in pHC is chemically similar to Na.</p><figure xml:id="anie202214717-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202214717:anie202214717-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202214717-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) <sup>23</sup>Na SS NMR spectrum of pHC. b) GC profile of gas from pHC immersed in ethanol. c) Optimized structures of Na<sup>+</sup> adsorbed on graphene layer of 60 carbon atoms containing five/seven‐membered rings. d,e) Charge density difference and Bader charge distribution of (010) surface of Na‐adsorbed graphene layer. f) DOS of Na‐graphene with K‐points in Brillouin zone (5×5×1).</p></caption></figure><p>The optimized structure with and without Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption was analyzed to illustrate the interaction between Na<sup>+</sup>, DK⋅<sup>−</sup> and HC in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>c and Figure S14–S16. The average Bader charge of carbon atoms in the five/seven‐membered rings is −0.009 in Table S1 and favors Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption due to the electrostatic interaction. It induces electron migration from DK⋅<sup>−</sup> to HC with decreased Bader charge of carbon atoms on the six‐membered and five/seven‐membered carbon rings, from 0.018 and −0.009 to 0.011 and −0.042, respectively, in Table S2. This is confirmed by the absent Dysonian peak in the EPR spectrum of the Na‐DK solution after presodiation in Figure S16, suggesting disappearance of radicals due to the conversion of DK⋅<sup>−</sup>→DK<sup>−</sup>.</p><p>Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>d exhibits distinct charge density difference of a single Na and carbons in an optimized structure, where Na is positively charged and carbons are negatively charged. The (010) cross section derived from Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>d is presented in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>e to reveal that 0.21 electron per atom is transferred from HC to Na<sup>+</sup> for the generation of quasi‐metallic sodium. This is consistent with the Bader charge change of Na<sup>+</sup> with 0.92 to quasi‐metallic sodium of 0.71 in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>c, Figure S17 and Table S3. This indicates reduction of Na<sup>+</sup> to quasi‐metallic sodium by the electrons of HC, which is originally donated from DK⋅<sup>−</sup>. The quasi‐metallic character is verified by an apparent increase of the Na 2 s (0.28 eV<sup>−1</sup>) at the Fermi level in the density of state of Na‐graphene in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0002">2</link>f and Figure S18, which shows the presence of free electrons around sodium. This agrees with that the preferentially adsorbed Na<sup>+</sup> on the five/seven‐membered carbon rings reacts with negatively charged carbons to generate quasi‐metallic sodium in pHC.</p><p>After immersed in 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in G2, a spontaneously formed SEI is observed on pHC, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0003">3</link>a. It is uniform and around 20 nm, comparing to the inhomogeneous SEI between 10 and 50 nm in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0003">3</link>b on HC after the first cycle. The enlarged high‐resolution TEM image of pHC in Figure S19 displays an interplanar spacing of 2.38 Å, matching well with the (200) plane of NaF. No SEI is observed on the desodiated pHC, which was treated by ethanol to remove quasi‐metallic sodium in Figure S20. This verifies the critical role of quasi‐metallic Na in pHC for the SEI formation when contacting with the electrolyte. In the in‐depth XPS of Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0003">3</link>c and 3d, the two peaks at 685.5 and 688.0 eV in the F1s spectra of pHC and HC belong to NaF and organic P−F species, respectively. It is found that NaF dominates and is uniformly distributed in the SEI of pHC in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0003">3</link>c, implying its inorganic‐rich nature. It agrees with the high atomic ratio of F in pHC in Figure S21–23. The mean Young's modulus of the SEI on pHC is measured to be 3.1 GPa by atomic force microscopy (AFM), much higher than that of HC (0.9 GPa) in Figure S24. This is attributed to the uniform and robust NaF‐rich SEI on pHC, which is induced by quasi‐metallic sodium upon presodiation.</p><figure xml:id="anie202214717-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202214717:anie202214717-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202214717-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>HRTEM images of a) pHC after contacting with electrolyte and b) HC after first discharge, and c,d) their respective XPS spectra of F 1s. e) HOMO and LUMO energy levels of different species.</p></caption></figure><p>The highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LOMO) energies of quasi‐metallic Na, solvents, and solvates of electrolytes are calculated with density functional theory (DFT) in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0003">3</link>e. The HOMO energy of quasi‐metallic Na is as high as −3.48 eV, implying its strong electron donicity and reducibility.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0028"><sup>[10]</sup></link> The solvate Na(G2)<sup>+</sup> presents LUMO energy of −1.00 eV, and is facile to accept electrons for reduction. This favors electron transfer from sodium to G2 in the solvate Na(G2)<sup>+</sup> at the electrode/electrolyte interface, inducing its decomposition for SEI on pHC. Simultaneously, NaPF<sub>6</sub> with LUMO energy of −2.14 eV exhibits an equilibrium for NaF and Lewis acid PF<sub>5</sub> in Note S1. The generated PF<sub>5</sub> interacts with the oxygen atoms in G2 to activate the C−O bonds.<link href="#anie202214717-bib-0031"><sup>[11]</sup></link> The C2−C3 (2.80 eV) and C3−O2 (2.72 eV) bonds are prone to dissociate, compared to the C1−O1 (3.01 eV) and C2−O1 (3.10 eV) bonds with higher energy in Figure S25. This is verified by the decomposition products of CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>O‐ and CH<sub>3</sub>O‐ in the <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectra in Figure S26. These manifest that the quasi‐metallic sodium donates electrons to the solvate Na(G2)<sup>+</sup> and NaPF<sub>6</sub> for the spontaneous formation of SEI.</p><p>The electrochemical performance of pHC and HC is evaluated against Na anode with 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/G2 between 0.01 and 1.5 V in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0004">4</link>a. The pHC exhibits high ICE up to 99.2 % with an optimized presodiation solution of 35 μL mg<sub>HC</sub><sup>−1</sup> (Figure S27 and S28), comparing to 84.5 % of HC. The content of active sodium in pHC is calculated to be 9.1×10<sup>−7</sup>, 8.9×10<sup>−6</sup>, and 2.1×10<sup>−5</sup> mol, based on desodiation capacity of 2.0, 25.0 and 43.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with 30, 35 and 40 μL of presodiation solution, respectively, as shown in Figure S29. This is consistent with the mitigated reduction peak at ≈0.8 V in the CV curve and differential capacity analyses (dQ/dV) of pHC in Figure S30 and S31. The pHC anode exhibits highly reversible capacity of 251.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0004">4</link>b and Figure S32, and it is 83.3 % of that at 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, in contrast with 73.1 % (237.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) of HC. The electrochemical impedance of pHC is almost no change during cycles, while it is increased by ≈50 % in HC in Figure S33. Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) curves in Figure S34 and S35 further indicate that the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient of pHC is higher than that of HC in the range of 10<sup>−12</sup>–10<sup>−9</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>. The superior ICE and rate capability of pHC is originated from its compact and robust NaF‐rich SEI and enhanced interfacial kinetics.</p><figure xml:id="anie202214717-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202214717:anie202214717-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202214717-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Charge/discharge curves at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in the first cycle, b) Rate capability, and c) Cycle performance of HC and pHC. d) Normalized charge/discharge profiles of half cells of pHC and NVP and their full cell. e) Cycle performance of full cells at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0004">4</link>c displays the cycle performance of pHC and HC at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, as well as that of pHC at 50, 100, and 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure S36–38. The pHC presents superior cycle stability with capacity retention of 82.4 % over 6800 cycles and average CE of 99.83 %, while the retention of HC is only 59.3 %. This is endowed by the robust SEI upon presodiation, as evidenced in the HRTEM images and XPS spectra in Figure S39 and S40. The pHC anode exhibits excellent cycle stability even at a high mass loading of 7.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> in Figure S41. The pHC anode shows limited air stability due to the presence of CO<sub>2</sub>, as shown in Figure S42 and S43. Full cells with pHC anodes and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathodes are evaluated in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0004">4</link>d. The NVP cathode in half cells delivers 108.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> with good stability in Figure S44. The ICE of pHC//NVP is as high as 92.6 %, in contrast to 52.3 % of HC//NVP in Figure S45. Its capacity retention is ≈100 % after 700 cycles in Figure <link href="#anie202214717-fig-0004">4</link>e, in comparison with significant capacity loss of HC//NVP in 150 cycles. The superior performance of pHC is ascribed to the presodiation of HC with Na‐DK for the Na<sup>+</sup> compensation and robust SEI.</p><p>In summary, the dual‐function presodiation of HC is realized by applying Na‐DK as a reducing reagent. It involves compensation of surface carboxy and hydroxyl groups by Na‐DK and simultaneous reduction of five/seven‐membered carbon rings of HC by the radical DK⋅<sup>−</sup> to yield quasi‐metallic sodium in pHC. The chemically formed sodium leads to formation of robust NaF‐rich SEI on pHC in the electrolyte. It facilitates the interfacial reaction kinetics and reversible Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction. This endows pHC with high ICE of 99.2 % and superior capacity retention during 6800 cycles. It enables a full cell of pHC//NVP to operate over 700 cycles with almost no capacity decay. This investigation highlights the dual‐function presodiation strategy and provides in‐depth understanding of SEI formation, and will promote the development of SIBs with long lifespan.</p><section xml:id="anie202214717-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>This work was supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (52171215 and 21835004), Tianjin Natural Science Foundation (19JCJQJC62400), and Haihe Laboratory of Sustainable Chemical Transformations.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202214717-sec-0003" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section></section><section xml:id="anie202214717-sec-0004" type="openResearch"><section xml:id="anie202214717-sec-0000" type="dataAvailability"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie202214717-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="anie202214717-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="anie202214717-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie202214717-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Hwang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Myung</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Sun</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
