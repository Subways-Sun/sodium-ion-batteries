In this work, a facile strategy is employed to successfully synthesize porous Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P hierarchical nanostructures supported on a flexible carbon fiber cloth (Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC), constructing a robust architecture of ordered nanoarrays. Via such a unique design, porous and bare structures can thoroughly expose the electroactive surfaces to the electrolyte, which is favorable for ultrafast sodium‐ion storage. In addition, the CFC provides an interconnected 3D conductive network to ensure firm electrical connection of the electrode materials. Besides the inherent flexibility of the CFC, the integration of the hierarchical structures of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P with the CFC, as well as the strong synergistic effect between them, effectively help to buffer the mechanical stress caused by repeated sodiation/desodiation, thereby guaranteeing the structural integrity of the overall electrode. Consequently, Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC as an anode shows a record‐high capacity of 279 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> with almost no capacity attenuation after 9000 cycles.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202108985-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The mixed‐valence strategy and construction of an “all in one” electrode is employed to prepare flexible carbon fiber cloth supported porous Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P hierarchical structures comprising nanoneedles and nanosheets. The as‐prepared Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC electrode exhibits enhanced sodium‐storage performance with a high reversible capacity (814 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), remarkable rate performance (279 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and record ultralong cycling life (almost 100% capacity retention after 9000 cycles).<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202108985:adma202108985-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202108985-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202108985-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202108985-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Nowadays, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered to be a very promising candidate for the next generation large‐scale electrochemical energy storage devices by virtue of the abundance of sodium resources and attractive cost‐effectiveness compared with the mature lithium‐ion batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the sluggish diffusion kinetics and larger radius of sodium ions tend to cause structure collapse of electrode materials during charge/discharge.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Design and development on high‐performance anode materials is hence highly desirable to promote the practical application of SIBs. Among the numerous anode candidates, cobalt‐based phosphides including CoP, Co<sub>2</sub>P, and CoP<sub>2</sub> have received considerable attention because of their diverse morphology, controllable preparation, and high theoretical capacity of 894 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the intrinsic low conductivity and poor charge transfer kinetics bring about inferior rate capability, and moreover active materials were usually physically mixed with adhesives, which would be easily peeled off from the current collectors in the absence of strong chemical bonding if suffering drastic volume variation and structure collapse after repeated sodiation/desodiation processes, inevitably resulting in rapid capacity decay and thus poor cycling life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The exfoliation of electroactive materials from current collectors should be mainly responsible for their heavy loss of electrochemical activity and capacity as well as electrochemical instability during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To overcome these hurdles, a common strategy is to downsize cobalt‐based phosphides and to integrate them with a highly conductive carbon matrix like graphene, carbon nanotube, and carbon fiber.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0005 #adma202108985-bib-0006"><sup>5b,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For instance, a hierarchical CoP nanostructures embedded into N/S‐codoped porous carbon sheets has shown a rate capacity of 162 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.0 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0003"><sup>3b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It also reports a kind of hollow CoP@C composites as anode for SIBs exhibiting excellent cycling stability, retaining 121 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 7000 cycles at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0005"><sup>5b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In spite of great efforts on improving the sodium‐storage performance of cobalt‐based phosphides, it cannot be considered as satisfactory after evaluating their capacity and long‐term cycling life at a high‐rate current until now. This is mainly caused by the lack of effective and reasonable fabrication of electrode structures and further integration of different functions and synergistic effects between components. Therefore, rational structure design seems indispensible for high electrochemical performance and cycling ability of electrode materials. In general, the traditional fabrication of electrodes cannot work without adhesives to help load electroactive materials onto current collector, however, the inevitable adhesive failure will surely cause serious exfoliation of electroactive materials, resulting in the rapid capacity and stability attenuation, and even the death of a battery.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The construction of “all in one” 3D hierarchical electrode can effectively solve these aforementioned issues via in situ growth of electroactive materials on flexible carbon substrates.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0001 #adma202108985-bib-0008"><sup>1c,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This strategy could help eliminate the utilization of insulated adhesives and provide a good 3D conductive network for transport of electrons, and more importantly avoid the attenuation of electrochemical performance due to the failure of adhesive.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, well‐tailored hierarchical nanostructures including nanosheets, nanoneedles, nanofibers, and nanotubes would be more favorable for integral structural stability during the electrochemical process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, the hierarchical poly(ethylene oxide)‐coated diatomite‐derived Li<sub>4.4</sub>Si‐Li electrode provided a 3D porous framework with rich transport pathways for electrons/ions and exhibited a rigid mechanical characteristic that could support the entire electrode with a negligible volume change while charging/discharging.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, the presence of mixed‐valence states is favorable for the electrochemical performance of electroactive materials. A large number of studies have reported that the existence of stable redox couples such as Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2+</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> V<sup>5+</sup>/V<sup>4+</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Fe<sup>3+</sup>/Fe<sup>2+</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Ce<sup>4+</sup>/Ce<sup>3+</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Ti<sup>4+</sup>/Ti<sup>3+</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Cu<sup>2+</sup>/Cu<sup>+[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> in electrode materials could improve electrical conductivity and ability of metal‐ion intercalation/deintercalation. For example, when V<sup>5+</sup> in V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanobelts was partially reduced into V<sup>4+</sup>, the in situ formation of V<sup>5+</sup>/V<sup>4+</sup> redox couples resulted in faster ion diffusion, higher electrochemical activity, and electrical conductivity compared to the pristine V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>. Specifically, the electrical conductivity of V<sup>4+</sup>–V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> (2.86 × 10<sup>−5</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>) was at least ten times higher than that of pristine V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> (2.33 × 10<sup>−6</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>), and the lithium‐ion diffusion coefficient of V<sup>4+</sup>–V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> (1.53 × 10<sup>−10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> S<sup>−1</sup>) was two orders of magnitude higher than that of pristine V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> (1.71 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> S<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0013"><sup>13a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Similarily, the Ti<sup>4+</sup>/Ti<sup>3+</sup> redox couples produced by hydrogenation of TiO<sub>2</sub> brought about a very high electrical conductivity of 8 × 10<sup>−2</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup> (much higher than that of the pristine TiO<sub>2</sub> for 2.2 × 10<sup>−7</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>) and an ultrafast exchange rates of lithium ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0016"><sup>16b</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Herein, we have tried to address the abovementioned issues commonly occurring in cobalt phosphides by aid of the mixed‐valence strategy and construction of the “all in one” electrode, hoping to synchronously improve the structure stability and sodium storage performance. The synthetic process of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC has been described in <b>Scheme</b> <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0004"><b>1</b></link> and Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information, which includes three steps, that is, an initial hydrothermal treatment to obtain the Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH)·0.11H<sub>2</sub>O@CFC (Co‐CO@CFC) precursor, the following calcination to realize in situ fabrication of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CFC, and the final phosphorization of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CFC into Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC. However, it is found that if the Co‐CO@CFC precursor was directly phosphated without experiencing calcination, only monovalent CoP could be obtained rather than Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P. So in this contribution, it is a key process by employing the intermediate of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to prepare mixed‐valence Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P composed by CoP/Co<sub>2</sub>P. The as‐obtained Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC was then served as a self‐supported and adhesive‐free anode for SIBs for further electrochemical tests.</p><figure xml:id="adma202108985-fig-0004"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202108985:adma202108985-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202108985-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Synthetic process for CoP@CFC and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC (I: Calcination; II: Phosphorization).</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202108985-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information, presents the digital photographs of pristine carbon fiber cloth (CFC) under bending and cutting, indicating its inherent flexibility that is suitable for working electrodes. The field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) images display the typical woven fiber structure of CFC and its smooth and impurity‐free surface with an average diameter of about 10 µm (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S3a–c</link>, Supporting Information). After treatment by urea and Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O, the ordered arrays of Co‐CO composed by nanoneedles and nanosheets were clearly observed grown on CFC (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S3d–f</link>, Supporting Information). X‐ray diffraction (XRD) analysis firmly identifies the pure phase of Co‐CO, and without calcination into Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> the subsequent phosphorization process only produced CoP rather than Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S4a</link>, Supporting Information). Only nanoneedle arrays were left (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S3g–i</link>, Supporting Information) after the complete structural evolution of Co‐CO nanosheets into CoP nanoneedles. This conclusion of pure‐phase CoP has been well supported by further transmission electron spectroscopy (TEM) characterization (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S3j–l</link>, Supporting Information). However, if Co‐CO@CFC was first oxidized into Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CFC, Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC could be obtained via phosphorization proved by XRD (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S4b</link>, Supporting Information). In the Raman spectra (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S4d</link>, Supporting Information), two typical peaks can be observed at 1342 and 1610 cm<sup>−1</sup> in either CFC, CoP@CFC, or Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC, which correspond to the D band and the G band of carbon in CFC, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Consequently, Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC was found to be of stronger interaction between components by comparing the intergral intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) of the pristine CFC of 1.32, CoP@CFC of 1.49, and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC of 1.88.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Nanoneedles and nanosheets could be simultaneously observed in Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>@CFC (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S5a–c</link>, Supporting Information), and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC was also close to the initial microscopic morphology of Co‐CO@CFC even after phosphorization treatment. The ideal hierarchical structure composed by nanoneedle and nanosheet arrays could be thus well maintained to a large extent (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>), proving its excellent structural stability. A closer observation from Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001">1b,c</link> indicates that there are abundant pores on these nanoneedles and nanosheets in good consistence with the TEM characterization (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001">1e,h</link>). This kind of porous structure has been proved to be very favorable for quick permeation of electrolytes and rapid transport of electrons/sodium‐ions, and is able to alleviate the mechanical strain and buffer volume expansion during sodiation/desodiation process so as to prevent structural pulverization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In sharp contrast, Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S4c</link>, Supporting Information) was actually in heavy aggregation (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S5d–f</link>, Supporting Information) if without support of CFC. Energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscope (EDX) analysis (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001">1d</link>) demonstrates the homogeneous distribution of C, Co, and P elements on Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC, in which Co ions possess mixed‐valence states of Co<sup>3+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup> with a molar ratio of 1:1.5 as verified by the X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis of Co 2p (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). The introduction of Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2+</sup> redox couples enables the formation of percolated pathways for electron hopping between Co<sup>3+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup>, and can encouragingly stimulate the built‐in electric field effect, which induces some localized electron centers to expedite electronic and ionic diffusion kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0013 #adma202108985-bib-0021"><sup>13b,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> More importantly, XPS firmly discloses the formation of CP bonds. As reported that transition‐metal phosphides could be anchored strongly on carbon surface via CPmetal bonds,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0002 #adma202108985-bib-0022"><sup>2e,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> so it is reasonably believed that the presence of CP bonds contributes to stabilizing Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P on the surface of CFC and building a robust electrical contact between them.</p><figure xml:id="adma202108985-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202108985:adma202108985-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202108985-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) Low‐ and high‐magnification FESEM images of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC. d) EDX elemental mapping images of C, Co, and P for Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC. e,h) Low‐ and f,i) high‐magnification TEM images of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC and g,j) their corresponding SAED patterns.</caption></figure><p>The high‐resolution TEM image of the nanosheets (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001">1f</link>) show that the spacings of 0.287 and 0.264 nm could be assigned to the (200) and (201) planes of Co<sub>2</sub>P, and that of 0.247 nm to the (111) plane of CoP, respectively. As for the nanoneedles, the lattice fringes of Co<sub>2</sub>P and CoP can also be observed at the same time (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001">1i</link>). The bright diffraction rings in the SAED patterns (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0001">1g,j</link>) demonstrate the inherent polycrystalline characteristics of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P. EDX spectrum reveals that the atomic ratio of Co and P in nanosheet is 2.8:1 (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S7a</link>, Supporting Information), and that in nanoneedle is 1.9:1 (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S7b</link>, Supporting Information). The much higher atomic ratio of Co to P on the surface of nanosheets indicate the presence of abundant phosphorus vacancy defects in Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC. Besides offering more active sites for sodium storage, these phosphorus vacancies can also induce excess electrons around Co atoms, which are considered to be the centers for negative charges to promote the rapid sodium ions diffusion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0021 #adma202108985-bib-0023"><sup>21,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However in contrast, it is firmly concluded that the atomic ratio of Co and P in the nanoneedles of sample CoP@CFC is only 1:1 (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S7c</link>, Supporting Information), further identifying the high purity of CoP.</p><p>The electrochemical performance of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC, CoP@CFC, and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P was then evaluated by assembling coin cells. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>, and Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S8a,b</link>, Supporting Information, present the representative cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of initial five cycles that show the similar redox reaction processes for Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC, CoP@CFC, and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P. In the first cathodic scan (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0002">2a</link>), the broad peak at around 1.10 V should correspond to the inevitable formation of a solid electrolyte interface (SEI) film,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> while the small peak at around 0.20 V is assigned to the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> in the porous Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P hierarchical structure to form Na<sub>3</sub>P (Equations (<link href="#adma202108985-disp-0001">1</link>) and (<link href="#adma202108985-disp-0002">2</link>)).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0003 #adma202108985-bib-0025"><sup>3b,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For anodic scans, two wide peaks at around 0.35 V and 2.10 V should be caused by decomposition of Na<sub>3</sub>P (Equation (<link href="#adma202108985-disp-0003">3</link>)) and phosphorization of Co species as well as deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> from Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0002 #adma202108985-bib-0003 #adma202108985-bib-0005 #adma202108985-bib-0026"><sup>2a,3a,5b,c,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, the ex situ XRD measurement reveals the reversible conversion reactions of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P into Na<sub>3</sub>P and Co upon the charge/discharge process (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information). Superior to CoP@CFC and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P, the CV curves of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC overlapped well with each other during the subsequent cycles, suggesting its good reversibility of sodiation/desodiation. Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0002">2b</link> shows that Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC has reached a much higher reversible capacity of 814 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles than those of CFC and Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P measured at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. It can be thus deduced that there should be strong synergistic effects between Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P and CFC in such a 3D electronic transport network. Even at as high as 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC could be of a rather high record value of 279 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. then carried out to analyze the electrochemical dynamics as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information, and the results disclose the capacitance‐controlled kinetics characteristics of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC. The high capacitive contribution of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC is mainly owing to the hierarchical porous nanosheet/nanoneedle configuration, which supplies rich transport pathways and active sites for repeated intercalation/deintercalation of sodium ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202108985-bib-0004 #adma202108985-bib-0027"><sup>4,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information, presents the Nyquist plots and the corresponding parameters have been listed in Table <link href="#adma202108985-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information, acquired by electrochemical impendence spectroscopy measurements. As seen, the fresh Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC electrode exhibits a much lower charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) and a higher apparent diffusion coefficient of sodium ions (<i>D</i><sub>Na</sub><sup>+</sup>) than those of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P and CoP@CFC, showing significantly improved electron and sodium‐ion transports caused by the synergistic effect between Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2+</sup> redox couples and phosphorus vacancies. The <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> values of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC before and after cycling drops from 131.4 Ω to about 90.0 Ω, which should be related to the growth of SEI into a final stable state. Additionally, almost unchanged <i>D</i><sub>Na</sub><sup>+</sup> of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC after cycling suggests that the formation of a firm SEI film is conducive to retaining stable transport pathways for sodium ions.</p><p>Based on the above analyses, the extraordinary sodium‐storage performance of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC could be ascribed to the distinctive hierarchical structure configuration and the strong synergistic effect between components. First, the stable existence of Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2+</sup> redox couples could encouragingly stimulate the built‐in electric field effect, which induces some localized electron centers to expedite electronic and ionic diffusion kinetics. The phosphorus vacancies offered more active sites for better sodium storage and induced excess electrons around Co atoms to promote the rapid sodium ions diffusion. Thus the Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2+</sup> redox couples and the phosphorus vacancies synergistically lower down the charge‐transfer impedance and promote rapid sodium ion diffusion. In addition, the interconnected carbon fibers in CFC construct a large 3D conductive network that can serve as expressways to ensure efficient transport of electrons required in the process of sodiation/desodiation for Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P (Figure <link href="#adma202108985-fig-0003">3g</link>). Second, the hierarchical structures composed of ordered arrays of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P nanoneedles and nanosheets vertical to the CFC surface has a high mechanical strength to guarantee the structural integrity during the repeated charge/discharge process. Meanwhile, the active sites of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P attached on CFC are able to be adequately exposed to electrolyte and sodium ions benefited from its porous and bare structure, greatly contributing to the ultrafast transfer of sodium ions. The unique nanostructures could effectively alleviate the mechanical strain and buffer volume expansion during sodiation/desodiation process. Third, Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P was in situ grown on CFC by forming CPCo chemical bonds, which would be coupled with CFC as long as the strong covalently bonding interaction existed. These stable and strong chemical bonds greatly help prevent the exfoliation of Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P from CFC during long‐term cycles, thus leading to remarkably enhanced cycling performance.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202108985-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>We have demonstrated a feasible strategy for successful fabrication of porous Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P hierarchical structures grown on CFC in situ as a self‐supported and adhesive‐free anode for SIBs. Owing to the enhanced electronic conductivity, abundant sodium ion reactive sites and fast electron/sodium‐ions diffusion pathways, and strong synergistic effect between components, the as‐prepared Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>P@CFC electrode exhibits enhanced sodium storage performance with a high reversible capacity (814 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), remarkable rate performance (279 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and record ultralong cycling life (almost 100% capacity retention after 9000 cycles). It is highly expected that this synthetic strategy can be easily extended to other multivalent transition metal‐based electrode materials, providing new insights for design and development of high‐performance SIBs and other energy‐storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202108985-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 51925202, 51972008, and 21771013).
