type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium secondary batteries have gained much attention as alternative power sources to replace lithium secondary batteries. However, some technical issues must be solved to ensure their success. Here, a highly safe and cost‐effective Na‐based dual‐ion battery system employing self‐formulated CuCl cathode material starting from a mixture of Cu and NaCl in conjunction with a nonflammable NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> inorganic liquid electrolyte is demonstrated. It is found that CuCl is spontaneously formed by redox coupling of Cu/Cu(I) and SO<sub>2</sub>/SO<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> anion radical. In the proposed battery, Na<sup>+</sup> and Cl<sup>−</sup> are employed as energy carriers for the anode and cathode, respectively, and it is further demonstrated that the Na‐metal‐free battery configuration is possible using a hard carbon anode. Owing to the use of cheap electrode materials and a highly conductive and safe electrolyte, the proposed batteries deserve to be regarded as a promising approach for next‐generation Na rechargeable batteries.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201902144-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Na‐metal‐free dual‐ion battery systems using a self‐formulating route for CuCl cathode materials with nonflammable SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic electrolyte are presented here. The discharged state active CuCl cathode materials are prepared from highly safe and cost‐effective Cu and NaCl in conjunction with a nonflammable SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic liquid electrolyte.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902144:smll201902144-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902144-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201902144-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="smll201902144-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Li‐ion batteries (LIBs) have dominated the global rechargeable battery market as a result of their proven performance and they also have held a major position in the battery market for electric vehicles (EVs). However, rapid expansion of the EVs market raises supply concerns of lithium resources, which are limited and localized in the crust of earth. Recently, Na secondary batteries have gained much attention as alternatives to replace LIBs due to the relatively abundant sodium resources. Na‐ion batteries (NIBs), Na‐S (NAS), and Na‐NiCl<sub>2</sub> (ZEBRA) batteries are representatives which are now being intensively pursued.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> NIBs have some advantages over other types of Na rechargeable batteries mainly because of their similarity to LIBs in terms of the materials and fabrication process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the energy density and reliability of NIBs need to be further advanced for their successful commercialization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na‐S and ZEBRA (or Na‐NiCl<sub>2</sub>) batteries also show some promising performances including a high energy density and good cycling stability, bringing them close to commercialization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, their major drawback is their high operating temperature of ≈300 °C, which limits their use in only large‐scale energy storage systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Recently, we demonstrated that a room‐temperature Na‐CuCl<sub>2</sub> battery employing nonflammable and highly conductive SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic liquid electrolyte showed a high energy density of ≈580 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> with excellent capacity retention over 1000 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Apart from outstanding battery performance, the Na‐CuCl<sub>2</sub> battery can guarantee safe operation owing to the intrinsic properties of the SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic liquid electrolyte such as nonflammability, five times higher Na ionic conductivity than that of organic electrolyte, and a wide operation temperature range from −40 to 60 °C.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the Na‐CuCl<sub>2</sub> battery has two major problems to overcome for its commercialization. First, the CuCl<sub>2</sub> cathode material used in this system is highly reactive to moisture. For this reason, additional facility for preventing the exposure of the cathode material to humid air, such as a dry room, are mandatory for the fabrication process, which increases the fabrication costs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The second problem arises from the use of a metallic sodium anode in the Na‐CuCl<sub>2</sub> battery system. Even though we reported that a sodium anode does not show dendritic growth in SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic electrolyte,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> one cannot rule out the possibility of safety issues associated with the dendritic growth of the Na anode during cycling, in particular, under abuse and extreme operation conditions.</p><p>To simultaneously resolve these technical problems of the Na‐CuCl<sub>2</sub> battery, we herein propose a new dual‐ion battery chemistry adopting a stable cathode material for the discharged state. By taking advantages of the discharged state cathode material instead of charged state cathode like CuCl<sub>2</sub>, one can avoid the use of the metallic sodium anode in the cell, significantly decreasing the safety concerns of the battery.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, by employing a stable cathode material, we can reduce the production costs by eliminating the additional facilities needed for humidity control as well as improving the safety of the battery fabrication process. Such a research strategy exploiting the cathode material of the discharged state is not new, but is highly effective toward commercialization of rechargeable batteries. For example, commercialization of lithium rechargeable batteries is possible by adopting the discharged state LiCoO<sub>2</sub> cathode material coupled with carbonaceous anode material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> C‐Li<sub>2</sub>S battery chemistry is also a successful example to show the efficiency of changing the status of battery from a charged system to a discharged system for commercialization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Taking technical advantages of carbon anode material compared to lithium metal in conjunction with the discharged state of the sulfur cathode, that is, Li<sub>2</sub>S, the C‐Li<sub>2</sub>S battery showed some promising battery performances such as much improved capacity retention and reliability over prolonged cycles. A similar mechanism has already been reported in the ZEBRA battery, in which discharged state Ni and NaCl are adopted as electrode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0001 #smll201902144-bib-0013" style="qv"><sup>1a,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, it should be noted that the monovalent discharged form of CuCl<sub>2</sub>, that is, CuCl, is also highly reactive in humid air and a very expensive chemical. To further address this issue, we propose that the inherent reaction phenomenon of SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic electrolyte can help us realize our strategy for preparing a manageable cathode material. In previous studies on the discharge reaction mechanism of the Li(Na)‐SO<sub>2</sub> battery system, we found that SO<sub>2</sub>‐based inorganic liquid electrolyte undergoes electrochemical reduction on the carbon electrode, which leaves NaCl and amorphous SO<sub>2</sub> containing species at a potential of ≈2.95 V (vs Li/Li<sup>+</sup>). The potential for this electrochemical reaction is lower than those for both the redox reaction between CuCl<sub>2</sub> and a CuCl cathode and the redox reaction between CuCl and a Cu cathode. Inspired by this potential difference between the redox reaction of Cu/CuCl and electrochemical reduction of SO<sub>2</sub>, we anticipate that spontaneous electrochemical reduction and oxidation reactions could occur when both Cu and carbon particles are coupled in the same cathode through the mixed potential theory. Indeed, the coupling of these two redox reactions in the same electrode resulted in a mixed potential, thus turning the electrode materials into CuCl and NaCl as products in the electrode. Moreover, we also found that NaCl can stabilize CuCl cathode material, thus improving the electrochemical performance of the newly designed cathode using Cu and carbon particles. This means that we can construct a new type of a dual ion battery using very cheap and highly stable Cu, carbon, and table salt. The resulting battery system showed some promising behavior when coupled with a nonmetallic anode system.</p><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> shows the designated conversion reaction of a Cu cathode with conducting agent in NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte through the aforementioned mixed potential theory. The change of the OCP of Cu electrode coupled with carbon can be attributed to the conversion reaction of Cu to CuCl, facilitated by the carbon in the Cu electrode, which provides the more favorable sites for cathodic reaction of the electrolyte based on the mixed equilibrium potential. CuCl in the electrode starting from Cu before cycle. The two phenomena, that is, electrochemical oxidation of Cu and the electrolyte reduction in the same electrode, lead to mixed potentials, as suggested in Equations (1) and (2). The formation of NaCl would follow the reaction route (3), which was also by‐product of electrochemical reduction of SO<sub>2</sub> on the carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0007" style="qv"><sup>7a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0001">1b</link> shows the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the Cu electrode, Cu electrode with carbon black particles, and the carbon black electrode taken after immersion in the electrolyte. The electrode composed of Cu and carbon particles showed the phase transformation of Cu into CuCl and NaCl, whereas the Cu‐only and carbon‐black‐only electrodes did not show any phase change after immersion in the NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte. The scanning electrode microscopy (SEM) observations (Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0001">1c,d</link>) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) with energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) (Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0001">1e</link>) analysis showed the same results, that is, the formation of NaCl and CuCl.</p><p>To verify the designed electrochemical reactions of the in situ formed mixture of CuCl and NaCl electrode, ex situ XRD and ex situ SEM analysis (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>) were performed on the cycled electrodes composed of Cu and carbon black particles. Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0002">2a</link> shows the voltage profile of the Cu electrode after storage for 12 h in the NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte. The difference of the charge and discharge capacities come from the small amount of CuCl<sub>2</sub> caused by further oxidation of CuCl into CuCl<sub>2</sub>. To clarify the oxidation state of the Cu electrode after storage in the electrolyte, in situ X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) analysis was employed (Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0002">2b</link>). Note that the Cu K‐edge X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure (XANES) profiles for CuCl [Cu(I)] and CuCl<sub>2</sub> [Cu(II)] are also shown as references. The Cu<sup>+</sup> features at 8983 and 8986 eV correspond to the Cu 1s→4p and 1s→4pπ* (8986 eV) transitions, respectively, whereas the Cu<sup>2+</sup> state has two intense shoulders at 8986 eV (1s→4pπ* transition) and 8992 eV (1s→4pσ* transition). The Cu electrode soaked in the NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte shows well‐resolved peaks between 8980 and 9000 eV, indicating the presence of Cu<sup>2+</sup> as well as Cu<sup>+</sup> states.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0006 #smll201902144-bib-0014"><sup>6,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0002">2c</link> shows the ex situ XRD patterns of cycled electrodes with respect to the degree of reaction for the first cycle. As anticipated, the converted Cu electrode composed of CuCl and NaCl was electrochemically oxidized into CuCl<sub>2</sub> after charging. During the discharge reaction, the reverse phase transformation of CuCl<sub>2</sub> into CuCl and NaCl could be found. SEM observation combined with EDS mapping for the charged and discharged electrode also showed the same tendency; the triangular shape of CuCl<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles was observed (Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0002">2d</link>) after charged state<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201902144-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and NaCl cubic crystals were present after discharged state (Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0002">2e</link>).</p><figure xml:id="smll201902144-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902144:smll201902144-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902144-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Charge/discharge profiles of the Cu electrode for the first cycle at 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Cu K‐edge XANES profiles of the Cu electrode during storage in the electrolyte. c) Ex situ XRD patterns of the Cu electrodes in NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte during the first cycle. d,e) SEM‐EDS mapping for the charged and discharged electrodes in a Na‐SO<sub>2</sub> cell.</caption></figure><p>From these results, we found that a mixture of CuCl and NaCl could be formed by the Cu electrode and showed the reversible electrochemical redox reaction between CuCl [Cu(I)] and CuCl<sub>2</sub> [Cu(II)]. This suggests that CuCl can also be utilized as an electrode material for the redox reaction between Cu(I) and Cu(II). To verify our hypothesis regarding the use of CuCl as an active material, CuCl and Cu electrodes were investigated as cathode materials for the dual‐ion battery proposed in this work. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link> shows the voltage profiles and cycle performances of Cu and CuCl electrodes with various amounts of NaCl. Note that additional NaCl is basically not necessary for the redox reaction mechanism mentioned before; however, we found that NaCl played an important role by acting as an acid‐base buffering agent to maintain basicity of the electrolyte and Cl source for the cathode reaction, and finally prevent the dissolution of CuCl or CuCl<sub>2</sub> active materials in the cell. Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0003">3a</link> shows the voltage profiles of the pure Cu cathode for the initial two cycles. Unwanted further electrochemical oxidation of Cu into CuCl<sub>2</sub> might result from redox coupling between the redox reaction of CuCl(I)/CuCl<sub>2</sub>(II) and electrochemical reduction of SO<sub>2</sub> in the electrolyte, which caused a marked difference between the charge and discharge capacities. Interestingly, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0003">3b</link>, the addition of NaCl in the Cu electrode can significantly decrease the discrepancy between the charge and discharge capacities. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0003">3c</link>, the CuCl electrode also needs additional NaCl for a more stable charge reaction as well as a decrease of the overpotential for the first charge process, which is probably due to a lack of Cl<sup>–</sup> anions consumed for self‐formulation of Cu into CuCl in the electrolyte. To clarify the effect of additional Cl<sup>−</sup> anions to stabilize the electrolyte as well as Cu electrode, several types of electrodes with various ratios of the additional Cl source to active material were investigated. The voltage profiles of the Cu nanoparticle electrodes with various amounts of NaCl for the first cycle have different charge/discharge capacities (Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0003">3b</link>). Compared to the Cu‐only electrode, the Cu electrode with additional NaCl (Cu:NaCl = 1 and 2 mol ratio) showed significantly increased charge capacities. Moreover, the introduction of additional NaCl also improved the capacity retention of the Cu electrode. When Cl anions are insufficiently supplied to the Cu and CuCl electrode, the reaction route (4) creates Cl anions to meet the requirements for the charge process. As a result, the electrolyte would become more Lewis acidic as neutral AlCl<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup> anions are replaced by acidic Al<sub>2</sub>Cl<sub>7</sub><sup>−</sup>. The pristine CuCl electrode shows four Bragg peaks at 44.6° and 51.8°, corresponding to the Ni current collector, and 47.3° and 56.4°, which are characteristic XRD peaks for CuCl. In other words, a very reactive metallic sodium anode is not needed in the proposed cell configuration in this work, which is a highly attractive feature in terms of fabrication cost and safety in manufacturing processes. To demonstrate our design of the new battery configuration proposed in this work, we constructed a Na‐metal‐free battery system adopting a Cu cathode and hard carbon anode, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>. In this system, Na cations are reversibly stored and released in the hard carbon anode during the charge–discharge reaction, while Cl anions are involved in the Cu chloride cathode reaction. Figure <link href="#smll201902144-fig-0005">5b</link> shows that our full cell well worked in the voltage range of 2.5–3.5 V. Note that the cell was designed under the anode‐limited condition with a mass loading ratio (cathode:anode) of 3. The full cell delivered a reversible capacity of about 130 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (based on the anode), demonstrating the technical feasibility of a Na‐metal‐free full cell adopting Cu with a NaCl cathode. The gravimetric capacity based on the hard carbon is about 128.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is similar to the hard carbon half‐cell capacity (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The Cu/hard carbon full cells maintained 92.2% of its initial capacity after 15 cycles. We also found that NaCl as an additive can stabilize our Cu/CuCl cathode by supplying Cl<sup>−</sup> anions in the electrolyte. Based on these results, we demonstrated that Na‐based dual‐ion battery adopting hard carbon anode for Na<sup>+</sup> with Cu cathode for Cl<sup>−</sup> well worked as we anticipated. These results show the possibility of a highly safe and cheap Na rechargeable battery using a nonflammable electrolyte with highly stable and cheap electrode materials. We also firmly believe that the proposed battery chemistry based on self‐formulated cathode concept can be further extended to other couples of dual‐ion batteries adopting Li, Mg, and Al cations as well as other kinds of halogen anions.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201902144-sec-0020"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Material Preparation</i>: Cu nanowires (&gt;99%, CNVISION), NaCl (&gt;99.9%, Alfa Aesar), KB‐600JD (Ketjen black EC‐600JD carbon black, Akzo Nobel, Japan), and PTFE powder were used as received for the preparation of the Cu, CuCl, and CuCl+NaCl cathode materials. The Cu+NaCl and CuCl+NaCl active cathode materials were obtained by ball‐milling the Cu (or CuCl) with KB‐600JD and NaCl at 500 rpm for 12 h using a SPEX ball mill and zirconia balls at a ball:powder ratio of 30:1.</p><p><i>Preparation of the NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> Electrolyte</i>: The NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> inorganic electrolyte was prepared as follows. NaCl (&gt;99.9%, Alfa Aesar) was vacuum‐dried at 120 °C for 24 h prior to use and anhydrous AlCl<sub>3</sub> (99.999%, Alfa Aesar) was used without any purification. The electrolyte was prepared by blowing SO<sub>2</sub> gas (anhydrous, Fluka) through a mixture of NaCl and AlCl<sub>3</sub> in a glass/Teflon vessel. The molar ratio of NaCl to AlCl<sub>3</sub> was 1.1 to avoid the presence of free AlCl<sub>3</sub>, which is known to be corrosive against alkali metals. As soon as the SO<sub>2</sub> gas contacted the mixture, it became a liquid with a transparent light ocher color. The SO<sub>2</sub> gas was blown until the desired SO<sub>2</sub> concentration was reached, as determined by weighing the electrolyte vessel. After the reaction was completed, the electrolyte vessel was transferred back into the Ar‐filled glove box and placed in a glass bottle containing small pieces of Na metal to remove possible AlCl<sub>3</sub> or H<sub>2</sub>O residue.</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurement</i>: The Cu cathode was prepared by mixing the Cu nanowires, KB‐600JD, and PTFE powder (20 wt% in the cathode). The homogeneously mixed powder was pressed on a Ni‐mesh current collector. The pressed electrode was dried in a vacuum oven at 120 °C for 2 h. The loading level of the electrode was 4.0−5.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The Cu+NaCl and CuCl+NaCl active materials were prepared using the material preparation process mentioned before, and then, the cathodes were prepared using ball‐milling with PTFE powder at 500 rpm for 12 h using a SPEX ball mill and zirconia balls with a ball:powder ratio of 30:1 before pressing on a Ni‐mesh current collector. A Na metal sheet was prepared as an anode by flattening a Na metal piece (Sigma‐Aldrich) in an Ar‐filled glove box. A glass microfiber filter with a thickness of 190 µm (GC50, Advantec) was used as a separator. A CR2032 coin‐type cell consisting of the electrodes, separator, and NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte was assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box for the discharge/charge tests. The assembled cells were aged for 12 h at room temperature and then electrochemically tested using a TOSCAT battery measurement system using the following protocols. The cells were operated galvanostatically at 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> within the voltage range of 3.25−3.65 V. The full cell was tested using a Cu cathode and a hard carbon anode between 2.5 and 3.5 V at a constant current of 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> based on the cathode. The battery components were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box and tested using a TOSCAT battery measurement system.</p><p><i>Characterization</i>: XRD patterns were obtained using an Empyrean diffractometer (PANalytical) equipped with monochromated Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.54056 Å). For the ex situ analysis of the discharged or charged Cu, CuCl cathodes, a gas‐tight sample holder was filled with Ar and covered with polyimide (Kapton) tape. After the cell reacted to a certain level, the cathode was carefully disassembled from the cell and then rinsed with SOCl<sub>2</sub> in an Ar‐filled glove box to remove residual electrolyte. The morphology, microstructure, and composition of the Cu and CuCl electrodes were observed by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM, JEOL JSM‐7000F). For ex situ SEM observations of the cycled cathode, the cathode was also carefully disassembled from the cell and then rinsed with SOCl<sub>2</sub> in an Ar‐filled glove box to remove residual electrolyte. In situ Cu K‐edge XANES spectra of the Cu nanoparticles stored in NaAlCl<sub>4</sub>·2SO<sub>2</sub> electrolyte were collected on the BL10C beam line (WEXAFS) at the Pohang light source (PLS‐II) in the top‐up operation mode under a ring current of 300 mA at 3.0 GeV. From the high‐intensity X‐ray photons of the multipole wiggler source, monochromatic X‐ray beams could be obtained using a liquid‐nitrogen‐cooled double‐crystal monochromator (Bruker ASC) with available in situ exchange under vacuum between the Si(111) and Si(311) crystal pair. The electrode was assembled in the home‐made in situ electrochemical cell made with aluminum‐laminate pouches in the transmittance mode. Energy calibration was simultaneously carried out for each measurement with reference Cu bulk metallic foil placed in front of the third ion chamber. The data reduction of the experimental spectra to normalized XANES was performed by employing the standard XAS procedure. Raman spectroscopy (Bruker Senterra Grating 400) with a He‐Ne laser at a wavelength of 532 nm was used to characterize the phase of the electrolyte materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201902144-sec-0030" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported mainly by the Samsung Research Funding Center for Future Technology (SRFC‐MA1501‐07).</p></section><section xml:id="smll201902144-sec-0040" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll201902144-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll201902144-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll201902144-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>G.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>X.</givenNames> <familyName>Lu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.
