Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>The instability of interfacial solid‐electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer of metallic sodium (Na) anode during cycles results in the rapid capacity decay of sodium metal batteries (SMBs). Herein, the concept of interfacial protection engineering of Na nanoparticles (Na‐NPs) is proposed first to achieve stable, dendrite‐free, and long‐life SMB. Employing an ion‐exchange strategy, conformal Sn‐Na alloy‐SEI on the interface of Na‐NPs is constructed, forming Sn@Na‐NPs. The stable alloy‐based SEI layer possesses the following three advantages: 1) significantly enhancing the transport dynamics of Na<sup>+</sup> ions and electrons; 2) enabling the well‐distributed deposition of Na<sup>+</sup> ions to avoid the growth of dendrites; and 3) protecting the Sn@Na‐NPs anode from the attack of electrolyte, thereby reducing the parasitic reaction and boosting the Coulombic efficiency of SMBs. Because of these virtues, the symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell shows an ultralow voltage hysteresis of 0.54 V at 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> after 600 h. Paired with the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>F (NaVPF) cathode, the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs full cell exhibits an initial discharge capacity of 89.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C and a high capacity retention of 81.6% after 600 cycles.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202102400-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The conformal Sn‐Na alloy interfacial layer on the surface of Sn@Na‐nanoparticles anode is able to effectively protect it from the attack of solvent, enabling the dendrite‐free Na plating/stripping and achieving stable long‐term cycling stability xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Due to the high theoretical specific capacity (1165 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and low standard electrode potential (−2.71 V vs the standard hydrogen electrode) of metallic sodium (Na) anode, sodium metal batteries (SMBs) are regarded as one of the most prospective secondary rechargeable energy‐storage equipment.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the practical application of Na anode is hindered by the following two aspects: 1) Owing to the high chemical reactivity, metallic Na easily reacts with electrolyte to form unstable solid electrolyte interface (SEI).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0004 #smll202102400-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During the repeated plating/stripping processes, the huge volume variety of Na anode unavoidably causes the fracture of SEI layer; thus the newly exposed metallic Na will be consumed in constant contact with the electrolyte, finally leading to the depletion of electrolyte and poor Coulombic efficiency (CE).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0006 #smll202102400-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> 2) More seriously, the formation and growth of Na dendrites during cycles easily induce the short circuit, overheating or even explosion of battery accompany with the penetration of dendrites through separator to reach the cathode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0008 #smll202102400-bib-0009"><sup>8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Relative researches indicate that the emergence of Na dendrites is mainly induced by the uneven distribution of Na<sup>+</sup> ions, high local current density, and heterogeneous nucleation of Na.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To solve these issues, a variety of strategies have been adopted, such as constructing 3D Na to reduce local current density,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> engineering artificial SEI layers to enable the uniform distribution of Na<sup>+</sup> ions,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> optimizing liquid electrolyte to reduce the parasitic reactions via depressing the activity of organic solvent,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> designing solid‐state electrolyte to improve the safety of SMBs by avoiding the penetration of Na dendrites,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and so forth. Among the aforementioned strategies, the formation of robust SEI film is able to effectively boost the cycling stability of SMBs. For example, Yu et al. prepared a Na<sub>3</sub>P film on the surface of Na foil to inhibit the growth of dendrites by adjusting the uniform deposition of ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Seh and co‐workers evaporated gaseous tin tetrachloride (SnCl<sub>4</sub>) onto the surface of Na foil to form Sn‐Na alloy based SEI layer, significantly lowering the interfacial charge transfer resistance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, Wang's group used N,S codoped carbon nanotube paper as the interlayer of Na foil to control the nucleation behavior of Na, achieving dendrite‐free plating/stripping of Na<sup>+</sup> ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the generally used Na foils with thickness of ≈100−200 μm (corresponding to the areal capacity of ≈20−40 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) are not suitable for the industrialization of SMBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> That is because excessive metallic Na not only increases the weight of SMBs to decrease their gravimetric energy density but also brings security risks. Furthermore, the electrochemical processes of anodes during cycles are confined to the 2D interface of Na foil, decreasing the transport rate of electrons and Na<sup>+</sup> ions. Therefore, 3D Na with limited areal capacity is highly desirable to boost the energy density and safety of SMBs. Nevertheless, the increased contact area between electrolyte and 3D Na will induce more side reactions and thereby effective protection of 3D Na is also important to improve the CE and prolong the cycling life of SMBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As far as we know, the interfacial protection toward 3D Na has not been reported up to now.</p><p>In this work, we propose the concept of interfacial protection engineering of Na nanoparticles (Na‐NPs) to achieve nondendritic and long‐life SMB. First, Na‐NPs anode is fabricated via using sodiophilic Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C nanoparticles@graphene hybrid as the host (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0001"><b>1a</b></link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0001">b</link>). Second, Sn@Na‐NPs is formed by conformably coating Sn‐Na alloy on the surface of Na‐NPs based on an ion‐exchange method, constructing a robust and stable alloy‐based SEI layer (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0001">1c</link>). The conformal alloy‐based SEI film on the interface of Sn@Na‐NPs anode possesses the following benefits: 1) significantly improving the transferring kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ions and lowering the interfacial impedance; 2) making Na<sup>+</sup> ions deposit evenly to avoid the growth of dendrites (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0001">1d</link>); and 3) protecting the Sn@Na‐NPs from the attack of electrolyte and thereby reducing the parasitic reaction. Thanks to these values, the symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell shows an ultralow average hysteresis of 0.54 V after 600 h at 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with the areal capacity of 1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. Paired with Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>F (NaVPF) cathode, the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs full battery delivers a high specific capacity of 89.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C and retains the 81.6% of initial capacity after 600 cycles (1 C = 130 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). More importantly, the idea of interfacial protection mechanism can be applied to other nanostructured alkaline metal anodes, while paving the ways for the development of high‐energy‐density and dendrite‐free alkaline metal batteries.</p><figure xml:id="smll202102400-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202102400:smll202102400-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202102400-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic depiction of Na plating/stripping behavior of Sn@Na‐NPs. a) Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@graphene where C nanoparticles comprising Sn and SnO<sub>2</sub> are uniformly anchored onto the flexible graphene nanosheet. b) Na‐NPs anode that is synthesized by plating 5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> of Na into sodiophilic Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@graphene host. c) Sn@Na‐NPs that is formed via an ion‐exchange method to construct conformal Sn‐Na alloy on the interface of Na‐NPs. d) Dendrite‐free behavior of Sn@Na‐NPs during cycles that is attributed to the stable alloy based SEI layer, showing the importance of interfacial protection engineering.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0030"><title type="main">Synthesis and Characterization of the Sn@Na‐NPs Anode</title><p>The preparation of Sn@Na‐NPs anode is shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002"><b>2a</b></link>−<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">c</link>. First, sodiophilic Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C nanoparticles@graphene (i.e., Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@G) hybrid was coated on the surface of Cu foil via the conventional slurry‐casting method, which was fabricated using Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐ZIF‐8@rGO (ZIF = zeolitic imidazolate framework, rGO = reduced graphene oxide) as the precusor (Figures <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1</link> and <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). The thickness of Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@G electrode was ≈100 μm (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">2a</link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">d</link>), in which the diameters of Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C nanoparticles were ≈80−420 nm (Figures <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S3</link> and <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). Second, 5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> of Na was plated into Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@G to fabricate Na‐NPs. Optical and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images showed the Na‐NPs electrode with a thickness of ≈170 μm exhibited nondendritic and silvery surface (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">2b</link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">e</link>). Third, an organic solution comprising SnTFSI salt was dropped onto Na‐NPs to form Sn@Na‐NPs (see the Experimental Section), accompanying with the color change from silver into dark brown and increased thickness approaching ≈210 μm (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">2c</link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">f</link>). Taking use of the spontaneous redox reaction, Na‐NPs induced the decomposition of SnTFSI salt to form Sn and SnO<sub>2</sub>, thereby constructing conformal Sn‐Na alloy‐based SEI on the surface of Sn@Na‐NPs anode. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image, elemental mappings, and energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) displayed that Sn@Na‐NPs with diameters of ≈93 nm included C, N, O, S, F, Na, and Sn, in which the content of Sn accounted for 13.89 at% (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">2g</link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0002">h</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns indicated there was a same diffraction peak located at 29.6° existing in both Na‐NPs and Sn@Na‐NPs, which was related to the (110) plane of metallic Na (Joint Committee on Powder Diffraction Standards, i.e., JCPDS card 03‐0598). In contrast, the newly appeared two peaks in Sn@Na‐NPs anode were attributed to Sn (2θ = 30.8° and 32.2°, JCPDS card 04‐0673) (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0003"><b>3a</b></link>). The SEI composition of the Sn@Na‐NPs anode was analyzed by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The Sn 3d spectrum was divided into two peaks, located at ≈484.6 and ≈486.7 eV were indexed to Sn/Sn‐Na alloy and SnO<sub>2</sub>, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0003">3b</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As for C 1s spectrum, the four peaks with binding energies of ≈284.8, ≈286.5, ≈288.2, and ≈289.8 eV were assigned to CC, RONa, RCONa, and ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na/Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>/NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0003">3c</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The S 2p spectrum showed four peaks located at ≈167.0, ≈168.7, and ≈169.9/≈171.0 eV could be identified as Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, and FSI<sup>−</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0003">3d</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For the F 1s spectrum, the two obvious peaks were referenced to NaF (≈684.0 eV) and FSI<sup>−</sup> (≈688.0 eV), respectively (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0003">3e</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Na 1s spectrum showed the peak at ≈1071.6 eV was corresponded to Na<sup>+</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0003">3f</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Based on the EDS, XRD, and XPS results, it could be concluded that the SEI of Sn@Na‐NPs anode was mostly made up of Sn‐Na alloy, Sn, SnO<sub>2</sub>, Na‐based inorganic salts (e.g., Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, and NaF), and Na‐organic species (e.g., RONa, RCONa, ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na, and NaFSI).</p><figure xml:id="smll202102400-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource The inset of (e) shows the chemical structural formula of NaFSI.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0040"><title type="main">Electrochemical Performance and Postcycling Characterization of the Symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs Cell</title><p>Symmetric cells were assembled to investigate the Na plating/stripping behavior. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0004"><b>4a</b></link> shows that the symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell exhibits good cycling stability at a current density of 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> and delivers an ultralow voltage hysteresis of ≈0.54 V after 600 h; the long cycling life exceeds many of the previously reported Na metal anodes (Table <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). SEM images and schematic displayed that the postcycling Sn@Na‐NPs resulting from the symmetric cell was occupied by nondendritic and irregularly shaped particles, which comprising C, O, F, Na, S, and Sn (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0004">4c</link>−<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0004">e</link>). To highlight the advantage of Sn@Na‐NPs, Na anode with the same areal capacity was designed to make a comparison, which was fabricated using graphite as the host (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). Electrochemical results showed the overpotential of the symmetric Na cell gradually increased and finally reached ≈1.94 V after 94 h (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0004">4a</link>). SEM image indicated there was obvious dendrite appearing on the surface of Na anode stemmed from the postcycling symmetric Na cell, which contained the elements of C, O, F, S, and Na (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0004">4b</link>). Besides, symmetric Na‐NPs cell was also assembled to compare with symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell. Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S7a,b</link> in the Supporting Information showed the overpotential of the symmetric Na‐NPs cell reached ≈1.3 V after 200 h, higher than that of symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell. Top‐view SEM images indicated many of dendrites formed on the surface of Na‐NPs anode originated from the postcycling symmetric Na‐NPs cell (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S7c,d</link>, Supporting Information). On the basis of above results, this conclusion can be summed up that conformal Sn‐based SEI layer within Sn@Na‐NPs helped to reduce the plating/stripping overpotential of Na<sup>+</sup> ions and avoid the formation of dendrites.</p><figure xml:id="smll202102400-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202102400:smll202102400-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202102400-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Cycling performance and postcycling characterization of symmetric cells. a) Voltage–time profiles of the symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell and symmetric Na cell at 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>. b) Top‐view SEM image and elemental mappings of Na anode resulted from the postcycling symmetric Na cell. c,d) Top‐view SEM images, elemental mappings and e) schematic of Sn@Na‐NPs anode originated from the symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell after cycles.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0050"><title type="main">Electrochemical Performance and Postcycling Characterization of the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs Full Cell</title><p>To further emphasize the remarkable performance of Sn@Na‐NPs anode, it was paired with a Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>F (NaVPF) cathode to assemble into the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs full cell (Figures <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S8</link> and <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information). Electrochemical results revealed that the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell delivered excellent rate capabilities of 136, 110, 91.8, 80.5, 68.9, and 53.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, and 5 C (1 C = 130 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), respectively (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005"><b>5a</b></link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">b</link>); and high initial CE of 82% at 0.5 C (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S10</link> and Table <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). When cycled at 1 C, it exhibited a reversible capacity of 89.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and retained the 81.6% of initial capacity over 600 cycles (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5c</link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">e</link> and Table <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information), surpassing a great deal of the previously reported SMBs (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5f</link> and Table <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). Besides, it also possessed low charge transfer resistance of 18.3 Ω (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5d</link>) and high average diffusion coefficient of 4.17 × 10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>−2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information), indicating the rapid transport kinetics of electrons and Na<sup>+</sup> ions. Compared with the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell, the NaVPF‐Na cell showed inferior rate capabilities (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5a</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S12b</link>, Supporting Information), cycling performance (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5e</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S12c</link>, Supporting Information), and transport kinetics of electrons/ions (Tables <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S2</link> and <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). It was worth noting that the phenomenon of abrupt capacity decline appeared in both rate and cycling profiles of NaVPF‐Na cell (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5a,e</link>), which could be attributed to the rapid consumption of electrolyte induced by the growth of Na dendrites, and thus led to low CE and irreversible capacity loss.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="smll202102400-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202102400:smll202102400-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202102400-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performances of full batteries. a) Rate performances, d) Nyquist impedance plots, and e) long‐term cycling of the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs battery and NaVPF‐Na battery. b) Galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles of the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs battery at different current rates. c) Galvanostatic discharge profiles of the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs battery at 1 C. f) Comparison of the capacity retention between the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs battery and recently reported SMBs.</caption></figure><p>To investigate the formation mechanism of interfacial Sn‐Na alloy in Sn@Na‐NPs, ex situ XRD patterns were conducted (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0006"><b>6</b></link>). When the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell was charged to 4.3 V for the first time, Na<sup>+</sup> ions originated from the NaVPF cathode were plated into the Sn@Na‐NPs anode (Na<sup>+</sup> + e<sup>−</sup> → Na), leading to the increase of Na intensity. Besides, the signal of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> appeared and the intensity of Sn gradually decreased, showing the formation of Na‐Sn alloy on the surface of Sn@Na‐NPs anode. On the contrary, as the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell was discharged to 2 V, the intensity of Na for the Sn‐NPs anode was gradually decreased, indicating the Na stripping process. However, the signal intensity of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> alloy had no obvious change during the first discharging process, displaying the high stability of Na‐Sn alloy. It could be concluded that the Na‐Sn alloy‐based protection layer contributed to improving the stability of Sn@Na‐NPs anode, and thereby ameliorating the cycling performance of NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell, which was in good consistent with electrochemical results (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0005">5e</link>).</p><figure xml:id="smll202102400-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202102400:smll202102400-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202102400-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Mechanism of the first Na plating/stripping for Sn@Na‐NPs. a) The first charging/discharging profiles of NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs battery and b) corresponding ex situ XRD patterns of Sn@Na‐NPs.</caption></figure><p>The postcycling NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs battery was unpacked to survey the morphological and structural changes of the Sn@Na‐NPs anode. SEM image showed there was no dendrite forming on the surface of Sn@Na‐NPs after cycling (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0007"><b>7a</b></link>). XPS results revealed that the main SEI components of postcycling Sn@Na‐NPs comprised Sn‐Na alloy, Sn, SnO<sub>2</sub>, Na‐based inorganic salts (e.g., NaF, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>), and Na‐organic species (e.g., RONa, RCONa, and ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na, NaFSI) (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0007">7b</link>−<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0007">f</link>). Compared with the original Sn@Na‐NPs anode before cycles, the contents of both SnO<sub>2</sub> and FSI<sup>−</sup> originated from the postcycling Sn@Na‐NPs were decreased. That was because the SnO<sub>2</sub> was partly converted into Sn, leading to the increase of Sn‐Na alloy and Sn contents; while the FSI<sup>−</sup> was further decomposed to yield Na‐based organic salts and Na‐organic species (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S13</link>, Supporting Information). Relative researches exhibited Na‐based organic salts (e.g., NaF) contributed to forming stable SEI layers and thus improved the CE and cycling stability of SMBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On the contrary, there were many Na dendrites appearing on the surface of Na surface stemmed from the postcycling NaVPF‐Na cell (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-fig-0007">7g</link>,<link href="#smll202102400-fig-0007">h</link> and Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S8a</link>, Supporting Information), in which its SEI film mainly contained Na‐based inorganic compounds (e.g., NaF, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>), Na‐organic substances (e.g., RONa, RCONa, ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na, and NaFSI), and electrolyte additive (fluoroethylene carbonate, FEC) (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S14</link>, Supporting Information). Compared with the pristine Na anode, the content of NaFSI for postcycling Na anode was significantly decreased, thereby leading to the formation of thick SEI layer and irreversible capacity loss.</p><figure xml:id="smll202102400-fig-0007"><label>7</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202102400:smll202102400-fig-0007" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202102400-fig-0007-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Characterization of anodes originated from the postcycling NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell and NaVPF‐Na cell. a) Top‐view SEM image and high‐resolution XPS spectra of Sn@Na‐NPs anode in the b) Sn 3d, c) C 1s, d) S 2p, e) F 1s, and f) Na 1s regions. g,h) Top‐view SEM images and high‐resolution XPS spectra of Na anode in the i) C 1s, j) S 2p, k) F 1s, and l) Na 1s regions.</caption></figure><p>To highlight the importance of interfacial alloy protective layer, NaVPF‐Na‐NPs cell was packaged for comparison. Electrochemical results showed the NaVPF‐Na‐NPs cell delivered poorer cycling and rate performance compared to NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs cell as well as higher charge transfer resistance and slower transport kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ions (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S15</link> and Tables <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S2</link> and <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). The postcycling NaVPF‐Na‐NPs battery was unpacked and SEM image displayed Na dendrites formed on the surface of Na‐NPs anode (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S16</link>a−c, Supporting Information). The SEI component of the pristine Na‐NPs before cycling mainly comprised Na‐based inorganic salts (e.g., NaF, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>) and Na‐organic species (e.g., RONa, RCONa, ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na, NaFSI) (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S16</link>d−g, Supporting Information). After cycles, the content of NaFSI originated from the SEI layer of Na‐NPs anode was decreased, resulting in the increase of NaF, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>, and RONa contents (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S16</link>h−l, Supporting Information). Owing to the absence of Sn‐Na alloy protection, the dendrites on the surface of Na‐NPs would induced the decomposition of electrolyte and thus form thicker and thicker SEI layer, leading to the low CE and poor cycling stability of the NaVPF‐Na‐NPs cell.</p></section></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0060"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>By constructing conformal Sn‐Na alloy protection layer on the exterior of Na‐NPs anode, the dendritic growth and low cycling stability of SMB were effectively solved. The Sn‐Na alloy‐based SEI layer possessed the following four merits: 1) making the Na<sup>+</sup> ions deposit well‐distributedly to avoid the growth of dendrites, 2) protecting the Sn@Na‐NPs from the attack of electrolyte to enhance the CE of SMB, 3) reducing the interfacial electrical resistance to speed up the transport of electrons, and 4) boosting the migration kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ions to improve the rate capability of SMB. Electrochemical results showed the symmetric Sn@Na‐NPs cell exhibited low voltage hysteresis and long cycling life. Besides, the NaVPF‐Sn@Na‐NPs full cell delivered dendrite‐free Na plating/stripping behavior, high cycling stability, and capacity retention. Overall, the strategy of the alloy‐based protection can be spread to other nanostructured metal anodes that inspired the research of dendrite‐free and long life‐time alkali metal batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0070"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0080" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Chemicals</title><p>Zn foil (99.9%, Sigma‐Aldrich), tin chloride dihydrate (SnCl<sub>2</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O, 98%, Aladdin), 2‐methylimidazole (C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>4</sub>N<sub>2</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), ethylene carbonate (EC, C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>4</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), diethyl carbonate (DEC, C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>10</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), propylene carbonate (PC, C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>6</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), FEC (C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>3</sub>FO<sub>3</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), 1,2‐dimethoxyethane (DME, C<sub>4</sub>H<sub>10</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, 99%, Aladdin), sodium perchlorate (NaClO<sub>4</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), sodium bis(fluorosulfonyl)imide (NaFSI, F<sub>2</sub>NNaO<sub>4</sub>S<sub>2</sub>, 98%, Aladdin), and tin bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide (SnTFSI, C<sub>4</sub>F<sub>12</sub>N<sub>2</sub>SnO<sub>8</sub>S<sub>4</sub>, Alfa Aesar) were used.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Electrolyte</title><p>NaFSI (4.0 <sc>m</sc>) was dissolved in DME to fabricate electrolyte A. SnTFSI (0.1 <sc>m</sc>) dissolving in EC and DEC (1:1, v/v) as electrolyte B. NaClO<sub>4</sub> (1.0 <sc>m</sc>) was soluble in EC/PC (1:1, v/v) with FEC additive (5 vol%) to generate electrolyte C.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@Graphene</title><p>The detailed preparation process of Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@graphene was presented in Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1</link> in the Supporting Information. First, Zn plate was dipped in 2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> of graphene oxide solution at 60 °C for 5 h, yielding ZnO/rGO hybrid on both sides of Zn foil (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1a,b</link>, Supporting Information). Second, the resulting Zn plate soaked in 2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> of SnCl<sub>2</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O aqueous solution for 15 min, forming Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐ZnO@rGO based on spontaneous reaction: Zn + Sn<sup>2+</sup> → Zn<sup>2+</sup> + Sn (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1c</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Third, this as‐obtained Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐ZnO@rGO/Zn plate was moved to 30 mL methyl alcohol involving 0.4 g of 2‐methylimidazole and maintained at 25 °C for 12 h to generate zeolitic imidazolate framework, which was denoted as Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐ZIF‐8@rGO (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1d</link>, Supporting Information). Finally, the Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐ZIF‐8@rGO was collected from Zn plate follow by washing, drying, and carbonizing at 800 °C for 2 h under H<sub>2</sub>/Ar atmosphere, fabricating Sn/SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C@graphene (Figure <link href="#smll202102400-supitem-0001">S1e,f</link>, Supporting Information).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Na, Na‐NPs, and Sn@Na‐NPs Anodes</title><p>A mixture comprising Sn@SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C/graphene (60 wt%), Super P (20 wt%), and binder (20 wt%) was mingled with ultrapure water to produce the well‐distributed slurry, in which the binder was composed of gum arabic and Xantan gum (mole ratio of 3:1). The slurry was pasted onto copper foil and dried in a vacuum oven 80 °C for 24 h. Then, the Sn@SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C/graphene@copper was sliced to a 12 mm disc, which was packaged into a CR2032 coin cell in an argon‐filled glove box using Na plate as the counter electrode. Electrolyte A (100 µL) and Celgard 2400 membrane were used as the electrolyte and separator, respectively. Finally, the Sn@SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C/graphene‐Na‐foil battery was discharged at 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for 5 h to deposit Na into the Sn@SnO<sub>2</sub>‐C/graphene, yielding Na‐NPs. After unpacking the coin cell, 50 µL of electrolyte B was dripped onto Na‐NPs to form Sn@Na‐NPs, whose weight was ≈8.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. According to the same fabrication method as Na‐NPs, Na anode was acquired via plating 5 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> of metallic Na into commercial graphite host.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of NaVPF Cathode</title><p>The Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>F (NaVPF) powder was obtained via the previously reported method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202102400-bib-0036"><sup>36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> NaVPF (70 wt%), SuperP (20 wt%), and carboxyl methyl cellulose binder (10 wt%) were evenly distributed in ultrapure water. The slurry was coated onto a C‐coated aluminum foil and dried at 80 °C under vacuum overnight. The average loading of the NaVPF cathode was 1.5−2.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0150" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurement of NaVPF‐Na, NaVPF‐Na‐NPs, and NaVPF║Sn@Na‐NPs Cells</title><p>The NaVPF cathodes were assembled with Na, Na‐NPs, and Sn@Na‐NPs anodes into CR2032 cells in an argon‐filled glove box, respectively. Electrolyte C (150 µL) and Whatman glass microfiber were utilized as electrolyte and separator, respectively. The charge and discharge measurements were conducted on a Neware battery testing system at 30 °C. Cyclic voltammetry was carried out in the voltage range of 2.0−4.3 V (vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) at different scan rates. Gamry electrochemical workstation was used to test the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy in a frequency range of 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0160" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization</title><p>Scanning electron microscope (SEM) images and X‐ray energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) were performed on Hitachi S‐3400N SEM (Japan). The microstructure of the sample was observed by TEM (Field Electron and Ion Company, Thermo‐Talos F200S, operated at 200 kV). The composition and crystal structure of the samples were confirmed by X‐ray powder diffraction (XRD, Rigaku MiniFlex600, Cu <i>K</i><sub>–</sub>). XPS (Thermo Fisher‐Escalab 250Xi) was applied to determine the compositions and chemical states. Nitrogen adsorption experiments and mesopore analysis were conducted at 77 K using an USA‐Micromeritics‐ASAP2020 apparatus. Before adsorption measurements, the samples were degassed in vacuum at 373 K for 10 h. The specific surface areas of the samples were calculated by Brunauere–Emmette–Teller analyses of their adsorption isotherms.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0170" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51771058, 51801030, and 22005064), Pearl River Talent Program of Guangdong Province (2017GC010030), Natural Science Foundation of Guangdong Providence (2018A030310571), One‐hundred Young Talents (Class B) of Guangdong University of Technology (22041375801), and Basic Research Project of Science and Technology Program of Guangzhou City (202102021081).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0180" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0190" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202102400-sec-0200" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="smll202102400-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll202102400-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll202102400-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>P.</givenNames> <familyName>Liu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>L.</givenNames> <familyName>Jiao</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
