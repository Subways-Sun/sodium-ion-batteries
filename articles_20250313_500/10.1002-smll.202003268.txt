type="main">Abstract</title><p>Considerable efforts have been exerted to understand the formation and properties of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) in sodium ion batteries. However, the puzzling existence and role of SEI behind the huge volume changes of the graphite electrodes need to be answered. Herein, the reason of how ether‐derived SEI maintains excellent reversibility despite the huge volume changes during cycling is unraveled. Theoretical simulations and Fourier‐transform infrared spectroscopy demonstrate the formation mechanism of an SEI between the graphite anode and electrolyte. Furthermore, the high mechanical tolerance of the ether‐derived SEI is confirmed in atomic force microscopy. A depth profile of X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy points to a multilayer structure of the ether‐derived SEI. The outer layer comprises organics (sodium alkoxide), while the inorganics (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaF) in interior region are mixed with some organics. Notably, the presence of organics ensures the adaptability of the SEI to the volume expansion of graphite during cycling, and the concentrated distribution of inorganics improves the Young's modulus (resistance to deformation). Therefore, the graphite anode exhibits high cycle stability (96.6% capacity retention ratio at 1 A g<sup>–1</sup> over 860 cycles) and efficiency (≈99.5%).</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202003268-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>In this study, the ether‐derived SEI on the graphite surface shows fast kinetics and excellent mechanical properties. The special properties are attributed to the multilayer structure of the ether‐derived SEI that comprised a primary inorganics interior and an organics exterior. The multilayer structure not only guarantees the stability of the SEI but also enhances the adaptability to high volume changes.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003268:smll202003268-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003268-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll202003268-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable batteries, particularly lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), have been the primary subjects of academic research and have dominated the commercial market following their development in the early 1990s.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0001 #smll202003268-bib-0002 #smll202003268-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, their rapidly increasing market demand will drastically decrease lithium resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0004 #smll202003268-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The use of replaceable cells that comprise plentiful materials is crucial for sustainability. Therefore, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered promising for future applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0006 #smll202003268-bib-0007 #smll202003268-bib-0008"><sup>6–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Graphite has been widely used as a negative electrode in LIBs, where the reversible intercalation of Li<sup>+</sup> into the graphite layers forms binary compounds (b‐GICs) with the stoichiometric composition of LiC<sub>6</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0009 #smll202003268-bib-0010 #smll202003268-bib-0011"><sup>9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, an early attempt to use graphite as an anode for SIBs was unsuccessful because capacities less than 35 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> were achieved.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0012 #smll202003268-bib-0013 #smll202003268-bib-0014"><sup>12–14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This may be mistakenly attributed to the higher radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (0.102 nm) as compared with that of Li<sup>+</sup> (0.076 nm).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0015 #smll202003268-bib-0016 #smll202003268-bib-0017 #smll202003268-bib-0018 #smll202003268-bib-0019"><sup>15–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the instability of the Na‐GICs resulted in poor sodium storage in graphite.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0020 #smll202003268-bib-0021 #smll202003268-bib-0022"><sup>20–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Owing to the diglyme‐graphene vdW interaction,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the co‐intercalation of the solvated Na ions effectively formed stable ternary graphite intercalation compounds (t‐GICs) with the common formula Na(solv)<i><sub>y</sub></i>C<sub>20</sub> (<i>y</i> = 1 or more likely 2).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0024 #smll202003268-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The formation of t‐GICs at approximately 0.5–0.6 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) is accompanied by a pronounced volume expansion in the ether‐based electrolyte. The average electrode thickness reportedly increases by approximately 100 µm (50 µm in the pristine electrode) after sodiation. Furthermore, the original value is 3.4 Å which increase to the value within the range of 11.3 to 11.9 Å that indicates a 300–340% volume expansion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0026 #smll202003268-bib-0027"><sup>26,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The huge volume changes resulted in the formation of an unstable interphase that causes a rapid decay in the capacity of the anode material. This is typified by the silicon‐based materials in LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0028 #smll202003268-bib-0029"><sup>28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) that is formed during the initial cycle hinders the continuous decomposition of the electrolyte and guarantees stable cycling; however, it repeatedly cracks and forms under drastic volume changes. Unexpectedly, the excellent reversibility of the graphite anodes of SIBs in ether‐based electrolytes is retained despite the huge volume expansion. This reinforces the necessity of the formation of a stable interphase to withstand the pronounced volume changes and replicate the excellent cycling performance of the graphite anodes in ether‐based electrolytes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0024 #smll202003268-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> If the ether‐derived SEI exists on the graphite surface and displays tolerance to huge volume changes, then the determination of their physicochemical states is the most intractable problem.</p><p>Researchers have found that the formation of a thin, compact, and uniform ether‐derived SEI on the graphite surface causes an irreversible capacity loss in the first cycle.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0021 #smll202003268-bib-0025 #smll202003268-bib-0030"><sup>21,25,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the excellent reversibility of graphite in ether‐based electrolytes is enhanced by the characteristics of the ether‐derived SEI. Similar results have been reported for other anode materials like reduced graphene oxide, hard carbon, Sn, and TiO<sub>2</sub> in ether‐based electrolytes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0031 #smll202003268-bib-0032 #smll202003268-bib-0033 #smll202003268-bib-0034"><sup>31–34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Conversely, it has been suggested that ether‐based electrolytes do not participate in the formation of SEI. The characterization of the graphite surface by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) revealed the absence of an amorphous film; however, a thick amorphous‐like SEI layer was observed on the surface of graphite that was cycled in a carbonate‐based electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Adelhelm et al. believe that the instability of the SEI under the huge volume changes results in the continuous decomposition of the electrolyte. However, they found that the gases that were generated from the decomposition of the electrolyte were detected only in the first cycle on an online electrochemical mass spectrometer (OEMS).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is well known that the generation of gases due to the decomposition of the electrolyte is always accompanied by the formation of the SEI.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0036 #smll202003268-bib-0037"><sup>36,37</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To figure out the physicochemical states of ether‐derived SEI and uncover the reasons behind the excellent electrochemical performance of graphite under huge volume change, more evidence that sketch at length the chemical/physical properties of the ether‐derived SEI on graphite are urgently needed. In this study, we investigate the interfacial mechanisms at the surface of the graphite electrodes during sodiation/desodiation in sodium batteries by systematic comparison with common carbonate‐based electrolytes. The existence of an ether‐derived SEI was verified by AC impedance and atomic force microscopy (AFM). Subsequently, theoretical calculations and infrared spectroscopy were performed to uncover the components of the ether‐derived SEI. In addition, we performed X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) with depth sputtering to determine the surface chemistry of the graphite electrode in the carbonate‐based electrolyte. Results indicate that a multilayer structure mode constitutes the ether‐derived SEI, which can tolerant to fracture over cycling and therefore guarantees the cyclic reversibility and fast kinetics.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0030"><title type="main">Macroscopic Properties of Interphase, Electrode Reaction</title><p>To understand the properties of the SEI that are formed in the ether‐based electrolytes, a common carbonate‐based electrolyte is utilized as a counterpart. The co‐intercalation of the solvent does not occur in the same apparatus as that of the carbonate electrolyte. The structural changes of the graphite materials in the ether‐based electrolytes are characterized by high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) and Raman spectroscopy. Graphite has a regular layered structure (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>,<link href="#smll202003268-fig-0001">b</link>). However, the structure becomes asymmetrical with a non‐uniform distance between the graphite layers after the electrodes are discharged in the ether‐based electrolyte (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0001">1c</link>). This is confirmed by the schematic of the co‐intercalation of the solvated Na‐ions (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0001">1d</link>). The results of Raman spectroscopy reveal that the occurrence of co‐intercalation in the ether‐based electrolytes significantly reduces the degree of graphitization; however, the ordering of the materials returns to the original state after charging (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0001">1e–g</link>) that is consistent with the results of X‐ray diffraction (XRD) in the literature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0016 #smll202003268-bib-0025 #smll202003268-bib-0026"><sup>16,25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, it demonstrates the high reversibility of the graphite structure. In addition, the electrochemical performance of graphite in the ether‐based electrolytes is markedly different from that in the carbonate‐based electrolytes (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>). The reduction at ≈ 0.5 V during the first discharge process corresponds to the decomposition of the carbonate‐based electrolyte and the formation of the SEI on the graphite surface. However, no redox reactions occur during the subsequent charge/discharge processes (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0002">2a</link>). In the ether‐based electrolytes, the voltage platform at ≈ 0.6 V (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0002">2b</link>) corresponds to the intercalation of the solvated Na ions and the formation of a stable ternary compound Na(solv)<i><sub>x</sub></i>C<i><sub>y</sub></i>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0024 #smll202003268-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Since the Na‐GICs are unstable, the graphite anode exhibits a low capacity (&lt;30 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) in the carbonate‐based electrolyte without the involvement of carbonate solvents (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0002">2c</link>). The cells that utilize the diglyme‐based electrolyte exhibit a relatively stable cyclic performance at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0002">2d</link>; furthermore, the capacity of the graphite increases slightly after cycling, which indicates that the adsorption sites of solvated sodium ions in graphite increase under the huge volume changes. Generally, the structural stability of the electrode material will gradually decrease with the increasing current density; however, the opposite trend is observed for the co‐intercalation (Figure S1, Supporting Information). One should note the capacity is increased by only 6% under the huge volume expansion, which exactly shows that the graphite structure is highly stable during the cycle. The initial coulombic efficiency (ICE) for the graphite electrode in the ether‐based electrolyte (64.9%) is higher than that for the graphite electrode in the carbonate‐based electrolyte (20.4%). This indicates that the redox activity of the carbonate‐based electrolyte is higher than that of the ether‐based electrolyte. Moreover, the high coulombic efficiencies (99.5%) in the subsequent cycles indicate that if the SEI existed, then it does not crack and re‐form during cycling.</p><figure xml:id="smll202003268-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003268:smll202003268-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003268-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Characteristics of the bulk graphite and the co‐intercalation phenomena. a) SEM and b) HR‐TEM images of raw graphite. c) HR‐TEM image of graphite after discharge in the ether‐based electrolyte (1<sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme). d) Schematic of the de‐intercalation of the solvated Na‐ions between the graphite layers. Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of the graphite electrodes in a) NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC (v/v: 1/1) and b) NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐diglyme, where the current density is 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Cyclic stability of the graphite electrodes in c) NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC (v/v: 1/1) and (d) NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐diglyme, where the current density is 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>The AC impedance method is utilized for a detailed analysis of the interphase between the electrode and electrolyte (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>,<link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">b</link>). The kinetic behavior of the electrode is reflected by a specific frequency in the electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS). The Randles model states that the EIS comprises the following four components: 1) <i>R</i><sub>S</sub> is the bulk resistance of the cell at high frequencies (10<sup>4</sup>–10<sup>5</sup> Hz) that reflects the combined resistance of the electrolyte, separator, and electrodes. 2) <i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub> and CPE<sub>1</sub> are the resistance and capacitance, respectively, of the ion diffusion inside the SEI on the surfaces of the two electrodes at approximately 10<sup>1</sup>–10<sup>2</sup> Hz. 3) <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> and CPE<sub>2</sub> are the charge‐transfer resistance and its relative double‐layer capacitance, respectively, at medium frequencies (1–10<sup>−1</sup> Hz). 4) <i>W</i> is the Warburg impedance that is related to the semi‐infinite diffusion of the Na ions or solvated Na ions inside the electrode materials. It corresponds to the sloping line at the low frequency (10<sup>−2</sup> Hz) end of the Nyquist plot. The EIS plots for both electrolytes (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">3a,b</link>) show two partially overlapping semicircles, which correspond to the impedances of SEI and charge‐transfer, and a straight sloping line at the low‐frequency end. The feature semicircle followed by the charge transfer semicircle confirms the presence of the SEI despite the overlapping with the contact impedance (see details in Supporting Information when the voltage (Figures S2 and S3, Supporting Information), loading (Figure S4, Supporting Information), and pressure (Figure S5, Supporting Information) of the cell are varied). An equivalent circuit (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">3a,b</link>) can fit the aforementioned pattern of the EIS.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0036 #smll202003268-bib-0038 #smll202003268-bib-0039 #smll202003268-bib-0040"><sup>36,38–40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The impedance‐fitting data for the carbonate‐ and ether‐based electrolytes (Table S1, Supporting Information) show the similarity in <i>R</i><sub>S</sub> for both the electrolytes. R<sub>SEI</sub> for graphite in the ether‐based electrolyte (15.63 Ω) (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">3a</link>) is significantly lower than that in the carbonate‐based electrolyte (140.7 Ω) (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">3b</link>) at approximately 10<sup>2</sup> Hz. This reveals the fast kinetics of ion diffusion across the interphase. The charge transfer is represented by a frequency of approximately 1 Hz. The results indicate that <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> in the ether‐based electrolyte (53.37 Ω) is significantly lower than that in the carbonate‐based electrolyte (105 Ω). Therefore, an SEI layer is formed on the surface of the graphite electrodes in the ether‐based electrolytes. This interphase exhibits a low impedance of <i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub> that facilitates the de‐intercalation of the solvated Na ions and the rapid formation of ternary compounds in the form of Na(solv)<i><sub>x</sub></i>C<i><sub>y</sub></i>. Meanwhile, the low charge‐transfer impedance reflects the fast redox reaction at the surface of the graphite electrode in the ether‐based electrolyte. The combination of the low interfacial ionic diffusion resistance and the low charge‐transfer resistance benefit the fast intercalation kinetics of the graphite electrodes in SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="smll202003268-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003268:smll202003268-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003268-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Three‐electrode EIS results and the mechanical properties of the electrode and interphase. AFM is performed to study the mechanical properties and determine the physicochemical state of the ether‐derived SEI. The pressing (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">3c</link>) and rising (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0003">3f</link>) of the indenter on the graphite electrode (before cycle) confirm the presence of the SEI in both electrolytes. Theoretical calculations and experiments are performed to detect the components of the SEI. The theoretical calculations determine the formation paths and the possible components of the SEI. The density functional theory (DFT) is utilized to determine the decomposition paths, molecular orbitals, and bond‐dissociation energies of the ethylene carbonate (EC) and diglyme solvents (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>). The lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) value is used to measure the reducing strength of the reactants. Fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) and diethyl carbonate (DEC) are used as reference solvents to increase the reliability of the calculation. The LUMO values of diglyme, DEC, EC, and FEC are different (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0004">4a</link>). FEC exhibits the lowest LUMO value (−0.57 eV) among these solvents, which indicates the preferential decomposition of FEC to form the SEI during reduction. Therefore, FEC is commonly used as an SEI film‐forming additive. The LUMO value of diglyme (1.15 eV) is the highest among the solvents. Furthermore, the LUMO value of EC is lower than that of DEC. Therefore, in the mixed solvents of EC/DEC, SEI is mainly composed of the decomposition products from EC, and not DEC, which is consistent with the current perceptions. The decomposition paths of EC are listed in Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0004">4b</link>. Each atom is numbered separately to conveniently describe the bond energies. The bond dissociation energies are 5.47 eV (C<sub>2</sub>C<sub>3</sub>), 0.74 eV (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>), and 2.05 eV (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>3</sub>). Therefore, the dissociation of the C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and C<sub>3</sub>O<sub>3</sub> bonds are responsible for the decomposition of EC to form (CH<sub>3</sub>OCO<sub>2</sub>Na)<sub>2</sub> and CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OCO<sub>2</sub>Na (Equations 1–3, Figure S8, Supporting Information). The bond dissociation energies of diglyme are in the range of 3.43–3.55 eV; therefore, a similar amount of energy is required for the dissociation of the different bonds in diglyme (Figure <link href="#smll202003268-fig-0004">4c</link>). Hence, the products of the decomposition of diglyme are sodium alkoxides like CH<sub>3</sub>OCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>ONa and CH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>OCH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>ONa (Equation 4, Figure S8, Supporting Information). The theoretical calculations indicate that the decomposition of the ether‐based solvents is more difficult than that of carbonate‐based solvents. Furthermore, the products of the ether‐based solvents are mainly sodium alkoxides, which are completely different from the carbonate‐based solvents.</p><figure xml:id="smll202003268-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202003268:smll202003268-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202003268-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Theoretical calculations of the molecular orbital values and the decomposition paths for different solvents. a) Molecular orbital values (eV) for the solvents (EC, DEC, diglyme, and FEC). b,c) Decomposition paths and the corresponding binding energies for EC and diglyme.</caption></figure><p>Since Fourier‐transform infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy is the most effective method to identify organic functional groups, it is utilized to identify the organic components of the SEIs in different electrolytes. In this paper, in a full‐scale comparison with the SEI generated in carbonate‐based electrolyte, a comprehensive understanding of the ether‐derived SEI tolerant to large volume change over cycling can be depicted at length. The presence of the ether‐derived SEI on the graphite electrode is confirmed by the characteristic responses in the AC impedance spectra and the AFM force curve. The interphase resistance in the ether‐based electrolyte (15.63 Ω) is approximately 9 times lower than that in the carbonate‐based electrolyte. This enables the fast ion transfer kinetics across the electrolyte/electrode interphase in the ether‐based electrolyte. The ether‐derived SEI is highly resistant to structural deformation during cycling due to its excellent mechanical properties such as a high Young's modulus of 435.86 MPa. The components and their distribution in the ether‐derived SEI are also investigated to uncover the reason for the outstanding properties. FTIR spectroscopy and theoretical calculations reveal that the organic components in the ether‐derived SEI are primarily sodium alkoxides. Furthermore, the multilayer structure of the ether‐derived SEI is revealed by XPS characterization at different sputtering times. The organic components (sodium alkoxide) are primarily distributed near the outer layer, while the inner layer is dominated by inorganic compounds (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaF) with sparsely distributed organic compounds. The concentrated distribution of the inorganic components in the inner layer significantly improves the Young's modulus of the ether‐derived SEI. The organic components enhance the interfacial wettability and the adaptability to volume expansion. Moreover, the inorganic components in the inner layer are bound by the sparsely distributed organic components during the huge volume changes of graphite. This results in excellent cycle stability in the ether‐based electrolytes despite the occurrence of huge volume changes.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0060"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0070" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0080" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Materials</title><p>In this study, graphite (99.95%, Aladdin) was dried in a vacuum environment at 120 °C for 72 h to remove traces of water. The electrolytes were 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> (98%, Alfa) dissolved in a mixture of EC (99%, Aladdin) and DEC (99%, Aladdin) (1:1 by volume) and 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in diethylene glycol dimethyl ether (diglyme, 99%, Adamas). The slurry was prepared by mixing graphite, Super‐P conductive agent, and LA132 binder (mass ratio = 8:1:1) under magnetic stirring for 24 h. The slurry was coated on a copper foil and dried at 120 °C for 12 h in a vacuum atmosphere to fabricate the graphite electrode.</p><p>The 2025‐type coin cells were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box using a graphite working electrode, Na metal counter electrode (Sigma, 99.9%), Whatman GF/D separator, and 200 µL of electrolyte. The three‐electrode glass electrolytic cells comprised a graphite working electrode (diameter = 11 mm) coated on a Cu foil that was used as the counter electrode and metallic Na (Sigma, 99.9%) that was used as the reference electrode. These three‐electrode cells were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box, which contained less than 0.1 ppm of both O<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O, and allowed to rest for 24 h. The coin cells were used only for the galvanostatic discharge/charge tests. The other experiments (EIS, AFM, FTIR, and XPS) were performed using the three‐electrode glass electrolytic cells.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The galvanostatic discharge/charge behaviors of the as‐assembled coin cells were tested using the LAND‐CT2001A battery testers with a voltage test range of 0.01–3.0 V at a constant temperature (30 °C). The EIS results were obtained using an electrochemical workstation (660E, CHI) and collected at the sodiation state, that is, after the electrodes were discharged to 0.01 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). A three‐electrode configuration was adopted to exclude the influence of the counter electrode.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material Characterization</title><p>The discharging experiments were performed by chronopotentiometry (CP) (660E, CHI) at 5 µA. Subsequently, the cell was disassembled and the graphite electrodes were swilled with the corresponding solvent (DEC or diglyme) to remove the residual electrolytes. The electrodes were desiccated at 60 °C for approximately 12 h under an Ar‐filled atmosphere and sealed in an airtight container to prevent oxygen and water from having an impact.</p><p>AFM (Dimension icon, AC240TS‐R3, Bruker,) was performed at 25 °C. The dried electrode was pasted on the platform without any other treatment; subsequently, the morphology and mechanical properties were directly examined. An n‐type Si indenter with a radius of 9 (± 2) nm was applied. The AFM images were obtained in the tapping mode. The scan size was set to 10 × 10 µm<sup>2</sup> to identify the microsized graphite in the electrodes.</p><p>XPS: XPS (Escalab 250Xi, Thermo Scientific) was performed for an analyzer pass energy of 30 eV using an Al Kα (1486.6 eV) source (150 W). The diameter of the X‐ray beam was 500 µm. The sample preparation for XPS analysis was similar to that for AFM. In order to reduce the influence of air on the samples as much as possible, the disassembly of the battery, the drying of the electrodes, and the preparation of the XPS sample were all carried out in a glove box, and the prepared XPS sample was sealed in a container filled with Ar and transferred to the vacuum chamber of the XPS device in a very short time (&lt;10s). An ionic gun with low energy (1 kV) was selected to lower the influence of the Ar ion beam on the graphite surface. Probing depth of XPS is related to the energy of the photoelectron and the properties of the material. Generally, the probing depth of the XPS was defined as 3 times the mean free path of the photoelectron. According to the mean free path data, the average probing path of various materials can be roughly estimated. The probing depth is 0.5 ≈ 2 nm for metal samples, 1 ≈ 3 nm for inorganic compounds, and 3 ≈ 10 nm for organics. The probing depth of XPS was relatively shallow and can only provide the element content on the surface, which was very different from the bulk composition. Usually, in order to determine the elements content change at different depths, the surface of the material is sputtered in our experiment. The estimated sputter rate of Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> was 0.1 nm s<sup>−1</sup> and the approximate depths are 5 nm and 10 nm after sputtering 50s and 100s. The photoelectron take‐off angle was 54.7°, and the spectral ranges for different elements were 1062–1079 eV (Na 1s), 678–696 eV (F 1s), 524–539 eV (O 1s), and 279–296 eV (C 1s). The area of the ion beam sputtering was 2 × 2 mm, and the number of scans for each range was 5. The energy step size was 0.1 eV, and the base pressure of the main chamber was &lt; 1 × 10<sup>−7</sup> Pa. The samples were mounted using double‐sided adhesive tape, and the binding energies were referenced to that of C 1s (adventitious hydrocarbon contamination) at 284.8 eV. Meanwhile, cross‐section values of elements are 0.13E‐1Mb (C), 0.4E‐1Mb (O), 0.6E‐1Mb (F), and 0.12Mb (Na).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202003268-bib-0052"><sup>52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> And the sensitivity factors of each element are 1 (C), 2.881 (O), 4.118 (F), and 10.588 (Na) from the database of Scolfield.</p><p>FTIR: FTIR spectroscopy (Nicolet 6700, Thermo Scientific) was performed in the range of 4000–400 cm<sup>−1</sup>. The active materials were collected and ground with a mortar in a glove box. The dried spectral grade potassium bromide (KBr) was homogenized using the mortar. Subsequently, the powder was pressed into a slice (diameter = 7 mm) and transferred to a sealed container filled with Ar. KBr can easily absorb water; furthermore, the compressed KBr slices were brittle. Therefore, a small amount of dried cotton was placed in the container during the transfer of the KBr slice to lower the duration of air exposure. To eliminate the influence of water and other impurities in air, FTIR spectroscopy was carried out under a vacuum atmosphere.</p><p>TEM: The electrodes were handled by the same method as that used in FTIR. Subsequently, the electrodes were ultrasonically dispersed in the corresponding electrolyte solvent (diglyme or DEC) for 5 min. The upper liquid was extracted and dropped onto a copper grid for TEM analysis (HRTEM, Tecnai G2 F30 S‐TWIN).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Calculation Details</title><p>The theoretical calculations were performed based on the DFT using the Becke 3‐parameter‐Lee‐Yang‐Parr (B3LYP) hybrid functional in Gaussian 09. To obtain accurate geometries, there were no symmetry constraints during structure optimization. The thresholds for the energy and force convergence were 10<sup>−5</sup> eV atom<sup>−1</sup> and 10<sup>−3</sup> eV Å<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. In addition, the atomic performances were represented by the 6–311+G (d) basis sets.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202003268-sec-0130" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The present work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 21975026).
