                  Sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) are promising candidates for large-scale energy storage. Increasing the energy density of SIBs demands anode materials with high gravimetric and volumetric capacity. To overcome the drawback of low density of conventional nanosized or porous electrode materials, compact heterostructured particles are developed in this work with improved Na storage capacity by volume, which are composed of SnO2 nanoparticles loaded into nanoporous TiO2 followed by carbon coating. The resulted TiO2@SnO2@C (denoted as TSC) particles inherit the structural integrity of TiO2 and extra capacity contribution from SnO2, delivering a volumetric capacity of 393 mAh cm−3 notably higher than that of porous TiO2 and commercial hard carbon. The heterogeneous interface between TiO2 and SnO2 is believed to promote the charge transfer and facilitate the redox reactions in the compact heterogeneous particles. This work demonstrates a useful strategy for electrode materials with high volumetric capacity.

            <ce:abstract id="abs0010" view="all" class="author"><ce:abstract-sec id="abssec0010" view="all">
                  <ce:simple-para id="abspara0010" view="all">Sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) are promising candidates for large-scale energy storage. Increasing the energy density of SIBs demands anode materials with high gravimetric and volumetric capacity. To overcome the drawback of low density of conventional nanosized or porous electrode materials, compact heterostructured particles are developed in this work with improved Na storage capacity by volume, which are composed of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> nanoparticles loaded into nanoporous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> followed by carbon coating. The resulted TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@C (denoted as TSC) particles inherit the structural integrity of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and extra capacity contribution from SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, delivering a volumetric capacity of 393 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup> notably higher than that of porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and commercial hard carbon. The heterogeneous interface between TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> is believed to promote the charge transfer and facilitate the redox reactions in the compact heterogeneous particles. This work demonstrates a useful strategy for electrode materials with high volumetric capacity.</ce:simple-para>

                  <ce:section-title id="sectitle0035">Introduction</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="p0045" view="all">With the increasing demand for the large-scale energy storage, sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) are becoming promising candidates due to the high abundance of sodium, low cost, and improved safety in comparison with lithium-ion batteries (LIBs).<ce:cross-ref refid="bib1" id="crosref0365">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">1</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib2" id="crosref0370">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">2</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib3" id="crosref0375">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">3</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib4" id="crosref0380">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">4</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib5" id="crosref0385">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">5</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib6" id="crosref0390">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">6</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib7" id="crosref0395">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">7</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib8" id="crosref0400">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">8</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> However, the relatively low energy density of SIBs represents one of the major drawbacks toward the large-scale applications.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib6" id="crosref0070">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">6</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib9" id="crosref0075">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">9</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> Over the past years, efforts have been made to develop advanced electrode materials for SIBs. For example, hard carbon has been widely employed as the anode material for commercialized SIBs in view of the reasonable cost and performance.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib10" id="crosref0405">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">10</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib11" id="crosref0410">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">11</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib12" id="crosref0415">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">12</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib13" id="crosref0420">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">13</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib14" id="crosref0425">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">14</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib15" id="crosref0430">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">15</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> Nonetheless, the insufficient energy density and potential safety concern associating with the low sodium storage represent the major drawbacks of hard carbon anode,<ce:cross-ref refid="bib16" id="crosref0080">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">16</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> which raises the urgency to develop alternative materials.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0050" view="all">As a typical insertion anodes, TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> has attracted much attention for its naturally abundance, non-toxicity, and easy preparation.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib17" id="crosref0435">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">17</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib18" id="crosref0440">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">18</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib19" id="crosref0445">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">19</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib20" id="crosref0450">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">20</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> Excellent cycling stability and high-rate performance was demonstrated when employing anatase TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> as the anode for SIBs,<ce:cross-ref refid="bib21" id="crosref0085">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">21</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib22" id="crosref0090">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">22</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> which could be attributed to the prominent pseudocapacitive behavior of nanostructured TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib23" id="crosref0095">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">23</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> However, the specific capacity of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> has been generally limited to ca. 200 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, while the nanostructure feature further lowers the volumetric capacity.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib24" id="crosref0100">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">24</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> Unlike most insertion anode, alloying and conversion anodes such as SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> would potentially deliver much impressive specific capacity (e.g., theoretically 1378 mA h g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> based on SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>+7.75Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>+7.75e<ce:sup loc="post">−</ce:sup>→Na<ce:inf loc="post">3.75</ce:inf>Sn +2Na<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O).<ce:cross-ref refid="bib25" id="crosref0455">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">25</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib26" id="crosref0460">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">26</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib27" id="crosref0465">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">27</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib28" id="crosref0470">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">28</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib29" id="crosref0475">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">29</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib30" id="crosref0480">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">30</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> However, the application of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> was restricted by its sluggish reaction kinetics and rapid capacity fading.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib31" id="crosref0485">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">31</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib32" id="crosref0490">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">32</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib33" id="crosref0495">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">33</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                  </ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0055" view="all">Composite materials of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> have been proposed to inherit the merits of each component. For instance, Liang et al.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib34" id="crosref0105">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">34</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> fabricated one-dimensional tubular SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> core-shell nanocomposites with slightly improved cycling stability. Yang et al.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib35" id="crosref0110">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">35</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> construct a hollow paramecium-like SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> delivering a limited capacity of 200 mA h g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>. Tian et al.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib36" id="crosref0115">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">36</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> reported SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@C@TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> hollow spheres with three-layer structure, delivering a high capacity of 381 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> and improved stability. Despite certain improvement in specific capacity, these composite electrode materials possess porous or hollow structure to facilitate electrolyte penetration and accommodate volume expansion,<ce:cross-ref refid="bib34" id="crosref0500">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">34</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib35" id="crosref0505">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">35</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib36" id="crosref0510">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">36</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                     <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                     <ce:cross-ref refid="bib37" id="crosref0515">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">37</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> which obviously sacrificed the volume utilization and low volumetric energy density of active materials, limiting their use in practical SIBs.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib38" id="crosref0120">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">38</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref>
                  </ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0060" view="all">In this work, compact heterogeneous particles composed of porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> framework and confined SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> have been demonstrated as anode materials for SIBs with improved volumetric capacity. We have previously shown that nonporous bulky particles with proper heterogeneous interface would possess notable interface-induced pseudocapacitance, leading to high volumetric capacity.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib24" id="crosref0125">
                        <ce:sup loc="post">24</ce:sup>
                     </ce:cross-ref> Herein, SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> nanoparticles were loaded into nanoporous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, forming a compact heterostructure that inherits the structural integrity of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and extra capacity contribution from SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>. Electrochemical analysis revealed the promoted charge transfer in the heterogeneous particles, which compensates the drawbacks brought by the extended charge transport length. As a result, the volumetric capacity of compact TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>/SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> heterostructured particles was twice that of porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and even much higher than commercial hard carbon anode.</ce:para>

                  <ce:section-title id="sectitle0040">Results and discussion</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:section id="sec2.1" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="sectitle0045">Synthesis and structural characterization</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0065" view="all">The preparation of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@C heterostructured particles was illustrated in <ce:cross-ref refid="fig1" id="crosref0130">Figure 1</ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:float-anchor refid="fig1"/>A. Porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> spheres were synthesized by a previous reported method.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib39" id="crosref0135">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">39</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> SnCl<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>·2H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O was introduced into the sub-micrometer porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> spheres by a solvent evaporation method to obtain the TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@SnCl<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>·2H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O precursor. After high-temperature treatment in air, Sn<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> was oxidized to Sn<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup> to form TS. The content of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> in TS was about 20 wt % based on the feed ratio of the precursors. To improve the electronic conductivity and structural stability, a thin carbon layer was coated on the surface of the TS spheres by using dopamine as carbon source, which was named TSC.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0070" view="all">
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="fig1" id="crosref0140">Figure 1</ce:cross-ref>B showed the X-ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the as-prepared TSC, TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, and SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0145">Figure S1</ce:cross-ref>). The XRD pattern of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> showed peaks at 25°, 48°, and 37.8°, corresponding to the (101), (200), and (004) crystal planes,<ce:cross-ref refid="bib21" id="crosref0150">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">21</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> respectively. SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> showed peaks at about 26.6°, 33.8°, and 51.7°, corresponding to the (110), (101), and (211) crystal planes, respectively.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib40" id="crosref0155">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">40</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> As for the XRD pattern of TSC, obvious peaks belonging to TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> were observed. The poorly observed diffraction peaks belonging to SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> resulted from the low crystallinity of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> confined in nanochannels.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib41" id="crosref0160">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">41</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> 
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="fig1" id="crosref0165">Figure 1</ce:cross-ref>C shows the thermogravimetric analysis profile of TSC and TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> under air. The weight loss about 1.50% from TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> below 100°C was related to loss of the surface hydroxide groups and adsorbed moisture. The curve of TSC showed obvious weight loss of 6.62% between 300°C and 400°C, which results from the loss of carbon. The pore structure of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and TSC was studied by nitrogen adsorption isotherms (<ce:cross-ref refid="fig1" id="crosref0170">Figure 1</ce:cross-ref>D). The specific surface area and pore volume of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> were about 79.84 cm<ce:sup loc="post">2</ce:sup> g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> and 0.45 cm<ce:sup loc="post">3</ce:sup> g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, which decreased to 37.98 cm<ce:sup loc="post">2</ce:sup> g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> and 0.19 cm<ce:sup loc="post">3</ce:sup> g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> after the introduction of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, respectively. These changes confirmed that the porous channels of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> particles were filled with SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> to form a compact structure with low porosity.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0075" view="all">Raman spectroscopy was performed to investigate the defective structures of different components in the TSC (<ce:cross-ref refid="fig1" id="crosref0175">Figure 1</ce:cross-ref>E). The Raman spectra of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> showed an obvious peak at about 143 cm<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, which was contributed to the Ti-O bond. The corresponding peak of TSC shifted to a higher position, indicating crystal distortion and presence of defects.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib42" id="crosref0180">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">42</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> The peaks at about 1350 and 1590 cm<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> resulted from a disorder-induced feature (D-band) and the E<ce:inf loc="post">2g</ce:inf> mode of graphite (G-band), respectively (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0185">Figure S2</ce:cross-ref>A).<ce:cross-ref refid="bib43" id="crosref0190">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">43</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> These peaks demonstrated the exitance of both graphitized and less-graphitized carbon. The X-ray photoelectron spectroscopic (XPS) test was performed to further investigate the surface chemical state of the TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and TSC (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0195">Figure S2</ce:cross-ref>B). For TSC, peaks of Ti 2p and Sn 3d both shifted to lower binding energy compared with pure TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (shifted by −0.1 and −0.5 eV, respectively) as shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="fig1" id="crosref0200">Figures 1</ce:cross-ref>F and 1G. Similar Raman shift has been reported to arise from the electronic interaction at heterogeneous interface.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib44" id="crosref0520">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">44</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib45" id="crosref0525">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">45</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib46" id="crosref0530">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">46</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib47" id="crosref0535">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">47</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> To exclude the effect of carbon layer, the Sn 3d XPS spectrum of TS was also carried out, showing similar results (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0205">Figure S2</ce:cross-ref>D). Meanwhile, the diffraction peak of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> for TSC also shifted to a lower degree in the XRD pattern (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0210">Figure S2</ce:cross-ref>E), suggesting the strong coupling and intimate contact between the TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib48" id="crosref0215">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">48</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                     </ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0080" view="all">The morphology and nanostructure of porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> spheres and TSC were studied by scanning electron microscope and transmission electron microscope (TEM). As shown in <ce:cross-ref refid="fig2" id="crosref0220">Figures 2</ce:cross-ref>A and 2B<ce:float-anchor refid="fig2"/>, the porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> spheres exhibit a flower-like morphology assembled by sheet-like subunits with a thickness of around 10 nm. The disappearance of the large space between TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> nanosheets confirmed the successful incorporation of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> to form the TS composite with a compact structure (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0225">Figures S3</ce:cross-ref>A and S3B). TSC nanoparticles with rough surface were synthesized by coating TS with carbon layer (<ce:cross-ref refid="fig2" id="crosref0230">Figures 2</ce:cross-ref>C and 2D). During the synthesis process, the size of sub-micrometer spheres was perfectly inherited.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0085" view="all">TEM images demonstrated the nonporous texture of TSC, which confirmed the existence of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> inside porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>. The corresponding high-resolution TEM demonstrated lattice fringes belonging to the (101) crystal plane of anatase TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and the (200) crystal plane of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (with lattice spacing of 0.357 and 0.237 nm, respectively) (<ce:cross-ref refid="fig2" id="crosref0235">Figures 2</ce:cross-ref>F and 2G). A carbon layer with thickness of about 2 nm could be observed (highlighted in <ce:cross-ref refid="fig2" id="crosref0240">Figure 2</ce:cross-ref>G). The diffraction rings belonging to the (101) crystal plane of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and the (101) crystal plane of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> were also obtained by selected area electron diffraction. Meanwhile, the weak diffraction ring belonging to SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> suggests the poor crystallinity, which was consistent with the XRD result. Elemental mappings by energy dispersive spectrometer indicated the uniform distribution of Ti, Sn, and C elements (<ce:cross-ref refid="fig2" id="crosref0245">Figure 2</ce:cross-ref>I), confirming the homogeneous loading of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> in the TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> framework as well as the conformal coating of carbon layer. The absence of chloride element (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0250">Figure S4</ce:cross-ref>) confirms the complete conversion of SnCl<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>·2H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O precursor into SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>.</ce:para>

                  <ce:section id="sec2.2" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="sectitle0050">Characterization of electrochemical properties</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0090" view="all">The electrochemical sodium storage performance of TSC was systematically evaluated. <ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0255">Figures S5</ce:cross-ref>A and S5B showed the CV curve of TSC with voltage window of 0.01–2.5 V at 0.1 mV s<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> sweep rate. After the first cycle, the CV curves showed good repeatability, indicating the excellent reversibility of the electrochemical reaction. The peak at the voltage of 0.02 V during the first cycle was related to the decomposition of electrolyte and the formation of solid electrolyte interface (SEI). The reduction peak at 1.2–1.5 V was attributed to the reduction of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> into metallic Sn. The reduction peak at 0.2–1.3 V resulted from the insertion of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> into TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and the alloy reaction of metallic Sn.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib49" id="crosref0260">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">49</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib50" id="crosref0265">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">50</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> The oxidation peak at 0.6–1.3 V was attributed to the dealloying reaction to Sn and the de-sodiation of Na<ce:inf loc="post">x</ce:inf>TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, which were the inverse processes of the above reactions.<ce:cross-ref refid="bib34" id="crosref0270">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">34</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib51" id="crosref0275">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">51</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                     </ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0095" view="all">
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="fig3" id="crosref0280">Figures 3</ce:cross-ref>A and 3B<ce:float-anchor refid="fig3"/> showed the galvanostatic charge and discharge performance of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and TSC. The discharge and charging-specific capacity of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> in the first cycle were 265 and 156 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>, respectively. TSC possessed much higher discharge and charging capacity (667 and 357 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>, respectively). The low initial Coulombic efficiency of TSC could be attributed to the formation of SEI and the irreversible conversion of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> into Sn. From the second cycle onward, the charge/discharge curves gradually stabilize. Note that the TSC only shows slightly improved reversible gravimetric capacity compared with TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, yet the improvement in volumetric capacity is much pronounced (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0285">Figure S5</ce:cross-ref>C). As compared in <ce:cross-ref refid="fig3" id="crosref0290">Figure 3</ce:cross-ref>C, after incorporating high-capacity component (SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>) and inactive carbon layer, the gravimetric capacity increases from 208 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> of pure nanoporous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> to 279 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> of TSC. Given that the SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> nanoparticles only occupy the inner cavity of nanoporous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, a significant increase of tap density was observed (1.39 and 0.88 g cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup> for TSC and TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, respectively). Collectively, the volumetric capacity of TSC (393 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>) is more than twice that of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (181 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>), and much higher than that of commercial hard carbon (221 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>).</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0100" view="all">
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="fig3" id="crosref0295">Figures 3</ce:cross-ref>D and <ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0300">S5</ce:cross-ref>D show the rate performance of TSC and TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> at different current densities. When the current density was 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, and 0.1 A g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, TSC nanoparticles maintained discharging capacities of 357, 326, 274, and 204 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>, respectively, which were much higher than that of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (153.6, 145.0, 124.8, 107, and 134.6 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>, respectively). Though the compact structure of TSC might extend the diffusion path for sodium ion and compromise the utilization of active component at high rates, TSC still delivered and higher volume capacity under a current density of 1 A g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>. When the current density was reduced back to 0.1 A g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, the discharging capacity of TSC recovered a high value of 343 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>, showing good reversibility. The decent high-rate performance of TSC could be attributed to the smaller charge transfer resistance (4.788 Ω) and diffusion resistance compared with TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (215 Ω) as revealed by the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0305">Figure S5</ce:cross-ref>E), suggesting improved electronic/ionic transport in the heterostructured TSC particles as discussed shortly.</ce:para>
                     <ce:para id="p0105" view="all">
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="fig3" id="crosref0310">Figure 3</ce:cross-ref>E compares the cycling stability of TSC, porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, and TS composite without carbon layer. The volumetric capacity of TSC was twice higher than that of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and maintained a high value of 261 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup> after 200 cycles at the current density of 200 mA g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, which is much higher than that of TS (135 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>) and TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (126 mAh cm<ce:sup loc="post">−3</ce:sup>). The rapid capacity decay of TS might be related to the structural degradation of SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> moieties upon repeated sodiation and poor stability of the electrode-electrolyte interface (<ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0315">Figure S5</ce:cross-ref>F).<ce:cross-ref refid="bib52" id="crosref0320">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">52</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> The conformal carbon coating layer helps to improve the structural and interfacial stability of the TSC particles in the electrode, thus minimizing the capacity degradation. Compared with many reported anode materials for SIBs (<ce:cross-ref refid="fig3" id="crosref0325">Figure 3</ce:cross-ref>F and <ce:cross-ref refid="mmc1" id="crosref0330">Table S1</ce:cross-ref>),<ce:cross-ref refid="bib53" id="crosref0540">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">53</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib54" id="crosref0545">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">54</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib55" id="crosref0550">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">55</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib56" id="crosref0555">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">56</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref>
                        <ce:sup loc="post">,</ce:sup>
                        <ce:cross-ref refid="bib57" id="crosref0560">
                           <ce:sup loc="post">57</ce:sup>
                        </ce:cross-ref> the TSC shows obvious advantages in terms of both gravimetric and volumetric capacity, which is of critical importance for high-energy SIBs.</ce:para>

The proper heterogeneous structure formed between TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> would enhance the charge transfer within compact particles. TSC exhibits a 4–5 times higher diffusion coefficient for Na ions than that of porous TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>, and displayed twice higher than TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> and commercial hard carbon in volumetric capacity. This work demonstrates a feasible design principle toward anode materials with high gravimetric and volumetric capacity for potential industrial applications.</ce:para>

                  <ce:section id="sec2.3" view="all">
                     <ce:section-title id="sectitle0055">Limitations of the study</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0125" view="all">The Na transport and phase transition behaviors in the heterogeneous structure lack direct experimental observation and theoretical analysis. The initial Coulombic efficiency of the compact TSC particles is relatively low, which demands additional Na compensation. The content of high-capacity component (SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>) is relatively low, limiting the capacity of TSC. Synthesis of the TSC involves multistep reactions and is less efficient.</ce:para>

                     <ce:section-title id="sectitle0090">Method details</ce:section-title>
                     <ce:para id="p0150" view="all">Identifiers of the reagents used in this work are given in <ce:cross-ref refid="sec3.1" id="crosref0360">key resources table</ce:cross-ref>.</ce:para>
                     <ce:section id="sec3.4.1" view="all">
                        <ce:section-title id="sectitle0095">Experimental section</ce:section-title>
                        <ce:section id="sec3.4.1.1" view="all">
                           <ce:section-title id="sectitle0100">Preparation of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>
                           </ce:section-title>
                           <ce:para id="p0155" view="all">Firstly, 16.7 ml of N, N-dimethylformamide (DMF) and 50 ml of isopropyl alcohol (IPA) were thoroughly mixed in a 100 ml Teflon liner, followed by adding 1.67 ml of terabutyl titanate (TBT). The Teflon liner was then sealed in an autoclave and heated at 180°C for 20 hours. After cooling to room temperature, the product was isolated by centrifugation, washed with ethanol, and dried at 80°C overnight. The precipitation was heated at 450°C under air for 2 hours at a heating rate of 2°C min<ce:sup loc="post">-1</ce:sup> to obtain the pristine TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>.</ce:para>
                           <ce:para id="p0160" view="all">Then, 100 mg of TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> was dispersed to 50 ml of ethanol (C<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>H<ce:inf loc="post">5</ce:inf>OH) by ultrasonication for 30 minutes. 50 mg of stannous chloride dihydrate (SnCl<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>·2H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O) was added to the above mixture and stirred for 10 minutes until fully dissolved. The obtained solution was heated in a water bath and stirred at 80°C until solvent was evaporated completely to obtain the TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@SnCl<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>·2H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O. The product was heated at 600°C for 5 hours under air at the heating rate of 2°C min<ce:sup loc="post">-1</ce:sup> to obtain TiO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>@SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> (denoted as TS). The SnO<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf> sample was fabricated from SnCl<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>·2H<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>O via the same heating method.</ce:para>
                        </ce:section>
                        <ce:section id="sec3.4.1.2" view="all">
                           <ce:section-title id="sectitle0105">Preparation of TSC composite</ce:section-title>
                           <ce:para id="p0165" view="all">Tris-HCl buffer solution was firstly prepared by dissolved 605.7 mg of Tris in 50 ml water, followed by slowly adding HCl solution until pH = 8.5. 100 mg of TS was then added to the above Tris-HCl buffer solution and sonicated for 30 min. Then, 25 mg of dopamine hydrochloride (HDA) were added into the above solution and stirred at 30°C for 24 hours. The precipitates were collected by centrifugation, washed with water and ethanol alternately, then dried at 80°C. The as-prepared products were annealed at 600°C for 5 hours at a heating rate of 5°C min<ce:sup loc="post">-1</ce:sup> under Ar atmosphere to obtain TSC.</ce:para>
                        </ce:section>
                     </ce:section>
                     <ce:section id="sec3.4.2" view="all">
                        <ce:section-title id="sectitle0110">Material characterization</ce:section-title>
                        <ce:para id="p0170" view="all">The structures of the materials were characterized by powder X-ray diffraction analysis (XRD, EMPYREAN PANalytical) with Cu Kα (λ=1.54 Å) radiation. The morphologies of the composites were examined via scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Phenom LE) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, HT770 120 kV). To identify their chemical status, X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Thermo Scientific K-Alpha) with an Al Kα excitation source was employed. The specific surface area and pore size distribution of the products were measured using the Brunauer-Emmett-Teller analyzer (BET) at 575 K (Micromeritics ASAP 2460).</ce:para>
                     </ce:section>
                     <ce:section id="sec3.4.3" view="all">
                        <ce:section-title id="sectitle0115">Electrochemical tests</ce:section-title>
                        <ce:para id="p0175" view="all">The TSC, acetylene black, and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) were mixed in N-methyl pyrrolidone (NMP) solvent with a mass ratio of 8:1:1 to form uniform slurry. The slurry was then coated onto copper foil evenly, and then dried in vacuum oven at 75°C for 10 hours. The electrode films were punched into disks with a diameter of 12 mm. The typical mass loading of active material was 1.5-2 mg cm<ce:sup loc="post">-2</ce:sup>.</ce:para>
                        <ce:para id="p0180" view="all">The CR2032 half cells were assembled in argon-filled glove box with glass fiber (Whatman, GF/A) as separator, sodium foil as the counter electrode, and 130 μl of liquid electrolyte for each cell (1 M NaPF<ce:inf loc="post">6</ce:inf> in DME). The galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were conducted in a battery testing system (Neware BLC-300) with potential range of 0.01-2.50 V (vs Na/Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>). The specific capacity was calculated based on the total weight of the TSC composite. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) was performed with scan rates of 0.1 mV s<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> and voltage range of 0.01–2.50 V (vs Na/Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>) on an electrochemical workstation (Biologic). Electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) test was performed with the frequency range from 20 kHz to 16 mHz on the same workstation.</ce:para>
