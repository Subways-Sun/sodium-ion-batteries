Transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) are considered as promising anode candidates for SIBs with high theoretical capacity, while their intrinsic low electrical conductivity and large volume expansion upon Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation raise the challenging issues of poor cycle stability and inferior rate performance. Herein, the designed formation of hybrid nanoboxes composed of carbon‐protected CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles anchored on nitrogen‐doped carbon hollow skeletons (denoted as CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC) via a template‐assisted refluxing process followed by conventional selenization treatment is reported, which exhibits tremendously enhanced electrochemical performance when applied as the anode for SIBs. This work provides a rational strategy for the design of advanced hybrid nanostructures as anode candidates for SIBs, which could push forward the development of high energy and low cost energy storage devices.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201902881-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>The designed formation of hybrid nanoboxes composed of carbon‐protected CoSe<sub>2</sub> xml:id="smll201902881-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>With the huge demand for energy consumption in modern society and increasing environmental concerns, develo‐ping affordable and sustainable energy storage systems has received widespread attentions in recent years.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0001 #smll201902881-bib-0002 #smll201902881-bib-0003"/> Commercial lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are widely used in portable electronic devices, and hybrid electric vehicles due to the high energy/ power density and long cycle life.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0004"/> However, LIBs also face the inevitable challenge of the scarcity of lithium resources derived high cost, which makes it inappropriate for future large‐scale energy storage systems.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0005 #smll201902881-bib-0006"/> Therefore, it is crucial yet challenging to explore alternative candidates possessing the features of both high performance and low cost.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0007 #smll201902881-bib-0008"/> Recently, the research on sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) has been revitalized owing to its much lower cost and comparable energy/power density to LIBs, which has been considered as one of most promising system for grid‐level applications.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0009"/> Unfortunately, the sluggish electrochemical kinetics and larger radius of Na<sup>+</sup> derived serious volume change of host could result in fast capacity fading and poor rate performance of SIBs.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0010 #smll201902881-bib-0011 #smll201902881-bib-0012 #smll201902881-bib-0013"/> Therefore, it is one of the central task to explore advanced architectures for electrode materials with dramatically enhanced electrochemical performance in order to push forward the development of SIBs.</p><p>Recently, transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDCs) have been investigated as a star material system as potential anode candidates for SIBs because of the easily controlled morphology, numerous varieties, and high theoretical capacity.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0014 #smll201902881-bib-0015 #smll201902881-bib-0016"/> However, their intrinsic low electrical conductivity and large volume expansion upon Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation will generally cause poor cycle stability and inferior rate performance.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0017 #smll201902881-bib-0018"/> Currently, numerous efforts have been devoted to solving aforementioned issues, among which nanostructure engineering and designing TMDC/carbon hybrid structures have been demonstrated as powerful toolbox.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0019 #smll201902881-bib-0020 #smll201902881-bib-0021 #smll201902881-bib-0022 #smll201902881-bib-0023 #smll201902881-bib-0024"/> For example, Zhao and co‐workers<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0025"/> synthesized hollow microflower‐shaped CoSe<sub>2</sub> through a facile hydrothermal reaction, which exhibited a reversible capacity of 220 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1690 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Li and co‐workers<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0026"/> reported the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/N‐CNFs hybrid structure that can deliver a high specific capacity of 308 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles at the rate of 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Although these inspiring pioneer work, there are still remained challenging issues regarding the TMDC‐based hybrid structures: i) it is still difficult yet desirable to build a complex micro/nanostructure combining the features of both hollow architecture and TMDC/carbon hybrid structure, which could furthest enhance the electrochemical performance; ii) generally, it can only achieve mediocre performance enhancement through constructing simple TMDC/carbon hybrids by anchoring TMDC nanoparticles on carbon surface since the incomplete protection of the surface of the active component will still experience partial destruction. Therefore, to build dual carbon protection in the hybrid structure could be highly meaningful to achieve extraordinary cycling performance, especially at high current density.</p><p>In this work, we present the designed formation of hybrid nanoboxes composed of carbon‐protected CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles strongly coupled on the walls of nitrogen‐doped carbon hollow skeletons (denoted as CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC) via a template‐assisted refluxing process followed by conventional selenization treatment. Benefiting from the synergistic effects of dual carbon protection, highly stabilized active CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, and facilitated electron and mass transfer in hollow architecture, the CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanoboxes exhibit tremendously enhanced electrochemical performance when applied as anode for SIBs. Specifically, it can deliver a high reversible specific capacity of 324 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles and exhibit outstanding high rate cycling stability with 90.3% capacity retention at the rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 2000 cycles.</p><p>The formation process of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanoboxes can be generally illustrated as shown in <b>Scheme</b> <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0005"/>. First, the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and polydopamine core/shell nanocubes (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@PDA) were fabricated through the conformal coating a PDA layer via a typical self‐polymerization of dopamine hydrochloride in tris‐buffer solution on the low cost and environmentally benign Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanocubes,<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0027"/> and their typical characterizations were provided in Figure S1 of Supporting Information. Then, the core of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@PDA was removed by oxalic acid etching to form the PDA nanobox template (Figure S2A,B, Supporting Information). After this, the conformal coating of a Co‐glycolate layer on the surface of PDA nanobox template (PDA@Co‐G) were obtained through the facile refluxing process, followed by conventional selenization conversion to form the final dual carbon‐protected CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanoboxes.</p><figure xml:id="smll201902881-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902881:smll201902881-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902881-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The pipeline of the formation process of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC hybrid nanoboxes.</caption></figure><p>The morphological and microstructural characterizations were collected via field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM), as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0001"/>. The shape of PDA@Co‐G core/shell nanoboxes (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0001"/>A,B) can be well retained after selenization process (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0001"/>C–F) while its X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern (Figure S3, Supporting Information) clearly indicates that pure CoSe<sub>2</sub> phase has been formed (JCPDS NO. 53‐0449). The enlarged TEM image (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0001"/>G,H) clearly shows the interface between CoSe<sub>2</sub> and nitrogen‐doped‐carbon nanobox and the out CoSe<sub>2</sub> layer is composed of small nanoparticles with sizes of about 10 nm. Importantly, it can also be observed that a thin carbon layer is covered on the surface of CoSe<sub>2</sub> generated during the selenization process of Co‐glycolate, which could form the extra carbon protection of electrochemical active CoSe<sub>2</sub> component besides the support of the nitrogen‐doped‐carbon nanobox to further strengthen the structural robust and will definitely contribute to the electrochemical performance.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0028 #smll201902881-bib-0029 #smll201902881-bib-0030"/> The high resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) analysis of an individual CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticle (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0001"/>I) exhibits clear lattice fringes with spacing of about 0.264 nm corresponding to the (110) plane of CoSe<sub>2</sub>. Moreover, element mapping result (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0001"/>J) exhibits uniform distribution of Co, Se, C, and N elements, implying the homogeneous decoration of CoSe<sub>2</sub> layer on NC nanoboxes.</p><figure xml:id="smll201902881-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902881:smll201902881-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902881-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Morph SEM and TEM images of A,B) PDA@Co‐G, and C–F) CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC and G–I) HRTEM images of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC, J) HAADF‐STEM image and corresponding elemental mapping results of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanoboxes.</caption></figure><p>The surface chemical states of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC are then investigated by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) characterization. The survey spectrum (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0002"/>A) indicates the presence of all four elements, i.e., Co, Se, C, N. The high resolution spectrum of Co 2p (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0002"/>B) possesses two peaks at 778.8 and 793.8 eV, respectively, which can be attributed to Co<sup>2+</sup> in CoSe<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0031 #smll201902881-bib-0032"/> The peaks with binding energies of 780.2 and 795.7 eV correspond to the Co<sup>3+</sup> due to the partial surface oxidation of CoSe<sub>2</sub> when exposed to air, which is consistent with previous result.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0033 #smll201902881-bib-0034 #smll201902881-bib-0035"/> The high resolution Se 3d spectrum shown in Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0002"/>C displaying two peaks at 55.7 and 54.9 eV can be assigned to Se–Co bonds.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0036 #smll201902881-bib-0037 #smll201902881-bib-0038"/> The weak peak at 59.9 eV could be assigned to Se–O bond due to the surface oxidation of selenium under air condition.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0039 #smll201902881-bib-0040"/> In the C 1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0002"/>D), peaks at 284.8, 286.5, and 288.4eV correspond to the existence of sp<sup>2</sup>‐C, CN, and CO, respectively.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0041 #smll201902881-bib-0042"/> The N 1s spectrum, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0002"/>E, exhibits two peaks locating at 399.2 and 401.1eV, which can be indexed to the pyrrolic‐N and graphitic N, respectively.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0043 #smll201902881-bib-0044"/> In addition, the Raman spectrum (Figure S4, Supporting Information) reveals that the two peaks at 188 and 663 cm<sup>−1</sup> are attributed to the A<sub>g</sub> and A<sub>1g</sub> modes of CoSe<sub>2,</sub><link href="#smll201902881-bib-0022 #smll201902881-bib-0045 #smll201902881-bib-0046 #smll201902881-bib-0047"/> and the existence of peaks of carbon, confirming the formation of hybrid structure. The calculated <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> value is about 0.91, implying a relatively higher graphitization degree of the incorporated carbon in hybrid nanobox, which could be favorable for electron transfer.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0048"/> The content of CoSe<sub>2</sub> in the hybrid nanoboxes is determined by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), as shown in Figure S5 of the Supporting Information. It can be observed that it exhibits slight weight increase between 200 and 335 °C, which could be due to the oxidation and conversion of CoSe<sub>2</sub> into CoO<i><sub>x</sub></i> and SeO<sub>2</sub> species. After that, there are step‐wise weight loss in the range of 335–580 °C, which could be attributed to the combustion of the carbon species, and the conversion of CoSe<sub>2</sub> to Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> along with the sublimation of SeO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0049 #smll201902881-bib-0050 #smll201902881-bib-0051"/> According to this, the content of CoSe<sub>2</sub> in hybrid nanobox is determined to be about 84.5 wt%.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0022"/></p><figure xml:id="smll201902881-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201902881:smll201902881-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201902881-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>XPS spectra of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanoboxes: A) survey spectrum, B) Co 2p, C) Se 3d, D) C 1s, and E) N 1S.</caption></figure><p>In order to validate the effect of dual carbon protection in the hybrid nanobox, the electrochemical performance of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC anode was investigated in a half‐cell configuration using sodium metal as the counter electrode. The galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles for the 1st, 2nd, 50th, and 100th cycles at the current density 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0003"/>A) indicate a high initial coulombic efficiency of 71.4% (545 and 389 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for discharge and charge, respectively), which is superior compared with previously reported literatures (Table S1, Supporting Information) and could be a great advantage for practical applications.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0052"/> The characteristic plateaus at around 1.33, 1.10, and 0.70 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) is consistent with the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves (Figure S6, Supporting Information), where these three reduction peaks originate from the intercalation reaction from CoSe<sub>2</sub> to Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub> and the successive conversion reaction derived formation of CoSe and Co, respectively. The corresponding oxidation peaks located in the range of 1.5–1.9V can be reversibly ascribed to the evolution from Co and Na<sub>2</sub>Se to Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub>, and finally to CoSe<sub>2</sub> at around 1.87 V.<link href="#smll201902881-bib-0053 #smll201902881-bib-0054"/> Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0003"/>B shows the cycling stability and the corresponding coulombic efficiency of CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanobox at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, where an outstanding capacity retention of almost 100% can be observed over 200 cycles after the relatively faster capacity fading in the first few cycles, which is superior compared with that of nitrogen‐doped carbon nanoboxes (Figure S7, Supporting Information). Impressively, the CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC nanobox exhibits extraordinary rate performance (Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0003"/>C,D), delivering a decent capacity retention of 39.9% with the current density increasing from 0.1 to 25 A g<sup>−1</sup> (146 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 25 A g<sup>−1</sup> vs 366 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>). Importantly, a high reversible capacity of 360 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can still be recovered when the current density returns to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, displaying the perfect reversibility. As an important factor to evaluate the potential for high power/energy SIBs, the cycling capability at a high rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> is further investigated, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll201902881-fig-0003"/>E. First, the hollow structure can effectively alleviate the volume change–induced stress upon charge/discharge process and the nitrogen‐doped carbon skeleton can not only largely strengthen the structural robust, which can be well demonstrated by the morphological observation of the products after cycling (Figure S8, Supporting Information), but also facilitate the electron transfer due to the better electric conductivity, as revealed by the EIS measurement (Figure S9, Supporting Information). Second, nanosized particles strongly coupled with the hollow carbon skeleton can shorten the diffusion path of sodium ions. Third, CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles encapsulated in carbon layer can effectively prevent the agglomeration and pulverization of the particles during cycling process. Finally, the hybrid structure incorporated partially capacitive contribution is also a non‐negligible factor for the enhanced performance.</p><p>In summary, the designed formation of hybrid nanoboxes composed of carbon‐protected CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles strongly coupled with nitrogen‐doped carbon skeleton (denoted as CoSe<sub>2</sub>@C∩NC) has been achieved via a template‐assisted refluxing process followed by conventional selenization treatment. Impressively, the hybrid nanoboxes exhibit outstanding electrochemical performance when applied as anode for SIBs benefiting from the synergistic effects of dual carbon protection, highly stabilized active CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, and facilitated electron and mass transfer in hollow architecture. Specifically, it can deliver a high reversible specific capacity of 324 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles and show superior high rate and ultralong cycling stability with capacity retention up to 90.3% at the rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 2000 cycles. This work can not only provide a rationale strategy for the design of advanced anode candidate for SIBs, but can also push forward the development of next‐generation high energy yet low cost battery technology.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201902881-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>B.Q.L. and Y.L. contributed equally to this work.
