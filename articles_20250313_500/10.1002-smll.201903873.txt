type="main">Abstract</title><p>SnS<sub>2</sub> has been widely studied as an anode material for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) based on the high theoretical capacity and layered structure. Unfortunately, rapid capacity decay associated with volume variation during cycling limits practical application. Herein, SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> hollow nanocubes anchored on S‐doped graphene are synthesized for the first time via coprecipitation and hydrothermal methods. When applied as the anode for SIBs, the sample delivers a distinguished charge specific capacity of 1141.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and there is no significant capacity decay (0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 50 cycles). When the rate is increased to 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, it presents 845.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling 100 times. Furthermore, the composite also exhibits an ultrafast sodium storage capability where 392.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> and the charging time is less than 3 min. The outstanding electrochemical properties can be ascribed to the enhancement of conductivity for the addition of S‐doped graphene and the existence of p–n junctions in the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> heterostructure. Moreover, the presence of mesopores between nanosheets can alleviate volume expansion during cycling as well as being beneficial for the migration of Na<sup>+</sup>.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201903873-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Nanocube‐shaped SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO composites are synthesized for the first time via coprecipitation of Sn<sup>4+</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, and OH<sup>−</sup> followed by alkaline etching and hydrothermal methods. The SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO electrode exhibits a high reversible capacity (1141.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles) and excellent rate performance (392.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) when used as an anode for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource xml:id="smll201903873-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Along with the continuous consumption of fossil resources and the intensification of environmental pollution, we urgently need to find renewable and pollution‐free energy storage applications.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0001"/> Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have been extensively studied followed by successfully commercialized in portable mobile electronic devices ascribed to their high security, excellent energy density, and superior cycle life.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0002"/> Although LIBs have been widely used, the high cost caused by the limited supply of lithium hinders its development in the future.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0001 #smll201903873-bib-0003" style="qv">1a,3</link> Sodium has attracted widespread attention due to its similar reaction mechanism compared with lithium, as well as its abundant resources and low price.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0004"/> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are looking forward to replacing LIBs as a new generation of energy storage devices.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0001" style="qv">1a</link> Unfortunately, Graphite anode applied in commercial LIBs merely possess a capacity of 35 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> severed as an anode in SIBs, because of the larger radius of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0005"/></p><p>Hence, extensive efforts have been focused on the exploring of new types of anode materials with high specific capacity for SIBs, such as carbonaceous materials,<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0006"/> alloying metals,<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0007"/> and metal oxides/sulfides of conversion reactions.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0008"/> Among them, SnS<sub>2</sub> with the advantages of low price attributed to abundant sources and relatively high theoretical capacity is regarded as a candidate anode material.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0002 #smll201903873-bib-0004 #smll201903873-bib-0009" style="qv">2b,4b,9</link> Generally, nanocrystallization and compounding with carbonaceous materials are common methods accepted to mitigate the volume expansion even boost the conductivity of tin sulfide electrode materials. Zheng et al.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0010"/> fabricated tin sulfide nanoparticles anchored on graphene delivering enhanced specific capacity of 753.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when testing its cycle performance at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Recently, some researchers demonstrated that the formation of heterostructures within active materials greatly improves the electrochemical performance.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0005 #smll201903873-bib-0009 #smll201903873-bib-0011" style="qv">5,9a,b,11</link> Except for the electric field presented at the heterointerface helps accelerate the feasible electron migration, the kinetics of sodium ion diffusion at the grain boundary also improved. More importantly, polysulfide produced during deep sodiated state will be anchored on the heterogeneous interface, then the reaction with electrolyte will be restricted and the reversibility of the conversion reaction will be enhanced.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0012"/> Ou et al.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0005"/> synthesized SnS<sub>2</sub>/Mn<sub>2</sub>SnS<sub>4</sub> /Carbon heterostructures as the anode of SIBs showing superior cycling stability and the first Coulombic efficiency can reach 90.8% while the initial charge capacity achieved 841.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>Herein, we fabricate SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> hollow nanocubes anchored on S‐doped graphene as the anode for SIB which has not been reported before. The synthesized composites display remarkable electrochemical performances. When tested at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the initial discharge capacities can reach 2212.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and maintain 1141.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling 50 times. As far as we know, such an excellent performance has scarcely been demonstrated for SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based anodes, and the reasons for the excellent performance can be described as follow: 1) The addition of S‐doped graphene and the existence of p–n junctions in SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> heterostructure enhance the conductivity of composites. 2) The presence of mesopores between nanosheets can alleviate volume expansion during cycling as well as beneficial for the migration of Na<sup>+</sup> and electrolyte penetration.</p><p>The synthesis procedure of the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO is briefly illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201903873-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. Nanocube‐shaped CoSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> are prepared first by fast coprecipitation (Equation <link href="#smll201903873-disp-0001"/>) where Sn<sup>4+</sup>, Co<sup>2+</sup>, and OH<sup>−</sup> will assemble into nanocubes under the presence of citrate ions.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0013"/> Afterward, CoSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> precursor will react with the high concentrated alkaline solution at room temperature (Equation <link href="#smll201903873-disp-0002"/>) because of the amphoteric nature.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0014"/> In this process, the surface of nanocubes will be covered with a passivation layer because of the property of [Co(OH)<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> species that tend to be oxidized in the air (Equation <link href="#smll201903873-disp-0003"/>). According to previous research, the effect of capacitance can be learned by the connection between sweep rate (<i>v</i>) and the measured current (<i>i</i>): <i>i</i>  =  <i>av<sup>b</sup></i> where <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are constants.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0008 #smll201903873-bib-0023" style="qv">8c,23</link> By the slope of the log <i>i</i> versus log <i>v</i> plot (Figure <link href="#smll201903873-fig-0008"/>b), we can get <i>b</i>‐value. As we all know, the <i>b</i>‐value of 1.0 matches a surface capacitive dominated behavior, whereas 0.5 seems like to be a diffusion‐controlled process.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0023" style="qv">23a</link> It clearly demonstrates that b‐value of peak 1 to peak 4 are 0.85, 0.88, 0.90, and 0.86, respectively, suggesting that the capacitive process play a key role in the electrochemical reaction. It leads to durable cyclic stability and high‐rate performance. The contribution of surface capacitive effects (<i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i>) can be calculated by the equation: <i>I</i> (<i>V</i>) = <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> <i>v</i> + <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>, where <i>I</i>(<i>V</i>) stands for the current response at a fixed voltage.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0008" style="qv">8c</link> The k<sub>2</sub>‐value and k<sub>1</sub>‐value can be managed by the intercept and slope of the <i>I</i>(<i>V</i>)/<i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> versus <i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> plot. Figure S8d (Supporting Information) shows the proportion of capacitive current at 0.8 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and the result turns out to be 78.7%. Capacitance contribution at other sweep rates also calculated using the same method which can be observed in Figure <link href="#smll201903873-fig-0008"/>c. It is obvious that the contribution of pseudocapacitance turns to dominate gradually with the increase of scan rate, even reaching 94.4% when the scan rate set as 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. These results indicate that pseudocapacitance plays a key role in the rapid charge/discharge process.</p><figure xml:id="smll201903873-fig-0008"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201903873:smll201903873-fig-0008" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201903873-fig-0008-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV curves of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO anode at various scan rates, b) the fitted linear relationship between log <i>i</i> and log <i>v</i>, c) the ratio of pseudocapacitive contribution at various scan rates, and d) EIS and the equivalent circuit diagram of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>.</caption></figure><p>To further study the outstanding properties of the fabricated composites, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> are shown in Figure <link href="#smll201903873-fig-0008"/>d. In addition, Figure S9 (Supporting Information) presents the results of SnS<sub>2</sub> and SnS<sub>2</sub>‐rGO. It is obvious that each curve consists of two semicircles and a straight line. Usually, the semicircle located at high‐frequency region represents the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> through the surface insulating layer (<i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub>) and the intermediate‐frequency semicircle ascribed to the charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>). As for the sloping line stands for the solid diffusion process inside the composites.<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0009" style="qv">9a</link> The fitting results can be checked in Table S1 (Supporting Information). Obviously, the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> (34.21 Ω) of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO are much smaller than other anodes, suggesting that the heterojunctions and introduced graphene increase the electrical conductivity and reduce the charge transfer resistance.</p><p>In conclusion, we have synthesized SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> hollow nanocubes anchored on S‐doped graphene for the first time via coprecipitation and hydrothermal methods. Benefiting from the involvement of S‐doped graphene, which provides numerous active sites and transport channels, the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO electrodes exhibit a reversible capacity of 1141.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 87.2% and 845.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling 100 times. Moreover, when tested at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, 392.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be attained. The introduced mild graphene sheets not only relieve volume variation but also prevent reunion of nanocubes. Meanwhile, the suitable pore size is ideal for reversible insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> and penetration of electrolyte. The pseudocapacitive analysis is also calculated in detail to study the excellent rate performance. As a result, the capacitive contribution increased gradually with the increase of scan rates. Overall, the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO anode seems to be a promising candidate for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201903873-sec-0020"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Synthesis</i>: Above all, 4 mmol of cobalt chloride hexahydrate (CoCl<sub>2</sub> · 6H<sub>2</sub>O, Aldrich) and 4 mmol sodium citrate were dissolved in deionized water (140 mL), after that, 20 mL ethanol solution containing 4 mmol tin chloride pentahydrate (SnCl<sub>4</sub> · 5H<sub>2</sub>O, Aladdin) added under room temperature drop by drop. Subsequently, 2 <sc>m</sc> NaOH (20 mL, Aladdin) added in the mixture and stirred for 1 h. At last, 80 mL of 8 <sc>m</sc> NaOH was used to etch solid CoSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> for 25 min in the air and the generated pink product was gathered by centrifugation (5000 r min<sup>−1</sup>). In order to remove the leftover deionized water and ethyl alcohol, the product was dried under vacuum condition (80 °C/12 h) and 553.6 mg of H‐CoSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> was obtained. Then, 40 mL of ethanol solution was obtained by ultrasound (30 min) containing H‐CoSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> nanocubes (40 mg). Afterward, 80 mg of TAA (Aladdin) was added stirring for 15 min. Subsequently, 20 mL of GO solution (0.5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added dropwise into the mixture. After thoroughly mixing, the homogeneous solution heated for 12 h at 140 °C within a 100 mL Teflon‐lined stainless‐steel autoclave. When getting cool, the resulting product was recovered by filtration followed by freeze‐drying for 24 h. Then 66.7 mg of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO was obtained and the yield of active material was 51.3%. For comparison purposes, pristine SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> nanocubes were similarly prepared, but without the participation of GO. SnS<sub>2</sub>‐rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub> were acquired when 1.3 g EDTA‐2Na (Aladdin) was introduced to remove cobalt ion during the hydrothermal process.</p><p><i>Characterizations</i>: XRD with Cu Kα radiation (Rigaku D/Max‐2550) was first applied to examine crystalline structures of these samples at a scanning rate of 10<sup>°</sup> min<sup>−1</sup>. Raman spectroscopy was adopted with laser excitation at 514 nm. TEM purchased from Hitachi (HT7700) together with SEM (Supra55, Zeiss) were used to observe morphology. Energy dispersive spectrometer (EDS) that linked with SEM was used to explore the elemental distributions. The details of the crystal structure and crystalline lattice parameters were further delivered by high‐resolution TEM that obtained from JEOL (HRTEM, JEM‐2010F). The surface chemistry was probed by XPS (PHI5300 system). The SSA was received using BET theory (Micromeritics, ASAP 2020) by nitrogen adsorption test. At the same time, pore size distributions were obtained by the Barrett‐Joyner‐Halenda (BJH) method. The content of rGO was evaluated by TGA (SDT Q600 TG‐DTA) when tested in the air from 40 to 800 <sup>°</sup>C (10 °C min<sup>−1</sup>).</p><p><i>Electrochemistry</i>: For the purpose of preparing the working electrode, active materials (80 wt%), polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) binder (10 wt%), and 10 wt% of acetylene black were mixed in organic solvents (N‐methyl pyrrolidone). After mixing and stirring into a slurry, the slurry was coated on copper foil uniformly. Subsequently, in order to remove the solvent, the obtained composition was placed in an oven at 80 °C for 12 h. Sodium metal foil acted as two roles in the battery that is counter and reference electrodes. The electrolyte was NaClO<sub>4</sub> (1 <sc>m</sc>) dissolved in the organic mixture that contained diethyl carbonate (DEC)/ethylene carbonate (EC) (volume ratio is 1:1). In addition, 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC)<link href="#smll201903873-bib-0027"/> was also put into the electrolyte and the separator was made of glass fiber. Electrochemical performances were tested by Standard CR2032‐type half‐coin cells. Here active materials stand for SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>‐rGO, SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>, SnS<sub>2</sub>, and SnS<sub>2</sub>‐rGO with a typical mass loading of 0.8–1.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Galvanostatic discharge/charge tests were conducted on a LAND CT2001A machine (Jiunuo Electronics Co., Wuhan, China). CHI660e electrochemical workstation was used to analyze Na<sup>+</sup> mobility kinetics by EIS and CV. In addition, the potential range was 0.01–3 V for all electrochemical tests.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201903873-sec-0030" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 51572012) and Doctoral Fund of Ministry of Education of China (20120010110001).</p></section><section xml:id="smll201903873-sec-0040" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll201903873-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll201903873-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll201903873-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>J.‐Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Hwang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.‐T.</givenNames> <familyName>Myung</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.‐K.</givenNames> <familyName>Sun</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
