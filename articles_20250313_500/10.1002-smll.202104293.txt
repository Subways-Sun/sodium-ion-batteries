Informatiom</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Antimony sulﬁde is attracting enormous attention due to its remarkable theoretical capacity as anode for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). However, it still suffers from poor structural stability and sluggish reaction kinetics. Constructing covalent chemical linkage to anchor antimony sulﬁde on two‐dimension conductive materials is an effective strategy to conquer the challenges. Herein, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composites are successfully achieved with monodispersed Sb2S3 uniformly pinned on the surface of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene through covalent bonding of Ti‐O‐Sb and S‐Ti. Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene serves as both charge storage contributor and flexible conductive buffer to sustain the structural integrity of the electrode. Systematic analysis indicates that construction of efficient interfacial chemical linkage could bridge the physical gap between Sb2S3 nanoparticles and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene, thus promoting the interfacial charge transfer efficiency. Furthermore, the interfacial covalent bonding could also effectively confine Sb2S3 nanoparticles and the corresponding reduced products on the surface of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene. Benefited from the unique structure, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> anode delivers a high reversible capacity of 475 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles, even retaining 410 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles. This strategy is expected to shed more light on interfacial chemical linkage towards rational design of advanced materials for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll202104293-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>This study constructs covalent chemical linkage to anchor antimony sulfide on flexible 2D conductive materials. Furthermore, SIBs share similar device architecture and working principle with LIBs, exhibiting potential to fill the niche in intermittent renewable and cleaner energy storage that LIBs have struggled to reach. Nevertheless, the larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å, ≈34% larger than Li<sup>+</sup>) generally leads to sluggish reaction kinetics within the electrode and poor structural stability. In addition, the strong metallicity makes it infeasible to directly applying sodium as anode for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0009 #smll202104293-bib-0013 #smll202104293-bib-0014"><sup>9,13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, developing advanced electrode materials compatible with SIBs is highly imperative.</p><p>The updated demand for safety‐credible and high‐capacity makes metal sulfides (MS<i><sub>x</sub></i>) promising anode candidates for SIBs due to their remarkable theoretical capacity and high abundance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0014 #smll202104293-bib-0015 #smll202104293-bib-0016 #smll202104293-bib-0017 #smll202104293-bib-0018 #smll202104293-bib-0019"><sup>14–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, antimony sulfide (Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>) based on multielectron conversion‐alloy reactions (Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> + 6Na<sup>+</sup> + 6e<sup>−</sup> ↔ 2Sb + 3Na<sub>2</sub>S and Sb + Na<sup>+</sup> + 3e<sup>−</sup> ↔ Na<sub>3</sub>Sb, theoretical capacity: 947 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) attracts intensive research interest.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0020 #smll202104293-bib-0021 #smll202104293-bib-0022 #smll202104293-bib-0023"><sup>20–23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, several drawbacks have seriously hindered commercial implementation of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>‐based anode materials for SIBs:<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0019 #smll202104293-bib-0024 #smll202104293-bib-0025 #smll202104293-bib-0026 #smll202104293-bib-0027 #smll202104293-bib-0028"><sup>19,24–28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> 1) Poor conductivity of the reduced products (Na<sub>2</sub>S) results in poor electrical integrity of electrode, leading to large interfacial charge transfer impendence during desodiation reactions. 2) Huge volume swelling and aggregation of the discharged products trigger poor structural of the electrode stability and insufficient desodiation reaction. 3) Shuttle effect induced by generation and dissolution of polysulfides (Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i>, <i>x</i> = 4‐8) evokes continuous active mass loss and sharply increased interfacial charge transfer impedance.</p><p>Developing advanced Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> host or polysulfide blocking materials is of great significance to address these issues. Graphene has captured extensive attention to serve as flexible host materials to buffer the volume change and maintain the structural integrity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0025 #smll202104293-bib-0026 #smll202104293-bib-0029"><sup>25,26,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, the nonpolar nature and the limited defect sites of graphene lead to insufficient localization of polar Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and polysulfides. Under this circumstance, anchoring polar metal oxides, nitrides, etc., on graphene to immobilize Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and polysulfides is considered to be effective strategy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0025 #smll202104293-bib-0029"><sup>25,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the poor intrinsic electrical conductivity of oxides/nitrides is detrimental for efficient charge transfer upon cycling. Another typical 2D materials named MXenes (a vast group of transition metal carbide and nitrides) have caught extensive attention due to its high specific surface area, excellent conductivity, and mechanical stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0030 #smll202104293-bib-0031 #smll202104293-bib-0032"><sup>30–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Furthermore, MXenes with a general formula of M<sub>n+1</sub>X<sub>n</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> (T<i><sub>x</sub></i> represent –F, –OH, = O, etc.) are generally terminated with various functional groups, providing abundant chemically active sites for confining sulfides/polysulfides on the surface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0033 #smll202104293-bib-0034 #smll202104293-bib-0035"><sup>33–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a typical representative MXene, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> has been widely investigated because of it high stability and reproducibility. Combining Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene with metal sulfides as anode for SIBs has been intensively reported, with Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene serving as both charge storage contributor and flexible buffer to sustain the structural integrity of the electrode. For instance, the FeS<sub>2</sub>@ Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanohybrid delivers a capacity of 563 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0036"><sup>36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> SnS/ Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> composites also exhibit an impressive specific capacity of 646 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the performance of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene/metal sulfide hybrid anodes are still far from the corresponding theoretical values.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0038 #smll202104293-bib-0039"><sup>38,39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Three key problems need to be addressed for boosting the sodium storage performance of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene/metal sulfide composites: 1) Insufficient lamellar stripping and restacking of MXene flakes result in inadequacy exposure of active sites, leading to low mass loading of metal sulfides. 2) Weak coupling between metal sulfides and MXene matrix induces poor interfacial charge transfer efficiency and insufficient surface confinement. 3) Irreversible morphology or even phase change always take place during the oxidation process, especially in aqueous solution.</p><p>Herein, sufficiently exfoliated Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> MXene anchored with monodispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles (Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>) is successfully achieved through exfoliation‐covalent bonding strategy. First, selective hydrofluoric acid (HF) etching of Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> powder and tetramethyl‐ammonium hydroxide (TMAOH) delamination result in generation of oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> MXene, with sufficient exposure of modifiable terminations. Subsequently, monodispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles are uniformly anchored on the surface of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> MXene through covalent bonding of S‐Ti and Ti‐O‐Sb, under mild solvothermal reaction. As evidenced by systematic analysis and density functional theory (DFT) calculation, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> MXene not only serves as flexible conductive matrix to buffer the volume change and maintain the electrical integrity of the electrode, but also provides ample active sites to bridge the gap between Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> MXene surface and Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles through covalent chemical linkage. Benefited from the unique hybrid structure and the synergistic effect between the monodispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles and sufficiently exfoliated Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> MXene, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> anode delivers a high reversible sodium storage capacity of 475 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles, even retaining 410 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles. Given the identified great sodium storage performance, the rational exfoliation‐covalent bonding strategy for Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> could be extended to other related fields.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The strategy for preparation of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite with efficient interfacial covalent bonding is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. First, oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene was prepared from Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> by selective etching and delamination methods in diluted HF and TMAOH solution, respectively. Meanwhile, most –F terminal groups were replaced by concentrated –OH groups during the delamination process, exhibiting higher chemical activity. Subsequently, the freeze‐dried oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene was dispersed in ethylene glycol (EG), mixed thoroughly with antimony chloride (SbCl<sub>3</sub>) and <sc>l</sc>‐Cys·HCl under sonication. The electrostatic attraction induced covalent bonding between Sb<sup>3+</sup> and concentrated terminal –OH groups of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene took place during the following solvothermal reaction (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information), forming interfacial covalent Ti‐O‐Sb bond, which greatly prevents oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene from restacking. Simultaneously, sustained‐released S<sup>2−</sup> ions bond with Sb<sup>3+</sup> forming Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles (NPs) anchored on the surface of oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene. In addition, the freshly formed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and the over‐released S<sup>2−</sup> could also bond with the coordination desaturated titanium atoms of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene, generating interfacial Ti‐S bond. Finally, oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene anchored with monodispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles through efficient covalent chemical linkage, denoted as Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, was successfully achieved for further systematic investigation.</p><figure xml:id="smll202104293-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll202104293:smll202104293-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll202104293-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration for the fabrication of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite.</caption></figure><p>Detailed morphology and structure of the as‐prepared samples are further characterized by SEM and TEM (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link> and Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). electrodes is also evaluated by galvanostatic charge and discharge (GCD) measurements at a current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The GCD curves of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrodes (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0005">5b</link>) delivers an initial discharge capacity of 790.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and charge capacity of 540.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a high initial columbic efficiency (ICE) of 68.4%, which is ascribed to the efficient electron transport and facile ion diffusion. The irreversible capacity loss should attribute to the formation of SEI layer on the surface of electrodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0040 #smll202104293-bib-0050"><sup>40,50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The following GCD curves are almost overlapped, and the discharge/charge capacity appears to stabilize at 540 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which demonstrates its good reversibility and stability. Moreover, the discharge/charge voltage platforms are coincidence with CV curves. Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0005">5c</link> illustrates the cycling performances of pure Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrodes at a current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode delivers a remarkable capacity of ≈460 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with capacity retention of 79.3% after 300 cycles. Noteworthily, the CE during the area between 10th and 30th cycles are more than 100%, which is due to the fact that the SEI film might partially dissolve in the oxidation process and contribute a part of capacity; besides, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> NPs and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXenes may make a small extra capacitive capacity contribution at low potential. For comparison, oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene electrode exhibits a lower but more stable capacity of 270 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, while the pure Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode with a high initial discharge capacity of 890 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> suffers an obvious fast capacity decay process and eventually decays to 80 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Apparently, the interfacial chemical bonding with oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene plays an important role in enhancing the long‐term cycling stability of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>.</p><p>Rate capacity is one of the most crucial factors for practical application of electrode materials. Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0005">5d</link> exhibits the rate performance of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> hybrid with current density step‐wise from 0.2 to 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>; the discharge capacities of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composites are around 470, 450, 440, 430, 410, and 370 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Even at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode still maintains a discharge capacity of 370 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much superior than that of oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene. Furthermore, when the current density returns to 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity rapidly recovers to 475 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating excellent structure robustness of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode. In addition, the corresponding discharge and charge curves at various current densities from 0.2 to 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> are displayed in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0005">5e</link>. The extremely stable voltage plateaus of the electrode under different current densities further demonstrates its structural stability. Long‐term cycling performance was performed under a current density of 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0005">5f</link>, the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode delivers a stable and reversible capacity of 410 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention rate of 70.7% after 500 cycles, which is superior to most recently reported results (Table <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>To further explore the differences in cyclability, we characterized the morphologies of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and pure Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrodes after 200 cycles (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). For the cycled Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite, the surface of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode is very flat (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S8a</link>, Supporting Information), and the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles tend to attach tightly to the surface of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S8b</link>, Supporting Information), implying a strong interaction between them. On the contrary, the surface of the cycled Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode crack and split (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S8c</link>, Supporting Information), and some of the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles are tend to aggregate into a bigger bulk (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-supitem-0001">S8d</link>, Supporting Information). This demonstrates that the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene has a stronger chemical affinity for Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> particles and the corresponding discharged products on the surface of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene, which is helpful to alleviate the pulverization and aggregation of nanoparticles, preserving structural integrity of the electrode.</p><p>To elucidate the reason for the excellent electrochemical performance of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite, detailed reaction kinetics should be revealed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0053 #smll202104293-bib-0054 #smll202104293-bib-0055 #smll202104293-bib-0056"><sup>53–56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> CV measurements with step‐wise scan rates (0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) are conducted (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a‐c</link>), where well‐defined and analogous redox reaction peaks are found. The capacitive effect plays an important role in enhancing the sodium storage performance of the electrode under large current density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0055 #smll202104293-bib-0056"><sup>55,56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The charge storage mechanism can be qualitatively analyzed by Equations (<link href="#smll202104293-disp-0004">4</link>) and (<link href="#smll202104293-disp-0005">5</link>), where a and b are adjustable parameters. Generally, the b value is between 0.5 and 1.0, which is calculated from the slope of log(i) versus log(ν). When b value is closest to 0.5, the electrochemical behavior is controlled by diffusion process, and when b value approaches 1.0, a surface capacitance process is dominant.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0056 #smll202104293-bib-0057"><sup>56,57</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As depicted in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0006">6f</link>, the scatter plot of log(i) and log(ν) exhibits a good linear relationship, and b values of three samples for the peak 2 are calculated to be 0.64, 0.87, and 0.91, respectively. This suggests the electrochemical behavior of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite are dominated by surface capacitance process, while Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> is controlled by diffusion‐controlled behavior. The capacitive contribution of charge storage mechanism can be further quantified according to Equations (<link href="#smll202104293-disp-0006">6</link>) and (<link href="#smll202104293-disp-0007">7</link>). The response current at fixed voltage i(<i>V</i>) can be separated as capacitive contribution (k<sub>1</sub> ν) and diffusion‐controlled process (k<sub>2</sub> ν<sup>1/2</sup>). According to the linear fitting of ν<sup>1/2</sup> versus i(<i>V</i>)/ν<sup>1/2</sup>, the values of k<sub>1</sub> and k<sub>2</sub> could be easily calculated. Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0006">6g</link> shows the detailed capacitive contribution of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> at 0.3 mV s<sup>−1</sup>; 87.54% of charge storage comes from the capacitive contribution. Similarly, capacitive contributions of the other samples at different scan rates are displayed in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0006">6h</link>. The capacitive contribution increases with scan rate, demonstrating that capacitive behavior is more meaningful for sodium storage under large current density. Such excellent rate performance should be attributed to the efficient interfacial covalent bonding, which is believed to be beneficial for accelerating the reversible conversion‐alloying reaction kinetics.</p><p>Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) is employed to investigate the reaction kinetics of oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene, pure Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrodes before cycling and after 200 cycles. Generally, the Nyquist plots contain a quasi‐semicircle at high frequencies and an inclined quasi‐line at low frequency. The slope of skew line at low frequency region represents the diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> and the higher the slope is, the faster the Na<sup>+</sup> diffuses.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0040 #smll202104293-bib-0058"><sup>40,58</sup></link><sup>]</sup> <b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0007"><b>7</b>a,b</link> shows that the slopes of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> are more vertical than Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, revealing the faster diffusion of sodium ions in Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrodes. In addition, the quasi‐semicircle at high frequencies can reflect the electrolyte transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>s</sub>) and charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>). Several key EIS parameters could be fitted by equivalent circuit diagram (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0008"><b>8</b>a</link>, inset). The simulations of <i>R</i><sub>s</sub> and <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> impedance for three samples before cycling and after 200 cycles are compared, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0007">7c,d</link>. The diffusion paths and barriers of Na<sup>+</sup> on the electrodes have been investigated by the CI‐NEB method. Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0008">8a,b</link> depicts the migration paths of Na<sup>+</sup> on the surface of oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composites, and the relative energy along the migration paths are shown in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0008">8c</link>. The sodiation/desodiation processes involve the coupling/decoupling reactions between electron and Na<sup>+</sup>, as demonstrated by the structural analysis and DFT calculation; the successfully constructed interfacial covalent bonding severely decreases the electron transfer barrier between Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> NPs and the oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene, thus facilitating Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics. Analogously, the migration paths and energy barrier of S<sup>2−</sup> on the electrodes confirm that S<sup>2−</sup> suffers a higher migration energy barrier in the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite (Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0008">8d–f</link>). Moreover, as shown in Figure <link href="#smll202104293-fig-0008">8g,h</link>, the adsorption energy of S<sup>2−</sup> on Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> is −1.759 eV, which is much larger than that of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene (−0.683 eV). The larger migration energy barrier and stronger binding energy of S<sup>2−</sup> within the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite suggest that oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene anchored with Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> NPs has a stronger affinity to Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and its intermediate discharge products; thus, it can more effectively resist the aggregation and pulverization of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> during cycling, and mitigate the “shuttling effect” of polysulfides, resulting in a more stable structure and improved cycling stability.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composites with monodispersed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> NPs anchored on the surface of oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene are successfully achieved. Integration of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene and Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> are realized through covalent chemical bonding of S‐Ti and Ti‐O‐Sb. Both experimental and theoretical calculation results suggest that the conductive and flexible oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene not only provides abundant active sites to confine Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> NPs, but also effectively buffers the volume change during repeated cycles to maintain the structural integrity of the electrode. Moreover, the elaborated interfacial S‐Ti and Ti‐O‐Sb bonds effectively bridge the gap between Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> NPs and the oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene, which significantly accelerates the charge transfer efficiency. As expected, Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> anode delivers high reversible capacity and superior long‐term cycling stability (475 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles, 410 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles), demonstrating its great potential for implementation in practical SIBs. This work sheds more light on construction of interfacial covalent chemical linkage to design advanced electrode materials for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Reagents and Materials</title><p>Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> crystals (400 mesh, purity &gt; 98%) were purchased from Laizhou Kaikai Ceramic Materials Co., Ltd. HF, TMAOH, and EG were purchased from Shanghai Chemical Agent Corporation. SbCl<sub>3</sub>, <sc>l</sc>‐cysteine hydrochlorides (<sc>l</sc>‐Cys·HCl), <i>N</i>‐methyl pyrrolidone (NMP), polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP), and polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF) were purchased from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent, Inno‐Chem, and Sangon Biotech, respectively. Deionized water (18.25 <sc>m</sc>Ω) was produced by a Qiqin pure water machine and used for all the experiments. All chemicals were used without further treatment.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of Oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene</title><p>Oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene was prepared from commercial Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> by room‐temperature intercalation and delamination methods on the basis of previous research work.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0040"><sup>40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Briefly, Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> crystals were immersed and stirred in diluted HF (20 wt%) for 2 h at room temperature (RT), and then it was dispersed in TMAOH aqueous solution (25 wt%) and stirred for 4 h at RT to delaminate into an oligolayered structure. Afterward, the oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene was collected by multiple washing, centrifugation, and vacuum drying.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> Composite</title><p>Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composites were synthesized via introducing Sb (III) and sulfur sources into the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> solution during the solvent‐thermal reaction process. In a typical experiment, 16.0 mg oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene was dispersed into 80 mL EG followed by ultrasonication for 30 min to obtain a homogeneous solution with Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> concentration of 0.2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>. 1.5 mmol SbCl<sub>3</sub>, 1.5 mmol <sc>l</sc>‐Cys·HCl, and 0.15 g PVP were added into the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐EG solutions to form reaction system. After stirring for 30 min, the above solution was transferred into a 100‐mL Teflon‐lined stainless‐steel autoclave and maintained at 120 °C for 6 h. In the end, the resulting precipitation was collected by centrifugation and washed several times with pure water, and dried at 50 °C overnight in vacuum. Comparable samples of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> were also synthesized by a similar procedure while in the absence of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were utilized to identify crystal structure of as‐prepared samples on a HaoYuan DX‐2700 PHASER X‐Ray Diffractometer with Cu‐Ka radiation (λ = 0.15406 nm). Surface morphologies and microstructure of samples were characterized using field emission scanning electron microscope (FE‐SEM, TESCAN, MIRA3 LMU, accelerated voltage of 0.2–30 kV) with an EDS. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image, HRTEM image, and mapping were performed on a TEM (Titan G2 60–300 with image corrector, equipped with Super‐X EDS). FT‐IR (Thermo Fisher IS‐50) spectra were collected between 400 and 4000 cm<sup>−1</sup> to explore the surface functional groups. Raman spectroscopy was carried out by a Thermo Fisher DXR 2xi microspectroscopic system. XPS was performed by a Thermo Fisher‐VG Scientific ESCALAB250Xi spectrometer with a monochromatic Al Kα X‐ray source (75–150 W).</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Computational Methods</title><p>The Vienna ab initio package was employed to perform DFT calculations within the generalized gradient approximation (GGA) using the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) formulation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0059 #smll202104293-bib-0060 #smll202104293-bib-0061"><sup>59–61</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The projected augmented wave (PAW) potentials<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0062 #smll202104293-bib-0063"><sup>62,63</sup></link><sup>]</sup> were chosen to describe the ionic cores and take valence electrons into account using a plane wave basis set with a kinetic energy cutoff of 400 eV. Partial occupancies of the Kohn−Sham orbitals were allowed using the Gaussian smearing method and a width of 0.05 eV. The electronic energy was considered self‐consistent when the energy change was smaller than 10<sup>−6</sup> eV. A geometry optimization was considered convergent when the energy change was smaller than 0.05 eV Å<sup>−1</sup>. Grimme's DFT‐D3 methodology<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll202104293-bib-0064"><sup>64</sup></link><sup>]</sup> was used to describe the dispersion interactions among all the atoms in adsorption models. The adsorption energies (Eads) were calculated as Eads = Ead/sub − Ead − Esub, where Ead/sub, Ead, and Esub are the total energies of the optimized adsorbate/substrate system, the adsorbate in the structure, and the clean substrate, respectively. Moreover, Na and S ion migration barrier energies had been evaluated using the climbing nudged elastic band (CI‐NEB) methods.</p></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurement</title><p>Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite was used to made anode for SIBs, similarly, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and oligolayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene are regarded as experimental control group. During the preparation of electrode materials, active materials (70 wt%), Super P carbon black (15 wt%), and PVDF binder (15 wt%) were dissolved in NMP solution, and the slurry was painted on a copper foil and dried at 60 °C overnight in vacuum, Then the dried one‐piece electrodes were cut into many round film electrodes (diameter = 12 mm). Coin‐type half‐cells were assembled using CR2016 coin cells with sodium foil as the counter and reference electrodes in a glovebox (Vigor) filled with Ar atmosphere. The electrolyte used was 1.0 <sc>m</sc> NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> in diglyme (volume% = 100%), and the glass fiber (Whatman GF/D) was used as the separator. The galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were cycled from 0.01 to 2.5 V using a LAND CT2001A Battery Testing System. CV and EIS were carried out on a GAMRY INTERFACE 1000E electrochemical workstation.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll202104293-sec-0130" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Science Research Initiation Fund of Central South University (No. 202045012), Natural Science Foundation of Hunan Provience (No. 2020JJ5688), the Key Research and Development Program of Jiangxi Province (No. 20181ACE50013), and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities of Central South University (No. 2019zzts708) and thanks eceshi (<url href="https://www.eceshi.com">www.eceshi.com</url>) for DTA and DFT analysis.
