Vacancy, as a kind of defect, can be introduced into transition metal layer to trigger oxygen redox, thus enhancing the energy density of layer‐structured cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries. Herein, the formation process, recent progress in working mechanisms of triggering oxygen redox, as well as advanced characterization techniques for transition metal (TM) vacancy were overviewed and discussed. Strategies applied to stabilize the vacancy contained structures and harness the reversible oxygen redox were summarized. Furthermore, the challenges and prospects for further understanding TM vacancy were particularly emphasized.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>This concept focuses on the formation of transition metal (TM) vacancy, vacancy contained configurations for triggering oxygen redox, advanced characterization techniques for detecting TM vacancy and viable strategies to improve the reversibility of vacancy‐induced oxygen redox for layered cathode materials of sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203586:chem202203586-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203586-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0001"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>In the past decades, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have served as a revolutionary technology due to the widespread use of smart grids, portable electronics and electric vehicles.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0001 #chem202203586-bib-0002"><sup>[1,2]</sup></link> However, LIBs are challenged by the limited abundance and uneven geopolitical distribution of Li resources. With the urgent demand for low‐cost and economic sustainable energy storage, rechargeable sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as the most appealing alternative to LIBs in large‐scale energy storage because of the globally abundant and even distribution of Na element, low cost of raw materials in sodium battery systems and low (de)solvation energy of Na<sup>+</sup> ions. As a vital part of the battery, cathode materials have received much attention.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0003 #chem202203586-bib-0004 #chem202203586-bib-0005"><sup>[3–5]</sup></link> However, conventional layered oxide cathodes face the challenge that charge compensation only originates from transition metal (TM) redox during charging and discharging, which reflected in the limitation of specific capacity for energy storage demand. Since the emergence of anionic redox, the charge compensation mechanism has been changed that extra capacity and higher working voltage synergized with cationic redox can be realized.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0006 #chem202203586-bib-0007 #chem202203586-bib-0008 #chem202203586-bib-0009"><sup>[6–9]</sup></link> Recent research works mainly focused on the rational design of the local structures for triggering anionic redox reaction by adjusting the Li/Na−O−X (X: metal ions) configurations,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0010 #chem202203586-bib-0011"><sup>[10,11]</sup></link> and it has broader space for exploration.</p><p>Defect engineering in energy storage materials has been proven as a versatile approach to maneuver their electrochemical performances by regulating the crystal and electronic structure.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0012 #chem202203586-bib-0013"><sup>[12,13]</sup></link> For example, introducing defects not only adjusts the bandgap or change the distribution of electron to improve charge transfer, but also disturbs the surrounding atoms and provides more storage sites to promote ion diffusion effectively. Vacancies in layer materials for secondary batteries have been studied with much attention in correlating the crystal structure with electrochemical performances.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0010 #chem202203586-bib-0011 #chem202203586-bib-0012 #chem202203586-bib-0013"><sup>[10–13]</sup></link> By tuning the synthetic methods (e.g., annealing time, cooling rate etc.), vacancies can be introduced into the TM layer. and trigger anionic redox. However, the understanding of this kind of defect chemistry is still too insufficient to obtain the ideal cathode materials with high capacity and reversible anionic redox.</p><p>Herein, we aim to overview how TM vacancy forms, the idea booms and alters the cathode materials design in SIBs. Based on the fundamental scientific issues behind anionic redox, we summarized the working mechanisms of vacancy‐triggered oxygen redox, revisited the advanced techniques for characterizing TM vacancies and reviewed the viable progress in stabilizing the reversibility of vacancy induced oxygen redox in detail. Finally, the problems deserving to be solved in the future were also highlighted.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0002"><title type="main">The Formation of TM Vacancies in SIBs</title><p>The formation of TM vacancies in Na‐based metal oxides has been observed over the past decades.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0014 #chem202203586-bib-0015 #chem202203586-bib-0016 #chem202203586-bib-0017 #chem202203586-bib-0018"><sup>[14–18]</sup></link> In the early 1970s, Hagenmuller's group had proposed a phase diagram for sodium manganese bronzes and suggested that Na<sub>0.70</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> would uptake oxygen during the cooling process after sintering below 600 °C,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0014 #chem202203586-bib-0015"><sup>[14,15]</sup></link> resulting in the elevated valence state of Mn ions and the formation of Mn deficient (denoted as “□”) phase Na<sub>0.70</sub>MnO<sub>2<i>+δ</i></sub> (0≤<i>δ</i>≤0.25), the whole reaction process can be written as:<displayedItem xml:id="chem202203586-disp-0001" numbered="no" type="mathematics"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203586:chem202203586-math-5001"/><mediaResource alt="original image" rendition="webOriginal" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203586-math-5001.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></displayedItem></p><p>Later, Dahn's group found a reversible mass increase below 600 °C and decrease above 600 °C in Na<sub>2/3</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, which represented the uptake and release of O during cooling and heating processes, resulting in the generation of Mn vacancies.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0016"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Guenne et al. further verified that these compounds should be expressed in terms of manganese‐deficiency containing phases as Na<sub>0.70<i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub><i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/(1+<i>y</i>/2)=2/(2+<i>y</i>)) rather than the “over‐stoichiometry” formulation Na<sub>0.70</sub>MnO<sub>2+<i>y</i></sub>. Among them, there are two kinds of layered allotropic phases, corresponding to two composition domains of hexagonal <i>α</i>‐Na<sub>0.62</sub>Mn<sub>0.89</sub>O<sub>2</sub>/Na<sub>0.68</sub>Mn<sub>0.975</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>α</i>‐Na<sub>0.70</sub>MnO<sub>2+<i>y</i></sub>, 0.05≤<i>y</i>≤0.25) and orthorhombic <i>β‐</i>Na<sub>0.68</sub>Mn<sub>0.975</sub>O<sub>2</sub>/Na<sub>0.70</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> (<i>β‐</i>Na<sub>0.70</sub>MnO<sub>2+<i>y</i></sub>, 0≤<i>y</i>≤0.05).<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0017"><sup>[17]</sup></link> Manthiram's group have successfully synthesized hexagonal P2‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>MnO<sub>2+<i>δ</i></sub> (<i>δ</i>≤0.25) and orthorhombic P2‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>MnO<sub>2+<i>δ</i></sub> without cation vacancies (<i>δ≈</i>0) by reducing NaMnO<sub>4</sub> with NaI in the alkaline environment followed by sintering process.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0018"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Besides the reaction solvents and sintering temperatures, the content of TM vacancy is directly related to the cooling rates. Komaba's group unveiled Mn vacancy formation in P2‐type sodium manganate during slow cooling. By inductive coupled plasma (ICP) and iodometry, they determined the accurate chemical formula Na<sub>0.59</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>□<sub>0.10</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (hexagonal), which exhibited moderate phase transitions compared to the distorted P′2‐Na<sub>0.64</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> (orthorhombic) without TM vacancies obtained through quenching process.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0019"><sup>[19]</sup></link></p><p>Modulating suitable content of TM vacancies and enhancing Mn redox center by doping can effectively improve comprehensive electrochemical performances, especially in the high voltage region. Kwon et al. clarified the effect of vacancies on phase evolution of <i>β</i>‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>[Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Li<sub><i>x</i></sub>]O<sub>2+<i>y</i></sub> through substituting Mn by Li. They found that Li ions extracted and vacancies formed during charging process. Similar as the disordered distribution of Li, Mn vacancies randomly distributed in the TM layer, which caused uneven electrostatic repulsion between Na and Mn, leading to the Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy disordered structure during desodiation and sodiation.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0020"><sup>[20]</sup></link> Yang's group demonstrated a synergetic strategy through liquid N<sub>2</sub> quenching to introduce vacancy and Fe/Al co‐doping to modulate the average valence of Mn. Besides, the stronger Al−O bond can stabilize the P2 structure by enlarging the spacing of sodium layers. It was also found that proper amounts of Fe‐doping can break cations ordered structure and suppress unexpected P2−O2 transition, thus enhancing cycle stability.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0021"><sup>[21]</sup></link> They also revealed that more TM vacancies (7.8 %) formed in natural cooling can suppress TMO<sub>2</sub> gliding and prevent stacking faults during cycling but delivered relatively low capacity. In contrast, low content of TM vacancies (1.6 %) resulted in higher capacity but poor cycle stability.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0022"><sup>[22]</sup></link></p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0003"><title type="main">TM Vacancies for Triggering Oxygen Redox</title><p>In Li‐rich oxide cathode materials for LIBs, two types of O 2<i>p</i> orbitals created: one is along Li−O−TM configuration and the other is along Li−O−Li configuration, as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0001">1</link>(a). The O 2<i>p</i> orbital along Li−O−Li configuration neither hybridizes with TM <i>d</i> orbitals nor Li 2<i>s</i> orbital because of their large difference in energy. This unhybridized state elevates the energy level to a higher position than the hybridized oxygen bonding states, but lower than the TM anti‐bonding states in the vicinity of the Fermi level. The unique isolated O state with respect to the TM (<i>t</i><sub>2<i>g</i></sub>) non‐bonding (NB) identity facilitates the oxidation of the isolated O electrons to participate in charge compensation.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0023 #chem202203586-bib-0024 #chem202203586-bib-0025"><sup>[23–25]</sup></link> However, it is difficult to directly synthesize Na‐rich layered oxide materials (e.g., Na[Na<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>) due to the large radius difference between Na<sup>+</sup> and TM<sup>n+</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="chem202203586-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203586:chem202203586-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203586-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Band structure and local structure of the oxygen oxidation along Li−O−Li configuration distinguished from Li−O−M configuration in Li‐rich layered or cation‐disordered Li−M oxides. Reproduced with permission.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0026"><sup>[26]</sup></link> Copyright 2018, Wiley‐VCH. Reproduced with permission.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0028"><sup>[28]</sup></link> Copyright 2019, American Chemical Society.</p></caption></figure><p>To date, the mainstream approaches to trigger oxygen redox in sodium deficient oxide materials is constructing Na−O−X configurations, where X includes non‐TM elements without <i>d</i> orbital electrons (Li, Mg, Al), and TM elements with <i>d</i><sup>10</sup> electrons (Cu, Zn). Corresponding works have demonstrated that relatively strong ionic interactions of Li−O, Mg−O, and Al−O generate (quasi) O 2<i>p</i> NB states, so that electrons are fully localized on O, promoting oxygen redox. Previous reports have disclosed that Zn<sup>2+</sup> exhibits a conspicuous intermediate state between systems having strong ionic character and strong covalent interaction with O, due to its higher electronegativity (1.65) than Li (0.98), Na (0.93), Mg (1.31), and the fully filled 3<i>d</i><sup>10</sup> orbital overlapped with O 2<i>p</i> orbitals, which still can make one of them form a quasi‐NB state staying above the rest of TM−O orbitals,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0026"><sup>[26]</sup></link> as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0001">1</link>(b). Besides, in the Cu contained systems, electrons are inclined to delocalize on the more covalent Cu−O bonds, and the largely overlapped Cu 3<i>d</i>−O 2<i>p</i> states ensure the robustness of O frame by suppressing the local distortion of O environment, which realize reversible lattice oxygen redox,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0027"><sup>[27]</sup></link> as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0001">1</link>(c).</p><p>In recent years, introducing intrinsic TM vacancies into the lattice has risen as another achievable approach to logically create oxygen NB states along Na−O−□ (O coordinated with two Na ions, two Mn ions, and two vacancies) and □−O−□ configurations, which can serve as highly ionic sites to stabilize electron holes on O atoms, thus lattice oxygen redox reaction can be preferentially activated along these configurations,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0028"><sup>[28]</sup></link> as shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0001">1</link>(d).</p><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0004"><title type="main">P‐type layered oxide materials</title><p>In 2013, Dahn's group had precisely confirmed vacancy concentrations by Rietveld refinement, Monte Carlo stimulation, density measurements and redox titration, proposing that metal site vacancies can facilitate the reactivity of oxygen in layered cathode materials.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0029"><sup>[29]</sup></link> Inspired by this, Meng's group designed P2‐Na<sub>0.78</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mn<sub>0.69</sub>□<sub>0.08</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with high Na content and TM‐vacancy, which provided a reversible capacity contributed by both cationic and anionic redox reactions. The charge compensation above 4.2 V is originated from oxygen redox, where O<sup>2−</sup> around □ can also participate at the unhybridized O 2<i>p</i> orbital, as seen in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0002">2a</link>.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0030"><sup>[30]</sup></link> Hu and co‐workers successfully prepared Na<sub>0.653</sub>Mn<sub>0.929</sub>O<sub>2</sub> layered oxide with Mn vacancy in TMO<sub>2</sub> subunits, which exhibited a distinct voltage plateau at 4.2 V originated from oxygen redox, delivering a total discharge capacity of 210 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The electronic structures of Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>11/12</sub>□<sub>11/12</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with TM vacancies and Na<sub>2/3</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> without any vacancy are presented in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0002">2b</link>. An increased projected density of states (pDOS) of O 2<i>p</i> orbitals near the Fermi level could be found in the Na−O−□ configuration for Na<sub>0.667</sub>Mn<sub>0.917</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compound with TM vacancies, thus triggering oxygen redox.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0031"><sup>[31]</sup></link> Bai et al.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0032"><sup>[32]</sup></link> reported that charge compensation of the P2‐Na<sub>0.63</sub>[□<sub>0.036</sub>Mg<sub>0.143</sub>Mn<sub>0.82</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> cathode depended on both cationic and anionic redox. They also found that Mg and vacancy provoked two kinds of voltage stage of oxygen redox. When charged above 4.0 V, Na<sub>0.72</sub>[Mg<sub>0.31</sub>Mn<sub>0.69</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> without TM vacancy exhibits an anionic voltage plateau (4.2 V), corresponding to 0.44 Na<sup>+</sup> extracted. However, Na<sub>0.63</sub>[□<sub>0.036</sub>Mg<sub>0.143</sub>Mn<sub>0.82</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> exhibits two voltage plateaus (4.10 and 4.35 V) and the voltage plateau at 4.1 V diminished after the second charging process, which might be ascribed to vacancy removal caused by the local structure distortion and irreversible phase transitions. Thus, the vacancy distribution mode and doped TM ions codetermine the reversibility of anionic redox.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0033"><sup>[33]</sup></link> Yang et al. studied the role of TM vacancy in enhancing the anionic redox in P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Mn<sub>7/9</sub>Mg<sub>1/9</sub>□<sub>1/9</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0002">2c</link>), and tailoring low‐spin Mn<sup>3+</sup> to enhance the structural stability in P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Mn<sub>7/9</sub>Zn<sub>1/9</sub>□<sub>1/9</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, respectively. By DFT calculations and magnetic susceptibility analysis, it becomes unveiled that the presence of the native TM vacancies makes the TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron more flexible against the structural distortion during desodiation.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0034 #chem202203586-bib-0035"><sup>[34,35]</sup></link> Our group created intrinsic vacancies in TM layers of P2‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>[Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> and P2‐Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.13</sub>[Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.07</sub>□<sub>0.13</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> cathode materials by incorporating Mg ions into the sodium layer, thus constructing Na−O−□, Mg−O−□ and □−O−□ configurations, and delivering reversible capacity originated from both the cationic and oxygen redox couples in 1.5–4.5 V.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0036 #chem202203586-bib-0037"><sup>[36,37]</sup></link> Schematic is shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0002">2d</link>. The O 2<i>p</i> NB orbitals that point to native TM vacancies induced reversible anionic redox in the initial stage, which is superior than that along the Na−O−Mg configuration. Based on our works, Liu and co‐workers successively synthesize P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Ca<sub>0.05</sub>[Ni<sub>0.23</sub>□<sub>0.08</sub>Mn<sub>0.69</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, P2‐Na<sub>0.75</sub>Ca<sub>0.05</sub>[Li<sub>0.15</sub>Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.05</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> and P2‐Na<sub>0.75</sub>Ca<sub>0.04</sub>[□<sub>0.03</sub>Li<sub>0.1</sub>Ni<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> compounds, and prove the successfully triggering of oxygen redox owing to the generation of O 2<i>p</i> NB states along the Na−O−□ configuration created by vacancy manufacturing.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0038 #chem202203586-bib-0039 #chem202203586-bib-0040"><sup>[38–40]</sup></link> pDOS and differential charge density results of DFT calculations ascertained that after Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation, O around TM vacancies lost electrons and contributed to the charge compensation, as seen in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0002">2e</link>. Later, Massaro et al. dissected oxygen redox behavior in Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.68</sub>O<sub>2</sub> by calculations, analyzing the formation of vacancies and peroxide species at different desodiation stages. The Mn‐bridged low‐energy intermediate superoxide (O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup>) formed near the Mn vacancy sites at low Na content stage, and further generated molecular O<sub>2</sub> at the deeply desodiation state. Early in 2017, Adamczyk and Pralong had investigated the electrochemical desodiation/sodiation performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> between 1.5 and 3.0 V but neglected its performance at higher voltage range.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0051"><sup>[51]</sup></link> Later, Boisse et al.,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0052"><sup>[52]</sup></link> Wang et al.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0053"><sup>[53]</sup></link> and Li et al.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0054"><sup>[54]</sup></link> successively reported the reversible oxygen redox in Na<sub>2</sub>Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> with negligible voltage hysteresis above 4.0 V. Combined with Rietveld refinement, the information about lattice parameters, atomic position, phase content and stoichiometry of the pristine or cycled electrode materials can be elaborately obtained. In situ XRD is also widely applied to dynamically monitor the structural evolution and phase transition of various electrode materials based on high‐energy synchrotron radiation (SR) source. Due to the advances in brilliance and collimation, SR‐XRD still enables faster imaging maintaining high signal‐to‐noise ratios.</p><p>In 1932, Chadwick et al. firstly discovered neutrons are subatomic particles that interact with the atomic nuclei rather than the electrons.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0077"><sup>[77]</sup></link> ND is sensitive to the light atoms with small atomic radius that are too small to be captured by XRD, such as H (and isotope) and O. The changes of bond length and the site of them can be probed together with neutron pair distribution function (nPDF).<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0078"><sup>[78]</sup></link> Different from XRD whose intensity is proportional to the atomic number, the cross sections of ND are independent of the Z numbers. Atoms with similar Z numbers have different coherent neutron scattering lengths, for example 2.49 and 10.3 femto meters for Co and Ni.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0079"><sup>[79]</sup></link> Therefore, ND is valuable in detecting the positions of light atoms in the lattice and recognizing the positions of elements with similar atomic numbers in multi‐metal oxide materials.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0080"><sup>[80]</sup></link></p><p>Our group used SR‐XRD and ND to precisely determine the content of TM vacancies in P2‐Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.13</sub>[Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.07</sub>□<sub>0.13</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0037"><sup>[37]</sup></link> where Mg ions were implanted into both 2<i>d</i> site of Na<sub><i>e</i></sub> and 2<i>a</i> site of TM layer. Rietveld refinement results of XRD and ND confirmed that vacancies exist in TM layers. Song's group also conducted Rietveld refinements of XRD and ND patterns to accurately unveil the occupancy of V vacancies in V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>: the concentration of V atoms at 12<i>c</i> sites was 94.3 %, containing 5.7 % V vacancies, but no O vacancy was detected at 18<i>e</i> sites, as seen in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0005">5b</link>.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0076"><sup>[76]</sup></link></p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0011"><title type="main">X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) and PDF</title><p>XAS is powerful in investigating the changes of local electronic structure and coordination environment in electrode materials during charging/discharging. Due to the difference in energy regions, XAS can be divided into two parts: X‐ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) with the energy range a little before (10 eV) and after (50 eV) the absorption edge and extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) with the energy range far beyond (50–1000 eV) the absorption edge. XANES is very sensitive to the orbital hybridization (pre‐edge) of the system, oxidation state (edge) of the absorbing atoms and spatial coordination structure (white line) of the adjacent atoms. Based on the single‐electron scattering theory, EXAFS can be used to quantitatively analyze the distance from absorbing atom to the first and second shell atoms (bond length), the coordination number (CN) in these shells, and the degree of ordering. PDF is a total scattering technique, in which peak position and intensity correspond directly to the atom‐atom distances and CNs.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0081"><sup>[81]</sup></link> PDF data are collected at a given distance <i>r</i> from an atom to another one in Fourier transformed real space and refined with crystallographic models, to identify phase ratios and long or short‐range order information.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0082"><sup>[82]</sup></link></p><p>Song et al. obtained a satisfactory result by reducing the TM−TM CN to 5.3, which confirms that vacancies exist in the TM layer, in accordance with the SR‐XRD refinement. In addition, this ordered vacancy model is further manifested by the small‐box least square refinement of nPDF, which proved the presence of 1/7 vacancy in the MnO<sub>2</sub> plane and the relative distorted sodium coordination environments.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0028"><sup>[28]</sup></link> Our group conducted least‐square fitting of Mn and Fe EXAFS in the pristine P2‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>[Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>. The CNs of Mn−TM and Fe−TM are 5.1 and 5.2, respectively, lower than 6 for the standard coordination in the octahedron, which further clarified that vacancies exist in the TM layer.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0037"><sup>[37]</sup></link> Similarly, Nam's group later studied Na<sub>2/3</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and Mn‐vacancy contained Na<sub>2/3</sub>[□<sub>1/6</sub>Mn<sub>5/6</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>. Structural parameters quantitatively evaluated the coordination environment surrounding Mn ions. In Na<sub>2/3</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, CN of the second shell is 6, identical to the theoretical value without Mn vacancy. While for Na<sub>2/3</sub>[□<sub>1/6</sub>Mn<sub>5/6</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, it was determined to be 5.35, confirming vacancies in TM sites,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0042"><sup>[42]</sup></link> as seen in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0005">5c</link>.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0012"><title type="main">Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) and Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR)</title><p>Recently, solid‐state NMR that originates from the isotropic chemical shift of the nuclear spin angular momentum under an external magnetic field, has been developed as a useful tool to probe the electronic structure and ionic dynamics of solid‐state materials. It can be used to discern the presence of cationic vacancies in cation‐deficient anatase<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0083"><sup>[83]</sup></link> or Prussian blue analogues.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0084"><sup>[84]</sup></link> EPR is a magnetic resonance technique that originates from the magnetic moments of unpaired electrons which is usually used to quantitatively analyze the electronic coordination of metal ions or molecules, as well as surface defects. Liu et al. performed EPR measurement and successfully detect the signal associated with Co vacancies center. EPR results also showed how the long‐range order changes in the structure: vacancy gradually decreased as the rise of temperature due to the atomic rearrangement.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0085"><sup>[85]</sup></link></p></section></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0013"><title type="main">Strategies to Harness Oxygen Redox in Vacancy Contained Structures</title><p>Cathode materials for SIBs with TM vacancies induced oxygen redox are often confronted with structure and interphase problems. House et al. pointed out that molecular O<sub>2</sub> formed both at the surface and the bulk of these cathodes. At the surface, O<sub>2</sub> formed, released and immediately densified, which self‐limits further O loss. In the bulk, O<sub>2</sub> were trapped in the vacancy clusters, and this state can be maintained after many cycles with the support of TMO<sub>6</sub> slabs and O<sup>2−</sup>/O<sub>2</sub> redox couple. The repeated formation of trace surface O<sub>2</sub> during charging accounts for the small but continuous voltage decay.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0061"><sup>[61]</sup></link> Profound understanding for the fundamental working mechanisms of defect chemistry and O‐redox induced local structure evolution are of great significance to inform strategies for mitigating the current limitations of such cathodes. Reports exhibited that substituting/doping elements in the appropriate bulk/surface sites can effectively relieve severe vacancy‐induced distortion and suppress TM migration. Meanwhile, surface modification can also be used to suppress O<sub>2</sub> loss. From the aspect of bulk and surface, some recent approaches are stated below.</p><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0014"><title type="main">Pillaring</title><p>In P‐type structure, as the activation of oxygen redox coupled with the evolution of TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron during charging, TM vacancies will indirectly cause in‐plane migration to form vacancy clusters. An effective strategy to prevent this migration is pillaring. Anchored cations like Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Ca<sup>2+</sup>, and K<sup>+</sup> are introduced into the alkali metal layers, which makes the occupation of alkali metal sites by the migrated TM ions less favorable, thus upholding the P‐type stacking framework and preventing the formation O‐type stacking in the alkali metal layers upon cycling.</p><p>Our group successively realized the incorporation of Mg<sup>2+</sup> into Na<sup>+</sup> sites in the Ni−Mn,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0086"><sup>[86]</sup></link> Fe‐Mn<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0036"><sup>[36]</sup></link> and Co‐Mn<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0037"><sup>[37]</sup></link> based binary layered oxide cathodes, and revealed that Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions with higher Pauling electronegativity exhibited larger electrostatic attraction force to the adjacent O, thus serving as pillars to stabilize the vacancy contained structure, as seen in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0006">6a</link>. Huang's group also extended this idea to O3‐type LiCoO<sub>2</sub>, P2‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>5/6</sub>Li<sub>1/4</sub>Mn<sub>3/4</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, prepared Mg‐pillared Li<sub>0.9</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>CoO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>0.25</sub>Mg<sub>0.083</sub>Mn<sub>0.55</sub>Ti<sub>0.117</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>0.773</sub>Mg<sub>0.03</sub>Li<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0087 #chem202203586-bib-0088 #chem202203586-bib-0089"><sup>[87–89]</sup></link> obtained improved lattice stability with suppressed slab sliding at deeply charged state, shown in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0006">6b</link>. Similarly, due to almost the same ionic radii and site‐occupation energy of Ca<sup>2+</sup> as Na<sup>+</sup>, a series of Ca<sup>2+</sup> pillaring strategies have also been conducted, as seen in Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0006">6c</link>. Liu and co‐workers reported three P2‐type cathode materials with TM vacancies and Na‐site Ca<sup>2+</sup> doping to prevent lattice O loss by strong Ca−O interactions.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0038 #chem202203586-bib-0039 #chem202203586-bib-0040"><sup>[38–40]</sup></link> Liu's group and Liang's group are both focused on P2‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, proposed F<sup>−</sup>/Ca<sup>2+</sup> and Mg<sup>2+</sup>/Ca<sup>2+</sup> dual‐doping strategies, respectively, achieving Ca<sup>2+</sup> located in Na layer to suppress O‐type stacking in high voltage region and lead to superior cycling performance.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0090 #chem202203586-bib-0091"><sup>[90,91]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="chem202203586-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem202203586:chem202203586-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem202203586-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Illustration of layered cathode materials with (a, b) Mg<sup>2+</sup>, reproduced with permission.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0036 #chem202203586-bib-0037"><sup>[36,37]</sup></link> Copyright 2021, Wiley‐VCH,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0086"><sup>[86]</sup></link> Copyright 2018, American Chemical Society,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0087"><sup>[87]</sup></link> Copyright 2021, Wiley‐VCH,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0088"><sup>[88]</sup></link> Copyright 2020, Elsevier,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0089"><sup>[89]</sup></link> Copyright 2021, Wiley‐VCH. In Figure <link href="#chem202203586-fig-0006">6e</link>, Zaghib's group and Li's group separately realized a part of K<sup>+</sup> ions remained in the alkali metal layer to form the “pillar‐beam” structure with the formula K<sub>0.08</sub>Na<sub>0−0.74</sub>[Ni<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#chem202203586-bib-0092"><sup>[92]</sup></link> and Na<sub>0.612</sub>K<sub>0.056</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub><link href="#chem202203586-bib-0093"><sup>[93]</sup></link> implemented by electrochemical and thermodynamic methods, respectively. The strengthened TMO<sub>6</sub> subunits and decreased Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy formation energy enable more active Na<sup>+</sup> sites and suppress inferior phase transitions. Zhao et al. also broaden the electrochemical reaction to Prussian blue analogs. The in situ formed K<sup>+</sup>‐modulated structure enables high structural robustness and reversible (de)sodiation process.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0094"><sup>[94]</sup></link></p><p>Besides, some transition metal ions were also manifested to be able to stay at the alkali metal sites to stabilize the layered structure. Kang's team and Zhang's team conducted alkali‐metal site doping by Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Na<sub>0.833</sub>Zn<sub>0.0375</sub>Li<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.7125</sub>O<sub>2</sub><link href="#chem202203586-bib-0095"><sup>[95]</sup></link> and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Zn<sub>0.05</sub>Ni<sub>0.18</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub><link href="#chem202203586-bib-0096"><sup>[96]</sup></link> were synthesized. Zn<sup>2+</sup> ion serves as O<sup>2−</sup>−Zn<sup>2+</sup>−O<sup>2−</sup> pillar to enhance electrostatic attraction between the two adjacent TMO<sub>2</sub> layers, and restrain the generation of O2 phase upon deep desodiation. Zhou's team reported a Na‐site Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinned P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.4</sub>Fe<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which is beneficial to prevent slab sliding and enhance the structural stability.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0097"><sup>[97]</sup></link></p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0015"><title type="main">Surface engineering</title><p>Surface instability is a key issue in oxygen redox based cathode materials. Oxygen loss can damage the electrode particles and lead to serious pulverization and poor capacity retention. From this aspect, surface modifications were taken into consideration. As a well‐known anti‐aging and oxygen‐radicals capturing element, Se was respectively introduced into LiCoO<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0098"><sup>[98]</sup></link> NMC811<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0099"><sup>[99]</sup></link> and Li‐rich Mn‐based oxide<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0100"><sup>[100]</sup></link> to catch the escaped oxygen to prevent it from attacking the electrolyte. Meanwhile, Se with weak electronegativity rearranged the electrons around lattice O and manipulate the molecular polarity within the structure, which benefit O 2<i>p</i>−Mn 3<i>d</i> hybridization and mitigate the lattice O loss.</p><p>Constructing a robust cathode/electrolyte interphase (CEI) also plays important roles in promoting the subsequent ion transportation and improving Coulombic efficiency (CE) of the cathodes.<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0101"><sup>[101]</sup></link> Hu and co‐workers tentatively constructed a NaF‐rich CEI in P2‐Na<sub>0.66</sub>[Li<sub>0.22</sub>Mn<sub>0.78</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#chem202203586-bib-0102"><sup>[102]</sup></link> which can mitigate the local structure degeneration significantly by restraining Li/Mn dissolutions and O<sub>2</sub> release, achieving a highly reversible oxygen redox with CE&gt;99 % after 100 cycles.</p></section></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0016"><title type="main">Summary and Perspective</title><p>In this concept, we overviewed the formation of TM vacancy, vacancy contained configurations for triggering oxygen redox in layered cathode materials of sodium‐ion batteries, as well as advanced characterization techniques for detecting TM vacancy and viable strategies to improve the reversibility of vacancy‐induced oxygen redox.</p><p>Vacancies in transition metal layer, which can be seen as a kind of point defect formed due to the lattice thermal vibration during annealing or ion doping process, is a double‐edged sword for battery cathode materials. One side, introducing vacancies in TM layers facilitates oxygen oxidation and makes it start at lower voltage than in vacancy‐free O‐redox materials. This strategy allows more charge compensation from oxygen and furnishes a substantial increase in energy density. In addition, vacancies in TM layers make TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron more flexible, which can accommodate the volume change and lower the lattice strain during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction and intercalation. The other side, native vacancies (intrinsically belong to lattice defect) plus oxygen redox (usually sluggish in kinetics) would possibly bring detrimental structure problems, such as irreversible phase transition, lattice O release, severe side reactions on the electrode‐electrolyte interface and voltage hysteresis, which accelerate electrochemical performance deterioration. Thus, pillaring and surface modification strategies provide potential directions to solve these problems. In addition, introducing metal ions with more stable electronic structure such as Co<sup>3+</sup>, Fe<sup>2+</sup> (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>3</sup><i>e</i><sub>g</sub><sup>0</sup>) and Sn<sup>4+</sup>, Sb<sup>5+</sup> (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>6</sup><i>e</i><sub>g</sub><sup>4</sup>) around vacancies would be another promising strategy.</p><p>The existing characterization techniques are available to determine the presence, sites, and clustering of TM vacancies, but it is still challenging to quantify the optimum TM vacancy contents, monitor the dynamic evolution of vacancies and gain more insight into the exact local environment, especially the nature of defect chemistry including the cation‐anion distribution and bond distances around the vacancies. Such problems are hardly appreciable using the limited experimental techniques and require the help of computational model, even the modern machine learning. Overall, the construction of TM vacancy in cathode materials for secondary batteries provides a new avenue for harvesting high energy density and high reversibility, and we urgently anticipate that great breakthroughs will be achieved in the future.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0017" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>The work was financially supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (Nos. 52071085, 52101242) and China Postdoctoral Science Foundation (Nos. 2021M690645, 2022T150123).</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0018" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0019" type="openResearch"><section xml:id="chem202203586-sec-0000" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="chem202203586-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="chem202203586-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Armand</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Tarascon</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Nature</journalTitle> <pubYear year="2008">2008</pubYear>, <vol>451</vol>, <pageFirst>652</pageFirst>–<pageLast>657</pageLast>.</citation></bib><bib xml:id="chem202203586-bib-0002"><citation type="journal" xml:id="chem202203586-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Lu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Chen</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Amine</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
