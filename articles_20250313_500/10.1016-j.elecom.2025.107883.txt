                  Due to the open 3D framework structure and relative high capacity, the NASICON type Na3V2(PO4)3 has aroused enormous attention as the cathode material for sodium-ion batteries. However, it still suffers from the toxicity and high cost of vanadium elements, coupled with low electronic conductivity. In this study, we partially substituted V with the environmentally friendly and cost-effective transition element Fe and doped K into the Na site to fabricate a series of Na4-x
                     K
                        x
                     FeV(PO4)3@C composites using a facile sol-gel method. The structural stability, Na+ mobility and electronic conductivity can significant improved by replacing Na+ with K+ and applying a carbon coating. Consequently, the Na3.9K0.1FeV(PO4)3@C electrode delivers a reversible discharge capacity of 83.85 mAh g−1 at 5C after 3000 cycles, with a capacity retention of 91.7 %. It also exhibits an outstanding rate performance with a specific discharge capacity of 83.88 mAh g−1 even at 20.0C. The kinetic analyses and ex-situ characterizations confirm that a small volume change, the pseudocapacitive- dominated sodium storage behavior and highly reversible redox reaction (Fe2+/3+ and V3+/4+) occur during the electrochemical reaction process. Finally, the optimized K-doped NFVP cathode also demonstrates great potential in practical utilization through the evaluation of electrochemical performance for full cells.

In this study, we partially substituted V with the environmentally friendly and cost-effective transition element Fe and doped K into the Na site to fabricate a series of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4-<ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">

Consequently, the Na<ce:inf loc="post">3.9</ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">0.1</ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C electrode delivers a reversible discharge capacity of 83.85 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> at 5C after 3000 cycles, with a capacity retention of 91.7 %. It also exhibits an outstanding rate performance with a specific discharge capacity of 83.88 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> even at 20.0C. The kinetic analyses and ex-situ characterizations confirm that a small volume change, the pseudocapacitive- dominated sodium storage behavior and highly reversible redox reaction (Fe<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup>/<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> and V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup>) occur during the electrochemical reaction process. Finally, the optimized K-doped NFVP cathode also demonstrates great potential in practical utilization through the evaluation of electrochemical performance for full cells.</ce:simple-para>

                  <ce:label>1</ce:label>
                  <ce:section-title id="st0030">Introduction</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="p0030" view="all">In recent years, the swift advancement of sustainable and renewable energy sources spurred the great progress in energy storage technologies [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0040" refid="bb0005 bb0010 bb0015">1–3</ce:cross-refs>]. Because of the rich resources, cost-effectiveness, competitive electrochemical properties, and even geographical distribution of sodium resources, sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) have been viewed as a promising candidate to replace lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) in the realm of large-scale energy storage systems [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0045" refid="bb0020 bb0025 bb0030">4–6</ce:cross-refs>]. However, the development of sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) still faces substantial challenges, primarily stemming from the larger radius of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> (1.02 Å) compared to Li<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> (0.76 Å). It will results in sluggish kinetics and significant volume variations during the sodiation and de-sodiation processes [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0050" refid="bb0035 bb0040 bb0045">7–9</ce:cross-refs>]. Hence, the exploration of advanced electrode materials that exhibit high-energy density, rapid and outstanding reversible Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> insertion and extraction properties holds significant importance. Among the various materials explored for SIBs, NASICON structure Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>(NVP) has garnered significant attention owing to its stable three-dimensional open-framework structure with a fast sodium transport channel and high reversible capacity [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0055" refid="bb0050 bb0055 bb0060 bb0065">10–13</ce:cross-refs>]. However, the toxicity of V elements and their high cost, coupled with the low electronic conductivity of NVP material, will hinder its commercial applications. For this reason, partially or entirely substituting V with the eco-friendly and more affordable transition metals like Co, Ti, Zr, Mn and so on, will be a promising solution [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0060" refid="bb0070 bb0075 bb0080 bb0085 bb0090 bb0095 bb0100">14–20</ce:cross-refs>]. For example, Hu et al. reported free-standing Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>MnV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C fiber membranes prepared via an electrospinning and stepwise sintering process, which could deliver 89.5 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> at 110 mA g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> after 150 cycles with a capacity retention of 91.2 % [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0065" refid="bb0095">19</ce:cross-ref>]. Liu et al. prepared Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>Mn<ce:inf loc="post">0.5</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">0.5</ce:inf>Ti(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@RGO composites through a sol-gel process, which exhibit a capacity of 135.2 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> and maintain 76.2 % of the capacity after 500 cycles [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0070" refid="bb0105">21</ce:cross-ref>].When V was entirely replaced with Ti and Mn, the Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>MnZr(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C- rGO sample can be prepared using a spray-drying strategy. After 1500 cycles, it demonstrates a capacity of 81.2 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> with a retention rate of 97.4 % [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0075" refid="bb0090">18</ce:cross-ref>]. However, introducing these elements into the NVP material also entails some other adverse effects. For instance, the Jahn-Teller effect arising from Mn<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> during the charge and discharge processes will cause structural deterioration and poor cycling performance [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0080" refid="bb0110">22</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0085" refid="bb0115">23</ce:cross-ref>]. Additionally, the Zr element is non-electrochemically active in SIBs, which will decrease the specific capacity of the NVP material. Meanwhile, the relatively low potential plateau (only 2.1 V vs. Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>/Na) originating from the redox couple Ti<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/Ti<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup> limits its utilization in terms of application as the cathode for SIBs. For comparison, substituting V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> with Fe<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> in NVP is one of the ideal choices, as it can generate an appropriate voltage platform around 2.5 V (vs. Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>/Na), deriving from the Fe<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup>/Fe<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> redox reaction [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0090" refid="bb0120">24</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0095" refid="bb0125">25</ce:cross-ref>]. Furthermore, compared to the V element, and even the aforementioned replaced elements, the Fe element is low-cost, abundant on the earth, and eco-friendly. Lu et al. successfully prepared Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C (NFVP) by a ball-milling assisted sol-gel method. After 800 cycles, it still exhibits a high capacity 87.7 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> at 5C [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0100" refid="bb0130">26</ce:cross-ref>]. However, the low ionic diffusion coefficient stemming from large ionic radius and mass of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>, as well as poor electronic conductivity of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>, still needs to be improved to obtain better electrochemical performance. Ionic doping combined with carbon coating is an efficient approach to addressing these challenges, which has been widely utilized in various electrode materials [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0105" refid="bb0135 bb0140 bb0145">27–29</ce:cross-refs>]. For example, Ma et al. successfully prepared Na<ce:inf loc="post">3.9</ce:inf>FeV<ce:inf loc="post">0.9</ce:inf>Zr<ce:inf loc="post">0.1</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C material via substituting V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> with a small amount of Zr<ce:sup loc="post">4+</ce:sup>, and the sample exhibits an outstanding rate performance and remarkable cyclic stability [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0110" refid="bb0150">30</ce:cross-ref>].</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0035" view="all">However, to our knowledge, there are limited studies that have concentrated on doping at Na-sites for the modification of NFVP materials. Due to the larger radius (1.38 Å), K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping at Na-sites can expand the sodium ion transport channels and increase the unit cell volume by substituting a portion of sodium ions, showing a notable improvement in both the rate capability and cycling stability of NASICON-type cathode materials. Cong et al. [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0115" refid="bb0155">31</ce:cross-ref>] reported a Na<ce:inf loc="post">2.9</ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">0.1</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>/C composite by using a lager radius K-doping to enhance the electronic conductivity of the material. Moreover, Shen et al. [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0120" refid="bb0160">32</ce:cross-ref>] reported that the K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> was employed to doped at Na site in Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>V<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>, which promoted Na ion migration, efectively alleviated volume variation and improved structural integrity. Cao et al. [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0125" refid="bb0165">33</ce:cross-ref>] confirmed that the K-doping can improve the structural stability and get high intercalation- deintercalation kinetic of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> for Na<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>Fe<ce:inf loc="post">2</ce:inf>(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>. In many different cathode materials, K-doped method is a significant way to improve the cycle stability and rate capability performance of cathode materials in SIBs. Motivating by the above researches, in this work, a series of low-cost and low-toxicity Na<ce:inf loc="post">4-<ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">
                        <ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C (<ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.15) composites were synthesized by using a facile sol-gel method followed a high-temperature calcination process. The influence of K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping on the structural characteristics, morphological features, and electrochemical properties was investigated. Furthermore, we also systematically evaluated the influence of K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping, combined with carbon coating, on the electronic conductivity and Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> diffusion in NFVP materials. From these analyses, K-doping significantly broaden the Na ion transport channels, enhances the Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> diffusion coefficient, stabilizes the structure, and thus improves the electrochemical performance for SIBs.</ce:para>

                  <ce:para id="p0055" view="all">Na<ce:inf loc="post">4-<ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">
                        <ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C(<ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.15) composites were characterized using XRD technique to investigate the impact of K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping on their structural properties. As shown in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0130" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(a)<ce:float-anchor refid="f0005"/>, all the diffraction peaks align well with the standard XRD spectrum of rhombohedral NASICON structure with a R-3c space group [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0135" refid="bb0130">26</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0140" refid="bb0170">34</ce:cross-ref>]. Meanwhile, no impurity phases corresponding to diffraction peaks are detected. It suggests that low-content K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping does not change the structure of the materials. Moreover, compared with the pristine sample, as the doping amount of K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> increases, the position of the enlarged (200) peaks shifts progressively to lower angles (in the inset of <ce:cross-ref id="cf0145" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(a)), suggesting an expansion of the lattice constant and confirming the successful incorporation of K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> into the structure. The enlargement of the lattice constant after K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping can broaden the Na ion transport channels and facilitate the diffusion of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> ions, thereby enhancing the electrochemical properties. The XPS spectra of the selected <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 samples were used to elucidate the chemical compositions and valence states of elements for the material surface. The Na1s, Fe2p, V2p, P2p, O1s, and C1s signals are detected in the XPS survey spectra of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 samples (<ce:cross-ref id="cf0150" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(b)), while the K2p signals are only observed in <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample(<ce:cross-ref id="cf0155" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(c)). Although the K2p XPS peak is not obvious causing by the low content of K, it is significantly different from that of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0. The results indicate that the K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> has been successfully introduced into the NVFP, which is consistent with the EDS mapping testing results. <ce:cross-ref id="cf0160" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(d)-(f) and Fig. S1(a)-(c) show the high-resolution XPS spectra of Fe2p, V2p, C1s for the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 samples. As can be seen, both samples exhibit similar valence states. The Fe 2p XPS spectrum (<ce:cross-ref id="cf0165" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(d)) can be resolved into four peaks: those located at about 710.8 eV (Fe 2p3/2) and 723.8 eV (Fe 2p1/2) correspond to Fe<ce:sup loc="post">2+</ce:sup> species, while the peaks at 713.7 eV (Fe 2p3/2) and 727.2 eV (Fe 2p1/2) correspond to Fe<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> species. Additionally, the other two peaks, situated at 719.5 and 731.0 eV, are satellite peaks [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0170" refid="bb0175">35</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0175" refid="bb0180">36</ce:cross-ref>]. The XPS results reveal that Fe exhibits a mixed valence state, which can be ascribed to unavoidable oxidation during the calcination process for <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample, and this is in agreement with previous reports [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0180" refid="bb0170">34</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0185" refid="bb0185">37</ce:cross-ref>]. The characteristic peaks of V 2p spectrum (<ce:cross-ref id="cf0190" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(e)) can be deconvoluted into two major peaks located at 517.08 and 523.98 eV, which can be assigned to the V 2p3/2 and V 2p1/2, respectively, corresponding to the V<ce:sup loc="post">3+</ce:sup> species. An additional weak peak at 520.4 eV can be attributed to shake-up satellite peak of the V 2p spectrum [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0195" refid="bb0190">38</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0200" refid="bb0195">39</ce:cross-ref>]. <ce:cross-ref id="cf0205" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(f) displays the C 1 s spectrum, which consists of three convoluted peaks centered at 284.8 eV, 286.3 eV and 288.7 eV, corresponding to the C<ce:glyph name="sbnd"/>C bond, C<ce:glyph name="sbnd"/>O bond, and O-C=O bond, respectively [<ce:cross-refs id="cf0210" refid="bb0200 bb0205 bb0210">40–42</ce:cross-refs>]. The specific surface areas of the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 composites are 48.8, 60.8, 83.1 and 54.2 m<ce:sup loc="post">2</ce:sup> g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, as shown in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0215" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(g) and Fig. S2(a)-(c). Moreover, pore size distributions are displayed in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0220" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(h) and Fig. S2(d)-(f), the average average pore diameter of the of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 samples are 3.26, 3.65, 3.78 and 3.70 nm, respectively. From the results, it can be seen that the BET surface area of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample is the largest among the four samples and it is approximately 1.7 times that of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 sample. The high surface area and the abundant mesoporous not only guarantees the high availability of active sites but also promote electrolyte permeation, which facilitates the diffusion of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> ions [<ce:cross-ref id="cf0225" refid="bb0215">43</ce:cross-ref>,<ce:cross-ref id="cf0230" refid="bb0220">44</ce:cross-ref>]. <ce:cross-ref id="cf0235" refid="f0005">Fig. 1</ce:cross-ref>(i) shows the TGA curves, a minor weight loss from room temperature to 200 °C can be attributed to the evaporation of physically absorbed water. Subsequently, the period of weight loss between 200 °C and 450 °C corresponds to carbon combustion. Finally, due to the oxidation of V(III) and Fe(II) to higher valence states and the absorption of oxygen, the curves show a slight increase between 450 °C and 700 °C. The carbon contents of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 composites can be calculated as about 13.3 wt%, 13.5 wt%, 12.7 wt% and 15.6 wt%, respectively. The appropriate amount of coated carbon can effectively enhance the electronic conductivity of materials and then and achieve improved electrochemical performance.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0060" view="all">To investigate the effects of K doping on morphological features, SEM measurements were conducted on the four samples. As seen, the low-magnification of the SEM images of the composites with <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05, and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 exhibit obvious differences in morphologies, as shown in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0240" refid="f0010">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>(a), <ce:cross-ref id="cf0245" refid="f0010">2</ce:cross-ref>(d)<ce:float-anchor refid="f0010"/>, Fig. S3(a) and S3(c), respectively. However, such aggregation has been significantly suppressed in the K-doped samples. As the level of K doping rises, the extent of particle aggregation initially diminishes and then increases. Specially, among these samples, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample displays a relatively uniform and smaller particle size, ranging from tens of nanometers to a few micrometers. <ce:cross-ref id="cf0250" refid="f0010">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>(b), <ce:cross-ref id="cf0255" refid="f0010">2</ce:cross-ref>(e), Fig. S3(b), S3(d) show the high magnification of the SEM images of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 composites. As can be seen, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 sample exhibits a smooth surface formed by the close packing of particles, while the K-doped samples show porous and loose surface morphologies. The high-resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of the selected <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 composites, shown in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0260" refid="f0010">Fig. 2</ce:cross-ref>(c) and (f), suggest that a uniform amorphous carbon coating layer with a thickness of approximately 7 nm thickness was continuously anchored on the surface of NFVP. From the results, the decreased particle size, porous and loose surface morphologies, as well as larger spacing of lattice fringes in K-doped samples, facilitate the diffusion and transport of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> within the electrode.</ce:para>

These observations align well with the findings from the cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements. With the increase in the doping amount of K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup>, the capacity of K-doped NFVP materials first increased and then declined. Specifically, the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample displays the highest capacity of 109.9 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> during the first discharge process, while the values of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05, and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 sample are 101.7 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, 104.6 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, and 102.6 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, respectively. Moreover, Fig. S6(d) and Table S1 shows the gap in potential plateau between charge and discharge for the four electrodes. As seen, the potential gap for the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, 0.05 and 0.15 samples are 0.05, 0.03, and 0.04 V respectively. The <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 electrode demonstrates the smallest potential difference (0.02 V), implying the enhanced redox kinetics.</ce:para>
                  <ce:para id="p0070" view="all">The long-term cycling capability of the Na<ce:inf loc="post">4-<ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">
                        <ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C were tested at 5C in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0295" refid="f0015">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>(c). As shown, compared to the pristine sample (<ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0), the K-doped samples present improved cycling capability, and the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample exhibits the best electrochemical performance. In detail, the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 composites deliver reversible discharge capacities of 50.54, 68.65 and 66.26 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> after 2500 cycles and the corresponding capacity retention rates are 65.5 %, 78.3 % and 76.8 %, respectively (based on the first discharge capacities of 77.16, 87.68 and 86.23 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> for <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 electrodes). The <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 composite can deliver reversible discharge capacity of 83.85 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> after 3000 cycles with a capacity retention of 91.7 % (based on the first discharge capacity of 91.43 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>). Correspondingly, the charge/discharge curves of all Na<ce:inf loc="post">4-<ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">
                        <ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> @C electrodes during the selected cycles are shown in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0300" refid="f0015">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>(d) and Fig. S7(a)-(c). As the number of cycles increases, the discharge/charge plateau <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 sample cannot be found obviously. However, the K-doped NFVP samples still remain the obvious potential plateau. Especially, the potential curves from 100th to 800th cycle for the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 electrode overlapped well, suggesting a relatively stable cycling performance. In addition to their exceptional cycling performance, the K-doped electrodes exhibit enhanced rate capacities compared to the pristine NFVP (<ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0). The rate performance of all the electrodes was measured at varying current densities, ranging from 0.2C to 20.0C, and then recovered 0.2C (in <ce:cross-ref id="cf0305" refid="f0015">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>(e)). The rate capacity testing was conducted for eight cycles at each C-rate. Obviously, the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 electrode possesses the best rate capability, delivering average discharge capacities of 109.85, 105.52, 102.22, 98.82, 93.98 and 88.50 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> at the rates of 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0 and 10.0C, respectively. Conversely, the corresponding values for the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0 electrode are only 100.06, 89.25, 85.22, 78.69, 68.08 and 56.63 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>. Remarkably, the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample still exhibits 83.88 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, even at 20.0C. However the values for <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.05 and <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.15 electrodes are only 39.15, 64.58 and 58.65 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>, respectively. Upon the current rate decreasing back to 0.2C, the reversible discharge capacity of 108.25 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> can be obtained for the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 electrode, which is about 98.5 % of its initial value (109.94 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>), demonstrating the good reversibility even after many cycles at high C-rate. For comparison, the ratio values of <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, 0.05 and 0.15 are only 90.1 %, 92.4 % and 91.8 %, respectively. <ce:cross-ref id="cf0310" refid="f0015">Fig. 3</ce:cross-ref>(f) and Fig. S8(a)-(c) exhibit the corresponding galvanostatic charge and discharge curves at 0.2C - 20.0C. Compared to the other three samples, we can see that the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 electrode still shows the two obvious voltage plateaus even at the high rates, and the curves are also well overlapped. In summary, compared to the pristine NFVP material, the K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doped materials, especially the <ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0.1 sample, exhibit a significant enhanced electrochemical performance. This can be attributed to the following three aspects: (1) The nano/μm-sized particles of the K-doped NFVP can increase the area of electrode/electrolyte contact and reduce the diffusion distance for Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> ions; (2) The uniform carbon layer coated on the surface of particles can effectively improve the electronic conductivity of the material, buffer the volume expansion and maintain a stable structure during the charge and discharge processes; (3) The expanded lattice caused by K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> doping can promote the diffusion of Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> ions in the lattice, and the K<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> in the NFVP also acts as a pillar to stabilize the host structure and suppress the volume change during the desodiation/sodiation process, thereby presenting an enhanced electrochemical performance.</ce:para>

                  <ce:section-title id="st0060">Conclusions</ce:section-title>
                  <ce:para id="p0105" view="all">In this work, a series of Na<ce:inf loc="post">4-<ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">
                        <ce:italic>x</ce:italic>
                     </ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C(<ce:italic>x</ce:italic> = 0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.15) composites were synthesized via a facile sol-gel method followed by a high-temperature calcination process. The Na<ce:inf loc="post">3.9</ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">0.1</ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C composite delivers a reversible discharge capacity of 83.85 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> at 5C after 3000 cycles, with a capacity retention of 91.7 %. In addition, the Na<ce:inf loc="post">3.9</ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">0.1</ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf>@C electrode also presents an outstanding rate performance, with a discharge specific capacity of 83.88 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup> even at 20.0C. The outstanding electrochemical performance of the Na<ce:inf loc="post">3.9</ce:inf>K<ce:inf loc="post">0.1</ce:inf>FeV(PO<ce:inf loc="post">4</ce:inf>)<ce:inf loc="post">3</ce:inf> @C electrode is attributed to its expanded Na ion transport channels, the improvement of structural stability, enhanced Na<ce:sup loc="post">+</ce:sup> diffusion coefficient, and lower charge resistance through K-doping, which can be confirmed by the (ex situ) XRD, GITT and EIS measurements. Moreover, the various sweep rate CV measurements are also confirmed the pseudocapacitive-dominated sodium storage behavior, which leads to enhanced electrochemical kinetics. Meanwhile, the as-fabricated K-NFVP//HC full cell demonstrates a high reversible specific capacity (90.38 mAh g<ce:sup loc="post">−1</ce:sup>) and good cycling performance. This work provides a facile and economical approach for NFVP modifications, it is believed that the combinations of high performance, low-cost and low-toxicity make the NFVP cathode a strong competitor for large-scale SIBs cathodes. In addition, to further achieve the practical application of NFVP cathodes in the large-scale storage field, high safety and electrochemical performance across a wide temperature range are also key focuses of future research.</ce:para>
