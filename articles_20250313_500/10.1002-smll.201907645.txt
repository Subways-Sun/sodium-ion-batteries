Isostructural and multivalent anion substitution combined with carbon coating is proposed to greatly improve the electrochemical properties of phosphate cathode in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). Specifically, multivalent tetrahedral SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitute for PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> in Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) lattice, preparing the optimal Na<sub>3.1</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2.9</sub>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>0.1</sub> with high‐rate capability (delivering a high capacity of 82.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at 20 C) and outstanding cyclic stability (<i>≈</i>98% capacity retention after 500 cycles at 1 C). Theoretical calculation and experimental analyses reveal that the anion‐substituted Na<sub>3.1</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2.9</sub>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>0.1</sub> reduces the bandgap of NVP lattice and enhanced its structural stability, Na<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion kinetics and electronic conductivity. This strategy of multivalent and isostructural anion substitution chemistry provides a new insight to develop advanced phosphate cathodes.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="smll201907645-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Na<sub>3+</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<i><sub>x</sub></i> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 0.15) cathode materials are prepared via substitution of the inactive PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> sites in Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with isostructural SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> anions. The substitution effects on crystal structure, electrochemical properties, Na<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion kinetics and electronic conductivity are systematically investigated. Multivalent and isostructural anionic substitution provides a new strategy for designing polyanionic materials of sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201907645:smll201907645-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201907645-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="smll201907645-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the rapidly growing demands of grid‐scale energy storage, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted huge attention and shown great promise to blossom out into next‐generation low‐cost battery system, owing to the high abundant and widespread geographical distribution of sodium resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to fabricate high‐performance SIBs, advanced cathode materials are one of the bottlenecks, compared to a variety of anode candidates with high Na‐storage capacity. Due to the large Na<sup>+</sup> radius and therefore sluggish diffusion kinetics, development of cathode materials with fast Na<sup>+</sup>‐transport kinetics is of great significance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In comparison to other cathodes including layered transition‐metal oxides<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Prussian blue analogues,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> polyanion‐type phosphates are one superior candidate owing to the Na‐super‐ionic conductor (NASICON) structure composed of stable and 3D open framework connected by MO<sub>6</sub> octahedra (M: transition metals) and PO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the NASICON materials, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) is one promising star cathode that exhibits high application prospects and has been studied extensively, due to the ultrafast 3D Na<sup>+</sup> transport tunnels and high lattice stability during the successive Na<sup>+</sup>‐intercalation/extraction with a theoretical specific capacity of 117 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and working voltage of 3.3–3.4 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the intrinsically poor electronic conductivity of lattice retards its application especially for high‐rate working, which is one of the main challenges for NVP cathode.</p><p>In order to improve the electrochemical properties (especially the rate performance) of NVP cathode, the strategies proposed in recent reports mainly include: i) the nanosizing of particles and/or morphology optimization,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> ii) coating or mixing with highly conductive carbonaceous materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0006 #smll201907645-bib-0008"><sup>6a,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and iii) foreign ion doping or substitution at different lattice sites.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> While nanometer and morphology controls are realized usually in the high‐cost processes, carbon mixing at high content and ionic doping at the active sites of V<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0009 #smll201907645-bib-0010"><sup>9a,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Na<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0009"><sup>9b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> are at the expense of reducing the theoretical capacity, although the capacity decrease is relatively low at low carbon content coating. By comparison, substitution at the inactive anion (PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>) sites can regulate and improve the electrochemical properties of phosphate cathode without the reduction and even with the increase of theoretical capacity. For example, F‐substituted V‐based NASICON cathodes, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2−2</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>F<sub>1+2</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1), deliver higher specific capacity and better rate performance than the maternal material of NVP.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In comparison to the monoatomic anion (F<sup>−</sup>) substitution, the polyatomic anions (multivalent anions) substitution should be more effective and easier to achieve the precisely regulated preparation due to the more similar anionic structure. For example, Cui and co‐workers introduced BO<sub>3</sub><sup>3−</sup> into NVP, preparing the B‐substituted Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) with new bonding states and optimizing crystal structure as enhanced cathode with fast charge‐discharge capability and cyclic stability for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0009"><sup>9c</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In the polyanion‐type NASICON materials, the commonly used anion building blocks including PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> BO<sub>3</sub><sup>3−</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0005 #smll201907645-bib-0015"><sup>5b,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> While the ionic structure of BO<sub>3</sub><sup>3−</sup> is triangle, the other three are tetrahedron, implying that the isostructural SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> or SO<sub>4</sub><sup>2−</sup> substitution for PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> may be much better than BO<sub>3</sub><sup>3−</sup> due to the structural compatibility. Herein, we modify the NVP cathode through substituting PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> with isostructural SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> to prepare Na<sub>3+</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<i><sub>x</sub></i> (NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i>, 0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 0.15) materials. A series of studies on NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15) are executed to indicate the evolutions of crystal structure, electrochemical properties and Na<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion kinetics along with the change of <i>x</i> values when used as cathode in SIBs. Due to the relatively larger ionic radius of SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> than PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>, the more stable Na<sub>3+</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<i><sub>x</sub></i> lattices can effectively improve Na<sup>+</sup>‐intercalation/extraction performance, particularly the high‐rate capability and long‐term cyclic stability. The optimal Na<sub>3.1</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2.9</sub>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>0.1</sub> (NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub>) remarkably enhances the specific capacity (109.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C) and rate performance (82.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C), both of which are distinctly larger than the values from the NVP control (viz, 99 and 49.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 and 20 C, respectively). Moreover, NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> has a longer cyclic stability, for example, delivering a capacity retention of 98% even after 500 cycles at 1 C. Such a much‐enhanced electrochemical performance of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> should benefit from the decreasing bandgap of NVP lattice and the enhancement of structural stability, Na<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion kinetics and electronic conductivity, which is demonstrated by the studies of DFT calculation, UV–vis absorption spectra, cyclic voltammetry (CV) scanned at different rates, galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT), electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and electrical conductivity tests. Furthermore, coupled with carbon cloth (CC) anode, the CC//NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> Na‐ion full cells are fabricated, exhibiting excellent energy‐storage properties (e.g., keeping a high capacity retention up to 96% with the reversible capacity of 92.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 1 C).</p></section><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Through the sol−gel preparation method followed by a high‐temperature calcination under inert Ar atmosphere, a series of NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15) are obtained successfully. The experimental details are shown in the Supporting Information. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns in Figure S1, Supporting Information show that, all samples of NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> with various amount of SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> own the similar diffraction patterns, which can be well indexed to rhombohedral structure of NVP with <i>R‐</i>3<i>c</i> space group (JCPDS card No. 62‐0345).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0006"><sup>6a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Bragg peaks of any impurities or other structures are unobservable in all samples, demonstrating the favorable maintenance of NVP‐type lattice after SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution without the formation of crystal impurities. Rietveld refinements were further performed to investigate the specific effect of SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution on the lattice parameters and size (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a–c</link>). Note that, only NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> is compared with the unsubstituted NVP because of its optimal electrochemical properties as revealed below. A rhombohedral unit cell with <i>R‐</i>3<i>c</i> space group was used as the refinement model, and its validity was considered to be reliable due to the low <i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> values (8.94 and 8.21 for NVP and NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub>, respectively). The obtained crystallographic data listed in Tables S1–S3, Supporting Information show that, the cell parameter c of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> is 21.8256 Å, which is obviously larger than 21.8233 Å of NVP. Conversely, the a (= b) value of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> (8.7312 Å) is slightly smaller than that (8.7321 Å) of NVP. Cell volumes of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> and NVP are 1441.949 Å<sup>3</sup> and 1441.085 Å<sup>3</sup>, respectively, demonstrating the substitution‐induced expansion of crystal lattice along with the enlargement in <i>c</i> direction and reduction in <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> directions, which is due to larger Si<sup>4+</sup> radius (0.24 Å) than P<sup>5+</sup> (0.17 Å).<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the occupancy of Na atoms has changed distinctly (Tables S2 and S3, Supporting Information). As is well‐known to us, there are two different Na sites, viz, the immobile Na(1) and the active Na(2) with extraction/intercalation capability during the charging/discharging.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0008"><sup>8a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution, the occupancy ratio at Na(2) site increases from 0.7090 to 0.7395, implying the increase of active Na<sup>+</sup> and hence accounting for the higher Na‐storage capacity as charging and discharging. One intrinsic shortcoming of NVP‐based cathode is its poor electronic conductivity due to the high electronegativity of phosphorus, strong covalency of P–O bonds, and the separation of metal‐oxygen polyhedrons in NVP‐based lattice. In comparison, the electronegativity of silicon is 1.90, which is smaller than 2.19 for phosphorus. Meanwhile, the strength of SiO bond is stronger than that of PO bond.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a result, structural changes induced by SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution may widen the transport channels of ions, raise electronic conductivity, shorten ion transport time, and stabilize the crystal structure during Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/extraction, making NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> deliver improved energy‐storage properties than NVP in SIBs as revealed below.</p><figure xml:id="smll201907645-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:16136810:media:smll201907645:smll201907645-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/smll201907645-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>XRD Rietveld refinement results of a) NVP and b) NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub>; c) schematic illustration for the change of crystal structure from NVP to NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub>; d) FTIR spectra of NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15) samples; high‐resolution Si 2p XPS spectra of e) NVP and f) NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub>.</caption></figure><p>Studies of Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra were carried out to verify the SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution in NASICON lattice. As shown in Figure <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0001">1</link>d, all FTIR spectra of NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15) own the similar characteristic peaks, including the asymmetric stretching vibration (ν<sub>as</sub>) of PO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedrons located at 950–1200 cm<sup>−1</sup>, bending vibration (ν<sub>b</sub>) of PO bonds at 578 cm<sup>−1</sup> and V–O stretching vibration (ν<sub>s</sub>) appearing at 630 cm<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0011"><sup>11b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, compared with typical chemical bonds of NVP, there is an extra characteristic peak at 543 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which originates from the ν<sub>b</sub> of SiO bonds.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> With the content increase of SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution, the vibrational peak at ≈860–1200 cm<sup>−1</sup> becomes broader and broader, due to the overlapping of peaks from SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> at 860–1200 cm<sup>−1</sup> and PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> at 950–1200 cm<sup>−1</sup>. The signals associated with the SiO bonds also indicate that SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> has been successfully introduced into the NVP lattice. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) tests were implemented to further characterize the change of elemental composition after SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution. As shown in Figure S2a,b, Supporting Information, compared with the XPS full spectrum of NVP, an additional peak is presented in NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0001">1e,f</link>). Deconvolution results for the high‐resolution Si 2p XPS spectrum of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0001">1f</link>) shows that, the evident peaks at 102.05 and 103.34 eV are assigned to Si 2p 3/2 and Si 2p1/2, respectively, corresponding to the tetravalent silicon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#smll201907645-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a result, both the FTIR and XPS tests indicate the achievement of SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution in NVP, which is well coincident with the above XRD refinement results.</p><p>Scanning electron microscope (SEM) and transmission electron microscope (TEM) were carried out to observe the changes of size and morphology of NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> at different <i>x</i> values of 0, 0.05, 0.10, and 0.15 (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>; Figures S3 and S4, Supporting Information). The SEM images show that, all samples exhibit similar shapes consisted of the irregular particles and aggregates. Although the SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup>substitution has not changed the overall appearance of materials particles, the particle size obviously decreases with the increase of the SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution content. For example, the size of NVP particle is about 5 µm (Figure <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0002">2a</link>), which decreases to about 1–2 µm of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> (Figure <link href="#smll201907645-fig-0002">2b</link>) and then to 300–500 nm of NVPSi<sub>0.15</sub> (Figure S3b, Supporting Information). A series of NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.05, 0.10, 0.15) materials have been synthesized. Experimental and theoretical researches are conducted to explore the evolutions of crystal structure, electrochemical properties and Na<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion kinetics after introducing SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> to NVP material. The XRD refinement results indicate the occupation of active Na(2) sites increases and the change of lattice parameters makes the crystal structure more stable. By FTIR spectrums and XPS, the aim of SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substitution into NVP lattice has achieved. SiO<sub>4</sub><sup>4−</sup> substituting NVPSi<i><sub>x</sub></i> significantly enhances electrochemical performances especially the NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> material, which exhibits superior rate and cycling performance. The NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> material delivers 109.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> discharge specific capacity at 0.2 C, even at 20 C the discharge specific capacity can still reach 82.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The capacity retention of NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> material can reach 98%, which is higher than that of pristine material NVP (75%) after 500 cycles at 1 C. Confirmed from CV with various scanning rates and GITT, the improved Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics properties prompt the better electrochemical performances. DOS calculation combined with UV–vis absorption spectra analysis, four‐point probe method, EIS proves promotional interior electronic conductivity. Assembled with CC anode, the NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub>//CC full cell manifests outstanding rate and cycling performance, indicating the practicability of the NVPSi<sub>0.1</sub> material. Researches on multivalent and isostructural anion substitution chemistry open a new view to design advanced polyanion‐type cathode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Sample Preparation</title><p>The NASICON structured Na<sub>3+</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<i><sub>x</sub></i> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 0.15) materials were synthesized by a feasible sol–gel process. NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>, NH<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, Si(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>4</sub>, and citric acid were used as raw materials. Typically, NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub> was dispersed in deionized water, and then citric acid was added into it, where the molar ratio of vanadium to citric is 1:0.8 in order to ensure full chelation with metal ions and the dropping speed is essential to the formation of gels. The solution was maintaining at 80 °C with violently stirring to obtain a transparent solution. And then stoichiometric amount of NH<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, and Si(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>4</sub> were added into the above solution while stirring. When deionized water was evaporated at 80 °C, sol was converted into gel. The wet gel was dried at 120 °C for 5 h in a vacuum oven to form dry gel. The dry gel was heat‐treated at 400 °C for 5 h under Ar atmosphere. After cooling to room temperature, the precursor was slightly grounded. At last, the powder was annealed at 800 °C for 12 h under Ar atmosphere to obtain the final compound Na<sub>3+</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>(SiO<sub>4</sub>)<i><sub>x</sub></i>.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>All as‐prepared power samples were characterized by XRD (Rigaku SmartLab) using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) in the scan range (2θ) of 5–60° to analyze the crystal structures. General Structure Analysis System was employed to refine XRD data testing by Dmax2200PC. VESTA was used to simulate the crystal structure model. Using SEM (Hitachi SU 8000), the size and morphology of synthesized material were explored. The images of TEM were taken on JEQL‐2100F. The FT‐IR spectrum (Nicolet 6700) was used to elucidate the chemical bond of the samples. XPS (ESCABMKLL) was carried out to test elemental composition. The ultraviolet absorption spectrums were performed by Cary7000 UV–vis‐NIR Spectrophotometer.</p></section><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurement</title><p>The electrodes were composed of 70 wt% active material, 20 wt% acetylene black and 10 wt% sodium carboxymethylcellulose. Then mixing them with deionized water, the slurry was coated on Al foil. The electrodes were dried at 60 °C for 10 h in vacuum oven. The coin cells (CR2032) were assembled in an argon‐filled atmosphere glovebox. The composition of the electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in the solvent of propylene carbonate with 5 vol% fluoroethylene carbonate as the additive and glassfiber was used as the separator. The Neware testing system was used to perform galvanostatic charge/discharge tests at the voltage range from 2.3V–3.9 V. CHI660E electrochemical workstation was performed to test cyclic voltammetry of different scanning rates. GITT was measured by Arbin battery tester in the same voltage window at 0.035 C for 10 min, following by 2 h relaxation.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0100" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (91963118), and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (2412019ZD010).</p></section><section xml:id="smll201907645-sec-0110" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="smll201907645-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="smll201907645-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="smll201907645-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>C.</givenNames> <familyName>Vaalma</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>D.</givenNames> <familyName>Buchholz</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Weil</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Passerini</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Nat.
