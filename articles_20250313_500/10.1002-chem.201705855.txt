Constructing heterostructures has been considered to be an effective approach to enhance charge transfer and ion‐diffusion kinetics. In this work, composites of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures with reduced graphene oxide nanosheets were synthesized by a facile one‐pot hydrothermal method. When applied as anode material in sodium‐ion batteries, the composite showed a high reversible capacity of 642 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and good cyclic stability without capacity loss in 100 cycles. In particular, SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures exhibited outstanding rate performance with capacities of 593 and 567 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at high current densities of 2 and 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, which could be ascribed to the dramatically improved Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics and electrical conductivity.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Heterostructured electrode</b>: Composites of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures with reduced graphene oxide (rGO) nanosheets were synthesized by a facile one‐pot hydrothermal method. The SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures show fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics, and the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composites exhibit superior rate performance when used as anodes for sodium‐ion batteries (see figure).<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201705855-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="chem201705855-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), as a promising alternative to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), have been attracting extensive investigations because of low‐cost and widespread sodium resources and similar operating mechanisms to LIBs. Some researchers have directly applied electrode materials for LIBs in SIBs; however, most of the anode materials suitable for LIBs have poor electrochemical properties in SIBs. For example, graphite has been widely used as anode material for LIBs, but shows nearly no activity for sodium‐ion storage. Thus, it is important to develop appropriate anode materials with high capacity and good rate performance for SIBs. Many anode materials have been investigated, including hard carbon, alloys (involving P, Sn, Sb, etc.), and transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs, e.g., MoS<sub>2</sub>, WS<sub>2</sub>, MoSe<sub>2</sub>).<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0001 #chem201705855-bib-0002 #chem201705855-bib-0003 #chem201705855-bib-0004 #chem201705855-bib-0005"/> Hard carbon can achieve a specific practical capacity of approximately 300 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a low operating potential (&lt;0.1 V) through absorption/desorption of sodium ions, but its unsatisfactory reversibility needs to be solved.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0003 #chem201705855-bib-0006 #chem201705855-bib-0007 #chem201705855-bib-0008"/> Compared with hard carbon, alloy anode compounds have potentially much higher theoretical capacities (elemental values are 2600 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for P, 847 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for Sn, and 660 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for Sb), originating from the alloying reactions between the constituent element and sodium. However, alloys of these elements suffer from extremely large volume changes during the alloying/dealloying processes, which is the main reason for their poor stability.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0004 #chem201705855-bib-0009"/></p><p>Recently, TMDs have attracted increasing attention, owing to their fast electron‐ and ion‐transfer kinetics on two‐dimensional sheets as well as high theoretical capacities. Taking MoS<sub>2</sub> as an example, the process of Na<sup>+</sup> storage in MoS<sub>2</sub> involves a four‐electron‐transfer conversion reaction [Eq. <link href="#chem201705855-disp-0001"/>]:<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0010"/><displayedItem xml:id="chem201705855-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/chem201705855-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><p>This reaction is different from both absorption in hard carbon and alloying reactions of alloy materials.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0011 #chem201705855-bib-0012 #chem201705855-bib-0013"/> SnS<sub>2</sub>, which combines both conversion and alloying reactions, exhibits an even higher specific theoretical capacity of up to 1137 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> compared to MoS<sub>2</sub> or Sn, follows the mechanism detailed below [Eqs. <link href="#chem201705855-disp-0002 #chem201705855-disp-0003"/>]:<displayedItem xml:id="chem201705855-disp-0002" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/chem201705855-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-math-0002"/></displayedItem><displayedItem xml:id="chem201705855-disp-0003" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/chem201705855-math-0003.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-math-0003"/></displayedItem></p><p>Compared with elemental tin and other tin‐based compounds, SnS<sub>2</sub> has the best buffering capability (large interlayer spacing <i>c=</i>0.5899 nm) for the huge volume changes in alloying processes.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0014 #chem201705855-bib-0015 #chem201705855-bib-0016"/> However, the large <i>c</i>‐direction atomic spacing weakens the interlayer van der Waals interaction, which results in poor electron transportation from sheet to sheet and finally causes remarkable capacity loss and inferior rate capability.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0017 #chem201705855-bib-0018"/> Recently, a number of works have focused on improving the electrical conductivity of SnS<sub>2</sub> by combining it with conductive carbonaceous materials such as reduced graphene oxide (rGO) and carbon nanotubes (CNTs).<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0019 #chem201705855-bib-0020 #chem201705855-bib-0021 #chem201705855-bib-0022 #chem201705855-bib-0023 #chem201705855-bib-0024 #chem201705855-bib-0025"/> Zhang et al. reported a facile solvothermal route to grow few‐layered SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates on few‐layered rGO sheets with excellent Na‐storage properties. They reported a high reversible capacity of 649 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a stable cyclic life with 61 % capacity retention at a high current density of 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles, and good rate performance.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0019"/></p><p>Another effective approach to enhance electrical and ionic conductivity of SnS<sub>2</sub> is to construct heterostructures to reduce ion‐diffusion resistance and promote interfacial electron transport due to the existence of a micro electric field at heterointerfaces between coupling semiconductors with different bandgaps.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0026 #chem201705855-bib-0027 #chem201705855-bib-0028 #chem201705855-bib-0029 #chem201705855-bib-0030"/> Zhang and co‐workers explored optimized conditions for preparing SnO<sub>2</sub>/SnS<sub>2</sub>/CNT heterostructures from SnO<sub>2</sub>/CNT sources. This anode material showed excellent conductivity and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0026"/> Jiang et al. investigated both the lithium‐ion and sodium‐ion storage capability of MoS<sub>2</sub>/SnS<sub>2</sub> heterostructures supported on graphene sheets. This composite delivered excellent cycling ability without capacity loss up to 150 cycles at 1.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, with a capacity of 340 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 6 A g<sup>−1</sup> in SIBs.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0027"/></p><p>Thus, we introduced antimony trisulfide to develop SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures anchored on rGO nanosheets (SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO) by a facile one‐pot hydrothermal approach without complicated steps, just through two main procedures: hydrothermal and calcining processes. All raw materials are easily accessible and cheap.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0031"/> Through investigating the formation mechanism, we deduced that SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates grew on the rGO nanosheets along the <i>c</i> direction before coating the as‐formed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods to form heterostructures. The SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composite exhibited excellent electrochemical performance as an anode material for SIBs, including a high reversible capacity of 642 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and stable cyclic stability. In particular, SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures achieved an outstanding rate capacity with specific capacities of 593 and 567 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at high current densities of 2 and 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201705855-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composites were synthesized by a facile one‐pot hydrothermal reaction. The crystal structures of the as‐prepared samples were determined by XRD, as shown in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). The XRD patterns of both composites exhibit seven broad peaks of hexagonal SnS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS no. 23‐0677, space group: <i>P</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/chem201705855-math-0004.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-math-0004"/><i>m</i>1) at 15.8, 28.2, 32.1, 46.1, 50.0, 52.5, and 60.6°, corresponding to the (001), (100), (101), (003), (110), (111), and (201) crystal planes, respectively. The diffraction peaks of orthorhombic Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS no. 06‐0474, space group <i>Pbnm</i>) were detected at 11.1, 17.5, and 60.5°, which can be indexed to (110), (120), and (242), respectively.</p><p>X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed to check the chemical composition and valence states of the elements in SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composites. The survey XPS spectra in Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0001"/> a and Figure S2 (Supporting Information) show the presence of C, Sb, Sn, S, and O elements in SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO composites. To detect the valence state and atomic fraction of each element, high‐resolution XPS spectra were measured for C 1s, Sb 3d, O 1s, and Sn 3d. Notably, the C 1s spectrum of GO in Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0001"/> b mainly shows two kinds of chemical bonds: carbon–carbon bonds (C−C, C=C) at 284.8 eV and carbon–oxygen bonds, including C−O at 286.8, C=O at 287.3, and O−C=O at 288.7 eV.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0019"/> However, most carbon–oxygen bonds in SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO have disappeared or faded. This indicates that GO is significantly reduced in the calcination process. Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0001"/> c shows the specific peaks of Sb 3d and O 1s in high resolution, which overlap in the range of 526.6–533.6 eV. The two peaks for O 1s at 529.8 and 531.0 eV can be ascribed to the small proportion of C=O and C−O bonds in rGO, respectively, and the peaks for Sb 3d<sub>3/2</sub> (539.4 and 539.9 eV) and Sb 3d<sub>5/2</sub> (529.9 and 530.4 eV) confirm the existence of Sb<sup>3+</sup>.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0032"/> As shown in Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0001"/> d, the binding energy of Sn 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and 3d<sub>3/2</sub> photoelectron peaks for Sn<sup>4+</sup> are 485.7 and 494.2 eV, respectively.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0019 #chem201705855-bib-0021"/> By calculating peak areas of Sn and Sb, the atomic contents of these two elements were determined to be 79.2 and 20.8 atom %, respectively. By combination with the results of thermogravimetric analysis (TGA, Figure S3 in Supporting Information), the contents of SnS<sub>2</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and carbon in the composite were calculated to be 71.6, 17.5, and 10.4 wt %, respectively, while the carbon content of SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO is 13.9 wt %.</p><figure xml:id="chem201705855-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201705855-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Survey XPS spectrum of a) SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO and high‐resolution XPS spectra of b) C 1s for GO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO, c) Sb 3d and O 1s, and d) Sn 3d for SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO.</p></caption></figure><p>Morphological features were characterized by SEM and TEM. Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0002"/> a and b show SEM images of SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO, respectively. Both composites consist of numerous flowerlike nanosheets. In low‐resolution SEM images (Figure S4 and S5, Supporting Information), well‐distributed morphologies are clearly observed. As shown in Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0002"/> c, d, SnS<sub>2</sub> and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoplates can be easily identified on rGO nanosheets in both composites, while Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> may exist as ultrathin nanorods hidden under SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates in SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO. To further investigate combinations among SnS<sub>2</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and rGO, high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) was performed. Figure S6 (Supporting Information) confirms the nanoplate‐on‐nanosheet microstructure of SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO. As shown in Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0003"/> a, SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates and Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods can be easily observed on the surface of rGO nanosheets, and their lattice fringes are marked in white. The lattice distance <i>d</i> of 5.89 Å corresponding to (001) planes of SnS<sub>2</sub> means that SnS<sub>2</sub> is likely to grow on graphene oxide nanosheets principally along the <i>c</i> direction. Likewise, for Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, <i>d</i> values of 3.18, 3.04, 2.78, and 2.23 Å are associated with the (021), (211), (221) and (141) crystal planes, respectively, which are also indexed in the selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (inset in Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0003"/> a). Indicating the distribution areas of SnS<sub>2</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and rGO by white dotted lines clearly reveals the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructure. Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0003"/> b shows a selected area of Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0003"/> a and clearly demonstrates the interfaces among SnS<sub>2</sub> Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and rGO, whereby lattice fringes of SnS<sub>2</sub> (white lines) explicitly indicate the existence of an SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructure. The dislocation indirectly reveals that SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates coated Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods. This type of heterostructure is uniformly distributed in the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composite, as shown in element mapping images (Figure <link href="#chem201705855-fig-0003"/> c).</p><figure xml:id="chem201705855-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201705855-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of a) SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO and b) SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO. TEM images of c) SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO and d) SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO.</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="chem201705855-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:09476539:media:chem201705855:chem201705855-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/chem201705855-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) HRTEM image of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO with SAED pattern (inset); lattice distances of SnS<sub>2</sub> and Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> are marked in white, respectively, and the boundaries among SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods, and rGO nanosheets are indicated by white dotted lines. b) Selected area of a) clearly revealing the interfaces among SnS<sub>2</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and rGO (white dashed lines). c) HAADF‐HRTEM image and the corresponding C, Sn, Sb, and S elemental maps.</p></caption></figure><p>On the basis of the SEM and TEM characterization, we propose the growth mechanism of the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructure in the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composite shown in Scheme <link href="#chem201705855-fig-5001"/>. Both Sn<sup>4+</sup> and Sb<sup>3+</sup> can be absorbed on the surface of GO nanosheets because of the attraction between positively charged metal ions and negatively charged functional groups on rGO. During the hydrothermal process, the growth rate of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> is faster than that of SnS<sub>2</sub>. This effect leads to the as‐formed Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods being coated by the SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates. The heterostructures consist of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods coated by SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoplates, which grow on graphene oxide nanosheets along their <i>c</i> direction. The SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composites delivered a high reversible capacity of 642 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> without capacity decline after 100 cycles and showed an excellent long‐term cyclic life with about 80.5 % capacity remained after 400 cycles at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO electrodes also showed superior high‐rate capability with capacities of 593 and 567 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at high current densities of 2 and 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Through GITT and EIS analysis, the desirable high rate performance is ascribed to excellent Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics and high electrical conductivity, profiting from the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures. The outstanding electrochemical characteristics of the SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composite make it a promising anode material for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201705855-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="chem201705855-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials synthesis</title><p>All chemical reagents were purchased from Aladdin (China) and received without further purification. Graphene oxide (GO) was prepared from graphite powder by the Hummers method.<link href="#chem201705855-bib-0044"/> In a typical synthesis of SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composite, SnCl<sub>4</sub><b>⋅</b>5 H<sub>2</sub>O (0.4 mmol) was dissolved in 15 mL of an as‐prepared 2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup> dispersion of exfoliated GO. SbCl<sub>3</sub> (0.1 mmol) was added and the mixture stirred under ambient conditions for 30 min, after which thioacetamide (2 mmol) was added with strong stirring for a further 30 min. Next, the solution was transferred to a 20 mL Teflon reactor for hydrothermal reaction at 160 °C for 12 h. The sediment was collected by filtration and washed with deionized water and absolute alcohol several times before drying under vacuum at 60 °C overnight. SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composite was obtained after sintering the dried product in Ar atmosphere at a ramp rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> to 400 °C and keeping it at that temperature for 2 h. SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO composite was synthesized by the same route, except that SnCl<sub>4</sub><b>⋅</b>5 H<sub>2</sub>O (0.5 mmol) was added to the GO solution without any SbCl<sub>3</sub>.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201705855-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials characterization</title><p>The phases of the SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO composites were characterized by XRD with a Brucker‐AXS/D8 Advance powder diffractometer equipped with Cu K<sub>α</sub> radiation (<i>λ</i>=1.541 Å). XPS was performed with a Thermo Fisher Scientific/Escalab250Xi spectrometer with monochromatic Al K<sub>α</sub> radiation (<i>hv</i>=1486.6 eV). The morphological observations were performed with a field‐emission scanning electron microscope (Hitachi/S‐4800), a transmission electron microscope (Philips/Tecnai 12), and a high‐resolution transmission electron microscope (FEI/Tecnai G2 F30 S‐TWIN). TGA analysis was conducted with a PerkinElmer DSC 8500 instrument from 20 to 1000 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> in air.</p></section><section xml:id="chem201705855-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The electrochemical tests were carried out on CR2032 coin‐type cells. The electrode slurry was made by manually grinding a mixture of 80 wt % active material (SnS<sub>2</sub>@rGO or SnS<sub>2</sub>/Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@rGO), 10 wt % acetylene black, and 10 wt % sodium carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) binder in deionized water for 20 min. The obtained homogenous slurry was spread on copper foil and dried at 60 °C under vacuum overnight.
