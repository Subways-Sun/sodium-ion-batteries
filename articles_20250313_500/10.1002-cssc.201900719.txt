However, the large radius of K<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> lead to sluggish kinetics and relatively large volume variations. Herein, a surface‐confined strategy is developed to restrain SnS<sub>2</sub> in self‐generated hierarchically porous carbon networks with an in situ reduced graphene oxide (rGO) shell (SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO). The as‐prepared SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO electrode delivers high reversible capacity (721.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and superior rate capability (397.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) as the anode material of SIB. Furthermore, a reversible capacity of 499.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and a cycling stability with 298.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles at a current density of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> were achieved in PIBs, surpassing most of the reported non‐carbonaceous anode materials. Additionally, the electrochemical reactions between SnS<sub>2</sub> and K<sup>+</sup> were investigated and elucidated.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Building better batteries</b>: A surface‐confining strategy is proposed to prepare dual carbon‐coated SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO composite as electrode material. xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the rapid development of portable electronics in the past two decades, the market demand for renewable and highly efficient energy storage devices is growing rapidly.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0001"/> However, owing to the scarce and uneven distribution of lithium sources, the cost of the state‐of‐art lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) is still high.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0002"/> Thus, it is practical to explore alternate battery systems.</p><p>Among the emerging battery systems based on earth‐abundant metals,<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0003 #cssc201900719-bib-0004 #cssc201900719-bib-0005 #cssc201900719-bib-0006"/> sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted a lot of attention owing to the similar electrochemistry as LIBs.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0007 #cssc201900719-bib-0008"/> Hence, there is a strong interest towards developing high‐performance anode and cathode materials for SIBs.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0009 #cssc201900719-bib-0010"/> Specifically, owing on the high specific capacity and low cost,<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0011"/> SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based materials have been regarded as a promising anode material for SIBs.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0012"/> However, the large volume expansion accompanying the formation of Sn–Na alloy (420 % volume expansion upon formation of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>) and low intrinsic conductivity have hindered the application of SnS<sub>2</sub> anodes.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0011 #cssc201900719-bib-0013"/> Thus, to achieve better electrochemical activity, coupling of nanostructured SnS<sub>2</sub> with various conductive carbonaceous networks (such as graphene, carbon nanotubes, and pyrolytic carbon) has been attempted to accommodate the volume expansion and enhance the electric conductivity, which has proven to be effective.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0014 #cssc201900719-bib-0015 #cssc201900719-bib-0016"/> However, most of the reported methods require harsh reaction conditions, which impede large‐scale production for practical applications. For example, the widely investigated solvothermal methods<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0011 #cssc201900719-bib-0012 #cssc201900719-bib-0014 #cssc201900719-bib-0017 #cssc201900719-bib-0018"/> could only be realized under high pressure and a constrained enclosure, and presents a risk of explosion if the solvent is overdosed or the temperature is too high. Similarly, the recently reported electrospinning method for the preparation of tin‐based anodes, which show good electrochemical performance in SIBs, is confined to lab stage owing to the high‐cost of the facility required along with the ultra‐low productivity.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0019 #cssc201900719-bib-0020"/> Consequently, there is an urgent need to develop a cost‐effective route for realizing large‐scale production of high‐performance tin‐based SIB anode materials.</p><p>Recently, potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs), owing to comparable reserves to Na and similar electrochemistry to Li, came into spotlight as the electrochemical formation of stable potassium‐intercalated graphite (KC<sub>8</sub>) was reported.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0021 #cssc201900719-bib-0022 #cssc201900719-bib-0023 #cssc201900719-bib-0024"/> Additionally, the more negative standard potential of K<sup>+</sup>/K [−2.93 V against −2.71 V for Na<sup>+</sup>/Na; vs. standard hydrogen electrode (SHE)] enables PIBs to operate within a wider potential range and, hence, achieve a higher energy density.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0002 #cssc201900719-bib-0025 #cssc201900719-bib-0026 #cssc201900719-bib-0027"/> However, until now, the anode materials reported for PIBs are mostly based on carbonaceous materials<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0021 #cssc201900719-bib-0026"/> and the development of carbon‐free electrodes is still in its early stages.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0028 #cssc201900719-bib-0029"/> Considering its high electrochemical performance in LIBs and SIBs,<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0016 #cssc201900719-bib-0030"/> SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode is expected to exhibit superior K<sup>+</sup> storage capability. However, reports on SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based anode materials for PIBs are scarce<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0031"/> and the reported reversible capacity is still relatively low and unsatisfactory in view of its excellent electrochemical performance in LIBs and SIBs. Moreover, the structure evolution and phase transformation during the potassiation/de‐potassiation process is still unclear, which motivated us to get deeper insight into the K<sup>+</sup>‐storage mechanisms of SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode, and further construct a relationship between its microstructure and K<sup>+</sup>‐storage capabilities.</p><p>Herein, we report the synthesis of surface‐confined SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO with a self‐generated carbon network inside and an in situ‐reduced GO layer outside. Owing to the synergistic effect of inner carbon network and surface‐confined rGO shell, the electrode exhibits outstanding reversible capacity, enhanced rate capability, and improved initial coulombic efficiency and cycling stability in SIBs. To shed light on the unique electrochemical performance, quantitative analyses were performed to distinguish the involved Na<sup>+</sup>‐storage processes. Furthermore, the electrochemical performance of SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based anode material for PIBs was thoroughly evaluated. Moreover, ex situ XRD, Raman spectroscopy, and high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) imaging were preformed and the phase transformation involved during potassiation/de‐potassiation process was revealed. To further evaluate the effects of surface‐confined strategy, galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was performed to compare the alkaline‐ion diffusion rate of the electrodes before and after rGO coating along with confirming the relatively sluggish diffusion rate of K<sup>+</sup> (compared to Na<sup>+</sup>). This work not only introduces a strategy for designing high‐performance electrode materials but also provides fundamental understanding and inspiration for subsequent research on carbon‐free anode materials for PIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Morphology and structure characterization</title><p>Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0001"/> illustrates the various steps involved in the process of preparing the composite materials. After direct calcination of disodium stannous citrate (DSC) at 650 °C CO<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O are released, whereas Sn and Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> particles are uniformly embedded in the pyrolytic carbon matrix (Sn@C@Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>). After washing with hot deionized water (≈50 °C), the Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> template could be removed and porous Sn@C particles are obtained. After a simple sulfuration process using sublimed sulfur, the Sn@C particles are transformed into SnS<sub>2</sub>@C composite. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0002"/> a and b, the Sn particles grow unrestrictedly during the sulfuration process and impale the carbon matrix to become flake‐like SnS<sub>2</sub>. Owing to the intrinsic low conductivity of SnS<sub>2</sub>, the flake‐like SnS<sub>2</sub>, disconnected from the continuous conductive carbon network, will have low electrochemical activity and gradually lose Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage capabilities. Considering the reduction conditions of Sn<sup>II</sup> and the citrate base of DSC,<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0032"/> GO flakes can be reduced in situ and strongly coated on the surface of the composites to confine the growth of Sn particles during the sulfuration process. To prove this, a certain amount of DSC was directly added into the GO solution, and after stirring for 1 h, the solution became black in color (Figure S1, see the Supporting Information) signifying the newly emerged rGO whereas the control solution remained brown in color (the original color of GO solution). Considering that excess amount of GO will decrease the specific energy density, which is calculated based on the mass of the whole electrode materials, GO solutions of various strength were investigated. As shown in Figure S2, insufficient GO will lead to uncovered surface, whereas excess GO will form a thick layer of rGO on the surface. Figure S3 further displays the SEM images of Sn@C and Sn@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> composites. Sn@C@rGO‐3 displays a thin and well‐covered rGO layer on the surface. Furthermore, after the sulfuration process, the rGO‐coating layer on the prepared SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 was still intact (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0002"/> c and d). The energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mapping results also confirmed the uniform distribution of Sn and S atoms under the rGO layer (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0002"/> e–h).</p><figure xml:id="cssc201900719-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201900719:cssc201900719-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201900719-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Synthesis process for SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> (white ball: sodium carbonate).</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="cssc201900719-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201900719:cssc201900719-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201900719-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Morphological characterization. SEM images of a) Sn@C, b) SnS<sub>2</sub>@C, c) Sn@C@rGO‐3, and d) SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3. e) Original SEM image and the corresponding EDS maps of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 for f) carbon, g) tin, and h) sulfur.</p></caption></figure><p>XRD analysis were conducted to track the phase transformation during the carbonization, washing, and sulfuration processes. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0003"/> a, after calcination the samples exhibit peaks indicating the co‐existence of Sn (JCPDS No.04‐0673), Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS No.19‐1130), and SnO (JCPDS No.06‐0395). Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0003"/> b shows the XRD patterns of samples after washing with de‐ionized water, in which the peaks assigned to Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> disappear completely. Subsequently, after the sulfuration process, the XRD patterns (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0003"/> c) of all samples closely resemble those of SnS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No.23‐0677), and the peak corresponding to the existence of carbon at around 26°(2<i>θ</i>) is weakened owing to the strong signal of Sn and SnS<sub>2</sub>. Thus, Raman spectra were acquired to confirm the existence of carbon. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0003"/> d, the strong signals of characteristic D (≈1330 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and G bands (≈1585 cm<sup>−1</sup>) representing carbonaceous materials were detected.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0024"/> Moreover, an additional band was detected at roughly 311 cm<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to the A<sub>1g</sub> vibration of SnS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0011"/> The clearly reduced signal intensity of SnS<sub>2</sub> in the SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐4 composite can be attributed to the thick rGO layer on the surface, which could partially shield the Raman signal of SnS<sub>2</sub>. This result is also in agreement with the SEM observation (Figure S2 e and Figure S3 e).</p><figure xml:id="cssc201900719-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201900719:cssc201900719-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201900719-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Phase characterization. XRD patterns of Sn‐based composite a) before water‐washing process and b) after water‐washing process. c) XRD patterns and d) Raman spectra of SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based composite.</p></caption></figure><p>During the calcination process, the released CO<sub>2</sub> gas (conventional pore‐creating reagent) is expected to create mico‐ and mesopores on the carbon matrix, whereas removal of the Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> hard template will create meso‐ and macropores.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0026"/> Thus, to evaluate the surface confinement effect and the pore structure, the gas‐sorption studies were performed using Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method. As depicted in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> a, the sorption isotherm curves for both SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 are composed of a weak knee‐like inflexion (<i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub>≈0.01), a hysteresis loop (<i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub> between 0.4–0.9), and an uplift (<i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub> between 0.9–1.0), corresponding to a combination of Type I (indicating micropores), Type IV (characteristic of mesoporous materials) and Type II (feature of macroporous structure).<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0026 #cssc201900719-bib-0033"/> The BET specific surface areas (SSA) of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 were calculated to be 26.67 and 16.02 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The decreased SSA of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 can be attributed to the coating of rGO, which restrains the SnS<sub>2</sub> particles from growing freely. This is also in accordance with the well‐maintained spherical morphology of SnS<sub>2</sub> particles in SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0002"/> c and d). The hierarchically micro–meso–macroporous architecture was further verified by pore‐size distribution calculated using quenched solid density functional theory (QSDFT) method.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0034"/> As exhibited in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> b, both samples show pore sizes from around 1.4 to 186.0 nm, indicating the co‐existence of micro‐, meso‐, and macropores. Besides, the signal corresponding to the pore sizes within the range of 10.0–186.0 nm is relatively strong indicating abundant meso‐ and macropores, which could serve as fast channels for the transport of electrolyte and improving electrochemical performance, especially the rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="cssc201900719-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18645631:media:cssc201900719:cssc201900719-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/cssc201900719-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Pore structure and surface chemical characterization. a) Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms and b) pore size distribution of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3 calculated from the adsorption isotherms using DFT method. c) XPS survey spectra and high‐resolution spectra for d) Sn 3d, e) S 2p, and f) C 1s of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3.</p></caption></figure><p>To elucidate the surface chemical compositions and valence states of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐3, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was conducted. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> c, the existence C, Sn, O, and S atoms could be confirmed. To further evaluate the valence state of these elements high‐resolution XPS spectra were also collected. As shown in Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> d, two peaks centered at binding energies of 486.2 and 494.6 eV can be assigned to Sn 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Sn 3d<sub>3/2</sub> of Sn<sup>4+</sup> photoelectron emissions (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> d).<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0013 #cssc201900719-bib-0035"/> Correspondingly, the high‐resolution spectrum of S (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> e) could be deconvoluted into S 2p<sub>3/2</sub> (161.3 eV) and S 2p<sub>1/2</sub> (163.0 eV) of S<sup>2‐</sup>, which further confirmed the existence of SnS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0013"/> A side peak was also observed at around 164.3 eV, corresponding to C−S−C bond. As the electron‐rich sulfur atom leads to increased regional electron density on the carbon matrix, an enhanced diffusion rate of the alkaline ions is expected.<link href="#cssc201900719-bib-0013"/> Moreover, the high‐resolution C 1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#cssc201900719-fig-0004"/> f) could be deconvoluted as C−C/C=C (283.8 eV) and C−S/C−O (285.0 eV), indicating the bonding between carbon and hetero‐doped oxygen and sulfur. Besides, the deconvoluted O 1s spectrum (Figure S4) further confirms the bonding between carbon and oxygen. Therefore, with abundant heteroatom‐doped sites on the surface, the carbon matrix coupled with the rGO shell can not only accommodate the volume expansion but also provide electron‐conductive network and facilitate the fast transportation of alkaline ions (such as Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup>), implying superior electrochemical performance.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical performance of SIBs</title><p>The rate performance of Na<sup>+</sup> storage was evaluated first. The surface‐confined structure plays a vital and multifaceted role in this work providing the following benefits: i) the self‐generated carbon network as a fast electronic conductor, ii) carbon matrix and rGO shell for alleviating the volume expansion, iii) continuous pore structure for fast ion transport, and iv) rGO shell for restraining the growth of SnS<sub>2</sub> enabling higher initial coulombic efficiency (ICE) and stable cyclability. The superior rate Na<sup>+</sup>‐storage behavior was investigated by carefully designed cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements. The SnS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode materials were also comprehensively evaluated for PIBs. The electrochemical reactions between SnS<sub>2</sub> and K<sup>+</sup> were initially studied and a two‐step potassiation process was proposed and verified (i: SnS<sub>2</sub>+4 K→Sn+2 K<sub>2</sub>S and ii: Sn+<i>x</i> K→K<sub><i>x</i></sub>Sn). Furthermore, galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) results provided more information on the effects of surface‐confinement and structural evolution during the potassiation process. Overall, we expect that our surface‐confined strategy could be further extended for the preparation of other non‐carbonaceous electrode materials, and that our work will provide foundations for subsequent studies on Sn‐/S‐based PIB anode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><b>Preparation of Sn@C and Sn@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> composites</b>: Sn@C was prepared by direct carbonization of C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>6</sub>O<sub>8</sub>SnNa<sub>2</sub> (DSC) (holding at 650 °C under Ar atmosphere for 1 h with a temperature ramp rate of 4 °C min<sup>−1</sup>) followed by water‐washing procedure to remove the self‐generated template (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>). As for the preparation of Sn@C@rGO‐<i>x</i>, in a typical experiment, DSC (5 mmol) was initially dissolved in deionized water (90, 80, 60, and 20 mL for <i>x=</i>1, 2, 3, and 4, respectively), after which a certain amount of GO solution (2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added into the as‐prepared solution (10, 20, 4, and 80 mL for <i>x=</i>1, 2, 3 and 4, respectively) followed by magnetic stirring for 1 h. Afterwards, the resulting dark solution was lyophilized to obtain the precursor powder. Sn@C and Sn@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> composite could be obtained by following the same procedure.</p><p><b>Preparation of SnS<sub>2</sub>@C and SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> composites</b>: To prepare SnS<sub>2</sub>@C composite, sulfur and as‐prepared Sn@C composite were placed separately in opposite ends of a quartz boat (S/Sn@C mass ratio=5:1). The boat was then transferred into a tube furnace and heated at 400 °C for 3 h with a temperature ramp rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. The SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> composite was prepared using the same procedure.</p><p><b>Characterization</b>: The morphology and microstructure of the as‐synthesized materials were characterized by FESEM (Hitachi SU‐70 and JEOL JSM‐7610F) and TEM (JEOL JEM‐2100). The phase structure was determined by XRD on a Rigaku Miniflex 600 X‐ray diffractometer operated at 40 kV with Cu<sub>Kα</sub> radiation (<i>λ</i>=1.5406 Å) at a scan rate of 4° min<sup>−1</sup>. Raman spectra were collected on a Renishaw Invia Reflex using a 532 nm laser as the excitation source. The SSA and PSDs were determined by N<sub>2</sub> sorption isotherms at 77 K using the Micromeritics ASAP 2020 analyzer, for which the samples were degassed at 150 °C for 8 h before the analysis. The mass ratio of SnS<sub>2</sub> in the as‐synthesized materials was determined by TGA (Mettler‐Toledo TGA2 Thermo Analyzer) from 35 °C to 800 °C with a temperature ramp rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> (atmosphere: condensed air, 20 mL min<sup>−1</sup>). XPS was performed on an ESCALAB 250 spectrometer with an Al<sub>Kα</sub> X‐ray source (excitation energy of 1486.71 eV).</p><p><b>Electrochemical measurements</b>: The working electrode was prepared by mixing 80 wt % active material (as‐synthesized SnS<sub>2</sub>@C or SnS<sub>2</sub>@C@rGO‐<i>x</i> composite), 10 wt % super P (Timcal, 99.5 % purity), and 10 wt % polyvinylidene fluoride (PVDF). PVDF (3 wt %) was pre‐dissolved in <i>N</i>‐methyl pyrrolidone (NMP) solvent before using as binder. The slurry was then coated onto a copper foil and dried at 90 °C in a vacuum oven for 10 h to evaporate the solvent. Circular electrodes with a diameter of 14 mm were obtained by using a punch machine. The coin cells (CR‐2032‐type) were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box (H<sub>2</sub>O&lt;1 ppm and O<sub>2</sub>&lt;1 ppm) with metallic sodium/potassium as the counter electrode, corresponding to sodium‐ion batteries and potassium‐ion batteries. The electrolyte for sodium‐ion batteries was NaClO<sub>4</sub> solution (1.0 <sc>m</sc>) dissolved in ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate (EC/PC <i>v</i>/<i>v</i>=1:1 with 5 wt % fluorinated ethylene carbonate as additive). For potassium‐ion batteries, the electrolyte was KPF<sub>6</sub> solution (0.8 <sc>m</sc>) dissolved in EC/diethyl carbonate (EC/DEC <i>v</i>/<i>v</i>=1:1). A Whatman GF/C glass fiber was applied as the separator. The galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were conducted within the voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V using a LAND‐CT2001A multichannel galvanostat (Wuhan, China) at room temperature (25 °C). The CV profiles were obtained within the voltage window of 0.01–3.0 V at various scan rates on an electrochemical workstation (Autolab, Metrohm). For ex situ XRD, Raman, and HRTEM characterizations, the cycled electrodes were disassembled from the coin cells in an Ar‐filled glove box and then rinsed in DEC solvent to dissolve the residual electrolyte followed by vacuum drying at 60 °C for 10 h before the measurements.</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by Startup Funding of Distinguished Professorship of “1000 Talents Program” (31370086963030), the “Taishan Scholar Program” (11370085961006), Shandong Provincial Science and Technology Major Project (2016GGX104001, 2017CXGC1010, and 2018JMRH0211), the Fundamental Research Funds of Shandong University (2016JC005, 2017JC042 and 2017JC010), and the Natural Science Foundation of Shandong Province (ZR2017MEM002)</p></section><section xml:id="cssc201900719-sec-0010" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p><i>The authors declare no conflict of interest</i>.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="cssc201900719-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="cssc201900719-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>N.</givenNames> <familyName>Armaroli</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>V.</givenNames> <familyName>Balzani</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Angew.
