However, the all‐weather SIBs with a wide operation‐temperature range are rarely reported. Herein, we propose a wide‐temperature range SIB, which involves a carbon‐coated Na<sub>4</sub>Fe<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (NFPP@C) cathode, a bismuth (Bi) anode, and a diglyme‐based electrolyte. We demonstrate that solvated Na<sup>+</sup> can be directly stored by the Bi anode via an alloying reaction without the de‐solvent process. Furthermore, the NFPP@C cathode exhibits a high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient at low temperature. As a result, the Bi//NFPP@C battery exhibits perfect low‐temperature behavior. Even at −70 °C, this battery still delivers 70.19 % of the room‐temperature capacity. Furthermore, benefitting from the high boiling point of the electrolyte, this battery also works well at a high temperature of up to 100 °C. These results are encouraging for the further exploration of wide‐temperature range SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A wide‐temperature range sodium‐ion battery (SIB), which involves a Bi anode, a NFPP@C cathode and a diglyme‐based electrolyte is successfully fabricated. Owing to the solvent co‐intercalation mechanism of the Bi anode, the high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient of the NFPP@C cathode at low temperature and the electrolyte with a high boiling point and low freezing point, the prepared SIB can operate in a wide temperature range from −70 to 100 °C.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202116930:anie202116930-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202116930-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202116930-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), which are widely used to power various portable electronic devices and electric vehicles (EVs), have become an indispensable part of human life. Currently, the rise of EVs greatly increases the annual production of LIBs, which thus evokes serious concerns about the limited reserves of lithium.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Therefore, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have come back into focus because of abundant resources, wide distribution, and cost‐effectiveness of sodium.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0009"><sup>[2]</sup></link> During the past ten years, many efforts have been made to develop electrode materials for advanced SIBs, and some industries have commercialized SIBs for demo application.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0017"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Owing to the lower energy density of SIBs than LIBs, one of the main potential applications of SIBs is the grid‐scale energy storage for renewables that is another battery market in addition to that for electric vehicles.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0025"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Accordingly, low‐temperature and wide‐temperature operation of SIBs should be required to accommodate various extreme weather conditions in different regions. However, current research mainly focuses on the increase of electrode capacity with the purpose of reducing the energy density gap between LIBs and SIBs.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0033"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Up to present, the low‐temperature and wide‐temperature operation of SIBs is still rarely reported.</p><p>It is well known that LIBs will lose most of their capacity when they operate at temperatures lower than −40 °C, and it is also almost impossible to achieve a charge/discharge cycle at low temperature.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0041"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Such phenomena for LIBs is attributable to the sluggish de‐solvent process at the interface (i.e., the de‐solvent of Li<sup>+</sup> at the interface of the graphite anode), the slow Li<sup>+</sup>‐diffusion in the crystalline framework of active materials and the high freezing point of the electrolyte.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0045"><sup>[7]</sup></link> SIBs might face the same challenges because they also depend on the intercalation chemistry and rock‐chair operation principle which are the same as those for LIBs. Especially, Na<sup>+</sup> generally shows a lower diffusion rate in the bulk of active materials because of its larger size than Li<sup>+</sup>. Therefore, it is also a huge challenge to explore the wide‐temperature range operation of SIBs.</p><p>Recently, several research groups demonstrated that the commercial bulk Bi anode exhibited excellent performance for Na<sup>+</sup>‐storage in ether‐based electrolytes, which was much superior to that achieved in the carbonate‐based electrolytes.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0049"><sup>[8]</sup></link> According to these previous reports, such improved Na<sup>+</sup>‐storage performance might arise from the solvent molecules co‐intercalation process in the ether‐based electrolytes, but without direct evidence.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0055"><sup>[9]</sup></link> This assumption indicates that the solvated‐Na<sup>+</sup> might be directly accommodated into the crystalline framework of Bi without de‐solvation process. Especially, ether‐based electrolytes generally exhibit a lower freezing point than conventional carbonate‐based electrolytes. That is to say, the combination of Bi electrode with ether‐based electrolytes might be used to fabricate low‐temperature SIBs. However, the direct evidence for the solvated‐Na<sup>+</sup> storage in the Bi electrode and the related low‐temperature SIBs are rarely reported.</p><p>In this work, we successfully demonstrate the solvent co‐intercalation process of Bi electrode in an ether‐based electrolyte (i.e., 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in diglyme) and the related alloying reaction by using in situ Fourier transform infrared attenuated total reflection (FTIR/ATR) spectroscopy and in situ/ex situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) characterization. Moreover, the tests of Na//Bi half batteries confirm that the Bi electrode exhibits excellent low‐temperature behavior in the diglyme‐based electrolyte. Furthermore, carbon‐coated Na<sub>4</sub>Fe<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (NFPP@C) is selected as cathode material because its three‐dimensional diffusion channel ensures the fast movement of Na<sup>+</sup> even at a low temperature. Finally, benefitting from the low freezing point (−71.2 °C) and the high boiling point (151.5 °C) of the diglyme‐based electrolyte, the proposed Bi//NFPP@C batteries can work well in a wide temperature range from −70 to 100 °C.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202116930-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Prior to the in situ FTIR/ATR analysis, we measured the FTIR spectra of the electrolyte (1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme) and the pure solvent (diglyme), showing that the characteristic peak corresponding to the solvated Na<sup>+</sup> (836 cm<sup>−1</sup>) is significantly enhanced after dissolving the NaPF<sub>6</sub> into the diglyme (Figure S1). Then, the in situ FTIR/ATR spectroscopy measurement was carried out to investigate the solvent co‐intercalation process at the Bi electrode, and the achieved results are summarized in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>a. In this in situ experiment, cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement within the window from 0.1 to 1.5 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) at a scan rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> was used to characterize the electrochemical behavior of the Bi electrode (the left of Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>a), and the corresponding 2D color‐filled contour plot of the in situ FTIR/ATR spectra at different potentials (wavenumber vs. time/intensity) is given on the right of Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>a. The 3D diagram of the relevant in situ FTIR/ATR spectra is shown in Figure S2. The FTIR spectra collected at the open‐circuit state was used as the background. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>a, the intensity of the characteristic peak at approximately 836 cm<sup>−1</sup> (that is indexed to the solvated Na<sup>+</sup>) rapidly decreases at ≈0.1 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) on the negative sweep process, which is attributable to that the reduction reaction greatly reduces the concentration of solvated Na<sup>+</sup> at the interface. On the consequent positive sweep process, it can be detected that the intensity of the characteristic peak at 836 cm<sup>−1</sup> rapidly increases at the location of the oxidization peak (≈1.2 to 1.5 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>), which is ascribed to the fact that the extraction of solvated Na<sup>+</sup> greatly increases the concentration of solvated Na<sup>+</sup> at the interface. Such periodic changes of the intensity of the characteristic peak at 836 cm<sup>−1</sup> can also be observed in the following cycles (Figure S3). This phenomenon clearly indicates that the concentration of solvated Na<sup>+</sup> at the interface changes with the redox reactions of Bi electrode, which verifies the solvent co‐intercalation process at the Bi electrode. Moreover, we also investigated the structure evolution of the Bi electrode in diglyme‐based electrolyte during the cycle process. As the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>b show, the initial electrode displays bulk Bi particles with a size of 15–30 μm. However, the initial Bi micron particles are broken into small particles (50 nm) after the first cycle (Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>c), which is due to particle breakage caused by 250 % volume expansion of Bi metal in the process of sodiation/desodiation. And, owing to the room‐temperature sintering phenomenon, these nanoparticles connect together to form a coral‐like porous structure after 50 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>d). Such a porous structure not only increases the electrolyte/electrode surface, but also facilities the diffusion of solvated Na<sup>+</sup>, which can improve the related electrochemical performance. This will be confirmed by the later low‐temperature tests.</p><figure xml:id="anie202116930-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202116930:anie202116930-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202116930-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) CV curve in current versus time form and corresponding 2D color‐filled contour plot in wavenumber versus time form of the in situ FTIR/ATR test of the Na//Bi half battery. SEM images of Bi power at b) pristine, c) 1<sup>st</sup> cycle and d) 50<sup>th</sup> cycle. e) The galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Na//Bi half battery during the in situ XRD test and corresponding 2D color‐filled contour plot.</p></caption></figure><p>In addition to the above in situ FTIR/ATR analysis, the in situ XRD measurement was carried out to further investigate the reaction mechanism of the Bi electrode during the sodiation/desodiation process. Charge/discharge curves within the voltage window from 0.1 to 1.5 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) at 0.1 C (1 C=386 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) are shown on the left of Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>e. XRD patterns are displayed in a 2D color‐filled contour plot (right of Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>e). A 3D diagram of relevant in situ XRD results is shown in Figure S4. The characteristic peaks of metal Bi (marked by red square) disappear gradually during the discharge process, while the characteristic peaks of NaBi (marked by blue circle) appear. When discharge to ≈0.5 V, the characteristic peaks of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi (marked by green triangle) appear, and the characteristic peaks of Bi disappear completely. Meanwhile, the characteristic peaks of NaBi decrease gradually and disappear completely at the fully discharged state (0.1 V). During the following recharge process, Na<sub>3</sub>Bi transforms gradually into NaBi at 0.6 V, and the emerged NaBi transforms gradually to Bi at 0.75 V. At the fully charged state (1.5 V), the NaBi transforms completely to Bi. The in situ XRD results indicate that the alloying/dealloying reaction mechanism of metal Bi involves highly reversible conversion of Bi↔NaBi↔Na<sub>3</sub>Bi. However, the presence of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi is unidentified in the ex‐situ XRD (Figure S5) and ex situ HRTEM (Figure S6) of the disassembled batteries, which can be assigned to the instability of the alloy phase. Recently, Peng's group also demonstrated that the Na<sub>3</sub>Bi and NaBi decomposed quickly, which results in only the Bi phase being detected under the ex situ conditions.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0053"><sup>[8d]</sup></link></p><p>The results shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link> clearly demonstrate that the solvated Na<sup>+</sup> can be directly stored by the Bi electrode via an alloying reaction without the de‐solvent process. Meanwhile, the selected diglyme‐based electrolyte (1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in diglyme) exhibits a low freezing point (−71.2 °C) and a high boiling point (151.5 °C) (Figure S7). Therefore, we think that when used in above electrolyte, the Bi electrode might exhibit perfect low‐temperature behavior because of the charge storage without a de‐solvent process and the low freezing point of the electrolyte. In order to confirm this assumption, we investigated the low‐temperature behavior of the coin‐type Na//Bi half batteries. In this experiment, Na//Bi half batteries were first pre‐cycled twice at room temperature (25 °C), and then were measured at different low temperatures from −20 to −60 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0002">2</link>a). When the temperature decreases from −20 to −40 °C, the capacity of the battery hardly decays, and the polarization is negligible. Even at −60 °C, it could still display a high capacity of ≈330 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and slight polarization, which demonstrates the superior low‐temperature performance of the Bi electrode. Herein, it should be noted that the pre‐cycle treatment at room temperature brings a significant effect on the low‐temperature performance of the Bi electrode. To clarify this point, the low‐temperature behaviors (at −20 °C) of the Na//Bi half batteries without and with the pre‐cycle treatment at room temperature are compared as given in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0002">2</link>b and c. At −20 °C, the Na//Bi half battery (without pre‐cycle treatment at 25 °C shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0002">2</link>b) shows a low capacity of 220 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which then increases gradually during the consequent cycles and finally stabilizes at ≈340 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the 10<sup>th</sup> cycle. On the contrary, the Na//Bi half battery (with pre‐cycle treatment at 25 °C shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0002">2</link>c) exhibits an initial discharge capacity of 346 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and the capacity remains stable during subsequent cycles. The difference between Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0002">2</link>b and c is attributable to that the pre‐cycle treatment at room temperature leads to the conversion from bulk Bi (15–30 μm) to nano‐sized Bi with porous structure, which is confirmed by Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0001">1</link>b–d. In other words, the perfect low‐temperature behavior of the Bi electrode in diglyme‐based electrolyte arises from both the charge storage without de‐solvent process and the in situ formed porous structure. The electrochemical properties of the Na//Bi half batteries at room temperature are shown in Figure S8. It is interesting that the Na//Bi half battery retains high cycle stability even with a high Bi loading (9.6 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>) in the electrode (see Figure S9 and the related discussion).</p><figure xml:id="anie202116930-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202116930:anie202116930-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202116930-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Na//Bi half battery at different temperatures. b, c) Charge/discharge curves at different cycles of Na//Bi half batteries tested at −20 °C b) without a pre‐cycle treatment at 25 °C and c) after pre‐cycle treatment at 25 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>The above results have successfully verified the excellent low‐temperature performance of the Bi anode in the diglyme‐based electrolyte, and thus it should be the next logical step to select a suitable cathode for the Bi anode to build the corresponding low‐temperature SIBs. Very recently, the polyanionic cathode material Na<sub>4</sub>Fe<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (NFPP) is attracting extensive attention because of its low cost and environmentally friendly nature.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0060"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Especially, Chou and his colleagues recently successfully demonstrated that NFPP can operate normally at −20 °C in a carbonate‐based electrolyte owing to the 3D Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathways and small volume change during cycle process,<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0065"><sup>[11]</sup></link> which originated from its open framework built by PO<sub>4</sub>‐P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> mixed polyanion units.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0066"><sup>[12]</sup></link> However, the low‐temperature behavior of NFPP in the ether‐based electrolytes has not been reported to date. Accordingly, we decided to use NFPP as the candidate cathode to accompany the Bi anode in the diglyme‐based electrolyte. We synthesized carbon‐coated Na<sub>4</sub>Fe<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (NFPP@C) by a conventional sol–gel method.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0067"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The XRD pattern, TG curve, SEM image and TEM image of NFPP@C are summarized in Figure S10. The room‐temperature performance of the Na//NFPP@C half batteries is given in Figure S11. Prior to the fabrication of the Bi//NFPP@C batteries, we employed a Na//NFPP@C half battery to investigate the kinetic characteristics of the NFPP@C cathode material at low temperature (−40 °C) by using galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurement. In this experiment, the pulsed charge/discharge tests of Na//NFPP@C were conducted at a current of 5 mA g<sup>−1</sup> with a pulse duration time of 0.5 h and a pulse interval of 2 h. The Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients calculated by applying the Fick's second law at different charge/discharge stages are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>a. Examination of Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>a indicates that Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients in the NFPP@C electrode at −40 °C mainly vary between 10<sup>−9</sup> and 10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, showing a high rate ability even under low‐temperature conditions. Then, the NFPP@C cathode was paired with the Bi anode to assemble the full battery, which is named as Bi//NFPP@C battery. Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>b presents the galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of the Bi//NFPP@C battery tested at different temperatures with low rates, where the applied current densities and the achieved capacities are calculated based on the mass of Bi in the anode. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>b, the Bi//NFPP@C battery displays discharge capacities of 286.9 and 283.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at −20 and −40 °C, respectively, which are close to that achieved at room temperature (305 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, Figure S12). At −60 °C, the battery maintains a capacity of 257 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (nearly 84.2 % of the room‐temperature capacity). Especially, it still exhibits a discharge capacity of 214.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at even −70 °C, which is approximately 70.19 % of the room‐temperature capacity. Then, we further investigated the rate performance and cycle stability of the Bi//NFPP@C batteries at −40 °C. And the achieved results are summarized in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>c and d, respectively. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>c, at −40 °C, this battery delivers a discharge capacity of 256 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a low current density of 30 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. This battery also exhibits a specific capacity of 173 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a high rate of 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, showing a promising rate behavior even at low‐temperature conditions. Moreover, the Bi//NFPP@C battery shows an excellent cycle stability with negligible capacity decay within 40 cycles at −40 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0003">3</link>d). The electrochemical properties of the Bi//NFPP@C batteries at room temperature are shown in Figure S13.</p><figure xml:id="anie202116930-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202116930:anie202116930-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202116930-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) GITT curves of NFPP@C electrode tested at −40 °C and the calculated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients at different potentials. b) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Bi//NFPP@C battery at different temperatures. c) Rate performance and d) cycle stability of the Bi//NFPP@C batteries at −40 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>As mentioned above, the selected diglyme‐based electrolyte exhibits not only a low freezing point (−71.2 °C), but also a high boiling point (151.5 °C). Accordingly, it can be expected that the Bi//NFPP@C batteries using such a diglyme‐based electrolyte could also be operated at high temperatures. Therefore, we further tested the high‐temperature behavior of this battery. After two cycles at room temperature (25 °C), the Bi//NFPP@C battery was tested at 60 °C (3<sup>rd</sup>, 4<sup>th</sup> cycle), 80 °C (5<sup>th</sup>, 6<sup>th</sup> cycle) and 100 °C (7<sup>th</sup>, 8<sup>th</sup> cycle). Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0004">4</link>a presents the selected data achieved at 60 °C (4<sup>th</sup> cycle), 80 °C (6<sup>th</sup> cycle) and 100 °C (8<sup>th</sup> cycle). As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0004">4</link>a, the battery shows a capacity of 320 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 60 °C and 80 °C, which is higher than that achieved at room temperature. However, the coulombic efficiency of the battery achieved at 80 °C is slightly lower than that achieved at 60 °C. When the operation temperature increases to 100 °C, the battery still works well with an obtained capacity of 270 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> but with a lower reversibility. It is well known that the ether‐based electrolytes generally exhibit the limited stable potential window, and thus the high operation temperature of 100 °C might lead to the decomposition of electrolyte. Finally, we tested the cycle stability of this Bi//NFPP@C battery at 60 °C. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202116930-fig-0004">4</link>b, the battery shows a high capacity retention rate of 86 % over 100 cycles, indicating its perfect high‐temperature behavior. The photos, microscopy images and SEM images at different magnifications of the cycled electrodes at different temperatures are shown in Figure S14. The achieved wide‐temperature range operation is much superior to those in previous reports<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0068"><sup>[14]</sup></link> (please see Table S1 for detailed comparison). However, it should be noted that Bi is much more expensive than hard carbon, oxidized graphite or expended graphite.<link href="#anie202116930-bib-0077"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Therefore, it is necessary to explore more cost‐effective electrode materials for constructing wide‐temperature range SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="anie202116930-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202116930:anie202116930-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202116930-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Bi//NFPP@C battery at 60, 80 and 100 °C. b) Cycle stability of the Bi//NFPP@C battery at 60 °C.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202116930-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we have successfully demonstrated a wide‐temperature range SIB with a bulk Bi anode, a NFPP@C cathode, and a diglyme‐based electrolyte. By using in situ FTIR/ATR analysis and XRD characterization, we verify that solvated Na<sup>+</sup> can be directly stored by the Bi anode through an alloying reaction without the de‐solvent process, which is critical for low‐temperature operation. Furthermore, we find that the carbon‐coated NFPP cathode shows a high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient at low‐temperature conditions, which is attributable to its stable three‐dimensional Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion channels in the crystalline framework of NFPP. Therefore, the Bi//NFPP@C batteries display promising rate and cycle performance at −40 °C, and even can deliver 70.19 % of the room‐temperature capacity at the ultralow temperature of −70 °C. Furthermore, the electrolyte shows not only a low freezing point, but also a high boiling point. Therefore, the Bi//NFPP@C batteries also work well at a high temperature of 100 °C. These achieved results might provide guidance for future design of wide‐temperature range SIBs.
