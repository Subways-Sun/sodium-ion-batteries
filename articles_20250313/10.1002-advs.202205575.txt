type="main">Abstract</title><p>Currently, the construction of amorphous/crystalline (A/C) heterophase has become an advanced strategy to modulate electronic and/or ionic behaviors and promote structural stability due to their concerted advantages. However, their different kinetics limit the synergistic effect. Further, their interaction functions and underlying mechanisms remain unclear. Here, a unique engineered defect‐rich V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> heterophase structure (donated as A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS) composed of mesoporous oxygen‐deficient amorphous <sub>−</sub> hollow core (A‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/HMC) and lattice‐distorted crystalline shell (C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>/S) encapsulated by carbon is rationally designed via a facile approach. Comprehensive density functional theory (DFT) calculations disclose that the lattice distortion enlarges the porous channels for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the crystalline phase, thereby optimizing its kinetics to be compatible with the oxygen‐vacancy‐rich amorphous phase. This significantly reduces the high contrast of the kinetic properties between the crystalline and amorphous phases in A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS and induces the formation of highly dense A/C interfaces with a strong synergistic effect. As a result, the dense heterointerface effectively optimizes the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy and lowers the diffusion barrier, thus accelerating the overall kinetics of A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS. In contrast, the perfect heterophase (defects‐free) A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐HCS demonstrates sparse A/C interfacial sites with limited synergistic effect and sluggish kinetics. As expected, the A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS achieves a high rate and ultrastable performance (192 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 6000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) when employed for the first time as a cathode for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). This work provides general guidance for realizing dense heterophase cathode design for high‐performance SIBs and beyond.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs4687-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A defect‐rich heterophase structure A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS composed of oxygen‐deficient amorphous hollow core and lattice‐distorted crystalline shell encapsulated by carbon is synthesized via a novel strategy. The defects significantly reduce the high contrast of the kinetic properties between the crystalline and amorphous phases and induce the formation of dense A/C interfaces, lowering the energy barriers, and enabling fast and stable SIBs performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs4687:advs4687-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs4687-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>In recent decades, rechargeable lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have witnessed great success in electric vehicles and portable electronic due to their high energy density and long cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0001 #advs4687-bib-0002"><sup>1,2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the uneven global distribution of lithium resources and ever‐increasing price, hinder their large‐scale applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0003 #advs4687-bib-0004"><sup>3,4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have emerged as a prominent alternative due to the abundance and low cost of sodium resources and their similar working principles to lithium‐ion batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0005 #advs4687-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the drastic volume expansion and sluggish reaction kinetics caused by the large Na<sup>+</sup> radius (1.02 Å) inhibit its development.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0007 #advs4687-bib-0008"><sup>7,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is critical to customize cathode materials for SIBs to better accommodate the bulkier Na‐ions. Vanadium oxides have been intensively explored as one of the most promising cathodes due to their multiple valence states (V<sup>2+</sup>‐V<sup>+5</sup>), appropriate operating voltages, high theoretical capacities, and facile synthetic chemistry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0009 #advs4687-bib-0010 #advs4687-bib-0011"><sup>9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, their oxidation state plays a significant role in their electrochemical properties. For example, vanadium pentoxide with a higher valence (V<sup>+5</sup>) shows a high theoretical capacity of 442 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much larger than currently used commercial cathodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Further, various V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanostructures have been widely reported to improve their electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0011 #advs4687-bib-0013"><sup>11,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unlike other vanadium oxides, vanadium trioxide V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with a low valence state (V<sup>3+</sup>) has been considered unusable as a cathode for lithium/sodium‐ion batteries by Tranchant et al. in 1980.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, it demonstrates great potential as an anode for Li/Na storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0015 #advs4687-bib-0016 #advs4687-bib-0017"><sup>15–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Notably, most of the reported cathodes are crystalline frameworks in which Na<sup>+</sup> intercalates into certain active sites and diffuses along specific paths, thus limiting the specific capacity and overall reaction kinetics. Worse still, the bulk framework will be pulverized during the repeated Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation process due to variation of stresses, leading to poor cycling stability.</p><p>To solve the above issues, much attention has been devoted to the amorphization strategy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0018 #advs4687-bib-0019"><sup>18,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In comparison, isotropic amorphous materials with abundant porous channels and unsaturated coordination sites provide extra Na<sup>+</sup> storage sites and improve Na<sup>+</sup> binding ability, ensuring rapid intercalation kinetics compared to their crystalline counterpart.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0018 #advs4687-bib-0019 #advs4687-bib-0020"><sup>18–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, amorphous materials with characteristic open frameworks allow only small volume expansion during sodiation/desodiation process, which is beneficial for long‐term cyclability. However, their inherently low electrical conductivity reduces the electrochemical activity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0021 #advs4687-bib-0022"><sup>21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recently, many researchers have reported that the construction of defects in amorphous metal oxides such as vacancies can boost intrinsic electronic conductivity and facilitate charge transport.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0019 #advs4687-bib-0023 #advs4687-bib-0024"><sup>19,23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the complex and unscalable synthesis limits their applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Hence, a promising cathode design for sodium storage should balance high stability and fast kinetics by harmonizing the individual advantages of crystalline and amorphous phases. To achieve this, the construction of the amorphous/crystalline A/C heterostructure is an ideal strategy to improve the electrochemical performance of SIBs, as they tend to have better physicochemical properties than their single counterparts.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0026 #advs4687-bib-0027"><sup>26,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The abundant unsaturated coordination sites of the amorphous phase offer plentiful active sites while the crystalline phase boosts electron transfer. In addition, the heterointerfaces A/C can significantly optimize the adsorption/desorption of ions, as well as, electron densities of the interfacial sites, thereby facilitating the charge transfer during repeated cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Further, the structural stability can be enhanced due to the synergistic effect between different phases.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite these promising prospects, the fabrication of such amorphous/crystalline heterostructure electrodes remains to be a great challenge. Furthermore, most reported heterophase materials are either heterogeneous, in which the crystalline and amorphous interfaces consist of different chemical compositions, such as Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (crystalline)/TiO<sub>2</sub> (amorphous),<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> or homogeneous materials with the sparse crystalline and amorphous interfaces, such as Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, Si, and SnO<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0026 #advs4687-bib-0031 #advs4687-bib-0032"><sup>26,31,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, their cycle life has been so far limited to less than 1000 cycles. Further, how to rationally design and controllably synthesize well‐defined crystalline/amorphous heterophase with similar chemical components remains a major challenge. Further, there is still a lack of powerful theoretical guidance for the design of high‐performance heterophase electrodes, and its interaction functions and underlying mechanisms still seem blind.</p><p>Herein, we report the design of a unique engineered defect‐rich V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> amorphous/crystalline heterophase structure (donated as A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS) composed of oxygen‐deficient amorphous V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow mesoporous core (A‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/HMC) and lattice‐distorted crystalline V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> shell (C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>/S) encapsulated by carbon, which is successfully prepared via a one‐pot hydrothermal method followed by a reduction reaction and activation process (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs4687-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>). Based on DFT calculations, in pristine heterophase (defects‐free) A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐HCS, the adsorption energy is significantly reduced at the heterointerface sites due to the large difference in kinetics properties between the crystalline and amorphous phases, indicating that the sparse interfacial sites limit the optimization of adsorption energy, thus sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> kinetics. In contrast, the defective heterophase A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS demonstrates the strongest Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy in either the crystalline phase, amorphous phase, or even at heterointerfaces sites, indicating that the defects induce the formation of highly dense A/C interfacial sites, which can not only modulate the electronic structure and enrich active sites, but also lowers the energy barriers, and promote adsorption energies, thus rapid Na<sup>+</sup> kinetics (Figure <link href="#advs4687-fig-0001">1b,c</link>). In particular, tailoring the unsaturated active site is an effective strategy to promote charge transfer. In addition, the ex‐HRTEM and ex‐XRD results demonstrate that the dense A/C interfacial sites can be well maintained even after long‐term cyclability, further confirming the robust stability of A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS. Benefiting from these merits, the A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS exhibits superior electrochemical properties when employed for the first time as a cathode for sodium storage in the range of 1.5–4.0 V: a high capacity of 261 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is achieved at a low rate of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even at a high rate of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS cathode still retains a high capacity of 192 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 6000 cycles, outperforming most of the reported vanadium‐based cathodes for SIBs. These results evidenced that the electrochemical properties of electrode materials depend not only on the oxidation state but also largely on their crystal structure and morphology. Further, this work sheds light on promising dense heterophase material design for next‐generation SIBs from both an experimental and theoretical perspective.</p><figure xml:id="advs4687-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs4687:advs4687-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs4687-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of interfacial defect engineering and the electrochemical advantages of high‐density heterointerfaces. a) Model of pure A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐HCS and defect‐rich porous structure A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS, b) bandgap, and c) diffusion path.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0030"><title type="main">Synthesis and Characterizations</title><p>The synthesis process of the amorphous/crystalline V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>‐based compounds core–shells encapsulated by carbon (denoted as A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐M) is schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information. Typically, the V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> spheres precursor was prepared via a one‐pot solvothermal method as described in our previous report (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Its XRD profile shows only one diffraction pattern at 21° corresponding to the (001) plane of V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). First, the precursor was reduced at 500 °C under Ar/H<sub>2</sub> for 2 h to obtain A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐CS (synthesis route in Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images show spheres with a diameter of ≈500 nm (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S4a,b</link>, Supporting Information). A transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image demonstrates a like core–shell morphology with an average shell thickness of ≈80 nm (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5a</link>, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image confirms its core–shell structure (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5b</link>, Supporting Information). The HRTEM of the core region shows no obvious lattice fringes, and its corresponding fast Fourier transform (FFT) pattern exhibits diffuse rings. In contrast, the HRTEM of the shell region demonstrates manifest interplanar spacing of 0.37 nm, which corresponds to the (012) plane of the rhombohedral V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Further, its FFT patterns reveal bright spots, indicating that a typical core–shell is composed of an amorphous core and crystalline shell (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5b1,b2</link>, Supporting Information). In addition, the HRTEM also displays a thin layer of carbon. HADDF and its corresponding EDX mapping images verify that V, O, and C elements are evenly distributed on the microsphere's surface (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5c</link>, Supporting Information). Second, the A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐WCS sample was achieved by annealing of A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐CS for 2 h under N<sub>2</sub> (see the synthesis route in Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). As shown in Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S4c,d</link> (Supporting information), the SEM images still reveal a spherical morphology with a smooth surface. The TEM image demonstrates a well‐defined core–shell structure, and the thickness of the crystalline V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> shell decreases from 80 to 60 nm (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5d</link>, Supporting Information). A clear interface has proved to be beneficial for long‐term cyclability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0026 #advs4687-bib-0033 #advs4687-bib-0034"><sup>26,33,34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The HRTEM image (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5e</link>, Supporting Information) and its corresponding FFT patterns (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5e1,2</link>, Supporting Information) demonstrate the amorphous nature of the V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> core and the single crystallinity of the V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> shell, which are well consistent with the A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐CS. The corresponding EDX mappings images reveal that the elements of V, O, and C are distributed uniformly in the as‐prepared composite, verifying that the V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> core shell is coated by carbon (Figure <link href="#advs4687-supitem-0001">S5f</link>, Supporting Information). The abundant unsaturated coordination sites created by lattice distortion enlarged Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion channels in the crystalline phase, thereby optimizing its kinetics to be compatible with the oxygen‐vacancy‐rich amorphous phase. The introduction of defects effectively enriches active sites and modulates the electronic configuration in heterointerface sites. This significantly reduces the high contrast of the kinetic properties between the crystalline and amorphous phases and induces the formation of dense highly dense A/C interfaces, with a strong synergistic effect. In contrast, the perfect heterophase (defects‐free) A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐HCS demonstrates sparse interfacial sites with a limited synergistic effect. DFT calculations further reveal that the dense heterointerfaces efficiently optimize Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energies, and lower Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barriers, thus promoting Na<sup>+</sup> kinetics. In addition, the ex‐HRTEM and ex‐XRD results indicate that the dense A/C interfacial sites can be well maintained even after long‐term cyclability, revealing the robust stability of A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS. As a result, such unique A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>@C‐HMCS exhibits outstanding electrochemical performances, with a high rate (261 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 2000 cycles) and ultrastable performance (192 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 6000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) when first used as a cathode for SIBs, outperforming most of the reported SIBs cathodes. According to our aforementioned experimental evidence, it has been demonstrated that the electrochemical properties of a cathodic material depend not only on the oxidation state of the material but also largely on its crystal structure and morphology. This work provides new fundamental insights into the design of dense heterophase electrodes for electrochemical energy storage and beyond.</p></section><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0080"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs4687-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Synthesis—Synthesis of A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐CS and A/C‐V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@C‐WCS</title><p>The V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> spheres precursor (V‐glycerate) was prepared as described in a previous hydrothermal method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4687-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In a typical procedure, 0.6 mL VO(OiPr)<sub>3</sub> vanadium isopropoxide was added to a mixture of 6 mL glycerol and 60 mL isopropyl alcohol and magnetically stirred for 15 min.
