However, the low conductivity and excessive volume variation of TMPs during insertion/extraction of sodium ions result in a poor rate performance and long‐term cycling stability, largely limiting their practical application. In this paper, NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles encapsulated in three‐dimensional graphene (NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO) were obtained from the flower‐like spherical α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> by phosphating and carbon encapsulation processes. When used as a sodium‐ion batteries anode material, the NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO composite shows an excellent cycling performance (117 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 8000 cycles). The outstanding electrochemical performance of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO is ascribed to the synergistic effect of the rGO and NiP<sub>2</sub>. The rGO wrapped on the NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles build a conductive way, improving ionic and electronic conductivity. The effective combination of NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles with graphene greatly reduces the aggregation and pulverization of NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles during the discharge/charge process. This study may shed light on the construction of high‐performance anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries and to other electrode materials.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Flower‐like spherical α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> derived NiP<sub>2</sub></b> wrapped by rGO display excellent ultralong cycling performance at high current density.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100387:asia202100387-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100387-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0001"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are one of the most successful secondary batteries, owing to the low cost, and high energy density, and the similar working principle as lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs).<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> However, the larger sodium ion's ionic radius than lithium (1.02 Å vs. 0.76 Å of Li<sup>+</sup>), lead to a slow reaction kinetics and poor electrochemical performance of SIBs.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Therefore, many of the electrode materials used for LIBs aren't suitable for SIBs<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> and it is desirable to find superior anode materials for SIBs, which have a long cycle life, and high specific capacity.</p><p>Among various anode electrode materials, such as transition metal sulfides, and phosphides, have displayed excellent rate performance and cycling performance as anode materials of SIBs.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0009"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Compared to sulfides, transition metal phosphides (TMPs; M=Cu,<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0012"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Co,<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0002 #asia202100387-bib-0007 #asia202100387-bib-0017"><sup>[1a,2b,6]</sup></link> Fe,<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0020"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Ni,<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0025"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Ge,<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0032"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Mo,<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0033"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Zn<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0036"><sup>[11]</sup></link>) have a huge potential, due to its high theoretical specific capacity (500–2000 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) and low operating voltage (0.4–1.3 V). Recently, it has been reported that nickel phosphide (NiP<sub>2</sub>) displays outstanding electrochemical properties in terms of overall water splitting and LIBs.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0030 #asia202100387-bib-0037"><sup>[8e,12]</sup></link> Previous works have proved that developing nanostructured nickel phosphide could enhance the electrochemical performance. For example, Luo et al. reported carbon‐coated NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles anchored on CNTs as outstanding anode materials for LIBs.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0030"><sup>[8e]</sup></link> It achieved a high capacity of 654.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles. Although NiP<sub>2</sub> exhibits high theoretical specific capacity of 1329 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, it has rarely been reported as anode materials for SIBs due to the low conductivity and excessive volume variation of NiP<sub>2</sub> during the discharge/charge process. Therefore, it is of great challenge to improve the electrochemical performance by developing novel approaches for the synthesis and engineering of NiP<sub>2</sub> as anode materials of SIBs.</p><p>In order to solve the above problems, the researchers have come up with various strategies to improve sodium storage performance.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0040"><sup>[13]</sup></link> Firstly, as is known to us, it is a valid way to improve electrochemical performance by compositing design and nanostructure engineering. Combining highly conductive carbon materials with TMPs can enhance electronic and ionic conductivity or improve the stability of TMPs structure by nanostructure engineering. For example, Chen et al. prepared CHCS−CuP<sub>2</sub> nanocomposites by cross‐linking strategy, which achieve 451 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.08 A g<sup>−1</sup> in 200 cycles.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0013"><sup>[5a]</sup></link> In addition, a stable SEI film can be formed on the electrode surface, which is another effective method to construct high performance electrode materials.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0041"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Although these methods can improve the sodium storage performance of TMPs, we still need to continuously explore new ways to reach the supplies of various systems.</p><p>Herein, we developed a facile protocol to obtain NiP<sub>2</sub> microspheres by phosphorization of spherical flow‐like α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> by using chemical vapor deposition (CVD). Subsequently, the NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles were encapsulated in a three‐dimensional graphene layer by carbon encapsulation processes, as illustrated in scheme <link href="#asia202100387-fig-5001">1</link>. In addition, the prepared NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO composite maintains spherical morphology of α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>. The rGO‐wrapped NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles build a conductive pathway, improve the ionic and electronic conductivity of the electrode composite and delivers an impressive long cycling performance at high current rate as anode materials of sodium ion batteries (117 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 8000 cycles).</p><figure xml:id="asia202100387-fig-5001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100387:asia202100387-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100387-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic diagram of the synthetic process of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials synthesis</title><p>Graphene oxide (GO) was synthesized by modified Hummers method. NaNO<sub>3</sub> (3.0 g), graphite powder (3.0 g) and H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (138 ml) were concurrently added into a beaker hold in an ice bath. Within 30 minutes, KMnO<sub>4</sub> (18 g) was added slowly to the above solution and stirred to form a homogeneous mixed solution. DI water (240 mL) was added dropwise to dilute the reaction solution and the temperature of which was maintained blow 30 °C for one hour. Afterwards, the reaction was heated to 95 °C and stirred for 30 min. Then, 30% H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (18 mL) and 5% HCl were added into the suspension. Finally, the solution was repeatedly washed by DI water until the pH value of reaction product is around 7 and dried by lyophilization.</p><p><b>The α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub></b> was prepared by a one‐step hydrothermal reaction: In general, 2 mmol NiCl<sub>2</sub> ⋅ 6H<sub>2</sub>O, 5 mmol urea, 30 mL methanol and 10 mL N, N‐dimethylformamide (DMF) were mixed to form green transparent solution by vigorous stirring. The mixture was transferred to a 50 mL autoclave, which was further put into an oven of 180 °C for 12 h. When the reaction is completed, the autoclave cooled to room temperature naturally and the precipitate in the autoclave were washed by DI water. The obtained product is α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>.</p><p><b>The NiP<sub>2</sub></b> were fabricated by the chemical vapor deposition (CVD). In a typical procedure, the obtained α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanospheres and red phosphorus were placed at two separated quartz boats. The masses of α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> and red phosphorus are 0.1 g and 1 g, respectively, and heated at 500 °C for 2 h under the Ar/H<sub>2</sub> (10% H<sub>2</sub>) atmosphere. Then, the temperature was lowered to 250 °C at 5 °C/min and maintained for four hours to remove excess white phosphorus during the cooling process.</p><p><b>The NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO</b> nanocomposites were prepared by directly adding weak reducing agent: simply, 100 mg NiP<sub>2</sub>@C and 240 mg L‐ascorbic acid were added into 40 mL of 5 mg/mL GO solution. The above solution was stirred for 10 h and sonicated for 1 h. Then, it was heated at 95 °C for 2 h. The excess L‐Ascorbic acid in the product was washed away with water and then lyophilized. Finally, the sample was annealed at 500 °C for 2 h to gain NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The morphology of the materials was examined by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (SEM, Hitachi S‐4800) and high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM, JEOL TEM‐2010). The crystallinity of the samples was examined by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) using a Bruker D8 diffractometer with Cu−Kα radiation (λ=1.5406 Å). The carbon content in the product was determined by the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). The elemental contents and chemical states were examined by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) on a Phi X‐tool instrument.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>The electrochemical performances of the NiP<sub>2</sub> and NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO as anode materials of SIBs were performed in a 2032 type coin‐cells assembled in an Ar gas glove box. The working electrode was manufactured by forming the slurry of polytetrafluoroethylene (PVDF,10 wt%), Super P (20 wt%), and the active materials (NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO or NiP<sub>2</sub>, 70 wt%), and the black slurry was spread on a copper foil and held in blast drying oven at 60 °C for 12 h. The load weight of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO or NiP<sub>2</sub> on the current collector is about 1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Metallic Na, Whatman GF/D and 1.0 M NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> in DIGL YME were used as the counter electrode, separator, and electrolyte, respectively. The galvanostatic charge/discharge measurements of coin‐cells were performed on a multichannel battery testing system (CT2001 A, LAND). Electrochemical impedance spectral (EIS) and cyclic voltammetry (CV) was conducted on CHI 660 electrochemical workstation (Shanghai CH Instrument Co., Ltd.). The CV measurements is performed at a scan rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in a potential window of 3.0 to 0.005 V vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. The multichannel battery testing system (CT2001 A, LAND) was serviced to carry out the galvanostatic charge/discharge measurements.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0006"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The morphology of α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub>, NiP<sub>2</sub> and NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO are revealed by SEM and TEM. Figure <link href="#asia202100387-fig-0001">1</link>a–b shows SEM images of α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> precursor nickel hydroxide at different multiples, which has a spherical shape and a diameter of about 2 μm. The growth dynamics of α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> spheres were further explored by SEM in details, as shown in Figure S1. When the hydrothermal time is 30 min (Figure <link href="#asia202100387-fig-0001">1</link>a), only aggregated α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles without wrinkles were observed. As the growth proceeds, the wrinkles start to appear, the flower‐like morphology becomes more and more clear, and the size of the spheres becomes larger. After the hydrothermal growth of 12 hours (Figure <link href="#asia202100387-fig-0001">1</link>i), the diameter of the microspheres is about 2 μm. We picked the α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> with growth time of 12 hours to prepare NiP<sub>2</sub> and NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO. Figure <link href="#asia202100387-fig-0001">1</link>c and 1 f reveal that NiP<sub>2</sub> was obtained after phosphating of the precursor, and the flower‐like spherical morphology were maitained. It can be clearly seen in Figure <link href="#asia202100387-fig-0001">1</link>d and 1 h that NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO has a spherical shape and is uniformly embedded in the graphene, as indicated by the red arrows. The element mapping images of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO show homogeneous distribution of phosphorus, oxygen and carbon. High‐resolution TEM image in Figure <link href="#asia202100387-fig-0001">1</link>g and 1i further present lattice fringes of NiP<sub>2</sub> with d‐spacing of 0.193 nm and 0.223 nm, corresponding to the (220) and (211) plane of NiP<sub>2</sub>, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100387-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100387:asia202100387-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100387-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of (a–b) α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> microspheres, (c) NiP<sub>2</sub>, (d) NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO, (e) element mapping images of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO, TEM and HRTEM images of (f–g) NiP<sub>2</sub>, (h–i) NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO.</p></caption></figure><p>The diffraction peaks of the synthesized precursor corresponds to α‐Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> ⋅ 0.75H<sub>2</sub>O (JCPDS: 38‐0715) (Figure S2a). For the NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO electrode, the initial discharge and charge capacities were 384.4 and 251.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, indicating an initial coulombic efficiency of 65%. The NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO eletrodes still delivered at 139 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 3 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles and 297 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 60 cycles in Figure S4. When it turns back to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the battery still maintains a reversible specific capacity of 256.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating superior rate performance to native NiP<sub>2</sub>. The long cycling performance of this materials is quite impressive as compared to that of the TMPs for sodium ion batteries in the past three years studies.</p><p>It is anticipated that repeated insertion/extrantion of large sodium ions into the NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO during cycling at high current density could result in the pulverization of the anode materials, thus leading to the decline of the charge capacity of the SIBs. It is clearly observed that NiP<sub>2</sub> were pulverized upon cycling at high current density. However, due to the presence of rGO, the fine NiP<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles were still hold together and both the electronic and ionic conductivity of the anode materials were maintained. In contrast, cycling of pure NiP<sub>2</sub> anode equally cause crushing and loss of the electronic conductivity of the anode materials, thus leading to the fast decay of the capacity. To further explore sodium storage performance, the EIS spectra of the NiP<sub>2</sub> and NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO electrodes were measured before the cycle. As shown in Figure S7, the Nyquist diagram is consisted of a semicircle at a high frequency and a sloping line at a low frequency region.<link href="#asia202100387-bib-0006 #asia202100387-bib-0048"><sup>[2a,19]</sup></link> As seen, the charge transfer resistance (R<sub>ct</sub>) of NiP<sub>2</sub> (60.42 Ω) is much larger than that of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO (17.38 Ω), due to the enhanced electronic conductivity and reaction kinetics by graphene encapsulation.</p><p>The electrochemical performance and the reaction kinetics were carried out by CV at a range of scan rates. (1) The NiP<sub>2</sub> microsphere was encapsulated uniformly in 3D graphene, establishing a unique structure. (2) During the charge and discharge process, the 3D graphene can alleviate the large volume change of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO and aggregation and crushing of active material. It was greatly accelerated reaction kinetics in the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion extraction process.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100387-sec-0007"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, flower‐like Ni(OH)<sub>2</sub> microspheres with a layered surface‐structure were prepared by a hydrothermal method and NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO microspheres were fabricated by phosphating reaction and encapsulation of rGO, with the spherical morphology maintained. The unique nanostructure of NiP<sub>2</sub> and the rGO layers significantly enhanced the structural stability and electrical conductivity of NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO nanocomposites. When used as anode material of sodium‐ion batteries, the NiP<sub>2</sub>@rGO delivers a capacity of 117 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 8000 cycles and maintains capacity retention of 84.4%, which is mainly contributed to the pseudocapacitance. The sodium storage reaction mechanism was further confirmed by the ex‐situ XRD. This study reports a new facile method for the construction of an outstanding anode material for sodium‐ion batteries, which can be extended to other energy storage devices.</p></section><section
