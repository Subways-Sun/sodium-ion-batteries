information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>It is still very urgent and challenging to simultaneously develop high‐rate and long‐cycle oxide cathodes for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) because of the sluggish kinetics and complex multiphase evolution during cycling. Here, the concept of accurately manipulating structural evolution and formulating high‐performance heterostructured biphasic layered oxide cathodes by local chemistry and orbital hybridization modulation is reported. The P2‐structure stoichiometric composition of the cathode material shows a layered P2‐ and O3‐type heterostructure that is explicitly evidenced by various macroscale and atomic‐scale techniques. Surprisingly, the heterostructured cathode displays excellent rate performance, remarkable cycling stability (capacity retention of 82.16% after 600 cycles at 2 C), and outstanding compatibility with hard carbon anode because of the integrated advantages of intergrowth structure and local environment regulation. Meanwhile, the formation process from precursors during calcination and the highly reversible dynamic structural evolution during the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation process are clearly articulated by a series of in situ characterization techniques. Also, the intrinsic structural properties and corresponding electrochemical behavior are further elucidated by the density of states and electron localization function of density functional theory calculations. Overall, this strategy, which finely tunes the local chemistry and orbitals hybridization for high‐performance SIBs, will open up a new field for other materials.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202202695-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>An abnormal heterostructured biphasic layered oxide cathode for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) is successfully constructed, and its dynamic formation process, intrinsic structural properties, and electrochemical behavior are elucidated by a series of in situ characterization techniques and density functional theory calculations. The concept of accurately manipulating structural evolution and formulating heterostructured cathode materials by local chemistry and orbital hybridization modulation is further demonstrated.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202202695:adma202202695-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202202695-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202202695-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202202695-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The year of 2019 has witnessed that the discovery and development of Li‐ion batteries (LIBs) regarded as indispensable in modern society was awarded with Nobel Prize in Chemistry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0001 #adma202202695-bib-0002 #adma202202695-bib-0003 #adma202202695-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The increasing demand from various applications surge and limited reserves of Li arouse a rapidly rising cost, casting a giant shadow on the LIBs sustainability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0005 #adma202202695-bib-0006 #adma202202695-bib-0007 #adma202202695-bib-0008"><sup>5–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is urgent to seek alternative batteries that are cost‐effective. Considering that sodium is readily available on account of the worldwide distribution, sixth abundance in the earth crust elements, and resultant low cost, as well as showing the similarity of ion storage mechanism with LIBs, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are ideal alternatives to LIBs for low/medium speed electric vehicles and large‐scale electrical energy storage systems (EESs).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0009 #adma202202695-bib-0010 #adma202202695-bib-0011 #adma202202695-bib-0012 #adma202202695-bib-0013 #adma202202695-bib-0014 #adma202202695-bib-0015 #adma202202695-bib-0016 #adma202202695-bib-0017"><sup>9–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Cathode materials, which could be viewed as one of the key determinants for the electrochemical performance of SIBs, have been vastly investigated.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0018 #adma202202695-bib-0019 #adma202202695-bib-0020 #adma202202695-bib-0021 #adma202202695-bib-0022"><sup>18–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among all the cathodes, layered transition metal oxides denoted as Na<sub>x</sub>MO<sub>2</sub> where M there stands for transition metal have become increasingly popular because of their exceptional ionic conductive ability, environmental friendliness, and feasible production at industrial level.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0023 #adma202202695-bib-0024 #adma202202695-bib-0025 #adma202202695-bib-0026 #adma202202695-bib-0027"><sup>23–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Developing high‐rate and long‐cycle Na<sub>x</sub>MO<sub>2</sub> for SIBs remains challenging. The concepts in composite structures of sodium oxide cathodes have attracted universal attention, which may be an effective way to solve the problem mentioned above. The heterostructured materials could integrate the merits of biphasic structures and realize multifunctional advantages as well as greatly enhance electrochemical performance when compared with their single‐phase counterparts.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0028 #adma202202695-bib-0029 #adma202202695-bib-0030"><sup>28–30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to the previous reports, depending on the sites Na occupied (prismatic or octahedral) and the O layer stacking sequence (ABBA or ABCABC), Na<sub>x</sub>MO<sub>2</sub> mainly include P2 (Na/M &lt; 0.8, prismatic, ABBA) and O3 (Na/M &gt; 0.8, octahedral, ABCABC) structures.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0031 #adma202202695-bib-0032 #adma202202695-bib-0033 #adma202202695-bib-0034"><sup>31–34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is worth noting that the P2‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode shows potential application for practical SIBs, however, it still suffers from intrinsic structural instability and sluggish kinetics. So, we precisely design and formulate composite structures to ameliorate sluggish kinetics and improve structural stability for high‐rate and long‐cycle life SIBs based on local chemistry and orbital hybridization modulation, which provides a new route of exploiting novel and high‐performance cathode materials. Moreover, the dynamic formation process as well as structural evolution, intrinsic structural properties and electrochemical behavior are also important to be clearly elucidated.</p><p>Accordingly, using a classical P2‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material as a proof‐of‐concept system, an abnormal P2‐ and O3‐type heterostructured biphasic layered oxide cathode material P2/O3‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Sn<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (P2/O3‐NaNMS) through substitution of Sn into Mn sites has been constructed. The local chemistry modulates the formation energy and crystal structural evolution, which induces a layered intergrowth oxide cathode. Meanwhile, the intrinsic structural properties and the orbitals hybridization mechanism between transition metal and oxygen atoms are further elucidating by density functional theory calculations, which account for the outstanding electrochemical performance in both sodium half‐cell and full‐cell systems. Also, the cathode material is systematically studied concerning underlying formation process, as well as atomic arrangements and phase transitions during Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation/intercalation via in situ high‐energy X‐ray diffraction (HEXRD), high‐angle annular dark‐field (HAADF) and annular bright‐field (ABF)‐scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) and in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) during charge and discharge techniques. The concepts of formulating high‐rate and long‐cycle heterostructured biphasic cathodes by local chemistry and orbital hybridization modulation and fundamental understanding of the electrochemical behavior of Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation open a new door of opportunity for designing novel high‐performance SIB cathode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202695-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The P2/O3‐NaNMS cathode material that synthesized by solid‐state reaction under high temperature presents a stoichiometric composition of P2‐type structure. The collected XRD patterns were used for the analyzation of the crystal structures, and the results show that the Bragg peaks of P2/O3‐NaNMS can be well indexed to the heterostructure consisting of a typical P2 phase with <i>P</i><sub>63</sub>/<i>mmc</i> space group and O3 α‐NaFeO<sub>2</sub> layered phase assigned to a rhombohedral R<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202202695:adma202202695-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma202202695-math-0001.png"><semantics><mrow><mover accent="true"><mn>3</mn><mo stretchy="true">¯</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">$\overline 3 $</annotation></semantics></math>m space group (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0035 #adma202202695-bib-0036 #adma202202695-bib-0037"><sup>35–37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> So, the Sn substitution could induce the phase transition and form a P2‐ and O3‐type heterostructured layered oxide cathode material. Meanwhile, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images at various magnifications (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0001">1b,c</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) show that the P2/O3‐NaNMS sample is composed of plate‐like particles with a size ≈500 nm to 2 µm. As presented in Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0002">2d</link>, in situ HEXRD unraveled the formation process of P2/O3‐NaNMS material at different temperatures from 25 to 1000 °C, the results of which under ambient temperature display typical peaks belonging to Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NiO, Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and SnO<sub>2</sub> in precursor.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0038 #adma202202695-bib-0039"><sup>38–39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During heating process, some of the chemical compositions above gradually disappear while new characteristic peaks including (002), (004) of P2 phase progressively emerged as the temperature rises to 300 °C. Meanwhile, (003), (006), and other O3‐type phase characteristic peaks begin to appear as the material is heated to 500 °C followed by the emergence of the stable P2‐ and O3‐type heterostructure with the temperature continuing to rise to 800 °C. Besides, the heterostructure maintains high stability when the temperature rises to 1000 °C. Moreover, all peaks shift toward a higher angle as a result of contracting lattice under ambient temperature during the cooling process. The transformation process of the P2/O3‐NaNMS from precursors during calcination at varying temperatures could be more elaborately revealed by additional intensity contour maps (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). In addition, in order for further check on the local structure at the atomic level, detailed structural information of a P2‐ and O3‐type heterostructured oxide P2/O3‐NaNMS was revealed via STEM techniques along with the colored patterns (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0001">1e,f</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information) wherein the dark dots in ABF‐STEM and bright spots in HAADF‐STEM represent transition metal atoms. Meanwhile, the light Na and O atoms correspond to faint dark spots in ABF‐STEM.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0040 #adma202202695-bib-0041 #adma202202695-bib-0042 #adma202202695-bib-0043 #adma202202695-bib-0044"><sup>40–44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The HAADF‐STEM image and line profiles clearly display the adjacent crystal plane with d‐spacing of 0.561 and 0.545 nm, which corresponds to the (002) plane of P2 phase and (003) plane of O3 phase, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). Furthermore, the arrangements of atoms shown in HAADF and ABF‐STEM images accord well with the inserted P2‐type and O3‐type atomic models, further testifying to the P2 and O3 heterostructure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0045 #adma202202695-bib-0046"><sup>45–46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> All of the above local observations display good consistence with the XRD and in‐situ HEXRD results, showing the existence of a P2/O3 biphasic structure. The energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping together with corresponding EDS spectrum confirmed the uniform elemental distribution of Na, Ni, Mn, Sn, and O elements in P2/O3‐NaNMS cathode material (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0001">1g</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information).</p><figure The P2‐NaNM electrode displays two obvious voltage plateaus (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>), which are identical with those reported in previous works.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0047"><sup>47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a contrast, the P2/O3‐NaNMS electrode presents smooth galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD) curves as displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0002">2b</link>, and it delivers a reversible specific capacity of 80.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C, displaying well consistence with cyclic voltammetry (CV) results collected at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information). Moreover, quantitative calculation of the <i>b</i> parameter values in the equation <i>i = av<sup>b</sup></i> could further elaborate the mechanism of Na<sup>+</sup> storage. The <i>b</i> values demonstrate a dominant capacitive mechanism feature of sodium storage in P2/O3‐NaNMS electrode (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0003">3i</link>; Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S10</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0049 #adma202202695-bib-0050"><sup>49–50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, the P2/O3‐NaNMS electrode maintains a remarkable 82.16% capacity retention even after 600 cycles even at a high 2 C current density together with an outstandingly stable Coulombic efficiency (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-fig-0003">3j</link>). In sum, a P2/O3 cathode material exhibiting superior long cycling stability and excellent rate capability has been successfully designed through local chemistry modulation strategy. Therefore, the dynamic phase evolution of P2/O3‐NaNMS is also monitored via in situ charge and discharge XRD upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction and intercalation within 2.5–4.15 V.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0051 #adma202202695-bib-0052 #adma202202695-bib-0053"><sup>51–53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The pristine P2/O3‐NaNMS could be identified by the (002), (004), (100), (102), and (104) characteristic peaks of P2 phase and the (003), (006), (101), (102), (104), and (015) peaks of O3 phase. Specifically, the P2‐type (002) characteristic peaks and O3‐type (003) characteristic peaks consecutively shift to lower angles, which suggests indicating an increase in interlayer spacing because adjacent layers present more enhanced repulsive interaction. As a contrast, the (10<i>l</i>) peaks of P2 and O3‐type phases shift toward higher angles, indicating the contracting ab‐plane. The (003) peak of O3 phase intensity weakened gradually and then disappeared with further extraction of Na<sup>+</sup>. Meanwhile, the complete disappearance of the (104)<sub>O3</sub> peak upon further charging could demonstrate P3 phase emerges as a result of a O3–P3 structural transformation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0054"><sup>54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During discharging, an exact opposite structural evolution occurs. Furthermore, it is worth mentioning that the excellent cycling stability could be ascribed to the identical dynamic evolution of phase to that of the first cycle occurring during the second charging stage (Figure <link href="#adma202202695-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information). The local chemistry regulation contributes to the highly reversible phase transition through the suppression of the slide of transition‐metal layers, leading to a stable intercalation host that could effectively resist deformation, thus bringing about outstanding electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202695-bib-0055"><sup>55</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In addition to the above‐mentioned properties, P2/O3‐NaNMS cathode more remarkably displays exceptional full batteries performance that could preliminarily show the practicability of the P2/O3‐NaNMS material in cathode. Owing to the electronic structure as well as similarity between ionic radii, Sn elemental substitution strategy not only realizes the existence of P2‐type and O3‐type heterostructured layered oxide and high structural reversibility through suppressing the gliding of transition‐metal layers, but also regulates redox potential through effectively and accurately manipulating hybridization between orbitals of oxygen and transition metal atoms. Also, the underlying working mechanism are detected through various advanced characterization techniques. These findings state a new viewpoint of local chemistry and orbital hybridization modulation, which could also be used in other high‐performance SIBs as well as further inspire the exploitation of new chemistries and materials.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202695-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 51971124, 52171217), the State Key Laboratory of Electrical Insulation and Power Equipment, Xi'an Jiaotong University (EIPE22208). the National Postdoctoral Program for Innovative Talents (Grant Nos.
