Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>The exploration of ideal electrode materials overcoming the critical problems of large electrode volume changes and sluggish redox kinetics induced by large ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> ions is highly desirable for sodium/potassium‐ion batteries (SIBs/PIBs) toward large‐scale applications. The present work demonstrates that single‐phase ternary cobalt phosphoselenide (CoPSe) in the form of nanoparticles embedded in a layered metal–organic framework (MOF)‐derived N‐doped carbon matrix (CoPSe/NC) represents an ultrastable and high‐rate anode material for SIBs/PIBs. The CoPSe/NC is fabricated by using the MOF as both a template and precursor, coupled with in situ synchronous phosphorization/selenization reactions. The CoPSe anode holds a set of intrinsic merits such as lower mechanical stress, enhanced reaction kinetics, as well as higher theoretical capacity and lower discharge voltage relative to its counterpart of CoSe<sub>2</sub>, and suppressed shuttle effect with higher intrinsic electrical conductivity relative to CoPS. The involved mechanisms are evidenced by substantial characterizations and density functional theory (DFT) calculations. Consequently, the CoPSe/NC anode shows an outstanding long‐cycle stability and rate performance for SIBs and PIBs. Moreover, the CoPSe/NC‐based Na‐ion full cell can achieve a higher energy density of 274 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, surpassing that based on CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC and most state‐of‐the‐art Na‐ion full cells based on P‐, Se‐, or S‐containing binary/ternary anodes to date.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202007262-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Single‐phase ternary cobalt phosphoselenide nanoparticles embedded in a layered metal–organic framework (MOF)‐derived N‐doped carbon matrix (CoPSe/NC) are fabricated through in situ synchronous phosphorization/selenization reactions using the MOF as both a template and precursor. xml:id="adma202007262-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202007262-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>The continuously increasing consumption of non‐renewable energy resources calls for sustainable and low‐cost energy storage devices.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs) as emerging energy storage systems are considered as potential substitutes for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), owing to the natural abundance of sodium/potassium with low cost.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0002 #adma202007262-bib-0003"><sup>2,3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the critical problems of the large electrode volume change and sluggish redox kinetics during electrochemical cycle induced by the large ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> ions resulted in poor cyclability and inferior rate capability, presenting an obstacle toward their practical applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0004 #adma202007262-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite of much progress made in the past few years, exploring new electrode materials for SIBs and PIBs with excellent cycle and rate performance is still challenging.</p><p>Transition metal sulfides/selenides (TMS/TMSe) have emerged as promising electrode candidates for SIBs and PIBs due to their high theoretical specific capacity, diverse electronic properties, and low cost.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Extensive effort has been devoted to improving the electrochemical Na/K‐storage performance of TMS/TMSe electrodes by multiple‐phase combination<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0007 #adma202007262-bib-0008 #adma202007262-bib-0009"><sup>7–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> cation doping,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0010 #adma202007262-bib-0011"><sup>10,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanostructure optimization,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0012 #adma202007262-bib-0013 #adma202007262-bib-0014 #adma202007262-bib-0015 #adma202007262-bib-0016"><sup>12–16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon modification,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0017 #adma202007262-bib-0018 #adma202007262-bib-0019"><sup>17–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and working voltage regulation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In particular, constructing single‐phase ternary materials which exert atomic‐level interface engineering and electric‐field effect has been expected as an effective strategy to promote the electrochemical performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0021 #adma202007262-bib-0022 #adma202007262-bib-0023"><sup>21–23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is noted that ternary metal phosphorus chalcogenides (MPX, X = S, Se) typically containing two different anions show the modulated electronic structure and enhanced activity for various energy applications compared with conventional TMS/TMSe.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0024 #adma202007262-bib-0025 #adma202007262-bib-0026 #adma202007262-bib-0027 #adma202007262-bib-0028 #adma202007262-bib-0029 #adma202007262-bib-0030 #adma202007262-bib-0031 #adma202007262-bib-0032 #adma202007262-bib-0033 #adma202007262-bib-0034 #adma202007262-bib-0035 #adma202007262-bib-0036"><sup>24–36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, ternary metal phosphorus sulfides (MPS) have been intensively synthesized and investigated in batteries, such as 2D MPS<sub>3</sub> (M = Fe, Co, Ni) nanosheets,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> 2D SnP<sub>2</sub>S<sub>6</sub> nanosheets/graphene hybrid,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Cu<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>/C composite.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0036"><sup>36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, scarcely studied in batteries were ternary metal phosphorus selenides (MPSe) due to their difficulty in synthesis despite a set of characteristics desirable for batteries including higher conductivity and lower shuttle effect derived from Se atoms relative to MPS<sub>3</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The development of new ternary MPSe materials with combined intrinsic advantages for long‐life and high‐rate SIBs/PIB is highly desirable.</p><p>Herein by employing a metal–organic framework (MOF) as both a precursor and template, we report the synthesis of ternary cobalt phosphoselenide (CoPSe) that is in the form of nanoparticle embedded in the layered MOF‐derived N‐doped carbon matrix (CoPSe/NC) via in situ synchronous phosphorization/selenization reactions. The N‐doped carbon (NC) matrix are made of coassembly of carbon nanosheets and carbon nanotubes, which are originated from the metal‐catalyzed carbonization reaction of the layered MOF precursor.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is noteworthy that this is the first time that pure‐phase CoPSe with the cubic pyrite structure has been synthesized and its intrinsic properties have been investigated in depth as anodes of SIBs and PIBs. The ternary CoPSe/NC composite as battery anodes holds a set of intrinsic merits relative to its counterparts of CoSe<sub>2</sub> or CoPS: i) the substitution of P atoms for Se atoms imparts CoPSe not only an enhanced theoretical Na/K storage capacity, but also a lower mechanical stress during discharge compared to CoSe<sub>2</sub>. ii) the presence of Se and P exerts a synergistic effect on the electronic structure of the center metal Co atoms in CoPSe, enabling the enhanced electrochemical reaction kinetics and low redox potential enabling higher energy density in a full cell than that of CoSe<sub>2</sub>; iii) CoPSe possesses a higher intrinsic electrical conductivity and, especially, suppressed shuttle effect compared to CoPS, due to the replacement of S atoms by Se atoms. Besides, the unique 3D layered carbon matrix of the CoPSe/NC composite can not only accelerate the transport kinetics of ions and electrons, but also inhibit the aggregation of CoPSe nanoparticles, which effectively enhance the electrode stability and alleviate the volume changes during the Na/K insertion/extraction process. Therefore, the CoPSe/NC anode exhibits superior comprehensive Na/K storage performance over that based on CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC and CoPS/NC, such as outstanding cycling stability and rate capability. Notably, the Na‐ion full cell based on the CoPSe/NC anode can achieve an energy density of 274 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, surpassing that of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC‐based full cell and most state‐of‐the‐art Na‐ion full cells based on P‐, Se‐, or S‐containing binary/ternary anodes.</p><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> illustrates the synthesis process of the CoPSe/NC composite. In brief, the layered dia‐Co(MeIm)<sub>2</sub> frameworks consisting of ultrathin nanosheets with a diameter of ≈2 µm (Figure S1, Supporting Information) were first synthesized by coordinating cobalt (II) acetate with 2‐methylimidazole ligands (Figure S2, Supporting Information) according to the reported method with modification.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Then the dia‐Co(MeIm)<sub>2</sub> frameworks converted into Co metal nanoparticles embedded in N‐doped carbon matrix (Co/NC) made of carbon nanosheets and carbon nanotubes upon a pyrolysis process under a N<sub>2</sub> flow (Figures S3 and S4, Supporting Information). Finally, the CoPSe/NC composite was synthesized through the conversion of the Co/NC under a synchronous phosphorization and selenization process by the thermal evaporation of a stoichiometric mixture of elemental phosphorus and selenium powders in a vacuum tube (Figure S5, Supporting Information). It is noted that the design of synchronous phosphorization and selenization is essential for the preparation of pure‐phase ternary CoPSe. The contrast experiments of step‐by‐step phosphorization or selenization lead to the formation of CoP<sub>3</sub> or mixture of CoP<sub>3</sub> and CoPSe (Figure S6, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma202007262-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007262-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the synthesis process of the CoPSe/NC composite.</caption></figure><p>The CoPSe possesses a similar crystal structure to that of cubic structured CoSe<sub>2</sub>, where four Se atoms among the eight in the unit cell of CoSe<sub>2</sub> are replaced by P atoms and each Co atom is coordinated in an octahedral ligand field (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). Instead of Co<sup>2+</sup> octahedra and Se<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> dumbbells in CoSe<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0040 #adma202007262-bib-0041"><sup>40,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> CoPSe has Co<sup>3+</sup> octahedra and P<sup>2−</sup>/Se<sup>−</sup> dumbbells with a uniform distribution of P<sup>2−</sup> and Se<sup>−</sup> atoms.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The charge density difference indicates that Co atoms of CoPSe are in a state of further charge depletion (shown as the cyan region) and the bonds of CoP and SeP are in a state of further charge accumulation (shown as the yellow region) compared to those of CoSe<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2b</link>), which lead to valence increase of Co atoms in CoPSe and stronger covalent interactions between the bonding atoms with enhanced structural stability, respectively. The energy level diagrams of the CoPSe and CoSe<sub>2</sub> are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2c</link>, where the Co 3d orbitals are split into t<sub>2g</sub> and e<sub>g</sub><sup>*</sup> orbital states that are of non‐bonding and anti‐bonding characteristics, respectively. The Co<sup>2+</sup> ions in CoSe<sub>2</sub> has the valence electronic configuration of 3d<sup>7</sup> (t<sub>2g</sub><sup>6</sup>e<sub>g</sub><sup>1</sup>), in which the highest occupied states are of anti‐bonding nature. In contrast, the high‐valence Co<sup>3+</sup> ions in CoPSe has the valence electronic configuration of 3d<sup>6</sup> (t<sub>2g</sub><sup>6</sup>e<sub>g</sub><sup>0</sup>) where the anti‐bonding e<sub>g</sub><sup>*</sup> orbitals are depleted, which strengthens the chemical bonding between Co and ligands and thus enhances the chemical stability of the CoPSe. Therefore, the CoPSe could show a lower discharge voltage as an anode material and a wider voltage windows with a higher energy density in full cells compared to the CoSe<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="adma202007262-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007262-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural characterization of the CoPSe/NC composite. a) Crystal structure illustration of pyrite‐phase CoSe<sub>2</sub> and CoPSe. b) Charge density difference of CoSe<sub>2</sub> and CoPSe structures. The cyan and yellow isosurfaces represent electron depletion and accumulation (isosurface value  =  0.01 e Å<sup>−3</sup>), respectively. c) Energy level diagrams of the frontier molecular orbitals for pyrite‐phase CoSe<sub>2</sub> and CoPSe structures. d) XRD pattern, e,f) SEM, g) TEM, h) HRTEM, i) SEM‐EDX element mapping, and j−l) XPS spectra of the CoPSe/NC composite.</caption></figure><p>The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the CoPSe/NC in Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2d</link> matches well the simulated pattern of cubic‐CoPSe (space group <i>Pa</i><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma202007262-math-0001.png"><mover accent="true"><mn>3</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover></math>, <i>a</i> = 5.608 Å, Materials Project collection code mp‐1226014) that is distinct from the standard pattern of CoSe<sub>2</sub> (space group <i>Pa</i><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/adma202007262-math-0002.png"><mover accent="true"><mn>3</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover></math>, <i>a</i> = 5.859 Å, JCPDS 88‐1712), indicating the cubic phase of the synthesized CoPSe. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images in Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2e</link> display that the CoPSe/NC composite holds a disk‐like 3D layered architecture with an average diameter of ≈2 µm, preserving the morphology of the Co/NC precursor (Figure S3, Supporting Information). It can be seen that CoPSe nanoparticles are uniformly embedded in the layered carbon matrix (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2f</link>). The average size of CoPSe nanoparticles is ≈30 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2f</link>,<link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">g</link>), slightly larger than that of Co nanoparticles in the Co/NC precursor due to the introducing of P and Se during the phosphorization and selenization process. The high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image of a CoPSe nanoparticle (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2h</link>) shows clear fringe spacings of ≈2.50 Å, corresponding to the (210) plane of the cubic‐phase CoPSe crystal. The energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping images (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2i</link>) show that C, N, Co, P, and Se elements are uniformly distributed over the entire CoPSe/NC composite. Specifically, the atomic ratio of Co, P, and Se in the CoPSe/NC is ≈1:1:1, confirming the elemental composition of CoPSe (Figure S7, Supporting Information). Raman spectrum of the CoPSe/NC (Figure S8, Supporting Information) shows a sharp peak at 170 cm<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to the in‐phase stretch (A<sub>g</sub>) for the chalcogenide dumbbells in the pyrite structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0043 #adma202007262-bib-0044"><sup>43,44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The two peaks at 1351 and 1586 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to D and G bands for graphitized carbon matrix with intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) of approximately 1.03 are also observed, respectively. The CoPSe/NC composite shows a BET surface area of 122.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> with a pore volume of 0.39 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S9, Supporting Information). Moreover, the pore size distribution curve indicates that the sizes of mesopore are mainly concentrated at 2.0 and 5.9 nm. The porosity of the CoPSe/NC is beneficial for the rapid ionic diffusion and mass transport.</p><p>The surface chemical states of the CoPSe/NC composite were further investigated using X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The full XPS spectrum (Figure S10a, Supporting Information) reveals the predominant signals of Co, P, Se, and C elements coming from the CoPSe/NC in addition to O element from oxygen absorbed on the surface of the CoPSe/NC sample. High‐resolution C 1s spectrum (Figure S10b, Supporting Information) can be fitted to the two main peaks of CC (284.5 eV) and CN (285.6 eV), which suggests the doping of N atom into carbon framework. Four N species including graphitic‐N (401.4 eV), pyrrolic‐N (400.1 eV), CoN<i><sub>x</sub></i> (399.0 eV), and pyridinic‐N (398.0 eV) could be distinguished from the N 1s spectrum (Figure S10c, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> High‐resolution Co 2p XPS spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2j</link>) shows two peaks located at 779.2 and 794.1 eV, corresponding to the 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and 2p<sub>1/2</sub> core levels of Co<sup>3+</sup>, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0046 #adma202007262-bib-0047"><sup>46,47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The absence of satellite peaks in Co 2p XPS spectrum is ascribed to the absence of unpaired valence electrons in the CoPSe (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2c</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0048 #adma202007262-bib-0049"><sup>48,49</sup></link><sup>]</sup> High‐valence Co<sup>3+</sup> ions in CoPSe provide more 3d electron orbits with a higher electron‐accepting ability than those of low‐valence Co<sup>2+</sup> ions in CoSe<sub>2</sub>, enabling a higher energy storage capacity. In the fitted P 2p spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2k</link>), two splitting peaks centered at 129.6 and 130.5 eV are assigned to 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and 2p<sub>1/2</sub> orbitals, respectively. The peak centered at higher binding energy (134.0 eV) could be assigned to the oxidized P species. Moreover, the additional peak at 138.2 eV can be attributed to the overlap of an Se Auger emission line and the P 2p core level, which is in agreement with the observation made elsewhere.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0026 #adma202007262-bib-0050"><sup>26,50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high‐resolution Se 3d spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0002">2l</link>) shows two peaks centered at 55.2 and 56.0 eV (Se 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Se 3d<sub>3/2</sub>), and the third peak at 61.8 eV corresponds to the oxidation state of Se species.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0050"><sup>50</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The electrochemical Na/K‐storage performance of the CoPSe/NC anode was investigated. For comparison, CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC composite where cubic‐structured CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles embedded in N‐doped carbon matrix was fabricated by direct selenization of the Co/NC precursor (Figures S11 and S12, Supporting Information) and employed as SIB/KIB anodes for electrochemical performance investigation. The cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of the CoPSe/NC electrode associating with sodiation/desodiation reactions during first‐three charge/discharge processes are shown in Figure S13a, Supporting Information. Compared with the CV curves of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode in Figure S13b, Supporting Information, it is found that the redox peaks of the CoPSe/NC electrode shift to lower voltage positions due to the introduction of P atoms. This phenomenon suggests that, when assembled as an SIB anode in a full cell with a given cathode material, the CoPSe/NC‐based full cell may possess a higher working voltage with a higher energy density. The galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) profiles of the CoPSe/NC electrode in the first three cycles at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> display obvious voltage plateaus during the charge/discharge processes (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>), which are in accordance with the CV analysis above. Besides, the CoPSe/NC electrode can deliver a high reversible capacity of 518 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, much higher than that of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode (470 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) (Figure S14, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma202007262-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007262-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SIB performance of the CoPSe/NC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrodes. a) Galvanostatic charge−discharge curves of the CoPSe/NC electrode at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Cycling performance at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 500 cycles. c) Long cycling performance at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 3000 cycles. d) Rate performance under different current densities from 0.05 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. e,f) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of Na‐ion full cells based on the CoPSe/NC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrodes in the fifth cycle at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> showing different average discharge voltages and energy densities.</caption></figure><p>The CoPSe/NC electrode delivers a reversible capacity of 303 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">3b</link>). And particularly, the long‐term cycling test at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> demonstrates that a capacity of 223 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is maintained after 3000 cycles, giving a low capacity fading of 0.01% per cycle (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">3c</link>). In contrast, the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode shows sharp capacity fading and poor cycling performance at both current densities. The serious capacity fading of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode may be due to the pulverization of the electrode material in the repeated cycle upon the insertion/extraction of Na<sup>+</sup> ions. The CoPSe/NC electrode exhibits a small volume expansion (20.3%) after full sodiation compared to the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode (34.4%) (Figure S15, Supporting Information). As can be seen in Figure S16, Supporting Information, the CoPSe/NC electrode can keep electrode integrity after 500 cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> while the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode gets pulverized and fallen off. The rate performance of the CoPSe/NC electrode in SIBs is investigated at various current rates ranging from 0.05 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">3d</link>). As can be seen in Table S1, Supporting Information, the CoPSe/NC electrode can deliver high average reversible capacities of 482, 426, 398, 370, 343, 317, and 292 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. When returns to the initial 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, an average reversible capacity of 421 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to a capacity retention of 87.3% is achieved for the CoPSe/NC electrode, times over that for the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode (139 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, 30.9%). The rate capability of the CoPSe/NC electrode is also verified by the GCD curves at different current densities from 0.05–5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S17, Supporting Information). The outstanding rate performance stems partly from the unique 3D layered architecture of the CoPSe/NC composite where layered conductive carbon matrix with large interlayer space provide fast transport channels for both electrons and ions, while CoPSe in the form of small nanoparticle can shorten electron/ion diffusion path to facilitate charge transfer and rapid pseudocapacitive reactions (Figure S18, Supporting Information). Moreover, galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurements confirm that the CoPSe/NC electrode possesses a significantly higher Na ion diffusion coefficient and faster diffusion kinetics (Figure S19, Supporting Information).</p><p>We further fabricated an Na‐ion full cell by pairing the CoPSe/NC anode with a commercial Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F cathode to investigate the practical application for SIBs. The representative GCD profiles of the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F cathode in a half cell in Figure S20, Supporting Information, show two evident plateaus at 4.0 and 3.6 V with a discharging specific capacity of ≈122 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The Na‐ion full cell based on the CoPSe/NC anode shows a stable cycling performance and achieves a specific capacity of 117 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 92% after 150 cycles, much better than that of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC full cell (Figure S21, Supporting Information). In addition, the CoPSe/NC full cell shows a higher average discharge voltage (≈3.11 V) than that based on the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC anode (2.88 V) during the main discharge process in the capacity region of 0–110 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (from the point I to point II) (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">3e</link>,<link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">f</link>). Accordingly, a high energy density of 274 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> can be delivered for the CoPSe/NC full cell in the fifth cycle, higher than that of 242 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> for the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC full cell (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">3e</link>,<link href="#adma202007262-fig-0003">f</link>). In addition, after 150 cycles, the energy density of the CoPSe/NC full cell can be maintained at 196 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, while for the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC full cell the energy density fades sharply to 60 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S22, Table S2, Supporting Information). The performance comparison of Na‐ion full cells based on the CoPSe/NC and other P‐, Se‐, or S‐based binary/ternary anode materials are provided (Table S3, Supporting Information).</p><p>The migration mechanism of Na ions was investigated for the CoPSe/NC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrodes. The possible diffusion pathways for Na ions on the (210) surface and through the interior of CoPSe and CoSe<sub>2</sub> are displayed in Figure S23, Supporting Information. The corresponding migration energy barriers for the diffusion pathways are also calculated and presented in Figure S24, Supporting Information. The most energy favorable pathways for the two systems are illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>. Apparently, the calculated migration energies at each site for CoPSe are lower than those for CoSe<sub>2</sub>. The result suggests a faster diffusion of Na ions for CoPSe system than CoSe<sub>2</sub> especially on the surface, which can greatly promote the charge transfer and Na ion transport during electrochemical reaction processes, and thus ensure outstanding kinetics and rate performances of the CoPSe/NC electrode.</p><figure xml:id="adma202007262-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007262-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Na ion surface migration mechanism and Structural stability investigation of the CoPSe/NC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrodes. a) Calculated migration energy barriers of Na on the (210) surface of CoPSe and CoSe<sub>2</sub>. Inset of (a) is the top views of the diffusion pathways. b) The calculated Young's modulus (<i>E</i>) and bulk modulus (<i>B</i>) based on the Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe and Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub> structures. c) Strain energy–volume deformation curves of Na<sub>1/8</sub>CoPSe and Na<sub>1/8</sub>CoSe<sub>2</sub> structures. d) Comparison of interior stress between Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe and Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub> structures versus the incremental Na concentration.</caption></figure><p>To further understand why the CoPSe/NC electrode can exhibit outstanding long‐cycling stability compared with the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode, the density functional theory (DFT) calculations were conducted to evaluate the difference of interior stress between sodiated CoPSe and CoSe<sub>2</sub>. The calculated second‐order elastic constants of the Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe and Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub> structures are shown in Tables S4 and S5, Supporting Information, respectively. It is noted that the value of <i>x</i> in the range of 0–1/8 for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe and Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub> was selected to satisfy the mechanical stability criteria. The Young's modulus (<i>E</i>) and bulk modulus (<i>B</i>) can be obtained from the elastic constants via the Voigt–Reuss–Hill approximations.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007262-bib-0051"><sup>51</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0004">4b</link>, it can be observed that the Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe has a higher Young's modulus and bulk modulus than those corresponding to the Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub>, indicating the better resistance‐to‐deformation ability of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe. The strain energies shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0004">4c</link> were further calculated by exerting different volume deformation for Na<sub>1/8</sub>CoPSe and Na<sub>1/8</sub>CoSe<sub>2</sub> and then fitted by the quadratic function curve. The stress values of 10.76 and 5.85 GPa are obtained for Na<sub>1/8</sub>CoSe<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>1/8</sub>CoPSe, respectively (Table S6, Supporting Information). The results indicate that the sodiated CoPSe/NC electrode could possess lower stress levels than the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC counterpart. From Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0004">4d</link>, it is clear that though the interior stress increases with the rise of Na concentration for both Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe and Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub>. The stress produced in Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoPSe is much lower than in Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoSe<sub>2</sub> under the condition of the same Na concentration, indicating that the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode is more likely to suffer damage and prone to pulverization during sodiation, while the CoPSe/NC electrode is mechanically stable against sodiation/desodiation due to its unique stress‐relieved property.</p><p>The electrochemical potassium storage performance of the CoPSe/NC electrode was then evaluated. Figure S25, Supporting Information, shows the CV profiles of the CoPSe/NC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrodes at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Like CV profiles in SIB, the redox peaks of the CoPSe/NC electrode shift to lower voltage values than the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode. The GCD profiles of the CoPSe/NC electrode were recorded at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> for PIBs, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>. Impressively, the CoPSe/NC electrode can deliver a reversible charge capacity of 506 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> higher than that of the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode (430 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) (Figure S26, Supporting Information). The CoPSe/NC electrode also exhibits superior cycling stability. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0005">5b</link>, the CoPSe/NC electrode delivers a reversible capacity of 317 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, in a long‐term cycling test for 2000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0005">5c</link>), the CoPSe/NC electrode can still deliver a reversible capacity of 203 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling, with a low capacity fading of ≈0.01% per cycle. In contrast, the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrode shows sharp capacity fading and poor cycling performance at both current densities due to the large volume variation during the potassiation and depotassiation process compared to the CoPSe/NC electrode (Figure S27, Supporting Information). The rate performance of the CoPSe/NC electrode for PIBs is investigated with current densities ranging from 0.05 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202007262-fig-0005">5d</link>; Table S7, Supporting Information). The CoPSe/NC electrode can deliver high average reversible capacities of 425, 361, 344, 320, 285, 252, and 218 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. When returned to the initial 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the average reversible capacities of 379 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and capacity retention of 89.2% are achieved for the CoPSe/NC electrode, three times over those for the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC (100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, 25.7%).</p><figure xml:id="adma202007262-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007262:adma202007262-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007262-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of the CoPSe/NC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC electrodes for PIBs. a) Galvanostatic charge−discharge curves for the first three cycles at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Comparison of cycling performance and Coulombic efficiency of as‐prepared electrodes at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Comparison of cycling performance and Coulombic efficiency of as‐prepared electrodes at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. d) Rate performance under different current densities from 0.05 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> of as‐prepared electrodes.</caption></figure><p>On the other hand, the corresponding ternary phosphorus sulfide, CoPS/NC composite that holds a similar structure to the CoPSe/NC was also prepared (Figures S28 and S29, Supporting Information) and investigated its SIBs/PIBs performance. The rate performance of the CoPS/NC electrode for SIBs is shown in Figure S30a and Table S8, Supporting Information, which is more inferior than the CoPSe/NC electrode. The poor rate performance of the CoPS/NC electrode may be due to its lower intrinsic electronic conductivity that renders a higher contact resistance (<i>R</i><sub>s</sub>) and charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) than the CoPSe/NC electrode (Figure S31, Table S9, Supporting Information). Moreover, due to the polysulfide intermediates produced in discharge processes over the CoPS/NC electrode for SIBs (Figure S32, Supporting Information) which dissolve into electrolyte causing severe capacity loss during the cycling process, the CoPS/NC electrode exhibits poor cycling performance at both current densities of 0.2 and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figures 29b). The PIB performance of the CoPS/NC electrode was also investigated. In the rate performance measurement, the CoPS/NC electrode delivers the reversible capacities of 189, 152, 121, 92, 65, 48, and 29 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 3, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure S33a, Table S10, Supporting Information). The reversible capacities and capacity retention at the corresponding current density are much lower than the CoPSe/NC electrode (Figures S33a,S34, Supporting Information). The lower K<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient over the CoPS/NC electrode may cause its inferior rate performance (Figure S34, Supporting Information). In addition, the CoPS/NC electrode remains a capacity of 138 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the cycle test (Figure S33b, Supporting Information), which is much lower than the typical CoPSe/NC electrode.</p><p>To further evaluate battery performance of the three electrodes with higher mass loading, the rate performance was carried out with mass loading of 2.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. As shown in Figures S35 and S36, Supporting Information, the CoPSe/NC electrode still exhibit excellent rate capability in both SIB and KIB compared to that of the other two electrodes. In addition, when cycled in SIB at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 800 cycles, the CoPSe/NC electrode still delivers a capacity of 217 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling, giving a low capacity fading of 0.03% per cycle (Figure S37, Supporting Information), demonstrating superior cycling stability with higher mass loading.</p><p>In summary, single‐phase ternary CoPSe nanoparticles that are embedded in the layered MOF‐derived N‐doped carbon matrix have been fabricated via in situ synchronous phosphorization/selenization reactions. The CoPSe/NC composite as battery anodes holds a set of intrinsic merits including lower mechanical stress, faster ions diffusion kinetics, and lower discharge voltage relative to its counterparts of CoSe<sub>2,</sub> and higher electrical conductivity and shuttle‐free property relative to CoPS, respectively. Accordingly, the CoPSe/NC electrode exhibits superior rate and long‐cycle performance for SIBs and PIBs. Moreover, the constructed Na‐ion full cell based on the CoPSe/NC anode can achieve a practical energy density of 274 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, much higher than that based on the CoSe<sub>2</sub>/NC anode. This work provides a new anode material for SIBs and PIBs with high rate capability and long cycling life toward practical energy applications.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202007262-sec-0020"
