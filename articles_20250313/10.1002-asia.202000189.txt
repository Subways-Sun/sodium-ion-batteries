Transition metal selenides present excellent conductivity and high capacity; nevertheless, their low conductivity and serious volume expansion raise challenging issues of inferior lifespan and capacity fading. Herein, an in‐situ construction method through carbonization and selenide synergistic effect is skillfully designed to synthesize a flexible electrode of bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorn coated on porous carbon cloth. The designed flexible CoSe<sub>2</sub> electrode with stable structural feature displays enhanced Na‐ion storage capabilities with good rate performance and outstanding cycling stability. As expected, the designed SIBs with flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode display excellent reversible capacity with 360.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 180 cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>The unique structure of</b> bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorn loaded on porous carbon cloth could eﬀectively numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium ion batteries (SIBs) have become a research hotspot in recent years as a promising large‐scale energy‐storage application in consideration of low‐cost and abundant resource nature of sodium.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0001 #asia202000189-bib-0002 #asia202000189-bib-0003"/> Although the ion storage mechanism of SIBs is similar to lithium ion batteries (LIBs),<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0004"/> fallaciously, a large proportion of electrode materials recently available for LIBs is not suitable for SIBs, owing to the larger ionic radium of Na<sup>+</sup> (∼1.02 Å) than that of Li<sup>+</sup> (∼0.76 Å).<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0005 #asia202000189-bib-0006"/> Furthermore, the relatively low energy density is also an urgent issue for Na‐ion storage devices.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0007 #asia202000189-bib-0008"/> Therefore, constant exploration and rational design are stimulating intensive studies on appropriate anode materials with impressive Na‐ion storage capabilities.</p><p>Transition metal chalcogenides (TMCs) with excellent sodium storage performance have been regarded as promising anode materials for SIBs.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0009 #asia202000189-bib-0010 #asia202000189-bib-0011"/> Especially, as one family member of TMCs (MO<sub>x</sub>, MS<sub>x</sub>, MSe<sub>x</sub> and MTe<sub>x</sub>), metal selenides based on alloying and conversion reactions possess the natures of high reversible capacity and suitable working potential.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0012 #asia202000189-bib-0013"/> Furthermore, CoSe<sub>2</sub> is particularly appropriate for Na‐ion storage owing to its stable physical and chemical properties as well as earth abundant nature, and has been extensively investigated for SIBs in recent years.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0014 #asia202000189-bib-0015 #asia202000189-bib-0016"/> However, low conductivity and large volume expansion of CoSe<sub>2</sub> are ongoing challenges leading to poor cycle stability, which cause the hindrance of potentially commercial applications.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0017"/></p><p>Substantial studies have been driven to address the aforementioned issues of similar electrode material.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0018 #asia202000189-bib-0019 #asia202000189-bib-0020 #asia202000189-bib-0021"/> Construction of flexibly integrated electrode has been proven to be an effective strategy to alleviate the pulverization and shedding from the surface of electrode material during sodiation/desodiation.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0022 #asia202000189-bib-0023"/> In addition, mass energy density of batteries could be improved by avoiding additional additives (conductors and binders).<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0023"/> Gao <i>et al</i>.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0024"/> have fabricated a flexible anode with MnS nanoparticles embedded on carbon nanofibers (MnS@CNF) to minimize Na‐ion diffusion length and sodiation/desodiation stresses. The prepared flexible anode verifies an excellent performance for sodium storage 240 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> reversible capacity at a current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, Kim <i>et al</i>.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0025"/> have also demonstrated that binder‐/additive‐free MWCNT and In<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanohybrid flexible electrode shows excellent rate performance and long cycle life. Astoundingly, despite extensive studies on flexible electrodes, the research of flexible CoSe<sub>2</sub> anode material for SIBs is quite rare. Thus, it is still a challenging task to tailor integrated and flexible CoSe<sub>2</sub> electrode.</p><p>In this paper, bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorn coated on porous carbon cloth (BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC) is tactfully fabricated by a facile in‐situ growth reaction. The nano‐thorn array could shorten ion diffusion path and nano‐construction can alleviate volume change pressure. Benefiting from the unique structure, BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode presents structural stability and excellent cycle stability as a flexible anode material for SIBs. Additionally, the selection of electrolyte also is optimized for assembling SIBs devices to further develop the electrochemical properties. As expected, the designed SIBs with flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode display excellent reversible capacity with 360.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (0.18 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) after 180 cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The schematic illustration of synthetic procedures for flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC is illustrated in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0001"/>. Firstly, the commercial carbon cloth (CC) is perforated by growing Co and etched with acid to obtain porous carbon cloth (PCC). Then Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O nano‐thorn is gown on the surface of PCC (noted as Co−CO/PCC) by hydrothermal method. Subsequently, flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC with nano‐thorn morphology is achieved by synchronously pyrolysis and selenide processes of Co−CO/PCC. Through the exquisite design, porous CC is in‐situ coated with bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub>, constructing a flexible electrode with a CoSe<sub>2</sub> rigid load.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of synthetic produces for BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC.</p></caption></figure><p>In order to improve the adhesive ability between active material and CC, a pore‐making treatment for CC substrate is designed. The formation mechanism of porous CC substrate (Figure S1) is examined through structural and morphological characterizations. XRD patterns of products during preparation process of PCC are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0002"/>a. Diffraction peaks of all products at 2θ=26° correspond to the characteristic diffraction peaks of CC. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0002"/>b, XRD patterns of Co−CO/PCC at a range of 30–80° verify that all the diffraction peaks can be well matched with orthorhombic Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O crystal (JCPDS No. 48‐0083). After being annealed, CC coated with cobalt particles (Co/CC) is successfully prepared as shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0002"/>a, and the XRD pattern displays three characteristic peaks at 2θ=45°, 52° and 76° of Co/CC, corresponding to the (110), (200) and (220) planes of Co (JCPDS No. 1‐1255), respectively. Subsequently, the prepared Co/CC is fully etched by acid to remove cobalt particles. The XRD pattern of PCC presents the disappearance of diffraction peaks of Co element. Corresponding FESEM images are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0001"/>c–j, and Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0001"/>c,d display that the surface of commercial CC is smooth. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0001"/>e and 1f, the growing Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O is nano‐thorn morphology, and the Co/CC is coated with Co particles (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0001"/>g,h). Moreover, the surface of PCC is covered with pores after the cobalt particles have been etched, which could effectively develop the construction of CoSe<sub>2</sub>, as shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0002"/>i,j.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD patterns of prepared samples, (b) XRD pattern of Co−CO/PCC; FESEM images of (c,d) Commercial CC, (e,f) Co−CO/CC, (g,h) Co/CC and (i,j) PCC.</p></caption></figure><p>To optimize the growth of Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O on PCC precursor, temperature and time of hydrothermal conditions are investigated, respectively. Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0003"/>ab, 3cd, 3ef and 3gh are the FESEM images of Co−CO/PCC at different hydrothermal temperature of 80, 85, 90 and 95 °C, respectively. The carbon fibers of CC are partly uncovered with nano‐thorns at lower temperature (80 and 85 °C), while the nano‐thorns tend to grow into nanosheets at higher temperature (95 °C). The specific surface area will be decreased due to the formation of nanosheets from nano‐thorns, which is not conducive to the exposure of electrochemical reaction sites. Therefore, a desired nano‐thorn‐like Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O fully coated on PCC is achieved at 90 °C. XRD patterns (Figure S2) present the pure phase compositions of Co−CO/PCC at different temperatures. As shown in Figure S3a‐d, the effect of hydrothermal time on morphology of Co−CO/PCC also has been investigated, and the mass of coated nano‐thorn Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O is getting more and more as time prolongs. Ultimately, 90 °C and 6 h are chosen as the final hydrothermal conditions. Figure S4 is the thermogravimetric and differential thermal analysis (TG‐DTA) curve, conducting to investigate the proper calcination temperature of as‐obtained Co−CO/PCC in an Ar atmosphere at a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> from 100 to 800 °C. The weight loss before 250 °C is mainly due to water evaporation absorbed by pore structure and crystal water, and the mass change after 250 °C is attributed to the thermal decomposition of Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O. Pyrolysis process of Co−CO/PCC is basically completed when temperature increases to 340 °C.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>FESEM images of Co−CO/PCC at different hydrothermal temperature. (a,b) 80 °C, (c,d) 85 °C, (e,f) 90 °C and (g,h) 95 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>Considering the effect of temperature on products, different temperatures (300, 350 and 400 °C) are respectively select for simultaneous carbonization and selenization of Co−CO/PCC. Figure S5a is the XRD patterns of selenide products at different temperature, displaying the successful generation of CoSe<sub>2</sub> when the temperature is above 350 °C. Furthermore, selenide time is also a key factor affecting phases. As shown in Figure S5b, XRD patterns indicate that pure phase of CoSe<sub>2</sub> can be well obtained when the reaction time is longer than 1 h. Hence, the final selenide conditions are determined to heat at 400 °C for 1 h at a heating rate of 1 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under Ar gas.</p><p>In order to explore the pivotal roles of porosity characteristic for PCC on flexible electrode performances, BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC are prepared, respectively. XRD patterns (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>a) show the formation of CoSe<sub>2</sub>, and Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>b,c show the high‐resolution of Co 2p, Se 3d and Co 3p spectra directly collected with carbon cloth. Four main peaks at 778.4, 780.6, 793.4 and 796.6 eV are attributed to Se−Co (ІІ) and Co−O (ІІІ) of Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, as well as Co−Se (ІІ) and Co−O (ІІІ) of Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0014"/> The appearance of metal‐oxygen binding energy can be ascribed to the passivation process after being exposed to air. As displayed in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>c, the high‐resolution Se 3d spectrum appears two obvious peaks of Se 3d<sub>5/2</sub> (54.4 eV) and Se 3d<sub>3/2</sub> (55.4 eV), which can be assigned to CoSe<sub>2</sub>, and peaks in the range of 57–63 eV are attributed to Co 3p<sub>3/2</sub> and Co 3p<sub>1/2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0026"/> Figure S6 shows the full spectrum and high‐resolution of C 1s spectrum of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC. The full spectrum confirms the existence of C, Co and Se elements, and the peak at 284.4 and 288.8 eV of C 1s spectrum are indexed to C−C and C=O bonds. FESEM images (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>d–f, S7 and S8) present similar nano‐thorn‐like Co(CO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>0.5</sub>(OH) ⋅ 0.11H<sub>2</sub>O precursor, bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub> on PCC and CC, respectively, which manifests that the carbon substrate has no obvious effect on morphology formation. Mapping images (Figure S9) indicate that PCC is well coated with CoSe<sub>2</sub>. The bone‐like structure of CoSe<sub>2</sub> is confirmed by the low‐resolution TEM images (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>g,h), building a strong architecture to alleviate the impact during sodiation/desodiation procedures. The CoSe<sub>2</sub> is further evidenced by high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) measurement as shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>i, and the lattice fringe with an interplanar spacing of 0.240 nm is consistent with that of (211) crystal plane of orthorhombic CoSe<sub>2</sub>. The existence of carbon layer is demonstrated by HRTEM and elemental mapping (Figure S10). In addition, element mapping results (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>j) exhibit homogeneous distribution of Co and Se elements of bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub>. Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0004"/>k presents the digital photograph of flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC, which could be directly assembled as an integrated electrode without any additive and current collector.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD patterns; XPS spectra of (b) Co 2p, (c) Se 3d and Co 3p for BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC; (d) SEM image of Co−CO/PCC; (e,f) SEM, (g‐i) TEM, (j) HAADF‐STEM and corresponding EDS mapping images of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC; (m) Digital photograph of flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode.</p></caption></figure><p>To further improving electrochemical properties, electrolyte for assembled SIBs has been optimized. As shown in Figure S11, NaClO<sub>4</sub>/EC+DEC+FEC ester‐type electrolyte is more compatible for BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode. Therefore, electrochemical properties of flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC as integrated anode electrodes for SIBs are evaluated by assembling 2032‐type coin cells with sodium metal as the counter and reference electrode and NaClO<sub>4</sub>/EC+DEC+FEC electrolyte. Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC are investigated in a potential range of 0.3–3 V (<i>vs</i>. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) at the initial three cycles. As displayed in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0005"/>a, an evident cathodic peak at 0.79 and 0.51 V is due to the decomposition of electrolyte and the formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer at the first cycle.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0016"/> During the subsequently cathodic sweeps, the three peaks appear at 1.41, 1.19, and 0.59 V correspond to the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into CoSe<sub>2</sub> crystals (CoSe<sub>2</sub>→Na<sub>x</sub>CoSe<sub>2</sub>) and the two conversion steps (Na<sub>x</sub>CoSe<sub>2</sub>→CoSe+Na<sub>2</sub>Se→Co+Na<sub>2</sub>Se), respectively.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0014 #asia202000189-bib-0027"/> The distinct anodic peaks appearing on desodiation process at 1.90 V are pertaining to the recombination of Co nanoparticles and Na<sub>2</sub>Se into CoSe<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0028 #asia202000189-bib-0029"/> The first three galvanostatic charge‐discharge profiles of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC present first discharge capacity of 1259.5 and 704.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with an initial coulombic efficiency (CE) of 43.9 and 56.9%, respectively, at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0005"/>b and S12). The large capacity loss and low CE at initial cycle are primarily attribute to the formation of SEI layer and electrolyte decomposition. Compared to CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC, the lower initial CE of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC results from the nano‐thorn structure and porous character with higher surface area, leading to more formation of stable SEI layer, side reaction and irreversible electrolyte decomposition in the initial cycle.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0030 #asia202000189-bib-0031 #asia202000189-bib-0032"/></p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of the BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode at a voltage range of 0.3–3 V <i>vs</i>. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na, (b) Galvanostatic discharge‐charge profiles of the BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode between 0.3–3.0 V <i>vs</i>. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na; (c) Cycling properties at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and (d) Rate performances of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrodes.</p></caption></figure><p>The cycling capabilities of the both electrodes at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and consistent CE are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0005"/>c. The BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode with mass loading of 0.50 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> delivers a stable reversible capacity of 360.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (0.18 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) after 180 cycles, and the CE of the electrode remains around 100% during cycling. Moreover, the electrochemical data of electrode with higher mass loading of 1.01 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> also delivers an excellent capacity of 305.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (0.31 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) after 55 cycles (Figure S13). The capacity retention of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode, measured from the thirty cycle, is 74%. In contrast, the capacity of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC electrode drops rapidly, exhibiting a discharge capacity of only 78.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles, and the capacity retention calculated from the thirty cycle is just 20%. The outstanding electrochemical properties of flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode are further examined by a rate performance test at various current densities from 0.05 to 2.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, as shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0005"/>d. The electrode exhibits an average reversible capacity of 492.4, 423.7, 392.1, 356.0, 322.2 and 292.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6 and 1.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Even at a high current density of 2.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a remarkable reversible capacity of 240.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> could still be preserved. Moreover, a reversible capacity of 351.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be restored after the current density reduced back to 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, revealing an impressive reversibility of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode. In vast contrast, the rate capability of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC electrode fades rapidly after experiencing the large rate. Furthermore, the discharge capacity of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode is 177.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at a large current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> as displayed in Figure S14. The above results unambiguously verify that the porous structure of PCC plays a pivotal role on sodium storage performances of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode. Through the FESEM images (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0006"/>a,b) of both electrodes after cycling, the original morphology of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode can be observed clearly. However, the structure of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC electrode after cycling has completely altered. The CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorns have agglomerated into spheres and most of it dropped from the surface of CC, leading to poor electrochemical performances of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC compared to BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode. Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0006"/>c–f are the digital photos of separators and cycled electrodes for cells with CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC and BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode, respectively, and separator of the former after cycling is defied with more dark active materials. We could conclude that the CoSe<sub>2</sub> grown on the surface of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC is more easily stripped away from the surface of the integrated electrode than that of flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode, which corresponds to the results of FESEM after cycling and the fading phenomenon of electrochemical performances.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0006"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>FESEM images of (a) BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and (b) CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC electrode after 50 cycles; Separators of cells after cycling with (c) BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode and (d) CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC electrode; Cycled electrodes of (e) BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and (f) CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC.</p></caption></figure><p>To further understand the excellent electrochemical performances of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode, kinetic researches based on CV analysis are performed. Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0007"/>a displays the CV curves of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode at various scan rates. All CV curves possess similar shapes and peak intensities gradually rise with increased scan rates, demonstrating a small potential polarization and an outstanding rate ability. According to the relationship (<i>i</i><b>=</b>a<i>v</i><sup>b</sup>) between current (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>v</i>), the b value can be calculated by linearity (log<i>i</i>=blog<i>v</i>+loga) of log(<i>v</i>) and log(<i>i</i>) plots at different potentials.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0033 #asia202000189-bib-0034"/> Specifically, when b‐value approaches to 1.0, charge storage is dominant by the pseudocapacitive‐controlled process, while b‐value is close to 0.5 represents a diffusion‐controlled behavior.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0035"/> Evidently, the b‐value (Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0007"/>b) of peak 1–3 is 0.89, 0.86 and 0.78, respectively, suggesting that pseudocapacitive behavior during Na‐ion storage is a major contributor to the reversible capacity which leads to a fast Na‐ion intercalation/extraction. The pseudocapacitive contribution to the total capacity at certain scan rates could be computed on the basis of equation: <i>i</i>=<i>k<sub>1</sub>v</i>+<i>k<sub>2</sub>v</i><sup>0.5</sup>, in which <i>k<sub>1</sub>v</i> and <i>k<sub>2</sub>v</i><sup><i>0.5</i></sup> represent the pseudocapacitive capacity and diffusion‐controlled contribution, respectively.<link href="#asia202000189-bib-0036 #asia202000189-bib-0037"/> As calculated in Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0007"/>c, the pseudocapacitive contributions are 71.2%, 73.9%, 75.8%, 77.1% and 86.4% at scan rates of 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0 and 5.0 mV s<sup><b>−</b>1</sup>, respectively. The gradually enhanced capacitive contribution at high scan rates could be attributed to the fast Na<sup>+</sup> transfer kinetics of unique bone‐like architecture of CoSe<sub>2</sub> coated on porous carbon cloth substrate. Moreover, Figure <link href="#asia202000189-fig-0007"/>d further illustrates the detailed pseudocapacitive fraction (shaded region) measured at a scan rate of 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000189-fig-0007"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000189:asia202000189-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000189-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of the BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode at various scan rates, (b) Determination of b‐values, (c) Percentage of capacitive contribution in BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC at different scan rates, (d) Ratio of the capacitive contribution fraction (shaded area) at a scan rate of 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, flexible electrode materials of bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorn coated on porous carbon cloth (BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC) have been developed by an in‐situ grown strategy for sodium storage. The unique structure could eﬀectively improve the structural stability, and electronic conductivity as well as alleviate volume expansion during processes of sodiation/desodiation. The assembled flexible BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode exhibits an excellent cycle life with 360.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 180 cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and superior rate properties of 240.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 2.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The remarkable electrochemical performances further confirm that the BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC electrode holds promise for broad practical application, notably in large energy‐storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of PCC</title><p>Commercial carbon cloth (CC, CeTech, W0S1009) was first tailored into pieces of 2×4 cm<sup>2</sup>, and the pieces were then ultrasonic with acetone, concentrated nitric acid, deionized water and anhydrous alcohol for 2 hours to obtain the pretreated CC, respectively. 2 mmol Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> ⋅ 6H<sub>2</sub>O (Alfa Aesar, 97.7%) and 10 mmol CO(NH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>2</sub> (Sigma‐Aldrich, 99.0%) were dissolved in 40 mL deionized water. After continuous stirring for 30 min, the solution and the pretreated 2×4 cm<sup>2</sup> CC were transformed into 50 mL of Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave, which was heated to 90 °C for 6 h in an oven. After cooling to ambient temperature, the obtained Co−CO/PCC was washed by water and alcohol for three times, respectively, and was dried at 80 °C for 12 h in a vacuum oven. The above‐prepared CC was then annealed at 900 °C for 1 h at the speed of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under Ar atmosphere and was immersed in concentrated hydrochloric acid for 8 h to obtain Co/CC. Then, the Co/CC was washed by water and alcohol for three times, respectively. Finally, porous CC (PCC) was achieved by being dried at 80 °C for 12 h in a vacuum oven.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC</title><p>The synthesis of Co−CO/PCC was the same as Co−CO/CC, except that CC was replaced by as‐prepared PCC. A 2×4 cm<sup>2</sup> piece of Co−CO/PCC and 60 mg Se powder were put at two separate porcelain boats, and Se powder was loaded at the upstream side of the tubular furnace. Subsequently, the furnace was heated up to 400 °C for 1 h with a heating rate of 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under an Ar atmosphere. After naturally cooling to ambient temperature, the bone‐like CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorn coated on PCC (BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC) was obtained. The synthesis method of CoSe<sub>2</sub> nano‐thorn coated on CC (CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC) was consistent with BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC, and the only difference was that the CC used for CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC was directly commercial CC.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Structural characterization</title><p>Powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns were carried out by a Rigaku MiniFlex II diffractometer (Cu Kα radiation source, λ=1.5408 Å) at a scan rate of 4 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscope (XPS) spectra were obtained by PHI5000 Versaprobe system. Field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were measured by JEOL (JMS‐7500F, 5 kV) and FEI Philips Tecnai (JEM‐2100, 200 kW), respectively. The thermal behavior was performed on Thermo Gravimetric Analyzer (TG, SETARAM SENSYS evo).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>Electrochemical performances were tested by assembling CR2032‐type coin cells in an Ar‐filled glovebox (O<sub>2</sub>&lt;0.1 ppm, H<sub>2</sub>O&lt;0.1 ppm). BL−CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC were punched into small wafers with a diameter of 10 mm. Then small wafer was directly performed as a flexible working electrode with sodium metal as the counter and reference electrodes. The average mass loading of CoSe<sub>2</sub> in CoSe<sub>2</sub>/PCC and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/CC all was 0.50 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The electrolyte was 1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> in a mixture of ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate (EC/DEC, 1 : 1 by volume ratio) with 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) and 1M NaClO<sub>4</sub> in diethylene glycol dimethyl ether (DEGDME), respectively, and fiberglass as the separator. The specific capacities were calculated based on loading weight of active material. The galvanostatically discharge/charge tests were investigated on a LAND CT2001A instrument. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves were operated on Chenhua CHI760E electrochemical working‐station with a variety of scan rates.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the Ministry of Science and Technology (2016YFA0202500), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51871123, 51501072 and 51571124), the Natural Science Foundation of Tianjin (17JCYBJC17900), the Ministry of Education (IRT13R30) and 111 Project (B12015).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000189-sec-0010" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="asia202000189-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="asia202000189-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>A.
