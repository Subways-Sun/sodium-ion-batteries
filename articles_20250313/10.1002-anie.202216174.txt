Although Na and TM show a big difference of ion radius, TMs in layered cathodes of sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) can still migrate to Na layer, leading to serious electrochemical degeneration. To elucidate the origin of TM migration in layered SIB cathodes, we choose NaCrO<sub>2</sub>, a typical layered cathode suffering from serious TM migration, as a model material and find that the TM migration is derived from the random desodiation and subsequent formation of Na‐free layer at high charge potential. A Ru/Ti co‐doping strategy is developed to address the issue, where the doped active Ru is first oxidized to create a selective desodiation and the doped inactive Ti can function as a pillar to avoid complete desodiation in Ru‐contained TM layers, leading to the suppression of the Na‐free layer formation and subsequent enhanced electrochemical performance.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The origin of cation migration in layered sodium‐ion battery cathode is first elucidated, which is attributed to the Na‐free layer forming under high charge potential due to the random desodiation reaction. numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Comprehensively considering the low cost, abundant reserves and wide distribution of sodium resources, and energy density, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been widely accepted to be one of the most promising candidates as next‐generation large‐scale energy storage devices.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> In SIBs, cost and energy density are dominated by cathode materials because cathode occupies approximately one‐third cost of a battery and determinates the capacity and output voltage.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Among layered oxides, tunnel oxides, Prussian blue analogues, and polyanionic compounds, layered oxides have been widely investigated and considered to be one promising cathodes for SIBs.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0009"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Layered oxide cathode materials (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>, 0&lt;<i>x</i>≤1, TM=transition metal) show flexible crystal structures, high capacities (theoretical capacity≥250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), rich available TMs (e.g., Mn,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0012"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Ni,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0013"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Co,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0014"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Fe,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0015"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Cu,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0016"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Cr,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0017"><sup>[9]</sup></link> V,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0018"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Ru,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0019"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Ir,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0020"><sup>[12]</sup></link> etc.), simple synthesis process, and so on.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0021"><sup>[13]</sup></link> However, layered oxide cathode materials suffer from unfavorable TM migration from TM layers to Na layers.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0024"><sup>[14]</sup></link></p><p>Cation migration in layered oxide cathode materials has been well investigated in lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) because the typical cation mixing of Li<sup>+</sup>/Ni<sup>2+</sup> happening in material preparation process and electrode charge–discharge procedure severely affects almost all the electrochemical performance including capacity, rate capability, and stability.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0027"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Generally, this unfavorable phenomenon is attributed to the similar ion radius of Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å) and Ni<sup>2+</sup> (0.69 Å). However, although the ion radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) and TMs shows a big difference, the TMs in layered oxide cathodes of SIBs can still migrate to the Na layer, such as Cr<sup>3+</sup> (0.615 Å),<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0017 #anie202216174-bib-0034"><sup>[9, 16]</sup></link> Fe<sup>3+</sup> (0.645 Å, high spin),<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0038"><sup>[17]</sup></link> Ni<sup>2+</sup> (0.69 Å),<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0043"><sup>[18]</sup></link> and so on. Particularly, Cr and Fe migration are reported the most. In NaCrO<sub>2</sub> (NC), it is widely accepted that Cr migration will be triggered when the charge potential is beyond 3.6 V. NC shows excellent electrochemical performance below the charge potential of 3.6 V, but a fast capacity attenuation happens when the charge potential is above 3.6 V. Therefore, it is reasonable to set the cut‐off potential higher than 3.6 V when exploring Cr migration. In NaFeO<sub>2</sub> (NF), the Fe migration is also serious. It is reported that approximately 40 % Fe migrated from TM layers to Na layers when the charge cutoff potential was 4.0 V.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0044"><sup>[19]</sup></link> Generally, the adverse TM migration can be alleviated by introducing other metals into the Cr site. However, up to now, the origin of TM migration in layered oxide cathodes of SIBs has not been clearly elucidated, and no universal strategy has been developed to address the issue.</p><p>In this work, selecting NC as a model material, we find that the TM migration phenomenon is originated from the Na‐free layer forming under high charge potentials due to the random desodiation reaction (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0001">1a</link>). The random desodiation can be attributed to only Cr in the TM layer to participate in the charge compensation. In the random desodiation process, the homogeneous oxidation of Cr in the two adjacent TM layers will induce the random extraction of Na in the same Na layer, leading to the formation of Na‐free layer. Theoretical calculations, high angle annular dark field‐scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) and galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) are conducted to characterize the formation of Na‐free layer and subsequent TM migration. A Ru/Ti co‐doping strategy is developed to address the above issue by creating a selective desodiation reaction, where the active 4<i>d</i> Ru is first oxidized to disrupt the random desodiation reaction and the inactive 3<i>d</i> Ti can function as a pillar to avoid complete desodiation in Ru‐contained TM layers (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0001">1b</link>). The combined characterizations of theoretical calculations, HAADF‐STEM and GITT reflect that the Na‐free layer is suppressed in Ru/Ti co‐doped Na<sub>0.85</sub>Cr<sub>0.85</sub>Ru<sub>0.10</sub>Ti<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (NCRT), and excellent rate capability (91.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and electrochemical stability (86.2 % in the 500th cycle and 81.3 % in the 1000th cycle) are obtained. Finally, the co‐doping strategy is also proven to be applied to the NF system in suppressing the adverse Fe migration and enhancing electrochemical performance.</p><figure xml:id="anie202216174-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202216174:anie202216174-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202216174-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Na‐free layer formation derived from the random desodiation reaction and its suppression by selective desodiation reaction in layered SIB cathodes. Atomic structures of a) NC with 0 (left), 0.25 (middle), and 0.67 (right) Na vacancies and b) NCRT with 0 (left), 0.33 (middle), and 0.67 (right) Na vacancies. The red, dark blue, blue, gray, and yellow balls represent oxygen, chromium, titanium, ruthenium, and sodium atoms, and the dotted circles describe vacancy sites. The selective desodiation reaction means that the Na vacancy sites are present nearby the mixed layers, which stems from different cationic redox potentials.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202216174-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The lowest‐energy configurations of NC (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2a</link>) and NCRT (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2b</link>) at each vacancy content are determined as ground and pseudo‐ground states. The ground state is a thermodynamically stable phase, and the pseudo‐ground state is an unstable or metastable phase. For NC, the formation energy diagram shows that the stability increases for the value below <i>x</i>=0.50–0.58, and further desodition leads to a thermodynamic instability. For NCRT, the formation energy reveals that the increase in stability is still observed for a value below <i>x</i>=0.67. These results imply that the desodiated NCRT at <i>x</i>=0.67 is thermodynamically more stable than NC. The atomic structures of NC with <i>x</i>=0, 0.25, and 0.67 are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0001">1a</link>. The desodiated structure at <i>x</i>=0.25 shows that the vacancy sites are observed in each Na layer, which can be deemed as a random Na‐extraction reaction derived from only Cr in the TM layer and Cr participates in the charge compensation. However, that at <i>x</i>=0.67 interestingly shows that the empty Na layer with fully desodiated layer (denoted as Na‐free layer) between the CrO<sub>2</sub> layers is formed. In the random desodiation process, the oxidation of Cr in one TM layer does induce the extraction of sodium from the adjacent two sodium layers, while the sodium in two adjacent layers does not always extract the same amount. Therefore, the homogeneous oxidation of Cr in the two adjacent TM layers will induce one sodium layer to extract more sodium than the adjacent two sodium layers, until the formation of Na‐free layer and subsequent Cr migration. This results in the thermodynamic severe instability accompanying by the severe Cr‐migration to the Na layers.</p><figure xml:id="anie202216174-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202216174:anie202216174-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202216174-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Theoretical calculations of the formation of Na‐free layer in NC and its suppression in NCRT. Formation energies as functions of Na vacancy content (<i>x</i>) in a) NC and b) NCRT over the vacancy range of 0.17≤<i>x</i>≤1.0. The black (NC) and red (NCRT) circles represent ground states, and the gray circles indicate pseudo‐ground states for the both layered oxides. c) Combined graphs of PDOSs of Ru 4<i>d</i>‐electron and Cr 3<i>d</i>‐electrons at <i>x</i>=0.17 and <i>x</i>=0.67 in the mixed layer of NCRT. d) Spatial electron distribution in the occupied bands segregated into the two ranges: i) −1.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤0, ii) −2.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤−1.0 based on the PDOS profiles. Atomic structures of e) NC and f) NCRT with 0.67 Na vacancy, and calculated interlayer distances between the TMO<sub>2</sub> layer and the sodium layer are also shown. The distances of adjacent TM layers in NC are 5.5621 and 4.9355 Å, and that in NCRT are 5.7621 and 5.5201 Å.</p></caption></figure><p>Taking geometric point of view into considerations, Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0001">1b</link> likewise illustrates the desodiated structures of NCRT with <i>x</i>=0, 0.33, and 0.67, and their TM layers contain the mixed layer including CrO<sub>6</sub>, RuO<sub>6</sub>, and TiO<sub>6</sub> octahedrons and the Cr‐rich layers. The detailed Ru and Ti positions are shown in Figure S1. Intriguingly, the desodiated structure at <i>x</i>=0.33 indicates that vacancy sites are present nearby the mixed layers. This can be deemed as a selective desodiation reaction, which likely stems from different cationic redox potentials and are in contrast to the desodiation mechanism for NC. In general, 4<i>d</i>‐based cation redox reactions occur in lower voltage window than 3<i>d</i>‐based ones in layered oxides for SIBs and LIBs (herein, Ru<sup>4+</sup>/Ru<sup>5+</sup> versus Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup>). Along this line, we can surmise that the Na related to the Ru redox reaction are first extracted from the host material and the inactive Ti can function as a pillar to avoid complete desodiation in Ru‐contained TM layers. In addition, the desodiated NCRT at <i>x</i>=0.67 shows the presence of Na in all Na layers without the Na‐free layer.</p><p>To unlock the origin of the selective desodiation mechanism and the all‐layer‐stacked Na under high desodiated state, the electronic structures of TM (Cr, Ru and Ti) in the mixed layer of NCRT was investigated. Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2c</link> and Figure S2a shows the combined graphs of partial density of states (PDOSs) of the TM n<i>d</i>‐electron (<i>n</i>=3 or 4) at <i>x</i>=0.17 and <i>x</i>=0.67 in NCRT. The PDOSs of Ru 4<i>d</i>‐electron at <i>x</i>=0.17 indicate an electronic structure of Ru<sup>4+</sup> having a redox capable electron (t<sub>2g</sub><sup>4</sup>) and the Ru(t<sub>2g</sub>)‐band is present in the higher energy states (−1.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤0) than the Cr(t<sub>2g</sub>)‐band (−2.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤−1.0), which imply that a single cation redox reaction of Ru<sup>4+</sup>/Ru<sup>5+</sup> first occurs because of the low‐lying Cr 3<i>d</i>‐electron, relatively. It indicates that these different t<sub>2g</sub>‐band positions lead to the selective desodiation mechanism of NCRT as compared NC. The PDOSs of Ti 3<i>d</i>‐electron unquestionably show a Ti<sup>4+</sup> electronic configuration in the octahedral complex, implying that there is no redox capable Ti 3<i>d</i>‐electron to compensate charge balance. For a better understanding, we calculated spatial electron distribution in the mixed layer based on the three‐divided occupied bands: i) −1.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤0, ii) −2.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤−1.0, iii) −3.0≤<i>E</i>−<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>≤−2.0, as illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2d</link> and Figure S2b. It is obvious that the hybridized Ru(4<i>d</i>)‐O(2<i>p</i>) electron is present in the first occupied band range, whereas there is no the Cr(3<i>d</i>)‐O(2<i>p</i>) electron densities. In the second occupied band range, the Cr 3<i>d</i>‐electron hybridized with the O 2<i>p</i>‐electron is strikingly observed. As shown in the PDOSs of Ti, the spatial electron density in the final region unambiguously reveals non‐existence of Ti 3<i>d</i>‐electron unhybridized with O 2<i>p</i>‐electron (i.e., lone‐pair O). These results imply that the first contributing species for charge compensation mechanism is the cationic redox reaction via the Ru(4<i>d</i>)‐O(2<i>p</i>). Based on the concrete understanding on the desodiation mechanism, we suggest that a well distributed mixed layer in NCRT plays a crucial role in suppressing the formation of Na‐free layer and alleviating subsequent crystal structure collapse and TM migration.</p><p>Interlayer distances between the Na layer and between the TM layer in charged NC and charged NCRT were further calculated, shown in Figure S3 and Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2e</link>, <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">f</link>. Considering that Na‐extraction from layered oxides generally leads to the increase of Na layer distances at the early stage accompanied by subsequent increase or sharp decrease,<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0045"><sup>[20]</sup></link> both the cathodes show the similar tendency as the vacancy content increases at the initial stage. However, in NC, severe distance shrinkage occurs at <i>x</i>=0.67 due to the formation of Na‐free layers, shown in Figure S3a‐c and Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2e</link>, and the distances of adjacent TM layers in NC are 5.5621 Å and 4.9355 Å. On the contrary, the distances of adjacent TM layers in NCRT at <i>x</i>=0.67 are 5.7621 Å and 5.5201 Å (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2f</link>), suggesting that the severe distance shrinkage of Na layer does not occur in NCRT, which can be attributed to the presence of non‐deintercalated Na in all Na layers. These results suggest that the Na‐free layer makes the NC structure very unstable and will activate the Cr‐migration to the Na layer, while this adverse issue can be addressed by Ru/Ti co‐doping.</p><p>It is excepted that both Ti and Ru can be simultaneously doped into NC since Ti (IV, 0.605 Å) and Ru (IV, 0.62 Å) show similar ionic radius with Cr (III, 0.615 Å), and they have been reported to be compatible with the layered sodium transition metal oxide structure.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0050"><sup>[21]</sup></link> Layered NCRT oxide was successfully synthesized through the classic solid phase reaction process. First, the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) was conducted to verify the crystal structure. NC (Figure S4a) and NCRT (Figure S4b) show the same diffraction peaks, which is consistent with the previously reported NC samples.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0054"><sup>[22]</sup></link> Rietveld refinement of the XRD profiles shows that both NCRT and NC exhibit O3‐type phase structure with the space group of <i>R</i>‐3<i>m</i>, and the detailed refinement parameters are listed in Table S1. Particularly, the lattice parameters <i>a/b</i> of NCRT (2.9612 Å) and NC (2.9699 Å) are almost the same, which can be attributed to 15 % Cr (III, 0.615 Å) replaced by 10 % Ru (IV, 0.62 Å) and 5 % Ti (IV, 0.605 Å), namely 0.615 Å×15 %=0.62 Å×10 %+0.605 Å×5%. High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) was used to further confirm the crystal structure of NCRT, and the lattice spacing (Figure S4c) corresponding to the lattice planes is well consistent with the XRD result. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was conducted to characterize the morphology of NCRT and NC, shown in Figure S5. NCRT shows the same micrometer particles with NC, and the NC shows the same morphology with the previous reports.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0058"><sup>[23]</sup></link> The energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) reveals that the elements of Na, Cr, Ru, Ti, and O are uniformly distributed in NCRT particles (Figure S4d).</p><p>To obtain the general structural evolution of NC and NCRT, in situ XRD and ex situ synchrotron‐based XRD (sXRD) were conducted. As shown in Figure S6a and S7a, the O3‐type NC is transformed into the mixed phases of P′3 and O3′ in full charge state, which is consistent with the previous reports.<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0035"><sup>[16a]</sup></link> The weak diffraction peaks of fully charged NC suggest the crystal structure may be damaged due to the severe Cr‐migration derived from the Na‐free layer formation. Under full charge state, NCRT is transformed from O3 to P3 phase with high crystallinity (Figure S6b and S7b), and corresponding lattice parameters <i>a</i>/<i>b</i> and <i>c</i> are 2.9180(5) Å and 16.9917(9) Å (Table S2), respectively. In contrast to the fully charged NC, fully charged NCRT shows more stable structural stability due to the restrained Cr‐migration by creating sodium‐occupation layer under full charge state. HAADF‐STEM was further conducted to observe sodium‐occupation layer and the Na‐free layer in both fully charged NCRT and fully charged NC electrodes. In the atomic HAADF‐STEM images of NC (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">3a</link>) and NCRT (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">3b</link>), the bright dots represent the heavy TM signal while the light Na and O cannot be observed in the crystal because the signal contrast is related to <i>Z</i><sup>1.7</sup> (<i>Z</i>=atomic number).<link href="#anie202216174-bib-0062"><sup>[24]</sup></link> The TM slabs of fully charged NCRT are still complete, while the TM slabs of fully charged NC show obvious fluctuations. Particularly, the slightly bright dots of fully charged NC in sodium layers are attributed to the migrated Cr from the TM layers because the Na‐free layer induces the TM migration. The atomic STEM X‐ray energy dispersive spectroscopy (STEM‐EDS) was conducted to confirm the formation of Na‐free layer and the presence of Cr in sodium layers for charged NC electrode. As shown in Figure S8, the middle sodium layer shows negligible sodium signals, corresponding to the formation of Na‐free layer, and obvious Cr signals in the middle sodium layer are also detected. Therefore, it is believed that the Na‐free layer is formed, and Cr migrates from Cr layer to sodium layer. These results are consistent with theoretical calculations. To directly show the destroy of Na‐free layer on crystal structure, geometric phase analysis (GPA) derived from the HAADF‐STEM was also conducted to observe the internal strain of fully charged NC (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">3c</link>) and fully charged NCRT (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">3d</link>). Slight internal strain is observed in fully charged NCRT while it is very serious in fully charged NC, suggesting more stable crystal structure of NCRT than NC in full charge state. Corresponding line profiles of Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">3a</link> and <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">b</link> are further shown in Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">3e</link> and <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0003">f</link>, respectively, and detailed TM slab spacing is also provided. The spacing of approximately 5.6 Å in fully charged NCRT and fully charged NC corresponds to the sodium‐occupation layer and the spacing of approximately 4.8/4.9 Å in fully charged NC corresponds to the Na‐free layer, which is consistent with the theoretical calculations (Figure <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">2e</link> and <link href="#anie202216174-fig-0002">f</link>) and sXRD (Figure S6a and b) results.</p><figure xml:id="anie202216174-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202216174:anie202216174-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202216174-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Atomic‐level observation of the Na‐free layer and the sodium‐occupation layer in charged NC and charged NCRT. Therefore, GITT and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were conducted to characterize the bulk and surface/interface kinetics, respectively. These results further confirm that successfully suppressing Na‐free layer can effectively enhance the electrochemical performance of layered sodium transition metal oxide cathodes.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202216174-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we find that TM migration in NC is induced by the formation of Na‐free layers under high charge potentials, and the random desodiation reaction accounts for the formation of Na‐free layer. This adverse issue in NC can be well addressed by our developed Ru/Ti co‐doping strategy. The active 4<i>d</i> Ru is easier to oxidize than active 3<i>d</i> Cr in NCRT, so the random desodiation reaction in NC can be transformed into a selective desodiation reaction after Ru/Ti co‐doping. Theoretical calculations, HAADF‐STEM and GITT reveal that the formation of Na‐free layer is suppressed in Ru/Ti co‐doped NCRT under high charge potentials, and an ultra‐high rate capability of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (91.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and excellent stabilities of 81.3 % for 1000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 62.8 % for 2200 cycles at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> are obtained in the voltage window of 1.5–3.92 V. Additionally, the co‐doping strategy is proven to show the same positive effect on suppressing the adverse Fe migration and enhancing electrochemical performance when the strategy is adopted to NF.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202216174-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (No. 2021YFA1202300), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 22209069 and 22075132), the Natural Science Foundation of Jiangsu Province, China (Nos.
