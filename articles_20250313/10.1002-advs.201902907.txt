Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>3D electrode design is normally opted for multiple advantages, however, instability/detachment of active material causes the pulverization and degradation of the structure, and ultimately poor cyclic stability. Here, a dually protected, highly compressible, and freestanding anode is presented for sodium‐ion batteries, where 3D carbon nanotube (CNT) sponge is decorated with homogeneously dispersed CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles (NPs) which are protected under carbon overcoat (CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C). The 3D CNT sponge delivers enough space for high mass loading while providing high mechanical strength and faster conduction pathway among the NPs. The outer amorphous carbon overcoat controls the formation of solid electrolyte interphase film by avoiding direct contact of CoSe<sub>2</sub> with electrolyte, accommodates large volume changes, and ultimately enhances the overall conductivity of cell and assists in transmitting electron to an external circuit. Moreover, the hybrid can be densified up to 11‐fold without affecting its microstructure that results in ultrahigh areal mass loading of 17.4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> and an areal capacity of 7.03 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> along with a high gravimetric capacity of 531 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Thus, compact and smart devices can be realized by this new electrode design for heavy‐duty commercial applications.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs1473-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A 3D and dually protected electrode design (carbon nanotube (CNT)/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C) is presented in which a CNT sponge is decorated with CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles and then externally protected with carbon layers. The representative 3D CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C heterostructure has the ability to be densified without influencing its microstructure that results in high areal capacity along with high gravimetric capacity for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1473:advs1473-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource xml:id="advs1473-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="advs1473-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Owing to even distribution and a large abundance of sodium (Na), sodium‐ion battery (SIB), the most promising alternative to lithium‐ion battery (LIB), has received considerable attention in the last few years.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0001 #advs1473-bib-0002 #advs1473-bib-0003 #advs1473-bib-0004"/> However, the larger radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (0.102 nm) compared with Li<sup>+</sup> (0.076 nm) sluggish its kinetics and collapse the structure of electrode materials during insertion/de‐insertion processes, therefore, better host materials need to be found that are suitable for SIBs.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0005 #advs1473-bib-0006"/> Transition metal diselenides, a class of transition metal chalcogenides (TMC) based material, are very promising candidates as an anode for SIB due to their higher theoretical capacity (≈494.36 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for CoSe<sub>2</sub>) than graphite (≈35 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>)<link href="#advs1473-bib-0007 #advs1473-bib-0008"/> and low synthesis cost.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0009 #advs1473-bib-0010"/> Nevertheless, severe volume changes during sodiation/desodiation process, slow transport of charges, and diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> along with high intrinsic resistance resulted in capacity fading and hindered the practical application of transition metal diselenides.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0009 #advs1473-bib-0011"/> Until now, a lot of efforts have been devoted to overcome these issues by synthesizing transition metal diselenides based nanostructures with various shapes, morphologies, and particle sizes.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0010 #advs1473-bib-0011 #advs1473-bib-0012"/></p><p>Although creating voids, hollow, or porous nanostructures have improved the gravimetric capacity due to their ability to accommodate the volume expansion, shorten the diffusion path for charge transportation, optimized the electrode and electrolyte contact area, etc.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0013 #advs1473-bib-0014"/> However, downsizing the materials at the nanoscale is not always in favor of rechargeable batteries for practical applications.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0015"/> Because the nanomaterials possess low tap density that results in low areal mass loading (&lt;1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>), consequently lowering the areal and volumetric capacities which are very important metrics in practical applications.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0016 #advs1473-bib-0017"/> Traditional high areal mass loadings of active material bring high electrode thicknesses which severely limits the in‐depth accessibility of active materials ultimately fading the reversible capacity.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0015 #advs1473-bib-0018"/> Moreover, mixing of the binder is required in traditional electrode preparation which decreases the overall conductivity of the electrode, increase cell resistance, and more profound to the volume variations. While these volume changes during repeated charging/discharging produce cracks and cause pulverization of electrode, hence bringing poor electrical contact of active material with the current collector or even detaching of the active materials from the current collector.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0019 #advs1473-bib-0020"/> To resolve these issues various electrode architecture designs have been proposed using copper as current collector.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0021"/> But, copper‐based current collector electrodes may increase the overall volume and decrease the energy density of the device. To resolve this issue 3D, freestanding electrode configurations are very attractive, where the active materials are directly grown over 3D conductive scaffold alleviating the need of any dead additives.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0022"/> Among various 3D conductive scaffolds, 3D carbon nanotube (CNT) sponges have found a lot of attention lately due to their ultrahigh porosity, conductivity, and well‐interconnected 3D networks that not only provide high mass flow but also allow high mass loading in minimal area.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0023 #advs1473-bib-0024 #advs1473-bib-0025 #advs1473-bib-0026"/> However, several challenges limit the application of 3D CNT sponge as conductive host; for instance: (i) the selected material should be compatible with the substrate (CNT sponge) and should have enough adsorption energy between the active material and substrate surface to guarantee the tight bond.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0027"/> (ii) The solubility of two precursors (i.e., transition metal precursor and Se precursor) together is another big issue where poor solubility results in suspension/precipitation instead of the clear transparent solution. Poor solubility affects efficient infiltration of the precursor into the porous host (iii) inability of current approaches to load high amounts of active materials, (iv) detaching of active materials from CNT surface, and (v) poor control over direct electrode/electrolyte contact, and (vi) inhomogenous solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film formation.</p><p>In this work, a novel dually protected electrode design was presented by fabricating CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C as an anode for SIB. The CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles (NPs) are decorated on each individual CNT which comprised a 3D network by simply two‐step hydrothermal processes and then externally protected with amorphous carbon layers through a carbonization process. This unique composite greatly improves the Na<sup>+</sup> storage by harnessing the unique morphological and electronic features and surface properties. In particular, the highly conductive and compressible 3D CNT sponge provides multichannel and continuous pathways for ionic and electronic conduction, enough space, and porosity for high mass loading of active CoSe<sub>2</sub> and electrolyte infiltration as well as accommodate the volume expansion upon cycling. In contrast to conventional 3D electrodes, the external dual protection by carbon layers on individual CNT through the 3D matrix not only increases the overall conductivity but also buffers the volume expansion and controlled the SEI film thickness by preventing active material from direct contact with corrosive surrounding. By harnessing all the features, our hybrid is used as a freestanding electrode for SIBs (i.e., without dead elements such as binders, conductive additives, and current collector) and its fabrication is simpler and lighter. It delivers a high gravimetric capacity of 531 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at high mass loading of 17.4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> which corresponds to the exceptional areal and volumetric capacity of &gt;7 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> and 185.2 mAh cm<sup>−3</sup>, respectively. Hence, by extending such a unique designing to other transition metal chalcogenides will take the electrochemical energy storage devices to the doorstep of commercial installations.</p><p>The carbon‐coated 3D CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> hybrid was obtained via three steps as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs1473-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a; initially, in situ Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs were uniformly decorated in a CNT sponge by a hydrothermal process, then, selenization of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> into crystalline CoSe<sub>2</sub>, resulting in a 3D CoSe<sub>2</sub>@CNT hybrid without disturbing the morphology. The NaBH<sub>4</sub> was used to reduce the Se atoms into Se<sup>−2</sup> anions and Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> into Co<sup>+2</sup> cations, which then reacted at 180 °C to form CoSe<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0028 #advs1473-bib-0029 #advs1473-bib-0030 #advs1473-bib-0031"/> Finally, every individual CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> hybrid structure was coated with a carbon shell by carbonization using glucose at high temperature under a neutral atmosphere. During the cell assembly various hybrid sponges with an initial thickness <i>h</i><sub>i</sub> were compressed into dense framework with a final thickness <i>h</i><sub>c</sub> and directly used as freestanding anodes in the coin‐type cell (Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0001"/>b). The areal mass loading (<i>M</i><sub>a</sub>) or density (<i>d</i>) of as‐obtained electrode was determined by <i>M</i><sub>a</sub> = <i>dV</i>/<i>A</i> = d<i>h</i>, where <i>d</i>, <i>V</i>, <i>A</i>, and <i>h</i> are the density, volume, base area, and thickness of the hybrid sponge, respectively. This indicates that the hybrid sponge having same percentage of active mass loading (CoSe<sub>2</sub>) may proportionally increase the <i>d</i> and <i>M</i><sub>a</sub> by simply choosing the CNT framework with larger <i>h</i><sub>i</sub> and compressing it to fitting <i>h</i><sub>c</sub> into coin cell. In this way, a densified framework was achieved and so enhanced the density, areal mass loading, and areal capacity.</p><figure xml:id="advs1473-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1473:advs1473-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs1473-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration for the fabrication process of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C sponge. b) Photographs of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C sponge before and after compaction during cell assembly with description of initial (<i>h</i><sub>i</sub>) and final (<i>h</i><sub>c</sub>) thicknesses.</caption></figure><p>Figure S1 (Supporting Information) shows that the CNTs have a smooth surface with interconnected nanotubes networks as an ideal surface to decorate the active materials. Importantly, it is found that the decoration of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs did not bring any change in the 3D network of CNTs, however, rough surface confirms the formation of CNTs/Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> core–shell structure (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs1473-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). The in situ grafting of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs on the surface of CNT ensures enhanced contact between them. Upon conversion of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> into CoSe<sub>2</sub> NPs on CNTs surface (Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0002"/>b) and coating of the carbon shell on the CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> hybrid (Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0002"/>c), the 3D structural integrity is well‐maintained pointing the high strength of 3D framework and carbon shell which has no effect on the morphology. Our hybrid morphology is unique and different from previously reported CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> composites, where a simple combination of CNT and CoSe<sub>2</sub> was obtained. The previous composites are in powder form and may aggregate during long electrochemical cycling.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0032 #advs1473-bib-0033"/> However, in our hybrid, a core–double shell structure like morphology was obtained, which is homogenous in its nature and unique from reported literature. Besides our glucose‐derived carbon shell was homogenously coated over the entire core–shell (CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>) structure that has strong interaction with active material and beneficial for long cycling, while its thickness kept as low which allows faster ionic flow between electrode and electrolyte.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0032 #advs1473-bib-0033"/> The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis is used to determine the size of NPs (both Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub>), the diameter of nanotube in each hybrid, and thickness of carbon coating. Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0002"/>d,g reveals that fine Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs having a size of ≈8 nm are well‐adhered to the CNTs surface which are about 40 nm in diameter. The analysis of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> revealed up to ≈25 nm increase in particle size due to the ripening of NPs under hydrothermal/calcination reactions as shown in Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0002"/>e,h. Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0002"/>f,i are showing the uniform coating of carbon on the surface of a hybrid with an average thickness of ≈4.0 nm. This carbon overcoat is uniformly covering every CoSe<sub>2</sub> NP forming an intertwined structure in which CoSe<sub>2</sub> NPs are tightly bound with 3D CNT networks. The new physical properties (such as high conductivity and mechanical strength) and synergistic effect between CoSe<sub>2</sub> and dual conductive network can be benefitted from no or low volume expansion, limited and stable SEI film, enhanced capacity, and structural integrity upon cycling. To verify the conversion of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to CoSe<sub>2</sub>, high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C were obtained that represent the lattice spacing of ≈0.30 nm correspond to the (011) plane of CoSe<sub>2</sub> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a). The selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C is given in Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"/>b, which is well matched with the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) measurements (Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"/>c). XRD patterns were noted at each synthesis process and are presented in Figure S2 (Supporting Information) and Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"/>c. The XRD spectrum of Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is well‐matched with the cubic phase (JCPDS No. 42‐1467) showing highly crystalline growth of NPs (Figure S2, Supporting Information), which upon selenization yielded pure CoSe<sub>2</sub> in hexagonal phase (JCPDS No. 53‐0449) as shown in Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"/>c. However, a reduced peak intensity for CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C hybrid further confirms carbon coating on the CoSe<sub>2</sub> decorated CNTs (Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"/>c). Figure S3 (Supporting Information) displays an energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX) mapping recorded with the help of scanning electron microscopy (SEM) for carbon, cobalt, and selenium where carbon concentrated at the core, and cobalt and selenium uniformly dispersed along the CNTs. The high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning TEM (HAADF‐STEM) and corresponding EDX mappings are given in Figure <link href="#advs1473-fig-0003"/>d. An intense signal of carbon is observed in the middle, whereas the cobalt and selenium are concentrated over the CNT core across the shell. A weak signal of carbon is also observed across the CoSe<sub>2</sub> NPs because of the thinner carbon overcoat.</p><figure xml:id="advs1473-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1473:advs1473-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs1473-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Morphological characterizations of hybrid sponges: a–c) SEM images of CNT/Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>, and CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C, respectively. In addition to the G‐band (1583 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and D‐band (1348 cm<sup>−1</sup>) coming from CNTs, the sharp peak at 188 cm<sup>−1</sup> in both CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> and CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C corresponds to the SeSe stretching mode of cubic CoSe<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#advs1473-bib-0034 #advs1473-bib-0035 #advs1473-bib-0036"/> whereas a small peak at about 672 cm<sup>−1</sup> is associated with A<sub>1g</sub> mode of CoSe<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0037"/> The valence states of different element in CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C were evaluated using X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) technique. The survey spectrum shows the peaks of Co, Se, C, and O indicating high purity of hybrid (Figure S4, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution spectrum of Co 2p region indicates binding energies of Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub> located at 778.4 and 793.45 eV, respectively, confirming the Co<sup>2+</sup> The repeated insertion–extraction of Na<sup>+</sup> in the active materials may turn the highly crystalline structure into amorphous that improved the electrochemical process.<link href="#advs1473-bib-0043"/></p><p>In summary, a dual protection strategy is applied on CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C electrode to achieve high cyclic stability, mass loading, and areal capacity. The Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> NPs grafted onto CNT are converted into CoSe<sub>2</sub> using safe and environmental friendly hydrothermal/calcination process with core–shell structure (CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>) and then intertwined with ≈4 nm thick carbon overcoat by carbonization approach. The CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C presented a high discharge capacity of 531 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. Moreover, it was densified by compaction with preserving its 3D networks and morphology due to high inherent porosity of hybrid sponge. Using this approach a high areal capacity up to 7.04 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> corresponds to an ultrahigh areal loading (17.4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>). The novel synthesis and improved areal capacity of the CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C sponge make it an appropriate anode material for practical energy storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="advs1473-sec-0020"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p>Information about the synthesis of CNT/Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, used characterization tools, and electrochemical methods are provided in the Supporting Information.</p><p><i>Synthesis of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub></i>: The CNT/Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> was converted into CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> by novel solvothermal/calcination process. In a typical synthesis, 4.0 mmole NaBH<sub>4</sub> was first dissolved into 10 mL deionized (DI) water and then 2.0 mmole Se powder was mixed by magnetic stirring. Afterward, CNT/Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> sponge was immersed into the freshly prepared solution and then the solution containing CNT/Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> sponge transferred into a 20 mL Teflon lined autoclave for the solvothermal reaction at 180 °C for 10 h. After the reaction, the sponge was again washed with ethanol and DI water and then freeze‐dried. Finally, to remove the Se impurity and attain a highly crystalline structure of CoSe<sub>2</sub>, the hybrid sponge was annealed at 400 °C for 2 h and obtained highly crystalline CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> sponge.</p><p><i>Synthesis of CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C</i>: The CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C was obtained by the carbonization process. First, the as‐synthesized CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> sponge was dipped into 1.0 <sc>m</sc> aqueous glucose solution and executed for hydrothermal reaction in an electric oven at 180 °C for 10 h. The glucose‐coated CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> sponge was washed with ethanol and DI water to remove impurity. Finally, glucose‐coated CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub> sponge was carbonized by heat treatment at 450 °C for 2 h in the inert environment and the final product CNT/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/C was obtained.</p></section><section xml:id="advs1473-sec-0030" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by Key Area R&amp;D Program of Guangdong Province (2018B030327001, 2018B010109009).
