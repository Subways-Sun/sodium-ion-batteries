Defect‐engineered amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub>‐reduced graphene oxide (rGO) nanohybrid was synthesized by soft‐chemical reduction of K<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub> in graphene oxide colloids. Mo K‐edge X‐ray absorption spectroscopy clearly demonstrates the rutile‐type local atomic structure of amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub> with significant oxygen vacancies and intimate electronic coupling with rGO. The defect‐introduced MoO<sub>2</sub>‐rGO nanohybrid shows excellent bifunctionality as electrocatalyst for hydrogen evolution reaction and electrode for sodium‐ion batteries, which are superior to those of crystalline MoO<sub>2</sub>‐rGO homologue. The beneficial effect of simultaneous defect control and rGO coupling can be ascribed to the provision of oxygen vacancies acting as active sites, the increase of electrical conductivity, and the improvement of reaction kinetics.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>The exploration of electrocatalyst/electrode materials</b> has evoked intense research activity because of their crucial roles in many renewable energy technologies. Here we report a novel synthetic strategy to economically‐feasible bifunctional electrocatalyst/electrode materials via the synergetic combination of defect engineering and rGO coupling. xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The exploration of economically‐feasible efficient bifunctional electrocatalyst and electrode materials has attracted a great deal of research interest because of their crucial roles in many emerging energy technologies such as secondary batteries, fuel cells, and electrolyzers.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> In comparison with transition metal dichalcogenides and hydroxides, transition metal oxides (TMOs) boast better chemical stability in a wide pH region, which renders them promising alternatives for conventional noble metal‐based electrocatalyst materials.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0007"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Among diverse TMOs, molybdenum dioxide (MoO<sub>2</sub>) has received prime attention as promising electrocatalyst and electrode material because of its high electrical conductivity and electrochemical activity.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0012"><sup>[3]</sup></link> To further improve the electrochemistry‐related functionalities of MoO<sub>2</sub>, many research strategies have been developed such as chemical substitution, nanostructure formation, composite formation, and so on.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0017"><sup>[4]</sup></link> The defect engineering is supposed to provide new synthetic way to enhance the electrocatalyst/electrode functionality of TMOs, since the creation of surface defects with enhanced structural disordering leads to the provision of many electrocatalytically active sites and to the efficient accommodation of structural distortion during the electrochemical cycling.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0022"><sup>[5]</sup></link> However, the structural disorder created by the defect introduction is detrimental to electrical conductivity and thus to electrochemistry‐related performances.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0027"><sup>[6]</sup></link> This drawback of defect‐introduced metal oxide might be circumvented by hybridization with highly conductive species like reduced graphene oxide (rGO).<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0028"><sup>[7]</sup></link> The solution‐based soft‐chemical synthesis of amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub> allows to easily hybridize with colloidal species like rGO and for large‐scale synthesis crucial for commercialization.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0031"><sup>[8]</sup></link> At the time of this submission, we are unaware of any other study about the synergetic combination between defect control and rGO coupling to improve the electrochemistry‐related functionalities of MoO<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>In this work, we report the synergetic advantage of simultaneous defect engineering and hybridization with rGO on the bifunctional performance of molybdenum dioxide as electrocatalyst for hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and electrode for sodium ion batteries (SIB). The evolutions of the local crystal and electronic structures, and transport properties of MoO<sub>2</sub> upon the defect introduction and rGO coupling are systematically investigated to elucidate mechanism responsible for the improved energy‐related functionality of this material.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The defect‐introduced amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystal was synthesized by reacting 2.5 M KBH<sub>4</sub> solution with 0.25 M K<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub> solution at room temperature. The defect‐engineered amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub>‐rGO hybrid material was obtained by the same synthetic process in the presence of graphene oxide (GO) colloids. The obtained materials are denoted as <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b>, respectively. The well‐crystalline homologues with negligible defect content were obtained by calcination of <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials at 400 °C in Ar atmosphere (these materials are denoted as <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b>, respectively). The crystal structures of the present MoO<sub>2</sub>‐based materials were determined by powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) analysis. As plotted in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0001">1</link>a, both the <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials commonly displayed very diffuse diffraction features, indicating the X‐ray amorphous nature of these materials. Conversely, well‐developed Bragg reflections of rutile MoO<sub>2</sub> phase were discernible for both the <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b> materials, confirming their crystalline nature.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0034"><sup>[9]</sup></link> In addition, both the <b>aMG</b> and <b>cMG</b> materials showed a broad peak at 2<i>θ</i> =∼26° corresponding to the (002) plane of rGO nanosheet, reflecting the successful hybridization of rGO and MoO<sub>2</sub> in these materials.</p><figure xml:id="asia202101013-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202101013:asia202101013-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202101013-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Powder XRD patterns, (b) micro‐Raman spectra, and (c) FE‐SEM images of <b>aM</b>, <b>cM</b>, <b>aMG</b>, and <b>cMG</b> materials.</p></caption></figure><p>The chemical bonding nature of rGO and MoO<sub>2</sub> components in the present materials was verified by micro‐Raman analysis. As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0001">1</link>b, both the nanohybrids exhibited strong phonon lines of D and G peaks in the wavenumber region of ∼1350 and ∼1600 cm<sup>−1</sup>, confirming the incorporation of graphene species in these materials.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0035"><sup>[10]</sup></link> According to transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis, the defect‐introduced amorphous <b>aM</b> showed 0D nanocrystal morphology with the size of several tens of nanometers, see Figure S1. As illustrated in the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns, <b>aM</b> did not display diffused ring without distinct diffraction spots, whereas well‐defined diffraction spots were discernible for <b>cM</b>, confirming the crystallization of amorphous <b>aM</b> upon the calcination.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0038"><sup>[11]</sup></link> As can be seen from Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0001">1</link>c, the field emission‐scanning electron microscopy (FE‐SEM) analysis for <b>aMG</b> and <b>cMG</b> clearly demonstrated the composite formation of molybdenum dioxide with 2D rGO nanosheets.</p><p>Despite the lack of long‐range crystal order, there might be a certain level of structural order in local region of amorphous material. Thus, the local atomic arrangements of amorphous <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials were examined with Mo K‐edge extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectroscopy as compared with the well‐crystalline homologues, since this technique can provide a sensitive measure for probing the local structure of amorphous solids.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0039"><sup>[12]</sup></link> As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0002">2</link>a, the well‐crystalline <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b> materials displayed well‐developed <i>k</i><sup><i>3</i></sup>‐weighted EXAFS oscillation of rutile MoO<sub>2</sub> structure, which is similar to that of the reference MoO<sub>2</sub> not to that of the reference MoO<sub>3</sub>. Even though amorphous nature of <b>aM</b> makes EXAFS oscillation notably diffuse, the overall EXAFS spectral feature and contour plot of the wavelet‐transformed EXAFS data (Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0002">2</link>b) of <b>aM</b> material are still similar to those of MoO<sub>2</sub> rather than those of MoO<sub>3</sub>, strongly suggesting the MoO<sub>2</sub>‐type local structure of this material. There are close similarities between the spectra of rGO‐containing <b>aMG</b>/<b>cMG</b> materials and those of rGO‐free <b>aM</b>/<b>cM</b>, indicating the maintenance of MoO<sub>2</sub>‐type local structure upon the hybridization with rGO. As plotted in the Fourier transformed EXAFS data (FT‐EXAFS) of Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0002">2</link>c, all the present materials of <b>aM</b>, <b>cM</b>, <b>aMG</b>, and <b>cMG</b> commonly displayed distinct FT peaks of MoO<sub>2</sub> phase at ∼1.6 and ∼2.1 Å corresponding to (Mo−O) and (Mo−Mo) coordination shells, respectively, confirming the rutile‐type MoO<sub>2</sub> local structure in these materials. In contrast to <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b>, both the <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials exhibited only a negligible intensity for the FT peak at ∼3.2 Å, reflecting the lack of long‐range crystal order in this amorphous material. The FT features related to (Mo−O) and (Mo−Mo) bonding pairs appear less intense for <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> than for <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b>, strongly suggesting the higher content of crystal defects in the amorphous materials. In addition, the hybridization with rGO results in the notable depression of these FT peaks for both <b>aM</b> and <b>cM</b>, reflecting the additional formation of crystal defects and/or the increase of local structural disorder.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0042"><sup>[13]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202101013-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202101013:asia202101013-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202101013-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Mo K‐edge <i>k</i><sup>3</sup>‐weighted EXAFS oscillations, (b) contour plot of the wavelet‐transformed EXAFS data, (c) FT‐EXAFS data, (d) crystal structure of MoO<sub>2</sub>, and (e) Mo K‐edge XANES spectra of <b>aM</b>, <b>cM</b>, <b>aMG</b>, and <b>cMG</b> materials.</p></caption></figure><p>For quantitative determination of local structure of the present materials, non‐linear curve fitting analysis was carried out for the present Mo K‐edge EXAFS spectra. As depicted in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0002">2</link>c, all the experimental EXAFS data of the present materials were well‐reproduced with MoO<sub>2</sub> structure (Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0002">2</link>d), confirming the rutile‐type local atomic arrangements of these materials. As listed in Table <link href="#asia202101013-tbl-0001">1</link>, smaller coordination numbers (CNs) of (Mo−O) and (Mo−Mo) bonds occur for <b>aM</b> than for <b>cM</b>, highlighting the formation of oxygen vacancies upon the amorphization. Similarly, <b>aM</b> displayed larger Debye‐Waller factors (σ<sup>2</sup>) for both the coordination shells than does <b>cM</b>, confirming the enhancement of structural disorder caused by the amorphization. As summarized in Table <link href="#asia202101013-tbl-0001">1</link>, the hybridization with rGO also causes the decrease of CN and the increase of Debye‐Waller factor for both <b>aM</b> and <b>cM</b> materials, verifying the creation of oxygen vacancies and the enhancement of local structural disorder upon the coupling with rGO.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0043"><sup>[14]</sup></link> The observed increase of crystal defect and the structural disordering of MoO<sub>2</sub> domain upon the hybridization can be ascribed to the creation of crystal strain caused by strong interfacial interaction with rGO nanosheet. The bond distances of (Mo−O) and (Mo−Mo) are shorter for <b>aM</b> and <b>cM</b> than for rGO‐hybridized homologues (i. e. <b>aMG</b> and <b>cMG</b>), confirming the significant interfacial charge transfer from electron‐rich rGO to MoO<sub>2</sub>. The changes in the CNs and bond length upon hybridization with rGO nanosheet were found to be more prominent in the amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub> than in the crystalline MoO<sub>2</sub>, therefore clarifying the enhancement of interfacial interaction with rGO upon the introduction of crystal defects. The present EXAFS results underscore the rutile MoO<sub>2</sub>‐type local atomic ordering for amorphous molybdenum dioxides and the creation of oxygen defects upon the amorphization and hybridization near‐edge structure (XANES) spectroscopy. As plotted in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0002">2</link>e, both the <b>aM</b> and <b>cM</b> materials as well as their nanohybrids of <b>aMG</b> and <b>cMG</b> commonly demonstrated similar edge energy and overall spectral features, confirming the tetravalent molybdenum dioxide phase of these materials. The pre‐edge peak P related to the dipole‐forbidden 1s→4d transition is stronger for the amorphous <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> than for the well‐crystalline <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b>.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0046"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Since the intensity of this pre‐edge peak is proportional to the structural distortion from regular octahedral geometry, an enhanced spectral weight of the peak P for the amorphous materials can be regarded as another evidence for the structural deformation caused by the formation of oxygen vacancies.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0047"><sup>[16]</sup></link> In the main‐edge region, well‐resolved features A, B, and C corresponding to the dipole‐allowed 1s→5p transitions appeared for <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b>,<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0048"><sup>[17]</sup></link> whereas both amorphous <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials exhibited less‐resolved main‐edge features with lower intensities. This observation is well‐consistent with the defective structures of <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> with low crystallinity.</p><p>The influence of defect engineering and coupling with rGO on the electrocatalytic activity of molybdenum oxide was studied by employing the present materials as HER electrocatalysts. The HER measurements were performed in 0.5 M H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> solution with a scan rate of 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0003">3</link>a, both defect‐introduced <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials display higher HER activities with smaller overpotentials and higher current densities compared with those of well‐crystalline <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b> homologues, clearly demonstrating the beneficial effect of defect engineering on the electrocatalyst performance.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0051"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Between both the defect‐engineered compounds, the rGO‐containing <b>aMG</b> showed a significantly lower overpotential of 179 mV at a current density of 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> with much larger current density than that of rGO‐free <b>aM</b> (220 mV), underscoring the improvement of HER activity upon the hybridization with rGO. The better HER performance of <b>aM</b> than <b>cMG</b> (overpotential: 330 mV) strongly suggests the overwhelming effect of defect control over the hybridization with rGO. The remarkable improvement of the electrocatalytic activity of MoO<sub>2</sub> upon the defect engineering is attributable to the efficient role of oxygen vacancies as highly active sites for HER.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0055"><sup>[19]</sup></link> The remarkable advantage of simultaneous defect engineering and rGO coupling was cross‐confirmed by Tafel calculation with the anodic sweep data of linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0003">3</link>b, both the defect‐introduced <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials exhibit lower Tafel slopes of 80 and 70 mV dec<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, than those of well‐crystalline homologues (102 mV dec<sup>−1</sup> for <b>cMG</b> and 98 mV dec<sup>−1</sup> for <b>cMG</b>), confirming the significant improvement of HER kinetics upon the defect control and hybridization with conductive rGO.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0056"><sup>[20]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202101013-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202101013:asia202101013-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202101013-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) LSV curves, (b) Tafel plots, (c) Δj vs. scan rate plots, and (d) EIS data of <b>aM</b>, <b>cM</b>, <b>aMG</b>, and <b>cMG</b> materials.</p></caption></figure><p>As illustrated in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0003">3</link>c, the electrochemical surface areas (ECSAs) of the present materials were calculated from the linear slope in the plot of ▵j (=j<sub>a</sub>–j<sub>c</sub>) vs. RHE against the scan rate, since the ECSA corresponds to twice of double layer capacitance (C<sub>dl</sub>).<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0057"><sup>[21]</sup></link> Both the defect‐introduced <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials possess have much greater ECSAs than those of well‐crystalline <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b> homologues (1.18, 2.69, 0.086 and 0.79 mF cm<sup>−2</sup> for <b>aM</b>, <b>aMG</b>, <b>cM</b>, and <b>cMG</b>, respectively). As shown in Figure S2, even after the ECSA normalization, <b>aMG</b> still displayed significantly better HER functionality than that of <b>cM</b>, confirming the beneficial effect of simultaneous defect engineering and rGO coupling on the intrinsic electrocatalytic activity of MoO<sub>2</sub> material. The evolution of charge‐transfer kinetics upon the defect control and coupling with rGO was also investigated with electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), because this tool provides a sensitive probe for the transfer of electrons and ions at the interfacial region between electrocatalyst and electrolyte.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0058"><sup>[22]</sup></link> As plotted in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0003">3</link>d, at the constant potential of −0.25 V (vs. RHE), all the present MoO<sub>2</sub>‐based materials exhibited similar Nyquist plots composed of a semicircle whose radius shows inverse relationship to the magnitude of charge transfer resistance (R<sub>ct</sub>).<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0059"><sup>[23]</sup></link> The R<sub>ct</sub> value of amorphous <b>aM</b> was found to be smaller than that well‐crystalline <b>cM</b>, indicating the facilitation of charge transfer upon the amorphization. Such an improved charge transfer kinetics of amorphous <b>aM</b> material is attributable to the increased surface defects acting as proton adsorption sites and the depressed orbital overlap inside catalyst lattice promoting the orbital interaction with surface‐adsorbed reactants.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0060"><sup>[24]</sup></link> In comparison with rGO‐free <b>aM</b>, <b>aMG</b> displayed much smaller radius of the semicircle, clearly demonstrating the improvement of interfacial charge transfer and ion diffusion upon the hybridization of MoO<sub>2</sub> with conductive rGO. The observed excellent charge transfer kinetics of <b>aMG</b> can be ascribed to the optimization of the electronic and surface structures upon the synergetic combination of defect control and coupling with rGO.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0064"><sup>[25]</sup></link> Additionally, the beneficial effect of the present synergetic control on the structural and electrochemical stabilities of MoO<sub>2</sub> was verified by chronopotentiometry, powder XRD, and micro‐Raman analyses for <b>aMG</b> material. According to the chronopotentiometry measurement at a current density of 10 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, the <b>aMG</b> material exhibited only a negligible voltage decay of ∼4.3% after 10 h, clearly demonstrating its excellent electrocatalytic stability (Figure S3). Also, powder XRD and micro‐Raman results clarified no notable modification of crystal structure and chemical bonding nature after the catalytic reaction, confirming its high structural stability during electrocatalytic reaction (Figure S4).</p><p>Considering the high electrochemical activity of rGO‐containing materials, both the <b>aMG</b> and <b>cMG</b> materials were tested as electrodes for SIB to investigate the effects of defect engineering and hybridization with rGO on the electrode performance of molybdenum dioxide. As depicted in Figures <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0004">4</link>a and 4b, both the materials demonstrate similar potential profiles of gradual potential slopes with proceeding the discharging process, strongly suggesting that the electrode activity of these materials originates from molybdenum dioxide component.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0067"><sup>[26]</sup></link> The absence of well‐defined plateau demonstrates the occurrence of single‐phase reaction during the electrochemical cycling. As presented in Figure S5, the defect‐introduced <b>aM</b> material delivers much greater discharge‐charge capacities than those of crystalline <b>cM</b> homologue, emphasizing the remarkable advantage of defect engineering in improving SIB electrode functionality of MoO<sub>2</sub>. This result provides strong evidence for the beneficial effect of crystal defect on the electrode functionality of MoO<sub>2</sub> for SIB. The hybridization with rGO leads to the additional improvement of discharge‐charge capacities of defect‐introduced MoO<sub>2</sub> material (Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0004">4</link>c). Among the materials under investigation, <b>aMG</b> delivers the much larger discharge capacity of 240 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for the 50th cycle than that of <b>cMG</b> (80 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), highlighting the synergetic merit of defect engineering and rGO coupling on the SIB electrode functionality of molybdenum dioxide. In Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0004">4</link>d, the <b>aMG</b> material shows better rate characteristics than <b>cMG</b>, indicting the beneficial effect of defect introduction on the rate performance of molybdenum dioxide.</p><figure xml:id="asia202101013-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202101013:asia202101013-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202101013-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a, b) Potential profiles, (c) capacity plots, (d) rate‐dependent capacity plots, (e) the fraction of the capacitive and diffusion‐controlled contributions, and (f) EIS data of <b>aMG</b> and <b>cMG</b> materials.</p></caption></figure><p>The effects of defect engineering and rGO coupling on the electrochemical process of molybdenum dioxide were investigated by calculating the capacitive contributions at different scan rates.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0068"><sup>[27]</sup></link> As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0004">4</link>e, the defect‐introduced <b>aMG</b> material exhibited larger capacitive contribution than that of <b>cMG</b> material, indicating the faster Na ions storage/release process on the surface of <b>aMG</b> electrode than <b>cMG</b> electrode.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0069"><sup>[28]</sup></link> In addition, the evolution of charge transfer kinetics upon the simultaneous defect engineering and coupling with rGO was investigated with EIS analysis. As depicted in Figure <link href="#asia202101013-fig-0005">5</link>, the beneficial effect of simultaneous defect engineering and rGO coupling on the electrocatalyst/electrode performances can be ascribed to the provision of highly active defect sites for H<sup>+</sup> adsorption and Na<sup>+</sup> insertion, the improvement of structural stability due to the amorphization of MoO<sub>2</sub> lattice, and the enhancement of charge transfer caused by the hybridized rGO.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0073"><sup>[30]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202101013-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202101013:asia202101013-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202101013-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic diagram for synergetic advantage of combinative synthetic strategy of defect engineering and graphene hybridization.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In this work, a novel efficient synthetic methodology to economically‐feasible bifunctional electrocatalyst/electrode is developed via the synergistic control of oxygen defects and rGO coupling for transition metal oxides (TMOs). The soft‐chemical redox reaction between K<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub> and KBH<sub>4</sub> in the presence of rGO colloids yields defect‐engineered MoO<sub>2</sub>‐rGO nanohybrid. The simultaneous defect control and hybridization with rGO is quite effective in improving the HER electrocatalyst and SIB electrode functionalities of molybdenum dioxide with the lowering of overpotential and the increase of HER current density and discharge capacity for SIB. The creation of crystal defects provides lots of highly active surface sites for HER process. Additionally, the enhancement of atomic disordering degrades the (Mo−O) orbital overlap in MoO<sub>2</sub> lattice, promoting the formation of double (Mo=O) bond acting as proton adsorption site during the HER process. The present soft‐chemical synthetic strategy with simultaneous defect engineering and rGO coupling can provide an economically‐feasible scalable method to synthesize efficient bifunctional electrocatalyst/electrode materials. Our current project is to explore other type of multifunctional hybrid materials via the simultaneous defect engineering of TMO and nanoscale hybridization with various conductive matrices.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis</title><p>The defect‐induced amorphous MoO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystal (<b>aM</b>) was synthesized by reacting variable amount of 2.5 M KBH<sub>4</sub> aqueous solution (pH=11) with 0.25 M K<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub> aqueous solution (pH=1) at pH=2 (pH control by HCl addition). The colloidal suspension of exfoliated GO nanosheets was synthesized according to the previously reported method.<link href="#asia202101013-bib-0078"><sup>[31]</sup></link> For the synthesis of defect‐induced MoO<sub>2</sub>‐rGO material (<b>aMG</b>), the colloidal suspension of GO was slowly added into 0.25 M K<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub> aqueous solution under mild stirring, which was followed by the addition of 2.5 M KBH<sub>4</sub> aqueous solution. In this step, GO was reduced to rGO. After the reaction for 2 h, the resulting products were separated by centrifugation, washed thoroughly with distilled water, and then dried at 50 °C in ambient atmosphere. For both syntheses, the molar ratio of reductant/K<sub>2</sub>MoO<sub>4</sub> was adjusted to 1. The weight ratio of GO/MoO<sub>2</sub> was fixed to 1/20. The well‐crystalline <b>cM</b> and <b>cMG</b> homologues were obtained by calcination of <b>aM</b> and <b>aMG</b> materials at 400 °C under Ar atmosphere, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization</title><p>Powder XRD analysis was carried out to determine the crystal structures of the present MoO<sub>2</sub>‐based materials using X‐ray diffractometer (Rigaku D/Max‐2000/PC, Ni‐filtered Cu K<sub>α</sub> radiation, λ=1.5418 Å, 25 °C). The crystal shape and/or hybrid structures of the present materials were determined with TEM and SAED analyses (Jeol JEM‐2100F). The crystal morphologies of the present materials were probed with FE‐SEM (Jeol JSM‐6700F microscope). Micro‐Raman spectra of the materials under investigation were measured using a Horiba Jobin Yvon LabRam Aramis with an Ar‐ion laser beam at an exciting radiation wavelength of 514.5 nm to probe their chemical bonding characters. The local atomic arrangement and electronic structure of the present materials were studied with Mo K‐edge XAS at beam line 10C/8C in the Pohang Accelerator Laboratory (PAL, Pohang, Korea). All the XAS measurements were performance at room temperature in a transmission mode using gas‐ionization detectors.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrocatalyst activity test</title><p>The HER electrocatalytic activities of the present materials were measured with a standard three‐electrode electrochemical cell, where a glassy carbon (GC) electrode (3 mm in diameter, ALS Co.), a saturated calomel electrode (SCE), and a Pt wire were utilized as a working electrode, a reference electrode, and a counter electrode, respectively. The potentials were converted to reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) scale. N<sub>2</sub>‐purged 0.5 M H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> solution was employed as an electrolyte. The catalyst ink was obtained by dispersing 6.0 mg of the catalyst, 4.0 mg of carbon black (Vulcan‐XC72R), and 40 μL of 5 wt.% Nafion solution in 5.0 mL of mixed solvent of isopropanol and Milli‐Q water (1/4, vol/vol), which was followed by homogenization with a high‐speed sonication. The 10 μL of ink was dropped onto the GC electrode and then dried at 50 °C in air. The LSV was performed with a RRDE‐3 A (ALS Co.) rotator and the IVIUM analyzer. The EIS experiment was carried out using IVIUM analyzer in the frequency range of 0.1–100000 Hz at −0.25 V (V vs. RHE).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrode activity test</title><p>The present MoO<sub>2</sub>‐based materials were tested as SIB electrodes using the 2032‐type coin cell with the potential range of 0.01–3.0 V vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup> for reversible charging‐discharging process. The sodium metal was used as anode whereas 1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> solution in ethylene carbonate:diethyl carbonate (EC : DEC, 1 : 1 in volume, 95 vol.%)+fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC, 5 vol.%) was employed as electrolyte. The composite cathode material was fabricated with active material (80 wt.%), Super P (15 wt.%), and poly (vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF, 5 wt.%) in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidence (NMP). The composite cathode material was deposited on Cu foil and drying under vacuum at 110 °C for 12 h. All the galvanostatic charge‐discharge tests were performed with WonATech (WBCS3000) multichannel galvanostat/potentiostat. The CV curves of the present materials were carried out using the IVIUM analyzer. The EIS data were collected in the frequency range of 100 kHz–10 mHz at open circuit potential.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia202101013-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Research Foundation of Korea (NRF) grant funded by the Korea government (MSIT) (No. NRF‐2020R1A2C3008671, No.  NRF‐2017R1A5A1015365).
