Their structure and composition allow these carbon‐coated Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> hollow bullets to exhibit enhanced sodium storage performance in terms of excellent rate capability and ultra‐stable cycle life.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201902988:anie201902988-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201902988-sec-0001"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as a promising alternative to lithium‐ion batteries for large‐scale energy‐storage application in view of the low‐cost and abundance of sodium.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0001 #anie201902988-bib-0002 #anie201902988-bib-0003 #anie201902988-bib-0004 #anie201902988-bib-0005 #anie201902988-bib-0006"/> Owing to the large size and sluggish diffusion kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ions, it is a great challenge to explore suitable host materials that can effectively and reversibly accommodate Na<sup>+</sup> ions.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0007 #anie201902988-bib-0008 #anie201902988-bib-0009 #anie201902988-bib-0010 #anie201902988-bib-0011 #anie201902988-bib-0012 #anie201902988-bib-0013 #anie201902988-bib-0014 #anie201902988-bib-0015 #anie201902988-bib-0016"/> Metal sulfides, such as Co<sub><i>x</i></sub>S, Fe<sub><i>x</i></sub>S, Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S, and Sn<sub><i>x</i></sub>S have come to attention in view of their excellent redox reversibility and high capacity for sodium storage.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0017 #anie201902988-bib-0018 #anie201902988-bib-0019 #anie201902988-bib-0020 #anie201902988-bib-0021 #anie201902988-bib-0022 #anie201902988-bib-0023 #anie201902988-bib-0024 #anie201902988-bib-0025"/> However, metal sulfide‐based electrodes commonly suffer from poor stability and poor rate capability resulting from the large volume expansion during sodiation/desodiation and moderate conductivity. To solve this problem, rational structural design and integration with carbonaceous materials are two effective strategies to enhance the electrochemical performance.</p><p>Hollow nanostructured materials with high surface area and well‐defined cavities often show improved electrochemical properties in high‐performance SIBs.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0026 #anie201902988-bib-0027 #anie201902988-bib-0028"/> The hollow interior can effectively accommodate the volume change during sodiation/desodiation, while the thin shell can shorten the diffusion length of electrons and Na<sup>+</sup> ions. For example, we have recently developed an effective templating method to synthesize Cu‐doped CoSe<sub>2</sub> microboxes with enhanced sodium storage performance.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0029"/> Metal‐rich Cu<sub>1.8</sub>S hollow octahedrons have also been synthesized by us for SIBs, however, the rate capability and long cycling performance still need to be improved.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0030"/></p><p>Herein, we demonstrate a template‐based approach to synthesize nitrogen‐doped carbon‐coated Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> (designated as Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC) bullet‐like hollow particles (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0001"/>). Briefly, ZnO bullet‐like solid particles are first prepared, followed by a facile anion exchange process to obtain ZnS hollow bullets. After polydopamine (PDA) coating and subsequent carbonization treatment, nitrogen‐doped carbon‐coated ZnS (designated as ZnS@NC) bullet‐like hollow particles are obtained. The ZnS@NC hollow bullets are further converted to Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow bullets through a subsequent cation exchange process. As expected, the as‐prepared Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow bullets exhibit enhanced electrochemical sodium storage performance with superior rate capability and excellent cycling stability.</p><figure xml:id="anie201902988-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201902988:anie201902988-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201902988-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of the synthesis of bullet‐like Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow particles.</p></caption></figure><p>Bullet‐like ZnO particles synthesized via a facile reflux method are used as the starting material. A panoramic field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) image shows that these ZnO particles are highly uniform (Figure S1a, Supporting Information). The magnified FESEM images reveal that the ZnO particles exhibit an asymmetric bullet‐like shape with an average length of about 1 μm (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0002"/> a and Figure S1b). Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0002"/> d and Figure S1c) demonstrate the solid nature of these bullet‐like particles. The crystallographic structure and phase purity of the particles are examined by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) analysis. All the diffraction peaks can be perfectly assigned to crystalline ZnO (JCPDS card no.36‐1451) (Figure S1d).</p><figure xml:id="anie201902988-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201902988:anie201902988-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201902988-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a)–c) FESEM images and d)–f) TEM images of the a),d) ZnO solid bullets, b),e) ZnS hollow bullets, and c),f) ZnS@NC hollow bullets.</p></caption></figure><p>An anion exchange process is first carried out through the solvothermal reaction of ZnO bullets with thiourea. The XRD pattern of the as‐obtained product can be indexed to the ZnS phase (JCPDS card no.01‐0792; Figure S2a). Energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX) spectrum further confirms the composition of ZnS (Figure S2b). A typical FESEM image (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0002"/> b) shows that the obtained ZnS particles still retain the bullet‐like morphology while the surface becomes very rough. A well‐defined hollow interior can be clearly observed from the TEM image (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0002"/> e). The generation of the hollow structure can be attributed to the diffusion effect.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0031 #anie201902988-bib-0032 #anie201902988-bib-0033 #anie201902988-bib-0034 #anie201902988-bib-0035 #anie201902988-bib-0036"/> Specifically, during the sulfidation process, S<sup>2−</sup> ions first react with Zn<sup>2+</sup> ions to form a thin layer of ZnS, which acts as a physical barrier to hinder the chemical reaction between outside S<sup>2−</sup> ions and inner Zn<sup>2+</sup> ions. Owing to the smaller size of Zn<sup>2+</sup> ions, the outward diffusion of Zn<sup>2+</sup> ions is dominant compared to the inward diffusion of S<sup>2−</sup> ions. As a result, the sulfidation reactions mainly take place on the preformed ZnS shell and the hollow interior is finally formed. After being coated with PDA and carbonized at elevated temperature, the bullet‐like ZnS hollow particles are coated with a carbon layer. As verified by XRD (Figure S3a), this carbon coating step causes no phase change to the ZnS hollow bullets. The EDX spectrum (Figure S3b) reveals the existence of Zn, S, C, and N elements, evidencing the successful coating of nitrogen‐doped carbon layer onto ZnS hollow bullets. FESEM (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0002"/> c) and TEM (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0002"/> f) images reveal that the morphology and hollow structure of the ZnS bullets are well maintained after the carbon coating.</p><p>A subsequent cation exchange process is performed via the reaction of the as‐obtained ZnS@NC hollow bullets with copper cations. As revealed by the XRD analysis (Figure S4a), the diffraction peaks of the as‐derived product can be indexed to the hexagonal Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> (JCPDS card no.47‐1748). EDX analysis (Figure S4b) further confirms the composition of Cu, S, C, and N in the structure with no signal from Zn, indicating the complete transformation of ZnS to Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> via the cation exchange process. The carbon content in the product is estimated to be about 7.2 wt % from the thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S5). The change of valance state of copper may result from the catalytically oxidative leaching of ZnS.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0037 #anie201902988-bib-0038"/> It should be noted that the reaction conditions have great effect on the final phase of the obtained Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S materials (Figure S6 and Table S1). FESEM images (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0003"/> a–c and Figure S7a) show that the bullet‐like structure is well maintained after the cation exchange process. TEM images (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0003"/> d–f) and FESEM image (Figure S7b) reveal that the particles well inherit the hollow structure of the ZnS@NC bullets. From the magnified TEM image of the edge of a single particle (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0003"/> g), a carbon coating layer with a thickness of around 20 nm can be clearly observed. The high‐resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) image (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0003"/> h) shows clear lattice fringes with a spacing of 0.321 nm, which matches well with the (0015) plane of Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>. Elemental mapping images of an individual Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC bullet demonstrate that Cu, S, and C elements are uniformly distributed throughout the whole nanostructure (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0003"/> i). And the elemental composition and chemical bonding information in the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC bullets are well resolved via X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis (Figure S8). For comparison, Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> hollow bullets without carbon coating are also prepared via a similar cation exchange process from the ZnS hollow bullets. The as‐obtained Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> hollow particles show similar morphology (Figure S9), XRD pattern, and EDX spectrum (Figure S10) to those of Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow bullets, but exhibit a rougher surface structure (Figure S11).</p><figure xml:id="anie201902988-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201902988:anie201902988-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201902988-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a)–c) FESEM images, d)–g) TEM images, h) HRTEM image of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow bullets, and i) elemental mapping images of an individual Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow bullet.</p></caption></figure><p>The sodium storage properties of the bullet‐like Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow particles are first studied by cyclic voltammetry (CV). The CV test is carried out at a sweep rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 0.4–2.6 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>. In the first cathodic scan, only one pronounced broad peak centered around 0.78 V can be observed (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0004"/> a), which corresponds to the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> structure with sequential phase transitions.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0040 #anie201902988-bib-0041"/> In the subsequent anodic scan, two peaks at about 1.58 and 2.14 V emerge, corresponding to the deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions from the Na<sub>α</sub>Cu<sub>β</sub>S<sub>γ</sub> structure.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0030 #anie201902988-bib-0040 #anie201902988-bib-0041"/> During the second cycle, the CV curve exhibits two new reduction peaks at about 1.95 and 1.55 V, which may be associated with phase transformation.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0042 #anie201902988-bib-0043"/> The CV curves of the 2nd to 5th cycles are well overlapped, which suggests the excellent electrochemical reversibility of the electrode. Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0004"/> b shows the representative discharge–charge voltage profiles of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode at a current density of 0.3 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode can deliver a reversible charge capacity of about 385 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a high initial Coulombic efficiency of about 94 %. The reversible capacity is higher than that of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> electrode (218 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, Figure S12) and most of the previously reported Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S‐based electrodes.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0041 #anie201902988-bib-0044 #anie201902988-bib-0045 #anie201902988-bib-0046 #anie201902988-bib-0047 #anie201902988-bib-0048"/> The capacity of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode shows some decay in the first few cycles but quickly stabilizes after 10 cycles. Ex situ XRD, HRTEM, and selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) measurements are performed for the electrodes at different states of charge (Figure S13). The electrode exhibits negligible phase change when discharged to 1.4 V. However, some intermediates could be observed from the XRD and SAED patterns when the electrode is discharged to 0.4 V. It is highly possible that the phase transformation is triggered by the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0048 #anie201902988-bib-0049"/> Some weak signals of the Na<sub>2</sub>S phase can also be observed, indicating that the conversion reaction also takes place. And the intermediates disappear when the electrode is charged to 1.6 V. Some signals of Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> are recovered at the fully charged state (2.6 V). Such an observation is consistent with the results of previously reported works,<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0048 #anie201902988-bib-0049"/> indicating that the intercalation reaction is accompanied by conversion reaction during sodium uptake.</p><figure xml:id="anie201902988-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201902988:anie201902988-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201902988-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The 1st and 2nd cyclic voltammograms of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Typical discharge–charge voltage profiles of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode at a current density of 0.3 A g<sup>−1</sup> within the voltage range of 0.4–2.6 V. c) Rate performances of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> and Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrodes. Cyclic performances of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> and Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrodes at the current density of d) 0.3 and e) 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>The rate capability of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode is studied by discharging and charging at various current densities ranging from 0.1 to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0004"/> c). At current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 1, 2 and 3 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the electrode can deliver an average reversible capacity of 350, 308, 300, 287, 276, 266, and 254 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Even at a high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a remarkable reversible capacity of 237 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can still be preserved. When the current density is reduced back to 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a reversible and stable capacity of 282 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be recovered, which suggests the excellent reversibility of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode. The excellent rate capability of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode is also verified by the discharge‐charge voltage profiles at various current densities (Figure S14). In contrast, the specific capacity of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> electrode fades rapidly with the increase of current density. The rate capability of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrodes is also superior to that of the bulk Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> particles (Figures S15 and S16) and most of the Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S‐based electrodes.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0030 #anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0040 #anie201902988-bib-0041 #anie201902988-bib-0042 #anie201902988-bib-0044 #anie201902988-bib-0045 #anie201902988-bib-0046 #anie201902988-bib-0047"/></p><p>Moreover, the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode also exhibits excellent cycling stability. Specifically, the electrode exhibits stable cycling performance over 500 cycles at 0.3 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0004"/> d). At a higher current density of 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, even after 4000 cycles, a capacity retention of 79 % can still be achieved (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0004"/> e), corresponding to an average capacity loss of only 0.0053 % per cycle. Impressively, the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode also exhibits excellent cycling performance at other current densities (Figure S17), showing ultra‐stable cycle life. And the Coulombic efficiencies of the electrode at all current densities remain around 100 % during cycling. For comparison, the capacity of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> electrode drops rapidly with cycling (Figure <link href="#anie201902988-fig-0004"/> d,e). The cycling performance of the bullet‐like Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow particles is also superior to that of other reported Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S‐based electrodes.<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0030 #anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0040 #anie201902988-bib-0041 #anie201902988-bib-0042 #anie201902988-bib-0044 #anie201902988-bib-0046 #anie201902988-bib-0047 #anie201902988-bib-0048 #anie201902988-bib-0050"/> The carbon coating layer helps to maintain the bullet structure of Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> as verified by FESEM observation after long‐term cycling (Figure S18). The effect of cut‐off voltage range on the electrochemical performance of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode has been also investigated. Like other Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S‐based electrodes,<link href="#anie201902988-bib-0030 #anie201902988-bib-0039 #anie201902988-bib-0040"/> the cut‐off voltage range shows a big effect on the cycling performance of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode. The Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC electrode shows poor cycling stability within the cut‐off voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V (Figure S19).</p><p>The above results clearly demonstrate that rational structural and compositional design can effectively improve the sodium storage properties of Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S‐based electrode materials. The enhanced sodium storage properties may be attributed to the unique hollow structure and desired nitrogen‐doped carbon coating. Specifically, the thin shell of the hollow particles could efficiently facilitate the diffusion of electrons and Na<sup>+</sup> ions thus to enhance the electrochemical kinetics. Both the hollow structure and carbon coating could effectively buffer the volume change during repeated insertion and extraction of Na<sup>+</sup> ions. And the nitrogen‐doped carbon coating layer can effectively improve the electronic conductivity and accelerate the ion/charge transfer process of the Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> material as revealed by the electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) (Figure S20 and Table S2). Importantly, the self‐templated synthesis method can be applied to prepare Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> hollow particles with various shapes such as hollow spheres, microboxes and nanotubes (Figure S21). And all the obtained hollow Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> particles exhibit similar XRD patterns (Figure S22) and improved cycling performances (Figure S23).</p><p>In summary, we have developed a template‐engaged strategy for the synthesis of bullet‐like Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub> hollow particles coated with a layer of nitrogen‐doped carbon for efficient sodium storage. Starting from bullet‐like ZnO particles, bullet‐like ZnS hollow particles are first synthesized through the anion exchange reaction with S<sup>2−</sup> ions and further coated with a layer of nitrogen‐doped carbon (ZnS@NC). The as‐obtained ZnS@NC hollow particles are further converted to Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow particles via a cation exchange reaction with copper cations. As a result of their unique structure and composition, the bullet‐like Cu<sub>9</sub>S<sub>5</sub>@NC hollow particles exhibit enhanced sodium storage properties with high reversible capacity (&gt;300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), excellent rate capability (237 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and ultra‐long cyclic life (79 % capacity retention over 4000 cycles).
