However, the low intrinsic conductivity and easy‐agglomerated feature hamper its applications. Here, a charge‐driven interlayer expansion strategy that Co<sup>2+</sup> replaces Mo<sup>4+</sup> in the doping form to endow MoS<sub>2</sub> layers with negative charges, thus inducing electrostatic repulsion, together with the insertion of gaseous groups, to drive interlayer expansion which once breaks the confinement of interlayer van der Waals force, single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is obtained and uniformly dispersed into carbon matrix arising from the transformation of carbonaceous gaseous groups under high vapor pressure, is proposed. Co atom doping helps enhance the intrinsic conductivity of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>. Carbon matrix effectively prevents agglomeration of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>. The doped Co atoms can be fully transformed into ultrasmall Co nanoparticles during conversion reaction, which enables strong spin‐polarized surface capacitance and thus significantly boosts ion transport and storage. Consequently, the prepared material delivers superb Li/Na/K‐ion storage performances, which are best in the reported MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based anodes. The proposed charge‐driven interlayer expansion strategy provides a novel perspective for preparing single‐layered MoS<sub>2,</sub> which shows huge potential for energy storage.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs5361-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> has great potential in energy storage but suffers from low intrinsic conductivity and easy‐agglomerated feature. Here, a charge‐driven interlayer expansion strategy is proposed. The interlayer electrostatic repulsion induced by Co doping breaks the interlayer van der Waals force to fabricate Co‐doped single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>, which uniformly disperses in carbon matrix and shows superior lithium/sodium/potassium‐ion transport and storage capability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs5361:advs5361-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs5361-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs5361-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs) have been extensively studied and partially commercialized.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0001 #advs5361-bib-0002 #advs5361-bib-0003"><sup>1,2,3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As the most popular anode material, graphite has shown huge success for LIBs over the past decade. However, graphite suffers from low specific capacity (372 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and unsatisfactory rate capability mainly caused by anisotropic lithium‐ion storage paths and small interlayer distance (0.334 nm).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Graphite is becoming increasingly difficult to cope with the growing demand for high energy density and fast charging capability in LIBs. Meanwhile, graphite is not suitable as anode material for SIBs owing to the thermodynamic instability of the binary Na‐intercalated graphite compounds,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0005 #advs5361-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and also exhibits low capacity of 279 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and poor rate performance for PIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this context, developing a universal high‐performance anode material for LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs is of practical importance.</p><p>MoS<sub>2</sub> is a potential anode material for LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs because of its high theoretical capacity (670 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and unique layered structure with a relatively large interlayer space of 0.62 nm.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, there are still some obstacles to be addressed in developing high‐performance MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes. The main challenges arise from its unsatisfactory intrinsic electrical conductivity (EC), large ion diffusion barrier, and large volume change upon cycling. These issues result in poor rate capability and cycle stability and thus severely restrict its application in LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0009 #advs5361-bib-0010 #advs5361-bib-0011"><sup>9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Many different strategies including enlarging interlayer distance of MoS<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0012 #advs5361-bib-0013 #advs5361-bib-0014"><sup>12–14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> constructing MoS<sub>2</sub>/graphene interoverlapped structure,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0015 #advs5361-bib-0011"><sup>15,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> doping heteroatom,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0016 #advs5361-bib-0017"><sup>16,17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> fabricating few‐layered or even single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0018 #advs5361-bib-0019"><sup>18,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and compositing with carbon materials<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been implemented to tackle with the above‐mentioned tricky problems. Although few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> have larger interlayer spaces and are cheap in production, the adjacent layers form barriers to ion transport, resulting in much longer and more tortuous ion transport paths. On the contrary, the single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is completely exposed and easily accessible to the ions transported from all directions, providing short and easy ion transport paths.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0021 #advs5361-bib-0022"><sup>21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the two surfaces of the single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> are capable of storing ions, increasing the ion storage capacity. Previous reports also showed that the electrochemical properties of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> are obviously better than few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0011 #advs5361-bib-0023"><sup>11,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> suggesting the great potential of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> on LIB/SIB/PIB applications. Many methods have been reported to prepare the single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>, e.g., one‐pot colloidal wet‐chemical approach,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> mechanical/chemical exfoliation,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0025 #advs5361-bib-0026 #advs5361-bib-0027"><sup>25–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and chemical vapor deposition.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Especially, the methods of using minerals as precursors based on top‐down exfoliation process show great potential in the mass production of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> and other 2D materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0029 #advs5361-bib-0030 #advs5361-bib-0031"><sup>29–31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Notwithstanding the outcomes in preparing single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>, its poor intrinsic EC and serious restacking/agglomeration problems severely inhibit its application in batteries. To circumvent these difficulties, various techniques including solvothermal synthesis,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> hydrothermal method,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and CTAB‐assisted synthesis<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been involved to construct the sandwich structure of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>/graphene, which effectively improves EC of the electrode and avoids agglomeration of the single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> and thereby enhanced electrochemical performances. However, such a sandwich structure reintroduces the interlayer ion diffusion barrier. Although the interlayer space of the adjacent MoS<sub>2</sub> layers in the sandwich structure is enlarged (≈0.96 to ≈1.15 nm) compared to the theoretical value (0.62 nm), the presence of graphene between adjacent MoS<sub>2</sub> layers makes the actual interlayer distance (from MoS<sub>2</sub> to graphene) available for ion transport to be only ≈0.53 nm, which is even smaller than that of bulk MoS<sub>2</sub>. This inevitably sets up barriers for the transport of Li<sup>+</sup> in the interlayer, let alone Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> with a larger radius. Given this situation, further efforts such as electrospinning,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> dual‐template method,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and emulsion‐templated solvothermal method,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0036"><sup>36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been carried out to synthesize composites with uniformly dispersed single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> in carbon matrix. This strategy eliminates the interlayer ion diffusion energy barrier while suppressing the agglomeration of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>. The corresponding electrochemical performances are also greatly improved based on the advantages of uniformly dispersed single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>. Unfortunately, the intrinsic electrical conductivity of MoS<sub>2</sub> is not increased, far from being sufficient to perform fast charge storage, thus causing the insufficient release of the huge potential of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> in the capacity and rate performances. Hence, it is of very importance to develop a novel route to synthesize single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> with enhanced intrinsic electrical conductivity and uniformly dispersed in carbon matrix to achieve superior Li/Na/K‐ion storage performances, which will certainly boost the application pace of MoS<sub>2</sub> materials in LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs.</p><p>Heteroatom doping can enlarge the interlayer distance of layered transition metal sulfides due to the electrostatic repulsion between the adjacent layers caused by atomic substitution during the doping process, showing high potential in MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recently, Li et al. demonstrated the presence of strong spin‐polarized surface capacitance on Co nanoparticles in LIBs system. Specifically, the CoO electrode can generate ultrasmall Co nanoparticles during conversion reactions, and numerous spin‐polarized electrons are injected into Co nanoparticles while lithium ions are stored in Li<sub>2</sub>O matrix, causing a spin capacitance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is reasonable to believe that a strategy that introduces Co atom into MoS<sub>2</sub> lattices in the form of doping cannot only induce the charge‐driven interlayer expansion effect to obtain the single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>, but also bring surface capacitance effect during conversion reactions, thus boosting ion transport and storage.</p><p>Herein, we propose a novel charge‐driven interlayer expansion strategy to prepare single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> with enhanced intrinsic EC in carbon matrix based on Co doping. Co<sup>2+</sup> replaces Mo<sup>4+</sup> of MoS<sub>2</sub> lattice to complete the doping process, which results in MoS<sub>2</sub> layers with negative charges to induce the interlayer electrostatic repulsion, together with the insertion of gaseous groups, to drive interlayer expansion. When the electrostatic repulsion is large enough to break the limitation of interlayer van der Waals forces, the Co‐doped single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is obtained and uniformly dispersed in N, O co‐doped carbon matrix (Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC). Utilizing Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC as anode material for LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs holds the following advantages: 1) the fully exposed single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> basal planes can perform fast charge transport due to the disappearance of interlayer ion transport barrier; 2) Co doping hugely enhances the intrinsic EC of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>, thereby significantly accelerating electrons transfer; 3) Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC has a maximization of contact area between single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> and carbon matrix to fully improve the electrical conductivity of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>, thus accelerating the transfer rate of charges; 4) the doped Co atoms in Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC can transform into ultrasmall Co nanoparticles (≈2 nm) during conversion reaction, which can generate strong spin‐polarized surface capacitance to enhance ion transport and storage. As a result, the Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC delivers the best comprehensive electrochemical performances in terms of capacity, cyclability, and rate performances compared with the recently reported MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based anode materials for LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="advs5361-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="advs5361-sec-0030"><title type="main">Fabrication of Samples</title><p>The samples are prepared by pyrolysis of the precursor solution containing distinct amounts of cobalt naphthenate, (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>MoS<sub>4</sub>, and <i>N</i>, <i>N</i>‐dimethylformamide (DMF) through a facile one‐step pressure‐induced vapor synthetic route (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs5361-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>, detailed experimental content in Supporting Information). The corresponding microstructure characterizations of the prepared samples are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0001">1a</link>, (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>MoS<sub>4</sub> thermally decomposes to form multilayered MoS<sub>2</sub> (MLMoS<sub>2</sub>, ∼10 layers) with a typical interlayer space of 0.62 nm, which is confirmed by high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) observation (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2b,c</link>). After introducing DMF (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0001">1b</link>), vast gaseous groups (OHCN, OHC, ·CH<sub>3</sub>, etc.) caused by the pyrolysis of DMF insert the MoS<sub>2</sub> interlayer, which induces the reduction of MoS<sub>2</sub> layers (3–5 layers, Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2d,e</link>) and the enlargement of interlayer distance (0.96 nm, Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2f</link>). Meanwhile, the pyrolysis products surrounding the few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> can be transformed into N, O co‐doped carbon matrix under large vapor pressure and annealing to obtain FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC sample. Cobalt naphthenate, an organic ionic compound ( [(C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>9</sub>)(CH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>n</sub>COO]<sub>2</sub>Co containing one positively charged Co<sup>2+</sup> and two negatively charged (C<sub>5</sub>H<sub>9</sub>)(CH<sub>2</sub>)<sub>n</sub>COO<sup>−</sup>), is further introduced into the system as the dopant to achieve Co doping for MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0001">1c</link>). It is worth noting that Co<sup>2+</sup> replaces Mo<sup>4+</sup> of the MoS<sub>2</sub> lattice to make MoS<sub>2</sub> layers with negative charges and leave negatively charged gaseous groups (carboxylic anions) in the interlayer, thus inducing interlayer electrostatic repulsion to achieve the charge‐driven interlayer expansion for MoS<sub>2</sub>. As can be seen in Figures <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2g‐i</link>, a further increased interlayer distance of 1.28 nm for few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> can be obtained when introducing a small amount of cobalt naphthenate (named as Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC). Continue to increase the amount of cobalt naphthenate can increase the number of the negative charges to bring stronger interlayer electrostatic repulsion (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0001">1d</link>), which is evidenced by the disappeared few‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> and the formed numerous single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figures <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2j,k</link>), thus obtaining Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC sample. This means that the increased interlayer electrostatic repulsion far surpasses the interlayer van der Waals interaction of MoS<sub>2</sub>. It is clearly indicated that the present material form with the MoS<sub>2</sub> layers embedded in the carbon matrix avoids agglomeration of the MoS<sub>2</sub> layers (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2k</link>). Meanwhile, the single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is extremely small (a length size of ≈6.5 nm) and thin (a thickness size of ≈0.4 nm, in agreement with the standard distance data of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS 37–1492); Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2l</link>). The lateral dimension of ≈6.5 nm enables extremely short lateral transport length of charges and small local electroactive mass. The latter point is crucial for the spin‐polarized surface capacitance (discussed below). Note that the lateral size decreased from ≈12 nm of Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC to ≈6.5 nm of Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC. This may be because 1) the short time for MoS<sub>2</sub> growth is not enough to obtain the large‐sized MoS<sub>2</sub> layers in the pressure‐induced vapor synthetic system, and 2) the repulsion of Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC system is much larger than that of Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC system during the synthesis process (discussed below), which may inhibit the growth of MoS<sub>2</sub>, thus leading to a smaller size of MoS<sub>2</sub> in Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC compared to Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC. In addition, the structure of MoS<sub>2</sub> remains intact after Co doping, demonstrating the successful fabrication of Co‐doped single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> in Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC. The morphology of the synthesized samples is exhibited in Figure <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information), irregular micron‐scale bulks are obtained for (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>MoS<sub>4</sub> and MLMoS<sub>2</sub>. However, FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC, Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC, and Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC display a nano‐sized morphology, which can effectively shorten the ion transport length and relieve the stress caused by the volume expansion of the electrode. As exhibited in the ex situ TEM observation (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0004">4d,e</link>, and Figure <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information), after discharging to 0.01 V, well‐dispersed nanoparticles with an average size of ≈2 nm can be observed. The diffraction rings of (310) and (321) planes of Mo (JCPDS:42‐1120), (102) and (110) planes of Co (JCPDS: 05–0727), and (220), (622), and (640) planes of Li<sub>2</sub>S (JCPDS: 26–1188) displayed in the SAED pattern (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0004">4f</link>) indicates that the nanoparticles produced in conversion reaction contain Mo and Co. Note that Co and Mo nanoparticles produced from Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC can induce the formation of the space charge zone to store extra Li<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037 #advs5361-bib-0052"><sup>37,52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Specifically, the d‐orbit electrons of Co and Mo are not full, spin‐polarized electrons can be injected into the d orbits of the metallic Co and Mo nanoparticles with a depth of the Thomas–Fermi screening length in an electric field. Meanwhile, lithium ions are stored at the surfaces of the Co and Mo nanoparticles to construct the space charge zone, thus leading to the spin‐polarized surface capacitance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> An interesting feature of space‐charge storage is that it possesses the potential to be very fast, which is because ions and electrons in the space charge zone travel along separate pathways rather than in bulk storage where ions and electrons must travel in the same phase.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0052"><sup>52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To evidence the formation of the space charge zone, ex situ XPS spectra of Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode at various discharge/charge states are performed and exhibited in Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0004">4g,h</link>. In the pristine state, the binding energy of Mo 3d and Co 2p contains 229.0 eV (Mo 3d<sub>5/2</sub>), 232.0 eV (Mo 3d<sub>3/2</sub>), 778.7 eV (Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub>), and 793.7 eV (Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub>). When the electrode is fully discharged to 0.01 V, these characteristic peaks shift obviously to lower binding energy (228.3 eV, Mo<sup>0</sup> 3d<sub>5/2</sub>; 231.3 eV, Mo<sup>0</sup> 3d<sub>3/2</sub>; 777.8 eV, Co<sup>0</sup> 2p<sub>3/2</sub>; 793.3 eV, Co<sup>0</sup> 2p<sub>1/2</sub>), confirming the production of metallic Mo and Co due to the conversion reaction.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037 #advs5361-bib-0053 #advs5361-bib-0054"><sup>37,53,54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the fully charged state, these peaks show a further negative shift (227.9 eV, Mo<sup>0</sup> 3d<sub>5/2</sub>; 230.8 eV, Mo<sup>0</sup> 3d<sub>3/2</sub>; 777.0 eV, Co<sup>0</sup> 2p<sub>3/2</sub>; and 792.0 eV, Co<sup>0</sup> 2p<sub>1/2</sub>). This may be because numerous positively charged lithium ions stored on the surface of Mo and Co nanoparticles increase the binding energy. Therefore, the binding energy shows negative shift after the delithiation from the Mo and Co particle surface. These results demonstrate the formation of the space charge zone.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Because the magnetism is sensitive to the change in the phase, structure, and electronic state of transition metal‐based materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0055"><sup>55</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the Li‐ion storage behavior of the Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode is further studied by magnetic characterization. As shown in the ex situ magnetic hysteresis loops in Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0004">4i</link>, a pronounced increase in magnetization for Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC can be detected from ≈0 emu g<sup>−1</sup> (before cycling) to 12.6 emu g<sup>−1</sup> (after discharging to 0.01 V), signaling the formation of Mo and Co nanoparticles due to the reduction of Mo<sup>4+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After charging to 3 V, the magnetization presents a further increase to 17.7 emu g<sup>−1</sup>. Specifically, many extra electrons stored in Mo and Co nanoparticles partially cancel out the spin majority bands of the 4d (Mo) and 3d (Co) energy levels, reducing the magnetization. Therefore, the magnetization shows a further increase after the delithiation from the surface of Mo and Co nanoparticles, which further confirms the formation of the space charge zone.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To reveal the main responsible for the strong surface capacitance effect of the Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode, MLMoS<sub>2</sub>, FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC, and Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrodes are also investigated by ex situ TEM and XPS spectra. After the full discharge, the size of Mo nanoparticles generated from MLMoS<sub>2</sub> is ≈10 nm (Figure <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S20</link>, Supporting Information), while that from FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC is ≈3.0 nm (Figure <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S21</link>, Supporting Information), suggesting the nanoscale few‐layered structure of MoS<sub>2</sub> and the presence of carbon matrix effectively inhibit the growth and aggregation of Mo nanoparticles during the conversion reaction. The smaller Mo nanoparticle with larger specific surface area can provide more interfaces for extra Li<sup>+</sup> storage and result in a stronger surface‐capacitance effect,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0056"><sup>56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which is confirmed by XPS spectra and magnetic hysteresis loops (detailed discussion in Figures <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S22</link> and <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">23</link>, Supporting Information). After introducing a small amount of Co doping, Co and Mo nanoparticles generated from Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC show an average size of ≈3.0 nm (Figure <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S24</link>, Supporting Information), which is similar to the size of Mo nanoparticles produced from FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC (Figure <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S21</link>, Supporting Information). However, the magnetization of Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC is much higher than that of FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC after discharge (0.56 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC; 6.6 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC) and charge process (1.31 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC; 11.1 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC), as shown in Figures <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S23</link> and <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S25</link> (Supporting Information). Theoretically, higher electrode magnetization enables more spin‐polarized electrons to inject into d orbits of transition metal,resulting in a larger space charge zone, thereby enhancing the spin‐polarized surface capacitance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5361-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This means that the strong spin‐polarized surface capacitance is mainly caused by the small amounts of Co nanoparticles. Notably, compared to Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode, Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode with a higher Co doping amount (Table <link href="#advs5361-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information) produces more Co nanoparticles with a smaller average size of ≈2 nm after fully discharging (Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0004">4e</link>). The smaller nanoparticles generated from Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode are due largely to the smaller MoS<sub>2</sub> microcell (lateral dimension×thickness: ≈6.5 × 0.4 nm, in Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC, Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2l</link>; ≈12×5 nm, in Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC, Figure <link href="#advs5361-fig-0002">2h</link>) and the confinement of carbon matrix. With the synergistic effect of the smaller size and larger amount of Co nanoparticles, the Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode exhibits higher magnetization than the Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode after discharge (6.6 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC; 12.6 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC) and charge process (11.1 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, Co‐FLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC; 17.7 emu g<sup>−1</sup>, Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC), thereby causing stronger spin‐polarized surface capacitance.</p></section><section xml:id="advs5361-sec-0060"><title type="main">Practical Exploration of Co‐SLMoS2/NOC in LIBs</title><p>We further explore the potential of Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC for practical applications in view of its superior electrochemical performances and advanced Li‐ion storage mechanism. Co‐doped single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> with fully exposed MoS<sub>2</sub> basal planes, increased intrinsic EC, low lithium ion diffusion energy barrier, and vanishing interlayer ion diffusion barrier hugely boosts ion transport and storage capability. The Co‐doped single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> uniformly dispersed into N, O co‐doped carbon matrix is highly stable during cycling, which shows an ultralow volume expansion of 10.3% after 100 cycles. The doped magnetic Co atom in single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> lattices can transform into ultrasmall Co nanoparticles (≈2 nm) during conversion reaction, which induces strong spin‐polarized surface capacitance to further enhance ion transport and storage. As a consequence, the Co‐SLMoS<sub>2</sub>/NOC electrode delivers superior electrochemical performances than all the previous reports on MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based anode for Li/Na/K‐ion batteries.
