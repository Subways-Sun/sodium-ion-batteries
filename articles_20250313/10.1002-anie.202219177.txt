However, enormous volume expansion and agglomeration of nano Sn lead to low Coulombic efficiency and poor cycling stability. Herein, an intermetallic FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer is designed via thermal reduction of polymer‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> coated hollow SnO<sub>2</sub> spheres to construct a yolk‐shell structured Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C. The FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer can relieve internal stress, avoid the agglomeration of Sn to accelerate the Na<sup>+</sup> transport, and enable fast electronic conduction, which endows quick electrochemical dynamics and long‐term stability. As a result, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode exhibits high initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE=93.8 %) and a high reversible capacity of 409 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles, corresponding to an 80 % capacity retention. In addition, NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C sodium‐ion full cell shows outstanding cycle stability (capacity retaining rate of 89.7 % after 200 cycles at 1 C).</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the gradual maturation of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) technology in recent years, the production and sales of new energy vehicles have increased, leading to a scarcity of lithium reserves and a rise in lithium metal prices.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Due to the key advantages of abundant sodium resources and low cost, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have great potential for new energy vehicles and large‐scale energy storage applications.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link></p><p>Sn with a high capacity (Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>: 847 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and low potential (≈0.2 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) has been a promising alternative to commercial hard carbon as anode material.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0012"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Nevertheless, during the alloying of Na with Sn, the intermediate phase a‐NaSn and the final phase Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> undergo a volume expansion of about 220 % and 520 %,<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0016"><sup>[4]</sup></link> respectively. As a result, huge stresses are generated within the Sn anode material, causing the material to crack (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0001">1a</link>), which leads to poor cycling performance and exposes new interfaces that react with the electrolyte to generate solid electrolyte interfaces (SEI). Such frequent creation of SEI will consume a large amount of electrolyte, resulting in a malfunction of the battery.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of the insertion/extraction of Na<sup>+</sup> into (a) yolk‐shell Sn@C anode and (b) Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode.</p></caption></figure><p>Besides, the Sn anode materials undergo different phase transformations during the sodiation process, which dramatically changes internal resistance, resulting in low energy efficiency. Recently, Lee et al.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0018"><sup>[4b]</sup></link> found that the resistance of the sodiation Sn anode remarkably increased and was eight orders of magnitude higher than that of the fresh Sn anode. This high resistivity was attributed to the formation of covalently bonded Sn clusters, the so‐called Zintl ions, which are tetrahedral structures consisting of four Sn atoms. Nanosized Sn anode materials can mitigate the considerable volume change and shorten the diffusion distance to improve the cycling stability and rate capability.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0021"><sup>[5]</sup></link> However, nanomaterials with a large specific surface area tend to generate more SEI during the initial charge and discharge, which consumes more Na<sup>+</sup> in the cathode materials and electrolyte, leading to a low initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE).<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0025"><sup>[6]</sup></link> As till now the reported Sn‐based anodes have exhibited an ICE of less than 80 %, so it is still a challenge to build anode materials with high ICE(&gt;90 %), superior rate capability, and long‐term cycling stability.</p><p>Herein, the yolk‐shell structure Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode material was successfully constructed by the thermal reduction of polymer‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>‐coated hollow SnO<sub>2</sub> spheres. The intermetallic FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer coated on the yolk core Sn was converted into Fe nanoparticles during the sodiation process, which can alleviate the huge volume change and prevent the coarsening of Sn particles to reduce the diffusion distance of Na<sup>+</sup> and improve the sodiation rate (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0001">1b</link>). Moreover, The FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer can afford more abundant electron conduction channels to enhance high electronic conductivity. Consequently, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode showed a charge/discharge capacity of 709.5/756.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, exhibiting a high 93.8 % of ICE and reversible capacity of 409 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with 80 % capacity retention after 1500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even when the current density was increased to as high as 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode still had a reversible capacity of 260 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, the assembly of Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode into a sodium ion full cell (NVP // Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell), which exhibited good cycling performance (a capacity retention rate of 89.7 % after 200 cycles at 1 C) and excellent rate performance (55.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 C).</p></section><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0002">2</link> displays the schematic presentation of the preparation of yolk‐shell structured Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C materials. Firstly, hollow SnO<sub>2</sub> nanospheres (SEM and TEM images in Figure S1) were synthesized by the solvothermal method.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0026"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Secondly, the hollow SnO<sub>2</sub> nanospheres were dispersed in a FeCl<sub>3</sub> solution and covered with a layer of amorphous Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to obtain the SnO<sub>2</sub>@Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> material.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0027"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Figure S2 shows the SEM and EDS‐mapping images of the SnO<sub>2</sub>@Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> material, the diameter of the spheres is about 300 nm (Figure S2a, b). and a uniform distribution of Sn, Fe, and O elements in the SnO<sub>2</sub>@Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> material is revealed by EDX‐mapping images. The diffraction peaks of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> were not found in the XRD spectra from the SnO<sub>2</sub> and SnO<sub>2</sub>@Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> materials (Figure S3), and only the SnO<sub>2</sub> peaks were observed, indicating the amorphous coating layer of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Then, the SnO<sub>2</sub>@Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>@RF precursor (TEM images in Figure S4) was developed on the surface of the SnO<sub>2</sub>@Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> material by the polymerization reaction of resorcinol and formaldehyde.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0028"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Finally, the precursors were reduced under H<sub>2</sub>/Ar atmosphere to obtain yolk‐shell structured Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C materials.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The synthetic procedure of Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material.</p></caption></figure><p>From the SEM (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0003">3a</link>) and TEM (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0003">3b</link>) images of the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material, it can be seen that the material has an obvious yolk‐shell structure with a diameter of about 350 nm. Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0003">3c</link> shows that the nanoparticle size inside the yolk‐shell structure is about 150 nm, and the thickness of the outer amorphous carbon layer is about 30 nm. The Sn@C material without Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> also has the same yolk‐shell structure in Figure S5. From the high‐resolution TEM figure (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0003">3d</link>), it is evident that the Sn (200) and FeSn<sub>2</sub> (211) crystal plane spacing is about 0.29 and 0.26 nm, respectively.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0029"><sup>[10]</sup></link> The Sn (200), Sn (211), and FeSn<sub>2</sub> (213) plane diffractions can also be seen from the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0003">3e</link>. The elemental mapping images (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0003">3f</link>) reveal the inner nuclei consisting of the orange Fe and red Sn elements and the outside green C shell. From Figure S6, EDS line scanning of the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C sample further explains the uniform coating of FeSn<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) SEM image, (b)–(c) low‐resolution TEM images, (d) high‐resolution TEM image, (e) SAED pattern, and (f) TEM image and elemental mapping (Sn: red, Fe: orange, C: green) of Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material.</p></caption></figure><p>It is obvious from the XRD patterns(Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0004">4a</link>) that the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material has distinct diffraction peaks at 2<i>θ</i>=30.4°, 31.8°, 43.8°, and 44.8°, corresponding to the (200), (101), (220), and (211) crystal planes of Sn, respectively, consistent with Sn (JCPDS Card No. 86‐2264). In addition, we found two minor diffraction peaks at 2<i>θ</i>=33.6° and 34.9°, responding to the (002) and (211) crystal planes of FeSn<sub>2</sub> in agreement with FeSn<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS Card No. 65‐2699).<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0032"><sup>[11]</sup></link> However, the FeSn<sub>2</sub> phase was not found in the XRD patterns of Sn@C in Figure S7. The diffraction peaks of carbon were not detected, mainly because an amorphous carbon layer was formed after carbonization. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0004">4b</link>, two distinct vibrational peaks are located at 1332.8 and 1595.3 cm<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to the D and G peaks of the carbon materials, respectively.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0034 #anie202219177-bib-0035"><sup>[11b, 12]</sup></link> The intensity ratios (I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub>) of the D peak/G peak in the Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C samples are 0.97 and 0.89, indicating a higher quality carbon layer in Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material due to the high‐temperature catalytic effect of Fe. Also, the TGA test found that the carbon content in the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material is about 18.2 % in Figure S8. From the XPS full‐spectrum (Figure S9), Sn, Fe, and C signal peaks can be seen in the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material and Sn 3p<sub>1/2</sub> at 757.9 eV and Sn 3p<sub>3/2</sub> at 715.8 eV can be found. Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0004">4c</link> shows the Sn 3d fine energy spectrum of the element Sn. The two distinct signal peaks of Sn 3d<sub>5/2</sub> at 486.3 eV and Sn 3d<sub>3/2</sub> at 494.7 eV are related to the Sn−O bonding in FeSnO<sub>x</sub> species in the outer shell of FeSn<sub>2</sub> because Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C is long‐term exposure to air to be oxidized.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0036"><sup>[13]</sup></link> Also, the weak XPS signal with binding energies at 492.6 and 484.4 eV was detected, suggesting the Sn (0) species in FeSn<sub>2</sub>. In addition, Fe 2p fine energy spectra reveal signal peaks at 720.2 and 711.8 eV for Fe 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, corresponding to FeO<sub>x</sub>.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0037"><sup>[14]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) XRD patterns, (b) Raman spectrum, and narrow XPS spectrum of elements (c) Sn 3d, (d) Fe 2p of the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C material.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0005">5a</link> presents the first charge–discharge curves of Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The specific capacities of the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode for the first charge and discharge are 756.6 and 709.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a high initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) of 93.8 %. Whereas, the Sn@C anode ICE is only 78.9 %. This is mainly because the intermetallic FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer lengthens the desodiation platform at 0.26 V to enhance the reversibility. The 0.26 V corresponds to the desodiation potential of the Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>. Here, the FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer has open channels that are parallel to the direction of the (001) crystal plane to promote the alloying reaction between Na<sup>+</sup> and Sn, resulting in the formation of more Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> to obtain high reversibility at 0.26 V. The images of FeSn<sub>2</sub> crystal structure at different directions are given in Figure S10–S14. In addition, the Fe nanoparticles derived from the FeSn<sub>2</sub> layer provide more electron transport channels to improve electrical conductivity during the cycle.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The initial charge–discharge curves at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and (b) the first CV curves at 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> of the Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes, (c) comparison of ICE of Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode with that of reported Sn‐based anodes, (d) rate performance, and (e) long cycling life at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0005">5b</link>, in the first negative scan, we found that Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes have a very weak reduction peak at about 0.4 V, corresponding to the formation of the SEI.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0038"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Meanwhile, a substantial reduction peak at about 0 V was detected, corresponding to the formation of Na<sub>x</sub>Sn alloys. During the first positive scan, the Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes have three distinct oxidation peaks at 0.25 V, 0.56 V, and 0.68 V, respectively, corresponding to the desodiation potentials of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, NaSn, and NaSn<sub>5</sub>.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0039"><sup>[16]</sup></link> It is worth noting that the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode has the strongest oxidation peak at 0.33 V, which can be ascribed to Na<sub>9</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0014 #anie202219177-bib-0040"><sup>[3b, 17]</sup></link> These results further confirm that the construction of the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> heterointerface can improve the reversibility of the Sn anode to obtain high Coulombic efficiency. In addition, it is evident from Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0005">5c</link> that the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode has the highest ICE=93.8 % compared to the ICE of 45–78 % reported in the literature of Sn‐based anode materials.<sup>[ 5a, 5c, 18,19c]</sup></p><p>Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode exhibited reversible specific capacity of 741.7, 491.8, 462.8, 431.2, 390.9, 373.6, 330.6, and 299.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 0.1, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 15 and 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Even back to 0.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode still has a reversible capacity of 480.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating that the electrode has good rate performance. In addition, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode showed the best rate performance than reported Sn‐based anodes<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0022 #anie202219177-bib-0043 #anie202219177-bib-0047"><sup>[5a, 18b, 19]</sup></link> in Figure S15.</p><p>Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0005">5e</link> shows the long‐cycle stability performance of Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Firstly, the Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes were cycled at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for several cycles to activate the electrodes. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0005">5e</link>, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode delivered a capacity as high as 409.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and a capacity retention rate of 80 % after 1500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating excellent cycling stability. Also, the shape of the charge/discharge curves for the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode is similar for the 100, 300, 600, 1200, and 1500 cycles, indicating good reversibility in Figure S16. In contrast, the reversible capacity of the Sn@C anode is only 130.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>after 500 cycles and a capacity retention rate of 23 %, showing rapid capacity decay.</p><p>To investigate the change of internal resistance during cycling, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed on Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes with fresh, 50<sup>th</sup>, and 100<sup>th</sup> cycles, respectively. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0006">6a</link>, in the high‐frequency region, the series resistance (R<sub>s</sub>) of all anodes was similar and rather small, indicating a well electrical contact of the whole‐cell system. In the medium frequency region, the charge transfer resistance values (R<sub>ct</sub>) of Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode with fresh, 50<sup>th</sup>, and 100<sup>th</sup> cycles were 20.3, 27.7, and 30.2 Ω, respectively, indicating small internal resistance changes. However, the Sn@C anode exhibited a huge change in internal resistance from R<sub>ct</sub> of 40.5 Ω to 141.6 Ω after 100 cycles. Besides, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was performed to evaluate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients (D<sub>Na+</sub>) for the Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes during the charging and discharging process in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0006">6b</link>.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0045"><sup>[18d]</sup></link> The average D<sub>Na+</sub> of the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode can be calculated as 5.84×10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, which is 18 times higher than that of the Sn@C anode (D<sub>Na+</sub>=3.16×10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>). These results indicate that the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> metal heterogeneous interface dramatically improves the charge transfer to increase the electrical conductivity and enhances Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion to boost mass transfer during cycling.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Nyquist plots, GITT curves and the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient of Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anodes, morphological changes of the fresh and after 100 cycles for the Sn@C (c–d) and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C (e–f).</p></caption></figure><p>In all, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> heterointerface showed the capability of resisting tensile deformation and suppressing the aggregation of Sn nanoparticles during the cycle to obtain highly efficient and stable sodium storage performance for the following reasons: (1) Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> heterointerface enhancing the interfacial bonding strength and weakening the interfacial Na<sup>+</sup> precipitation by reducing the charge accumulation.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0048"><sup>[19a]</sup></link> (2) Nano‐Fe particles derived from the FeSn<sub>2</sub> coating layer effectively prevent coarsening of Sn, decreasing the diffusion distance of Na<sup>+</sup> and electrons, which is in favor of high electron conductivity, fast Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics, and the utilization ratio of sodium.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0043"><sup>[18b]</sup></link> (3) Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> heterointerface has higher surface energy for more accessible contact with the electrolyte, increasing penetration and improving reaction kinetics.</p><p>The morphologies of the Sn@C and Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode materials after 100 cycles were examined to reveal the reasons for electrode failure. Compared to the SEM image of the Sn@C material without cycling (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0006">6c</link>), the yolk‐shell structure after 100 cycles can be seen as severely damaged because the internal Sn core undergoes massive volume changes, resulting in the rupture of the outer carbon layer to leave open hollow carbon spheres (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0006">6d</link>). These phenomena further explain the poor cycling performance of Sn@C electrodes. In contrast, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode material did not show any pulverization phenomenon and still maintained a perfect yolk‐shell structure (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0006">6e</link>, <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0006">f</link>).</p><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0007">7a</link>, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode and the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode were assembled into a Na‐ion full cell (NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell). The SEM image and XRD spectrum revealed that the NVP cathode material had a porous structure and high crystallinity in Figure S17. Also, the NVP cathode exhibited high ICE=97.6 % and a reversible capacity of 91.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at 1 C=110 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S18). From the initial charging and discharging curves (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0007">7b</link>) of the NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell at 1 C, it can be observed that there is a long charging plateau at 3.3 V during the charging process, which corresponds to the desodiation process of NVP. Meanwhile, there are two distinct discharge plateaus at 3.2 V and 3.0 V during the discharge process, corresponding to the desodiation of the Na<sub>x</sub>Sn alloy.<link href="#anie202219177-bib-0051"><sup>[20]</sup></link> Thus, the full cell's charge/discharge specific capacities were observed as 105.7/83.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C. The NVP // Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell can provide reversible capacities of 101.9, 87.4, 83.2, 71.8, and 55.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 C in Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0007">7c</link>, respectively. Figure S19 shows the voltage‐capacity profiles of NVP // Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell at various current densities. when the current density increases to 10 C, the internal resistance of the full cell increases and exhibits large polarization probably due to the poor conductivity of NVP cathode material. Moreover, the full cell has a high discharge capacity of 75.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at 1 C and a capacity retention of 86.8 % (Figure <link href="#anie202219177-fig-0007">7d</link>). These electrochemical measurements demonstrate that the NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell exhibits high capacity, superior rate performance, and promising stability.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219177-fig-0007"><label>7</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219177:anie202219177-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219177-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Schematic diagram of NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C sodium‐ion full battery, (b) the initial discharge/charge curves at 1 C, (c) rate performance, and (d) long‐cycle stability at 1 C for NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C full cell.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, utilizing hollow SnO<sub>2</sub> nanospheres as raw material, we successfully prepared yolk‐shell structure Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode material with the heterointerface of metal phase Sn and the FeSn<sub>2</sub> through thermal reduction. The Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> heterointerface can effectively relieve the volume expansion and prevent Sn particles from agglomeration. Intermetallic FeSn<sub>2</sub> can be converted into highly conductive and uniformly dispersed Fe nanoparticles during the sodiation process, providing a fast electron transport channel. Therefore, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode exhibits a high charge–discharge capacity of 709.5/756.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (ICE=93.8 %) at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 409 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention rate of 80 % after 1500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even if the current density increases up to 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub>@C anode still has a capacity of 260 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, the NVP//Sn/FeSn<sub>2</sub> full cell still has a reversible capacity of 75.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (a capacity retention rate of 89.7 %) after 200 cycles at 1 C.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>This is work was supported by the Science and Technology Commission of Shanghai Municipality (20520710400), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (22171180), and the Shanghai Municipal Science and Technology Major Project.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0005" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0006" type="openResearch"><section xml:id="anie202219177-sec-0000" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie202219177-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="anie202219177-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="anie202219177-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie202219177-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>L.
