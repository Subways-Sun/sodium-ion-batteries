Inspired by the disordered Rubik's cube, here, we report that the high‐entropy (HE) concept can lead to a very substantial improvement in the sodium storage properties of hexacyanoferrate (HCF). Inspired by the disordered Rubik's cube, herein, Peng et al. report that the high‐entropy concept can lead to a very substantial improvement in the sodium storage properties of hexacyanoferrate. which has achieved impressive cycling stability over 50 000 cycles<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202215865:anie202215865-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202215865-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Advanced lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have led to the commercialization of portable electronics and electric vehicles in the past several decades, but the application of LIBs in grid‐scale energy storage systems (GESS) has been severely hindered by the rising cost and the uneven distribution of lithium resources.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Compared with the well‐known LIBs, the environmentally sustainable, low‐cost and long‐lifespan sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have recently received significant attention for GESS due to the rapid development of sustainable energy resources (e.g., wind, solar, tidal, and geothermal energy).<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> The major challenge of SIBs is the lack of advanced cathode materials with fast charge/discharge capability and long lifespans. During the past decade, four main kinds of materials have been widely studied as cathode materials for SIBs, including transition metal hexacyanoferrate (HCF),<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0011"><sup>[3]</sup></link> oxides,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0014"><sup>[4]</sup></link> polyanionic compounds,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0015"><sup>[5]</sup></link> and organic compounds.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0016"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Among these HCF is regarded as one of the most promising cathodes for commercial SIBs owing to its low‐cost and three‐dimension open framework for fast Na<sup>+</sup> transportation.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0019"><sup>[7]</sup></link> The general formula for a transition metal HCF could be represented as A<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub><i>M<sub>1</sub></i>[<i>M<sub>2</sub></i>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>1−<i>y□y▪</i></sub>⋅<i>n</i> H<sub>2</sub>O, where A is usually alkali ions, <i>M<sub>1</sub></i>/<i>M<sub>2</sub></i> are transition metals (with <i>M<sub>2</sub></i> typically Fe), and <sub><i>□</i></sub> denotes the [<i>M<sub>2</sub></i>(CN)<sub>6</sub>] vacancies (0&lt;<i>x</i>&lt;2, 0&lt;<i>y</i>&lt;1).<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0023"><sup>[8]</sup></link> The crystal structure of HCF consists of a double perovskite framework with <i>M<sub>1</sub></i>N<sub>6</sub> and <i>M<sub>2</sub></i>C<sub>6</sub> octahedra bonded by C≡N, while A ions and H<sub>2</sub>O occupy the interstitial sites.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0027"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Typically, the HCFs could be divided into the one Na<sup>+</sup> storage type (OS‐HCF) and the two Na<sup>+</sup> storage type (TS‐HCF) according to the amount of the Na<sup>+</sup> storage during electrochemical reactions (Li<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>/Mg<sup>2+</sup>/Al<sup>3+</sup>, etc. could also be stored).<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0030"><sup>[10]</sup></link> OS‐HCF usually suffers from low specific capacity due to its lower electron transfer number, while TS‐HCF shows a short lifespan and poor rate performance due to the large structural distortion when more Na<sup>+</sup> is inserted.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0033"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Tremendous efforts have been devoted to the improvement of the electrochemical performance of HCF by the following four routes: crystal structure control,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0037"><sup>[12]</sup></link> morphology modification,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0040"><sup>[13]</sup></link> surface modification,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0041"><sup>[14]</sup></link> and fabrication of a superstructure.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0042"><sup>[15]</sup></link> The long‐term cycling stability and rate performance of HCFs are still far from satisfactory for GESS due to their intrinsic poor electronic conductivity and large structural distortion.</p><p>As an emerging concept in the field of functional materials, high‐entropy (HE) oxides/alloys combine multiple elements (the compounds with more than 5 elements are usually defined as HE compounds) into a single phase and have been widely applied in various fields, including catalysis,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0043"><sup>[16]</sup></link> medicines,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0046"><sup>[17]</sup></link> thermoelectricity,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0047"><sup>[18]</sup></link> and energy storage,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0048"><sup>[19]</sup></link> with unique properties that cannot be achieved by conventional materials with only one or two dominant elements. Inspired by the Rubik's cubic and booming research activities in the fields of HE oxides and metal alloys, herein, we applied five mostly used transition metals as raw materials to prepare HE‐HCF as a “proof‐of‐concept” to demonstrate the application of high‐entropy metal organic frameworks (MOFs) into energy storage devices. The results reveal that the HE‐HCF is indeed truly entropy stabilized, leading to an unprecedented cycling lifespan with near 80 % capacity retention after 50 000 cycles, fast‐charging capability at 75 C, and all‐climate properties when working under a wide temperature range of −10 to 60 °C. We investigate both the origin of the structural variations induced by the entropy increase and their relationship to electrochemical properties using the neutron powder diffraction (NPD) and the extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) techniques. The sodium‐ion storage mechanism was further investigated based on in situ synchrotron powder diffraction (PD), in situ X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), and in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM), together with density functional theory (DFT) calculations. These results pave the way for the development of high‐entropy metal–organic frameworks as long‐lifespan and all‐climate SIBs for GESS application.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Conventionally, Prussian blue analogues (PBAs) are formulated by only one or two kinds of transition metal with limited surface chemistries and crystal structures. Taking the crystal structure suitability into consideration, five most commonly used transition metals were used as raw materials to prepare HE‐HCF as a “proof‐of‐concept” to demonstrate the application of HE MOFs herein. The NPD pattern of the HE‐HCF was first indexed and then refined by the Rietveld method (weighted profile R factor, <i>R</i><sub>wp</sub>=3.58 % and profile R factor, <i>R</i><sub>p</sub>=2.50 %). The results show that the HE‐HCF exhibits a typical cubic crystal structure (space group Fm‐3m, lattice parameter, <i>a</i>=<i>b</i>=<i>c</i>=10.26000 Å) with no observable impurity as shown in supplementary Table 1. As depicted in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0001">1a</link>, the Mn/Fe/Co/Ni/Cu−N−C‐Fe chains form the cubic structure of HE‐HCF along the cube edges. Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out from room temperature to 400 °C, and it showed that the weight losses of the HE‐HCF at 170 and 270 °C were 4.4 % and 13.4 % (Supporting Information Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0001">1a</link>), respectively. The weight loss could be attributed to the adsorbed water on the surfaces of the nanoparticles, and the interstitial and coordinated water inside the vacancies of the crystal structure.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0052"><sup>[20]</sup></link> The chemical formulas of all the samples are listed in Supporting Information Table 2, e.g., Na<sub>1.41</sub>Mn<sub>0.32</sub>Fe<sub>0.11</sub>Co<sub>0.28</sub>Ni<sub>0.32</sub>Cu<sub>0.32</sub>[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]⋅2.89 H<sub>2</sub>O of HE‐HCF according to the inductively coupled plasma—optical emission spectroscopy (ICP‐OES) results and TGA. According to the equation shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0001">1a</link>, the configurational entropy of the HE‐HCF is 1.6 times higher than that of the typical TS‐HCF. The vacancy content in HE‐HCF is at the average level for other kinds of PBAs (≈26 %) due to the rapid co‐precipitation reaction process, while the vacancies could be suppressed through increasing the ratio of chelating reagent to transition metal salts or increasing the reaction temperature to enhance the crystallinity of the HE‐HCF.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0012 #anie202215865-bib-0055"><sup>[3a, 21]</sup></link> The structure was also characterized by Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) and Raman spectroscopy. The strong Raman peaks at 2101.9 and 2141.5 cm<sup>−1</sup> together with the broadband at 2082.9 cm<sup>−1</sup> in the FTIR spectra can be attributed to the C≡N groups bonding with the transition metals.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0034 #anie202215865-bib-0058"><sup>[11a, 22]</sup></link> The FTIR absorption peaks located at 1619.7 and 3422.6 cm<sup>−1</sup> were due to the H−O−H and H−O bending modes of the water molecules.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0059"><sup>[23]</sup></link> The morphologies of the HE‐HCF are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0001">1c</link> and Supporting Information Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0002">2</link>, which are 3D stacking morphology with a narrow particle size distribution of ≈15 nm and a smooth surface. The specific surface area of the HE‐HCF is 103.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> according to the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area analysis, which is higher than that of the low‐entropy PBAs (Fe‐HCF of 67.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) and medium‐entropy PBAs (FeMnCo‐HCF of 65.8 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) synthesized under same conditions. The uniform particle size distribution is ideal for the slurry coating process and could alleviate the side reactions between the electrolyte and electrode with benefits for their practical application.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0060"><sup>[24]</sup></link> The existence and uniform distribution of the polymetallic elements of Mn, Fe, Co, Ni, and Cu, as well as C, N, and Na elements in HE‐HCF were confirmed by the X‐ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) spectra (Supporting Information Figure 4 and 11) and the energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mapping (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0001">1e</link>), further suggesting the crystal integrity and high purity of HE‐HCF. The EDS mapping images of other as‐prepared samples are shown in Supporting Information Figures 12–15, all other elements of the samples are evenly distributed in the particles and the Cu appears to form the cluster, which indicates that the co‐precipitation method is universally applicable to prepare PBAs containing both single and multiple transition metals.</p><figure xml:id="anie202215865-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202215865:anie202215865-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202215865-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structure characterization of HE‐HCF. a) Schematic comparison of the crystal structure of the typical dual‐metal PBAs versus HE‐HCF and the corresponding ball‐and‐stick model of HE‐HCF. b) Neutron powder diffraction Rietveld refinement pattern of the HE‐HCF sample. c) and d) High‐resolution scanning TEM (STEM) image and inverse fast Fourier transform analysis. e) STEM image and the corresponding elemental maps with scale bar representing 25 nm. f) Line scan elemental analysis of HE‐HCF.</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="anie202215865-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202215865:anie202215865-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202215865-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performance of HE‐HCF. a) Charge and discharge curves of the HE‐HCF at the current density of 15 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Inset: cyclic voltammetry curves at the scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Rate performance of the HE‐HCF at various current rates. c) Cycling performance of HE‐HCF at 1500 mA g<sup>−1</sup> over 54 000 cycles.</p></caption></figure><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Properties Investigations of HE‐HCF Materials</title><p>The electrochemical performance of HE‐HCF was investigated by using a carbonate electrolyte composed of 1 M NaClO<sub>4</sub> in ethylene carbonate and diethyl carbonate (v/v=1 : 1) with the addition of 5 % fluoroethylene carbonate.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0061"><sup>[25]</sup></link> Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0002">2a</link> shows the charge‐discharge curves of the HE‐HCF electrode from 2.0 to 4.2 V, which delivers a moderate specific capacity of 105.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current density of 15 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (achieved ≈83 % of their theoretical specific capacity). The cyclic voltammetry (CV) curve in the inset in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0002">2a</link> shows a pair of well‐defined redox peaks at 3.05/3.43 V, which completely overlap in the first three cycles, indicating the superior reversibility of Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction in the framework of HE‐HCF. The HE‐HCF electrode also shows excellent electrochemical performance at various current densities from 150 mA g<sup>−1</sup> to 7500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to 1.5 C to 75 C (1 C=100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0002">2b</link>). Its reversible discharge capacity reaches ≈92.6, 84.3, 76.0, 66.3, and 55.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the rates of 1.5, 3, 7.5, 15, and 30 C, respectively. Even at an ultra‐high rate of 75 C, the HE‐HCF electrode materials could deliver a specific capacity of ≈35.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which means that the batteries could be fully charged/discharged in less than 20 seconds. The excellent fast‐charging ability of the HE‐HCF indicates that the HE‐HCF framework could provide desirable Na<sup>+</sup> migration channels as well as enlarged specific surface areas. When the current density gradually returned to 1.5 C, the specific capacity could also recover to 87.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is about 94 % of the initial value at 1.5 C. The long‐term cycling performance was further investigated, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0002">2c</link>. The HE‐HCF delivered capacity retention of ≈95 % during the initial 10 000 cycles, corresponding to a capacity fading ratio of 0.0005 % per cycle. Furthermore, the capacity fading ratio further decreased to 0.0004 % per cycle, even when the HE‐HCF was cycled for more than 50 000 cycles, while the cycling lifespan of the widely reported dual metal PBAs could hardly reach 5000 cycles. Although partly consisted of expensive elements of Ni and Co, the use‐cost per cycle of the HE‐HCF is still significantly lower when compared with typical PBAs owing to their excellent lifespan. High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HR‐TEM) was employed to investigate the surface of the HE‐HCF particles during the cycling process. The Supporting Information Figure 16 shows that the cathode‐electrolyte interphase (CEI) formed on the smooth surface of the pristine HE‐HCF particles with the layer of ≈6 nm after cycles. It has been reported that the formation of the CEI would benefit the cycling stability of the electrode.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0062"><sup>[26]</sup></link> To the best of our knowledge, this is the longest cycling lifespan among the reported PBA‐based cathode materials for organic SIBs.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0012 #anie202215865-bib-0021 #anie202215865-bib-0024 #anie202215865-bib-0056"><sup>[3a, 7b, 8a, 21a]</sup></link> This suggests that increasing the configuration entropy of the materials could significantly improve the stability of the electrode materials, which can be regarded as one of the key factors behind the outstanding cycling and rate performance of the HE‐HCF.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0049 #anie202215865-bib-0051 #anie202215865-bib-0065"><sup>[19a,c, 27]</sup></link></p></section><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Air stability, Thermal Stability, and All‐Climate Electrochemical Performance</title><p>As Guo's group reported, the typical Fe‐based PBAs nano‐particles are unstable in the ambient environment.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0066"><sup>[28]</sup></link> The air stability and the thermal stability of HE‐HCF were further evaluated to provide more material‐level information relevant to its practical applications. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern comparison between the fresh powder and the stored powder that had been exposed in air for 3 months shows no noticeable peak shift or variation, confirming that the crystal structure of HE‐HCF could remain stable in the ambient environment (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0003">3a</link>). Moreover, in situ heating XRD characterization was carried out to further investigate the thermal stability and the dehydration behavior of HE‐HCF from room temperature to 305 °C, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0003">3b</link>. The cubic crystal structure of HE‐HCF (at room temperature) gradually transforms to tetragonal from 165 °C, which remains stable when the temperature gradually increases to 265 °C. The crystal structure would be collapsed due to the thermal decomposition of HE‐HCF when the temperature exceeds 265 °C. Conceivably, there is no surprise that the charge‐discharge curves and the rate performance of the air‐exposed HE‐HCF sample are almost the same as those of the fresh sample in consideration of the unchanged crystal structure (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0003">3d</link>, <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0003">e</link>). The specific capacity of HE‐HCF is about 105 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.15 C, and it could maintain the capacity of 61 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈58 %) at the high rate of 15 C. Meanwhile, we also cycled the air‐exposed HE‐HCF at 7.5 C for up to 4500 cycles, and the capacity retention was nearly 90 %, corresponding to the capacity decay ratio of ≈0.22 % per 100 cycles. It was found that the cycling stability remained unchanged when compared with the fresh HE‐HCF. Therefore, we could assume that the storage cost of the HE‐HCF could be reduced due to its tolerance of the storage conditions.</p><figure xml:id="anie202215865-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202215865:anie202215865-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202215865-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The air and thermal stability investigation, and low/high‐temperature electrochemical performance. a) XRD comparison of the HE‐HCF powder in the fresh state and after exposure to air for three months. b) Contour plot of the in situ heating XRD from room temperature to 305 °C. c) Corresponding illustration of the thermally‐induced structure evolution. d) The initial five charge and discharge curves of the HE‐HCF (exposed in air for three months) at the current density of 15 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. e) Rate performance of the air‐exposed HE‐HCF at current densities from 0.3 C to 15 C (1 C=100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). f) Cycling performance of HE‐HCF at 1.5 C at different temperatures. g) Cycling performance of the fresh and air‐exposed HE‐HCF at 7.5 C.</p></caption></figure><p>Despite the material‐level merits of HE‐HCF, the cell‐level all‐climate performance (operating under low‐ and high‐temperature conditions) was also tested at both −10 °C and 60 °C. The specific capacities of the HE‐HCF could reach 98 and 102 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (at 0.3 C) at −10 °C and 60 °C (Supporting Information Figure 18), respectively. Even when tested at 1.5 C, the HE‐HCF showed outstanding cycling stability at both −10 °C and 60 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0003">3f</link>). There was almost no difference between the room temperature and high‐temperature performances, in which the specific capacity of HE‐HCF could reach 92.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with the capacity retention of 85.8 % after 200 cycles up to 60 °C, making it suitable for hot climate conditions. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was employed to investigate the charge transfer and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion process (Supporting Information Figure 19). The charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) of the HE‐HCF electrode is 213.1 Ω at room temperature and 11.4 Ω at a high temperature of 60 °C while the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> obviously increased to 1434.0 Ω at a low temperature of −10 °C. The decreased charge transfer resistance could be ascribed to the enhanced conductivity of the vacancy‐suppressed crystal structure of the PBA. Enhanced all‐climate suitability of the HE‐HCF‐based SIBs may be achieved by applying the specially designed high/low‐temperature electrolytes. In addition, we also carried out a cycling stability test of the HE‐HCF at the low temperature of −10 °C. The cycling stability of HE‐HCF remained outstanding without noticeable capacity fading after 200 cycles. The results show that the crystal structure of the HE‐HCF could remain stable under both low and high temperatures, further indicating that the HE‐HCF is a promising candidate cathode material for practical sodium‐ion batteries over a wide application range. Furthermore, we are still putting much effort into practical full cells based on HE‐HCF cathode and commercial hard carbon anode with optimized electrolytes (all‐climate suitable), rational mass loading, cell balance, etc. to progress one step further towards their practical application in large‐scale energy storage systems.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Sodium‐Storage Mechanism and Dynamics</title><p>In order to obtain a more comprehensive understanding of the sodium‐ion storage mechanism and dynamics of the HE‐HCF, in situ synchrotron powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD), in situ synchrotron X‐ray absorption spectra (XAS), in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and density functional theory (DFT) calculations were carried out. Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0004">4a</link> shows the two‐dimensional in situ PXRD patterns with the corresponding charge‐discharge curve. It can be seen that all the indexed peaks are highly reversible during the charge and discharge process with the general pattern remaining unchanged, indicating that the robust crystal framework of HE‐HCF can be maintained during the whole Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction process.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0067"><sup>[29]</sup></link> In particular, all main peaks of (200), (220), and (400) planes gradually shift towards higher degree during the first charging process, suggesting slight unit cell shrinkage of HE‐HCF, while the phase changes from cubic to tetragonal are accompanied by the Na<sup>+</sup> extraction from the crystal framework (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0004">4b</link>). Compared to the typical dual‐metal type PBAs that transfer ≈1.5 sodium‐ion per unit cell,<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0060"><sup>[24]</sup></link> the HE‐HCF shows suppressed structure distortion. More importantly, the HE‐HCF only undergoes a highly reversible two‐phase transition rather than the three‐phase transition which indicates this high‐entropy strategy contributes to the cycle stability of the HE‐HCF as long‐life SIBs. The highly reversible phase transition mechanism as well as the 3‐dimensional open framework structure could also benefit the fast sodium‐ion extraction/insertion process, resulting in enhanced rate performance when applied as cathode materials for SIBs. Furthermore, the valence states of the transition metals in HE‐HCF during the electrochemical reactions were evaluated by in situ synchrotron X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure (XANES) analysis (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0004">4c</link>). Here, transition metal oxides were applied as the references for the valences of the corresponding transition metals. The contour plot of the normalized XANES spectra clearly shows the reversible valence variation of each transition metal. During the charging process, the XANES spectra of Mn, Fe, Co, and Cu elements shift to a higher energy area, indicating that these elements were oxidated to higher valence states, while Ni element remained electrochemically inert, as reported previously.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0051 #anie202215865-bib-0070"><sup>[19c, 30]</sup></link> During the next discharge process, all peaks of both in situ synchrotron PXRD and XANES finally reverse back to their original positions, manifesting the phase conversion from tetragonal back to cubic, and the valence states of each element recover to their original state when Na<sup>+</sup> is inserted into the crystal framework of HE‐HCF again. According to the result of the ICP‐OES and the in situ XANES, the theoretical sodium‐ion storage numbers of the HE‐HCF are ≈1.5. Furthermore, in situ Raman spectra were collected to investigate the evolution process of the C≡N bond in HE‐HCF (Supporting Information Figure 20), which is sensitive to the oxidation state of Fe bonded with C. The strong peaks ranging from 2000–2300 cm<sup>−1</sup> could be ascribed to the C≡N bonds connected with the Mn/Fe/Co/Cu/Ni and the Fe atoms. During the sodiation process, the peak located at ≈2175 cm<sup>−1</sup> gradually shifts to lower wavelength and splits into three peaks due to the reduction of Fe<sup>3+</sup> to Fe<sup>2+</sup>. The peaks then recover to their initial position during the desodiation process, which further proves the high structural reversibility of HE‐HCF for sodium‐ion storage. Besides the atomic investigation, in situ TEM was employed to investigate the morphology evolution during the charge and discharge process from the micro‐scale viewpoint. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0005">5a</link>, the representative HE‐HCF secondary particle has an average size of ≈50 nm. A 3 V bias was applied to the sample to achieve the desodiation process, and it can be seen that the cluster gradually expanded to form a secondary nanoparticle with a diameter of ≈200 nm during the first 10 minutes. Furthermore, the nanocluster continuously expanded and formed an ellipsoidal secondary particle during the sodiation process. To further investigate the morphology evolution, we carried out a second desodiation and sodiation process. The as‐formed secondary particle could maintain its morphology without noticeable particle size variation during the second desodiation process. In addition, the particle could retain its morphology during the second sodiation process for up to 300 seconds. It should be pointed out, however, that the particle became amorphous and was broken by the high‐voltage TEM beamline due to the beam‐sensitive nature of PBAs.<link href="#anie202215865-bib-0060 #anie202215865-bib-0074"><sup>[24, 31]</sup></link> Obviously, the highly reversible phase transitions, suppressed structural distortion, and stable morphology of HE‐HCF during Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction could also explain its excellent cycling stability and rate performance.</p><figure xml:id="anie202215865-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title calculations were then used to study the reaction mechanism of the HE‐HCF. Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0006">6a</link> and <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0006">b</link> show the electronic structures of this HE‐HCF. The Mn, Fe (N‐bonded), and Ni are in high spin states, while Co, Fe (C‐bonded), and Cu are in low spin states. In addition, according to various <i>d</i> band positions, the reactivity of each transition metal and the redox potential can be simply obtained. The Cu element exhibits small density‐of‐states (DOS) peaks above the Fermi energy, and thus it is the first redox site during sodium insertion and contributes a small amount of capacity. The Co and Fe elements exhibit large DOS peaks above that of Cu and thus are subsequent reaction sites after Cu. The sodiation of Mn follows those of the Co and Fe. The DOS peak of Ni is far away from the Fermi level. These results can also be verified by partial charge analysis (from 0 to 4 eV above Fermi level) as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0006">6c</link>. The Cu shows a little charge distribution in this region, while Ni has essentially no charge distribution in this region. Moreover, the Jahn–Teller effect of Mn‐based cathodes is generally considered as an important reason that leads to electrochemical instability. In the HE‐HCF configuration, the Jahn–Teller effect of Mn‐N<sub>6</sub> can be effectively suppressed, improving the structural stability of Mn‐N<sub>6</sub> during electrochemical cycling. The high entropy strategy also improves the bonding strength in PBA by increasing the integrated crystal orbital Hamilton population (ICOHP) value (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0006">6e</link>). Combined with the higher <i>TS</i> (<i>T</i>=temperature and <i>S</i>=entropy) contribution, these allow high entropy materials to exhibit better thermal stability. Furthermore, owing to the superb diffusion kinetics of Ni‐HCF, this HE‐PBA also exhibits superior Na‐ion diffusion kinetics (Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0006">6f</link>). As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202215865-fig-0006">6g</link>, the diffusion kinetics is essentially isotropic, which leads to a significant enhancement of the sluggish kinetics of the electrochemically active Co‐HCF, Mn‐HCF, and Fe‐HCF.</p><figure xml:id="anie202215865-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202215865:anie202215865-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202215865-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Theoretical calculations on HE‐HCF. a) The partial density of states (pDOS) of metals in HE‐HCF (Tet). b) The spin states of these metals. c) The partial charge density isosurface of HE‐ HCF‐Tet at 0.02 e Bohr<sup>−3</sup>. d) Jahn–Teller effect induced distortion of Mn‐N<sub>6</sub> in tetragonal phase. e) The integrated crystal orbital Hamilton population values of various bonds. f) The diffusion activation energies of MFe‐ HCF and HE‐ HCF in cubic phase. g) The Na diffusional pathways within the HE‐ HCF framework.</p></caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, a configuration of HCF with five metals was prepared as a demonstration of the high‐entropy versions of MOFs as cathode materials for rechargeable SIBs. They exhibit, to the best of our knowledge, the longest cycling lifespan over 50 000 cycles among the MOFs as cathode materials for organic SIBs and excellent fast‐charging capability. Multiple characterization techniques and theoretical calculations have demonstrated that the increased configuration entropy in the HCF could enhance the structural stability as well as the thermal/air stability and provide a highly reversible zero‐strain two‐phase Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism. These configuration modifications are critical to suppress the Na<sup>+</sup> migration energy barriers. Our work might guide the rational design of high‐entropy materials (especially with less amount of Ni or Co) for application in various application fields besides rechargeable battery systems.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Supporting Information</title><p>Methods, XRD, TGA, Raman spectra, FTIR, SEM images, K‐edge EXAFS, EDS elemental mapping, TEM patterns of the HE‐HCF, Charge and discharge curves of typical single metal hexacyanoferrates, Charge and discharge curves of HE‐HCF at different temperature, EIS pattern, In situ Raman spectra, Structural information and chemical formula of the as‐prepared samples.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202215865-sec-0008" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>This work is financially supported by an Australian Research Council (ARC) Discovery Project (<link href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2211285518309509">DP180101453</link>), an Australian Renewable Energy Agency (ARENA) Project (G00849), and the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Nos. 51971124, 52171217, and 52102285).
