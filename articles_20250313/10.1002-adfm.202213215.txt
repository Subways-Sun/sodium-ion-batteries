Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Layered transition metal oxide (Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>), being one of the most promising cathode candidates for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), have attracted intensive interest because of their nontoxicity, high theoretical capacities, and easy manufacturability. However, their physical and electrochemical properties of water sensitivity, sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> transport kinetics, and irreversible multiple‐phase translations hinder the practical application. Here, a concept of surface lattice‐matched engineering is proposed based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction to design a spinel coating P2/P3 heterostructure cathode material with enhanced air stability, rate, and cycle performance. The novel structure and its formation process are verified by transmission electron microscopy and in situ high‐temperature X‐ray diffraction. The electrode exhibits an excellent rate performance with the highly reversible phase transformation demonstrated by in situ charging/discharging X‐ray diffraction. Additionally, even after a rigorous water sensitivity test, the electrode materials still retain almost the same superior electrochemical performance as the fresh sample. The results show that the surface spinel phase can play a vital role in preventing the ingress of water molecules, improving transport kinetics, and enhancing structural integrity for Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub> cathodes. The concept of surface lattice‐matched engineering based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction will be helpful for designing new ultra‐stable cathode materials for high‐performance SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adfm202213215-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The formation process and function mechanism for inhibiting phase transformation and enhancing air stability of surface lattice‐matched engineering based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction are studied. This strategy of designing heterostructure with in situ interfacial reconstruction will inspire the exploitation of new chemistries and materials.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adfm202213215-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Developing advanced energy storage techniques is crucial in achieving efficient utilization of intermittent renewable energy such as solar and wind. Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), as one of the most powerful tools for energy conversion, have been commercialized for several decades.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0001 #adfm202213215-bib-0002 #adfm202213215-bib-0003 #adfm202213215-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the increasing price of lithium caused by the uneven distribution and limited reserves obstructs the development of LIBs to deliver reliable electric power for households and businesses alike. To this end, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been explored as a candidate to meet the demand for large‐scale electrochemical energy storage owing to the abundance of sodium reserves, low cost, and similar chemistry to that of traditional LIBs currently in commercial settings.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0005 #adfm202213215-bib-0006 #adfm202213215-bib-0007 #adfm202213215-bib-0008 #adfm202213215-bib-0009 #adfm202213215-bib-0010 #adfm202213215-bib-0011 #adfm202213215-bib-0012"><sup>5–12</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Among the various cathode candidates, layered transition metal oxide (Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>) cathodes have attracted much interest over recent years due to their large specific capacity, high ionic conductivity, environmental benignity, and potential for commercial‐scale synthesis methods.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0013 #adfm202213215-bib-0014 #adfm202213215-bib-0015"><sup>13–15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The major bottleneck however in designing high‐performance SIBs is the lack of novel cathode materials with ultra‐stability in the condition of charging/discharging process and ambient air, which requires cathodes to possess excellent highly reversible structural stability and hydrophobic performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0016 #adfm202213215-bib-0017 #adfm202213215-bib-0018 #adfm202213215-bib-0019"><sup>16–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to different Na<sup>+</sup> chemical environments and stacking sequences of oxygen ions, there are several categories of typical Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>, such as P2, P3, O2, and O3.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0020 #adfm202213215-bib-0021 #adfm202213215-bib-0022"><sup>20–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The O‐type Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub> usually delivers a great capacity (≈160 to 220 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) because of the high Na contents stacking sequence, however, it suffers from the sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> transport kinetics and fast capacity decay caused by unfavorable Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathway and complicated phase translation when going through the process of embedding and extracting Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0023 #adfm202213215-bib-0024 #adfm202213215-bib-0025 #adfm202213215-bib-0026"><sup>23–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared with O‐type, the P‐type Na<sub>x</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub> is able to achieve a better rate and cycling performance because of the open prismatic structure which is a benefit to Na<sup>+</sup> mobility.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0027 #adfm202213215-bib-0028 #adfm202213215-bib-0029"><sup>27–29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Whereas volume expansion occurring through irreversible P2‐O2 phase translation at high voltage would result in the capacity decrease, which would hinder the achievement of high‐energy density SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0030 #adfm202213215-bib-0031"><sup>30,31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To solve these issues, a biphase intergrowth structure has been proposed such as P2/O3 and P2/P3 layered composited cathode materials, which implements the integration of merits of different structures to enhance electrochemical performance compared with separated counterparts.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0032 #adfm202213215-bib-0033 #adfm202213215-bib-0034 #adfm202213215-bib-0035 #adfm202213215-bib-0036 #adfm202213215-bib-0037"><sup>32–37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, designing air‐stable layered oxide cathodes is important to enable the practical application, since most of oxides are vulnerable to humidity molecular.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among many oxide species, the <i>Fd‐3m</i> spinel phase has been verified the advantages of boosting electron mobility, fine air stability as well as structural stability, which is widely studied as cathode material for LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Following this line of thinking, integrating the spinel phase into the layered phase is an accessible way to enhance cathodes for SIBs electrochemical performance. Therefore, it is necessary to have access to information about the formation process and function mechanism of heterostructure based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction, which is beneficial to take full advantage of inherent high stability derived from the spinel phase to realize improvement in battery properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0040 #adfm202213215-bib-0041 #adfm202213215-bib-0042"><sup>40–42</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In this work, we have designed a surface lattice‐matched heterostructure based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction P2/P3‐Na<sub>0.5</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM) as a cathode for SIBs, delivering an excellent rate performance and satisfied air stability. In situ high‐temperature X‐ray diffraction (in situ HTXRD) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were utilized to study the structural evolution process. Then, the phase transition of the intergrowth structure during the charge/discharge process was investigated by in situ X‐ray diffraction (in situ XRD). Besides, the ability against the water of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM was studied by TEM, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), X‐ray diffraction (XRD), and dynamic contact angle (DCA). Even after a rigorous water sensitivity test, the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM electrode materials still possess superior electrochemical performance (76% capacity retention at 5C vs 0.5C). The results show that the surface spinel phase could be the hydrophobic layer to prevent the ingress of water molecules following exposure to moist air. The concept of surface lattice‐matched engineering based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction will provide the insight required to design better stability cathode materials for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0030"><title type="main">Formation of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM</title><p>The in situ HTXRD technique was conducted to observe the dynamic formation process during calcination, which could show the precise phase transformation and then provide guidance on the rational calcination program. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, the dynamic formation process has three periods of phases translation: P3→spinel/P3→spinel/P2/P3 during the calcination process. In the initial stage of calcination, amorphous peaks can be observed from 25 to 300 °C. And then, (003) and (104) characteristic diffraction peaks belonging to P3 layered phase emerge when the temperature is up to 450 °C and then (111) and (311) characteristic peaks of spinel phase display with the temperature rising to 675 °C. At the end, (106) characteristic peaks of P2 layered phase present at 775 °C.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0032 #adfm202213215-bib-0043"><sup>32,43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As the increase of temperature, the intensity of characteristic diffraction peaks is stronger than before which proves that the crystallinity of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM is enhanced and the spinel/P2/P3 heterostructure could stabilize at high temperature. To futher display the phase evolution during the heating process, the typical in situ HTXRD patterns selected at different temperatures with phase translation and bird's eye view of in situ HTXRD patterns with range from 10° to 80° are shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0001">1b</link> and Figures <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S2</link> and <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S3</link> (Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adfm202213215-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural transformation during calcination. a) In situ high‐temperature XRD patterns at different temperatures of the precursors of P2/P3@spinel‐NaNCM cathode materials during calcination and corresponding intensity contour maps. b) Bird's eye view of in situ high‐temperature XRD.</caption></figure><p>To obtain more detailed information on surface lattice structure change, NaMCM‐600, NaMCM‐750, and NaMCM‐800 were synthesized by calcining precursors with different temperatures of 600, 750, and 800°C and observed by TEM and XRD (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>; Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). From Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2a</link>, at low magnification, normal ordered lamellar structure samples (dark substance) are dispersed on the matrix made up of small and irregular fragments in NaMCM‐600. The high‐resolution image enlarged from Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2a</link> confirmed the main phase of NaMCM‐600 is P3 possessing lattice distance of 0.53 nm shown in intensity profile (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2d</link>). When the calcination temperature rises to 750 °C, the rectangle plates gather like being melted shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2e</link>. It is worth noting that the grain boundary (white dashed line shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2f</link>) is performed by the spinel phase (yellow dashed line shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2g</link>) and the bulk the maintains P3 phase (red dashed line shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2g</link>), the interlayer spacing of 0.53 nm and 0.48 nm respectively correspond to P3 and spinel phases (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2h</link>). It displays that the spinel phase appears gradually on the surface separating each NaMCM‐750 particle with the growth of P3 phase crystalline grain. The emergence of the spinel phase might be the consequence the of degeneration of the layered structure (<i>R‐3m</i>) since the release of carbon dioxide or oxygen was derived from the precursor, which could lead to losing oxygen atoms from the lattice.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0044"><sup>44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Since the P2 structure is obtained under higher temperatures, NaMCM‐800 still maintains the same structure as NaMCM‐750 with the presentence of P2 (0.57 nm) in the bulk area (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2l</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To offer more intuitionistic information on formation process of NaMCM, the illustration is presented in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2m</link> which exhibits structure evolution process with different temperatures and compositions systematically. Furthermore, XPS was carried out to characterize the element charge and get information about structural positions, since Co<sup>2+</sup> possesses the specific electronic configuration which is related to the tetrahedral sites in the spinel phase (Figures <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S5–S7</link>, Supporting Information).The content ratios of Co<sup>2+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> in NaMCM‐600, NaMCM‐750, and NaMCM‐800 are 40.9%, 58.7%, and 61.8%, respectively. As shown in Table <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information), it is clear that the content of Co<sup>2+</sup> in composites increases with the temperature rising up implying that more spinel phase generates.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0044"><sup>44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The results of XPS are well consistent with TEM images, indicating spinel structures are stable at high temperature and could enhance structural stability.</p><figure xml:id="adfm202213215-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The formation process of the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode material. a–c) TEM images of NaMCM‐600. d) Line profiles and measured interlayer of Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2c</link>. e–g) TEM images of NaMCM‐750. h) Line profiles and measured interlayer of selected areas in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2g</link>. i–k) TEM images of NaMCM‐800. l) Line profiles and measured interlayer of Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0002">2k</link>. m) Schematic showing the structural transformation under different calcination temperatures.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0040"><title type="main">Structural Characterization</title><p>The composited crystal structure of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM has been demonstrated by powder XRD, which could be finely indexed to the hexagonal P2 phase with space group <i>P6<sub>3</sub>/mmc</i>, the hexagonal P3 phase with space group <i>R‐3m</i> and the spinel phase with space group <i>Fd‐3m</i> respectively (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>). The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image also proves the existence of three phases which is consistent with the result of XRD (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0003">3b</link>). It is worth noting that the spinel phase is coated on the surface of cathode materials while the position of layered structures like P2 and P3 are located in the bulk. The more detailed lattice fringes are shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0003">3c</link>‐<link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0003">3e</link>. The interplanar lattice distance of 0.26 nm with each other at an angle of 85° should be assigned to the (311) and (−131) crystal facets of the spinel phase (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0003">3c</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The two kinds of layered structure could be characterized by the (101) and (102) planes which belong to P2 and P3 phases respectively (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0003">3d,e</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0047 #adfm202213215-bib-0048"><sup>47,48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high‐angle annular dark field (HAADF) images also verify that the obtained P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM exhibits three distinct phase strucutres. Integrated with the above‐advanced techniques, it is able to prove that the spinel phase coating P2/P3 intergrowth structure has been indeed prepared.</p><figure xml:id="adfm202213215-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structure of the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode material. a) Powder XRD pattern. b) TEM image. c–e) HRTEM images of the spinel structures, P2 and P3 respectively. f) TEM‐EDS mapping results for selected elements.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0050"><title type="main">Na Storage Performance of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM</title><p>To achieve the aim of high energy density SIBs, it is vital to investigate the electrochemical performance in a wider voltage window.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0049 #adfm202213215-bib-0050 #adfm202213215-bib-0051"><sup>49–51</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The electrochemical performance of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM was tested in Na half coin cells (1C = 160 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) with the voltage window of 1.5 V–4.3 V (vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). It is well known that there are several voltage plateaus in typical P2‐phase cathode materials according to the Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy rearrangement, P3‐P3’ and P2‐O2 phase transformation accompanied by transition metal layers gilding and indicative of Jahn–Teller induced structural transitions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0052 #adfm202213215-bib-0053"><sup>52,53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM displays smooth charging/discharging profile curves in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>. It could be attributed to preventing Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy ordering and stabilizing the charging balance via remaining more into the structure when Mg<sup>2+</sup> are introduced in the transition metal lattice.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0054"><sup>54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The rate performance of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode was investigated under various rates in the range of 0.1 to 5 C for five cycles which displays a reversible specific capacity of 135.9, 131.1, 125.9, 119.7, 111.9, 101.7, 94.6, and 85.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>(Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0004">4b,c</link>). The capacity retention at different current density corresponding to 0.1 C is shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S10</link> (Supporting Information). To further explore the electrochemical reactions occurring in the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM sample and the ability of reaction reversibility, cyclic voltammetry (CV) was carried out. A pair of board oxidation and reduction peaks (2.15 V and 1.95 V) was observed owing to the Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> redox reaction of the layered phase, which is well consistent with the charge/discharge curves. In addition, the Na<sup>+</sup> transport kinetics properties were studied by using CV with different scan rates shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0004">4d</link>. The quantitative calculation of the <i>b</i> parameter values in equation <i>i = av<sup>b</sup></i> could further determine the mechanism of sodium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0055 #adfm202213215-bib-0056"><sup>55,56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The <i>b</i> values demonstrate a dominant Faradaic mechanism feature of sodium storage in P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM electrode (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0004">4e,f</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0057"><sup>57</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The cycling capability of the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM composite was investigated in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0004">4g</link> and Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S11</link> (Supporting Information). The spinel‐coated cathodes present a respectable reversible capacity with 97.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C delivering 81.3% capacity retention, even at 2 C current density still exhibit a capacity of 81.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention 88.9% after 100 cycles. The energy density of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM is shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S12</link> (Supporting Information). In summary, an air‐stable P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode material has been synthesized to achieve superior excellent rate capability. Designed spinel phase coating enables to accelerate electron transport as well as electrochemical behavior for layered oxide cathode materials.</p><figure xml:id="adfm202213215-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode material. a) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves of specific capacity at 0.1C. b,c) Rate performance at various rates and corresponding charge/discharge 5 curves. d) Cyclic voltammograms at different scan rates. e,f) Linear fitting of peak current versus square root of the scan rate as well as log (i) versus log (v) plots at different oxidation and reduction peaks. g) Cycling performance during 100 cycles at 2C current density after rate test.</caption></figure><p>To further demonstrate the dynamic phase evolution of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM, in situ charges and discharges XRD is applied to monitor the structural change during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction and intercalation within the potential range of 1.5 V–4.3 V. The pristine P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM could be identified by the (002) and (004) characteristic peaks of the P2 phase, the (003), (006), (102), and (015) peaks of P3 phase and (311) of spinel phase.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0058"><sup>58</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Remarkably, there is no change in angle shift and intensity of (311), which means the spinel phase remains structural stability and then could lead to a stable interface during electrochemical process for improving cycling durability. As displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>, the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode presents the highly reversible ability of structural transitions. During the Na<sup>+</sup> extraction process, the (002)/(004) of P2 peaks constantly shifted to lower angles, indicating an increased distance of interlayer caused by the repulsive interaction of adjacent layers. However, in the following embedding Na<sup>+</sup> process, the diffraction peaks of the (002)/(004) of P2 and (003)/(006) of P3 restored to the original degree, exhibiting excellent structural reversibility. Meanwhile, the phase translation of P2‐O2 was prevented in the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM, P2‐type crystal structure was retained during the charging/discharging processes with more vivid illustrations in the intensity contour maps. The whole structural evolution process is illustrated in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0005">5c</link>, the pristine phase is Spinel/P2/P3, the same structure remains until charging progress is complete, and then P3'’ begins to begin to gradually appear instead of P3 phase when discharging to 3.6 V. According to the established literature, the spinel phase exhibits excellent air stability, which is of key importance in reducing the cost of the synthesis procedure and storage of cathodes for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0061 #adfm202213215-bib-0062"><sup>61,62</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, in order to confirm the practical application, the property of being stable in the air and rigorous aging experiment concerning soaking the samples in the water were tested. The negligible difference between fresh samples and aging samples in XRD reflections implies that the structure of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM remains intact after exposure to the air (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link>). The tailored spinel‐coated structure still maintains complete as shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S16</link> (Supporting Information) after exposure to the wet air with a humidity of 50% for 7 days. The intensity profiles show more clearly distinguishable differences between the yellow region (surficial area) and the red region (interior area). The former interlayer spacing is 0.47 nm assigned to the spinel‐like phase, while the latter interplanar crystal spacing of the aging sample is maintained at 0.56 nm and 0.55 nm corresponding to (002) of P2 and (003) of P3. Additionally, to verify the hydrophobic of samples more directly, the wettability of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM was defined by DCA in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006">6b</link>. The result shows that water does not make the surface wet well and beads up on the surface accompanied by the average contact angle with water being 100.86°, which suggests spinel layers could prevent water molecular damage to the water‐sensitive surface of P‐type effectively. The enhancement of air stability may be determined by the spinel‐like structure that obstructs the intercalation of water. Because of the surface sensitivity, XPS was utilized to conduct the surface valance state of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM, being exposed in the air and soaked samples, which is helpful to clarify the function of the interfacial spinel phase. Mn 2p, Co 2p, and Mg 1s fine spectra for the fresh and aged samples are shown in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006">6d–f</link>, proving the composited elements of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM. The positions of all peaks are assigned to the transition metal elements remaining virtually unaffected after 7 days of aging, confirming the obtained P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM possesses desirable air stability. Especially, the Mn element in the sample mainly exists in the valence state of +4, which is characterized by the binding energy of 642.19 eV and 653.6 eV in Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006">6d</link>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0063"><sup>63</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the high‐resolution Co 2p spectrum, two sets of spin‐orbit‐resolved peaks can be resolved as Co<sup>2+</sup> and Co<sup>3+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0064"><sup>64</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared with the pulverization generated from Jahn‐Teller distortion of Mn<sup>3+</sup>, more content of Mn<sup>4+</sup> could provide better thermodynamic and structural stability for electrode materials and improve the ability of the long‐term cycle of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adfm202213215-bib-0065"><sup>65</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This consequence is well consistent with the observation in the HRTEM, indicating that the spinel phase plays a key role in preventing the ingress of water molecules and endowing a protective layer on the interface of cathode materials. To further investigate the ability of against moisture molecular and electrochemical performance, rigorous aging measurements were conducted in water for P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathodes. XRD patterns of the two samples show similar diffraction shifts following being soaked in the water, however, the peak intensity of the soaked sample is lower than that of the fresh sample (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006">6c</link>). The Na storage performance of the soaked sample was also studied. From Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006">6g–i</link>, even being soaked in the water, P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM still exhibits excellent rate performance, achieving 85.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> under a current density of 5C. After 100 cycles, the specific capacity could also achieve 84.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>at 2 C (Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0006">6i</link>; Figure <link href="#adfm202213215-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information). All results illustrate that in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction greatly improve the air stability of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM, which could be also applied to other cathodes to enhance environmental stability.</p><figure xml:id="adfm202213215-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Air stability of the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode material. a) XRD patterns of fresh samples and samples exposed to air respectively. b) Contact angle measurements. c) XRD patterns of fresh sample and soaked sample respectively. d) Mn 2p; e) Co 2p; f) Mg 1s high‐resolution XPS spectra of fresh sample and aging sample g) Charge/discharge profile curves of soaked samples. h) Rate performance at various rates. i) Cycling performance during 100 cycles at 2C current density after rate test of soaked samples.</caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0070"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, an air‐stable spinel phase coated P2/P3 intergrowth Na<sub>0.5</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<b>Scheme</b> <link href="#adfm202213215-fig-0007"><b>1</b></link>) has been prepared. The surface lattice‐matched engineering based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction could endow an effective protection layer to mitigate the direct contact of the active material with H<sub>2</sub>O and enhance air stability. Besides, the tailored spinel phase could not only inhibit translation metal slabs sliding and keep the structure intact during charging‐discharging processes, but also provide fast electron transport pathway to enhance rate performance of P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM. The concept of surface lattice‐matched engineering based on in situ spinel interfacial reconstruction displays promising prospects for high‐performance cathode materials for SIBs as well as further inspires the exploitation of new chemistries and materials.</p><figure xml:id="adfm202213215-fig-0007"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm202213215:adfm202213215-fig-0007" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm202213215-fig-0007-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic figure of the P2/P3@spinel‐NaMCM cathode material.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adfm202213215-sec-0080" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>J.‐Y.L. and H.‐Y.H. contributed equally to this work.
