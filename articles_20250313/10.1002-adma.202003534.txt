Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>To attain both high energy density and power density in sodium‐ion (Na<sup>+</sup>) batteries, the reaction kinetics and structural stability of anodes should be improved by materials optimization. In this work, few‐layered molybdenum sulfide selenide (MoSSe) consisting of a mixture of 1T and 2H phases is designed to provide high ionic/electrical conductivities, low Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier, and stable Na<sup>+</sup> storage. Reduced graphene oxide (rGO) is used as a conductive matrix to form 3D electron transfer paths. The resulting MoSSe@rGO anode exhibits high capacity and rate performance in both organic and solid‐state electrolytes. The ultrafast Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics of the MoSSe@rGO anode is attributed to the surface‐dominant reaction process and broad Na<sup>+</sup> channels. In situ and ex situ measurements are conducted to reveal the Na<sup>+</sup> storage process in MoSSe@rGO. It is found that the MoS and MoSe bonds effectively limit the dissolution of the active materials. The favorable Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics of the MoSSe@rGO electrode are ascribed to its low adsorption energy of −1.997 eV and low diffusion barrier of 0.087 eV. These results reveal that anion doping of metal sulfides is a feasible strategy to develop sodium‐ion batteries with high energy and power densities and long life‐span.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202003534-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Innovatively, a hybrid 1T/2H phase is constructed by the synergistic effect of dual‐anions in MoSSe. Superior ionic/electrical conductivities can be attained due to the hybrid phase and rGO composite. Meanwhile, the stable structure for Na<sup>+</sup> storage is ascribed to the enlarged layer spacing and remarkable pseudocapacitive effect. The strong interactions between MoS and MoSe bonds can effectively limit the dissolution of S/Se electrodes.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202003534:adma202003534-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202003534-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202003534-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202003534-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Currently, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are regarded as a feasible candidate to replace lithium‐ion batteries because of the lower cost and toxicity of sodium‐based materials than those of lithium‐based materials. After a decade of investigation, the electrochemical performance of SIB cathode materials is now close to the theoretical value.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the low energy density and power density of SIB anodes currently limit the practical application of SIBs in electronics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0002 #adma202003534-bib-0003"><sup>2,3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Metal sulfides/selenides/phosphides and intermetallic compounds have been developed to improve the performance of SIB anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0004 #adma202003534-bib-0005 #adma202003534-bib-0006"><sup>4–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In conversion reactions, 2D metal sulfides/selenides exhibit high energy densities and low diffusion energy barriers.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In particular, molybdenum disulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>) and molybdenum diselenide (MoSe<sub>2</sub>) are regarded as the potential materials for high‐performance SIBs. However, the complex reaction mechanism,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> substantial volume distortion,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> strong NaS bonds,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> low electrical conductivity,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and dissolution of polysulfides/polyselenides<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> restrict the development of MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes. To overcome the sluggish kinetics of MoS<sub>2</sub> and narrow the gap between its theoretical and practical performance, previous studies have focused on advanced MoS<sub>2</sub> structures, including a specific morphology with buffer spaces,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> a conductive material composite,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> phase structure tailoring,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and ion‐exchange modification.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Generally, MoS<sub>2</sub> anode materials should possess buffer spaces to accommodate the volume expansion after sodium‐ion (Na<sup>+</sup>) insertion, such as vertical MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheet arrays,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> petal‐like MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and 3D MoS<sub>2</sub>/reduced graphene oxide (rGO) nanovesicles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nanostructured active materials and moderate void content are critical structural design elements for high‐performance SIB anodes. Furthermore, the low electrical conductivity of MoS<sub>2</sub> can be improved via composite formation with a carbon material. Typically, carbon materials such as carbon nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon nanofibers,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon spheres,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and rGO<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> provide paths for rapid electron and prevent the agglomeration of anode active materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0024 #adma202003534-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Besides these routine strategies, there are several ways to enhance the Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance of MoS<sub>2</sub>. An enlarged layer spacing provides broad Na<sup>+</sup> migration paths and lowers the diffusion energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, the diffusion energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> decreased from 0.7 to 0.3 eV when the layer spacing was increased from 6.17 to 7.0 Å.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Na<sup>+</sup> transfer in 2D materials is strongly influenced by the bond strength.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high inductive effect and electronegativity of oxygen ions (O<sup>2−</sup>) enables the formation of strong Na–O bonds, leading to sluggish dynamics. The introduction of selenium ions (Se<sup>2−</sup>) into SIB anodes can improve the Na<sup>+</sup> transfer rate and provide a stable host structure. Recently, the positive effects of the 1T phase of MoS<sub>2</sub> for attaining metallic conductivity and weak van der Waals interaction in its 2D materials have been noted.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0015 #adma202003534-bib-0029"><sup>15,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The hydrophilic nature of the 1T phase is conducive to electrolyte wetting.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the 2H phase of MoS<sub>2</sub> usually has a stable structure and low formation energy, so exists in nature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A 2H/1T hybrid phase of 2D material may exhibit rapid Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics and a stable structure.</p><p>In this manuscript, MoSSe@rGO is synthesized via a high‐temperature hydrothermal method. MoSSe boasts the high energy density of MoS<sub>2</sub> and outstanding cycling stability of MoSe<sub>2</sub>. The metallic 1T phase is formed in MoSSe, which enhances its electrical conductivity and reactivity. Moreover, the high pseudocapacitance of MoSSe@rGO improves its Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics. Based on this mechanism, a MoSSe@rGO anode achieves breakthroughs in cycling stability and rate performance in both organic and solid‐state electrolytes even at high/low temperatures. The phase transformation and conversion reaction process in MoSSe are studied. The highly reversible Na<sup>+</sup> storage process between S/Se, Na<sub>2</sub>S/Na<sub>2</sub>Se, and MoS<sub>2</sub>/MoSe<sub>2</sub> can be ascribed to the strong adsorption effect of rGO and chemical interaction of sulfur (S) and selenium (Se) with Mo.</p><p>The synthetic process of the MoSSe@rGO nanoribbons with a hierarchical 3D conductive structure is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. MoSSe was produced by the solvothermal reaction of ammonium tetrathiomolybdate as a source of both molybdenum and sulfur and selenium powder as a selenium source. When graphene oxide (GO) nanosheets were added into the system, a uniform coating of MoSSe was formed on GO because of the induction effect of GO functional groups.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hydrazine hydrate behaved as a reducing agent, providing Se<sup>2−</sup> and deacidifying GO.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The unique morphology of MoSSe@rGO improved both ion/electron transfer kinetics and structural stability.</p><figure xml:id="adma202003534-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202003534:adma202003534-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202003534-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the self‐assembly routes of earthworm‐like MoSSe nanoribbons. b) XRD patterns, c) Raman spectra, and d) FT‐IR spectra of as‐prepared MoSSe@rGO, MoSSe, MoS<sub>2</sub>, and MoSe<sub>2</sub> samples. e,f) SEM images of MoSSe@rGO with different amplified factors. The inset shows partially coated rGO nanosheets by earthworm‐like MoSSe. g) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms and particle size analysis of MoSSe@rGO. h) TGA and DSC curves of MoSSe@rGO. i) TDOS and PDOS patterns of MoS<sub>2</sub>, MoSe<sub>2</sub>, and MoSSe. The Mo, S, Se, and total contributions are marked by the blue, red, cyan, and black lines, respectively.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1b</link> shows X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of MoS<sub>2</sub>, MoSe<sub>2</sub>, MoSSe, and MoSSe@rGO. MoSSe and MoSSe@rGO possessed similar crystal structures to those of pure MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No.37‐1492) and MoSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No.29‐0914). The characteristic peaks of MoSe<sub>2</sub> shifted to slightly lower angle compared to MoS<sub>2</sub>, which is because the ionic radius of Se<sup>2−</sup> (1.98 Å) is larger than that of S<sup>2−</sup> (1.84 Å). The (002) peak of MoSSe at 13.5° corresponded to a lattice spacing of 6.6 Å, which is approximately the same as that of MoSe<sub>2</sub> (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The enlarged lattice spacing of MoSSe compared with that of MoS<sub>2</sub> provides broader paths for Na<sup>+</sup> transfer and sufficient sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage. Raman spectra of MoSSe (Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1c</link>) revealed that it consisted of a mixture of 2H and metallic 1T phases. For MoS<sub>2</sub>, the Raman peaks located at 380 and 405 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponded to the E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> (in‐plane vibration) and A<sub>1g</sub> (out‐of‐plane vibration) modes of the 2H phase, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For MoSe<sub>2</sub>, the peaks at 237 and 287 cm<sup>−1</sup> were assigned to the A<sub>1g</sub> and E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> vibration modes of the 2H phase, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Raman spectrum of 2H/1T hybrid MoSSe contained two peaks at 143 and 279 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to the J<sub>2</sub> and E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> modes of 1T MoSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively, and two weak peaks around 200 cm<sup>−1</sup> (J<sub>1</sub> and J<sub>2</sub> modes) that indicated the existence of 1T MoS<sub>2</sub>. The peak observed at 374 cm<sup>−1</sup> from the E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> mode is exclusive to the 2H structure. The formation of a 2H/1T hybrid phase is conducive to fast electron migration and low transformation energy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0036"><sup>36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The presence of rGO in MoSSe@rGO was confirmed by the presence of Raman peaks from the D band (1344 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and G band (1590 cm<sup>−1</sup>) of rGO with an intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) of 1.21 (Figure S2, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1d</link> displays the Fourier transform infrared (FT‐IR) spectra of the samples, which provide further information about their composition. The peaks observed at 592, 789, and 976 cm<sup>−1</sup> in the FT‐IR spectrum of MoSSe@rGO were characteristic of CS, CSe, and COS/Se, respectively, demonstrating the strong interaction between MoSSe and rGO in MoSSe@rGO.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The morphology of MoSSe@rGO was visualized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1e</link>). The rGO nanosheets were uniformly loaded with MoSSe nanorods. The rGO substrate was clearly seen because of the incomplete coverage of the MoSSe nanorods. The magnified view in Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1f</link> reveals that the MoSSe nanorods with staggered structure covered the external surfaces of the 2D rGO nanosheets. Energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS; Figure S3, Supporting Information) revealed the chemical components of MoSSe@rGO. The quantitative EDS results indicated the presence of S and Se with a molar ratio of 0.95:1, which agreed with the results of element analysis. The thickness and roughness of the MoSSe nanorod coating layer on the rGO nanosheets were investigated by atomic force microscopy (AFM; Figure S4, Supporting Information), which revealed that the average thickness of the MoSSe monolayer was ≈1.2 nm.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0037"><sup>37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The slight and regular fluctuation of layer thickness further indicated the uniform distribution of MoSSe nanorods on rGO. MoSSe@rGO exhibited a high Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 99.9 cm<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1g</link>), which was attributed to the uniform MoSSe nanorod coating without agglomeration. The pore sizes of MoSSe@rGO ranged from ≈1.8 to 50 nm, indicating the coexistence of micro‐ and mesopores (Figure S5, Supporting Information). Such a structure facilitated the contact between the electrolyte and active material, leading to enhanced pseudocapacitive behavior to promote Na<sup>+</sup> storage. The average particle size distribution was 400 nm.</p><p>To confirm the carbon content of MoSSe@rGO, thermogravimetric analysis was performed (Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1h</link>). The weight loss was mainly attributed to the volatilization of CO<sub>2</sub>, SO<sub>2</sub>, and SeO<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to the chemical formula of the combustion reaction, the calculated rGO content of MoSSe@rGO is 19.5 wt%, which is sufficient to form an effective electron transport network and provide abundant material attachment sites. Meanwhile, the inherent conductivity of MoSSe is improved by Se introduction. The bandgaps of bulk MoS<sub>2</sub>, MoSe<sub>2</sub>, and MoSSe were calculated to be 1.295, 0.983, and 1.066 eV, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0001">1i</link>). The delocalized Se 3d orbital decreases the bandgap of MoSSe compared with that of MoS<sub>2</sub>, resulting in high electrical conductivity of 5.6 × 10<sup>−4</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>, as confirmed by the four‐probe method (Figure S6, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The morphology and microstructure of the MoSSe@rGO nanorods were further observed by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). The MoSSe nanorods were uniformly attached on the rGO nanosheets to form a 3D conductive network and maintained ample spaces between nanorods. A high‐angle annular dark field (HAADF) image of MoSSe@rGO showed the 3D composite structure of MoSSe nanorods and rGO nanosheets, illustrated by the different contrasts of the metal and carbon materials. The MoSSe nanorods are rather small, consistent with their broad XRD diffraction peaks. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image of MoSSe@rGO in Figure <link href="#adma202003534-fig-0002">2b</link> shows its few‐layered nature (&lt;6 layers) and large interlayer spacing (≈0.8 nm), which is conductive to relieve lattice strain and optimize Na<sup>+</sup> migration paths. The HRTEM image reveals MoSSe@rGO has a lattice spacing of 0.228 nm, consistent with the (103) plane of MoSSe (Figure S7a, Supporting Information). The hybrid S and Se sites can be observed intuitively in the scanning TEM‐HAADF images (Figure S7b, Supporting Information). Anion substitution not only introduced Se<sup>2−</sup> into MoS<sub>2</sub> but also formed a hybrid of hexagonal and trigonal phases. MoSSe exhibited a low <i>E</i><sub>d</sub> of 0.087 eV, which was attributed to its large layer spacing and the weak NaSe interactions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202003534-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Correspondingly, the Na<sup>+</sup> chemical diffusion coefficients of MoSSe@rGO calculated by the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique ranged from 10<sup>−11.5</sup> to 10<sup>−10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S19, Supporting Information).</p><p>MoSSe@rGO was fabricated by a simple one‐pot hydrothermal approach. The rGO nanosheets provided fast electron diffusion paths and accommodated the volume expansion during the sodiation process. The synergistic effect of dual anion (S<sup>2−</sup> and Se<sup>2−</sup>) doping was verified by the enhanced electrochemical performance of MoSSe@rGO. The introduction of Se<sup>2−</sup> increased the interlayer spacing of MoS<sub>2</sub> and formed a 2H/1T hybrid phase, leading to wide ion channels and metallic properties. The MoSSe@rGO electrode exhibited excellent cycling durability and outstanding rate capability suitable for both organic liquid‐state and inorganic solid‐state batteries during wide temperature range. Even at a current density of 6.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the MoSSe@rGO electrode maintained a high capacity of 361.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> in liquid electrolyte because of its remarkable pseudocapacitive contribution and low Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier. Furthermore, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism in MoSSe@rGO electrode involved a conversion and alloying reaction process with high capacity and good reversibility. The presence of rGO and metallic Mo in the cycled electrode improved both the dissolution of S and Se and the sluggish conversion kinetics.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202003534-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Key Research and Development Program of China “New Energy Project for Electric Vehicle” (2016YFB0100204), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51772030, 51972030), Beijing Outstanding Young Scientists Program (BJJWZYJH01201910007023).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202003534-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202003534-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202003534-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202003534-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>H.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Xu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Lai</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Ma</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
