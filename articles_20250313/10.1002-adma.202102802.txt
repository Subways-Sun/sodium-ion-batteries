6</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Because of the large abundance of sodium (Na) as a source material and the easy fabrication of Na‐containing compounds, the sodium (Na) battery is a more environmentally friendly and sustainable technology than the lithium‐ion battery (LIB). Na‐metal batteries (SMBs) are considered promising to realize a high energy density to overtake the cost effectiveness of LIBs, which is critically important in large‐scale applications such as grid energy storage. However, the cycling stability of the Na‐metal anode faces significant challenges particularly under high cycling capacities, due to the complex failure models caused by the formation of Na dendrites. Here, a universal surface strategy, based on a self‐regulating alloy interface of the current collector, to inhibit the formation of Na dendrites is reported. High‐capacity (10 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) Na‐metal anodes can achieve stable cycling for over 1000 h with a low overpotential of 35 mV. When paired with a high‐capacity Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> cathode (7 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>), the SMB delivers an unprecedented energy density (calculated based on all the cell components) over 200 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> with flooded electrolyte, or over 230 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> with lean electrolyte. The dendrite‐free SMB also shows high cycling stability with a capacity retention per cycle over 99.9% and an ultrahigh energy efficiency of 95.8%.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202102802-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A self‐regulating alloy interface, which contains chemically sodiophilic yet electron‐hindered semiconducting Na<sub>3</sub>Sb nanoislands and a sodiophobic yet electron‐favored conductive Ni matrix, is reported. Benefiting from the unique self‐regulating mechanism and textile‐structured current collector, full cells paired with high‐capacity cathode deliver a ultrahigh energy density exceeding 200 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup> with ultrahigh energy efficiency of 95.8%.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202102802:adma202102802-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202102802-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202102802-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Grid energy storage is a critical and indispensable component for the utilization of clean and sustainable energy,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0001 #adma202102802-bib-0002 #adma202102802-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the realization of smart city, and the insurance of resilient and reliable electricity to the industry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In comparison to conventional grid storage technologies, including pumped hydro, thermal storage, and compressed air, rechargeable batteries show significant advantages in the conversion efficiency, energy density, and power management.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, batteries are easier to deploy for a wide variety of distributed and centralized applications. It is expected that the demand of battery‐based grid storage system will reach 4500 GWh in 2050.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are the most widely deployed battery technology in grid energy storage nowadays. Although LIBs fulfill the requirement of energy density for grid storage, i.e., 140–200 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the high cost of lithium, which is one of the rare elements, prohibits their large‐scale application.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the purification of Li salt in the industrial manufacture consumes massive energy and toxic chemicals. The mining process of many Li resources will unavoidably sacrifice the environmental benefits, which significantly hinders the sustainable use of Li.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In comparison, sodium (Na) batteries are considered as more cost‐effective and sustainable options for grid energy storage<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0009 #adma202102802-bib-0010"><sup>9,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> due to the high abundance and accessibility of sodium.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0011 #adma202102802-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The battery‐grade sodium‐containing compounds can be easily produced from the sea water without the need for complex purification processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The production of the cathodic and anodic materials of Na batteries is much more environmentally friendly when compared with that of LIBs.</p><p>The cost‐effectiveness of Na batteries are expected to overtake that of LIBs when the practical energy density is above 200 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To achieve this goal, it is critical to develop stable and high‐capacitive Na anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Anodes based on Na‐ion chemistry, including conversion type (metal oxide,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> metal sulfide<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0016 #adma202102802-bib-0017"><sup>16,17</sup></link><sup>]</sup>), insertion type (modified graphite,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> hard carbon,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup>), and alloy type (phosphate,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> tin,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> antimony<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) have been studied the most in the past decades. However, the energy densities of those state‐of‐the‐art prototypes of Na‐ion batteries (SIBs) reported to date are still well below the desirable target, only reaching ≈130 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0014 #adma202102802-bib-0024 #adma202102802-bib-0025 #adma202102802-bib-0026"><sup>14,24–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In recent years, Na metal is proposed as anode to build high‐energy‐density sodium‐metal batteries (SMBs) owing to the highest capacity (1166 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>) and lowest redox potential (−2.71 V vs standard hydrogen electrode) of Na.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the cycling performance of SMBs is seriously limited by the uncontrolled growth of Na dendrites,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0024 #adma202102802-bib-0027"><sup>24,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> as a result of the uneven heterogenous deposition of Na during the discharge process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0028 #adma202102802-bib-0029"><sup>28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite the development of several approaches to suppressing Na dendrites, which include employing new electrolyte chemistry,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0030 #adma202102802-bib-0031 #adma202102802-bib-0032"><sup>30–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> building artificial solid‐electrolyte interface (SEI),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and designing sodiophilic 3D host materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0034 #adma202102802-bib-0035"><sup>34,35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> SMBs nowadays still require a large excess of Na metal with an N/P ratio (area capacity ratio of negative to positive electrodes) much higher than 10 (Table <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0036 #adma202102802-bib-0037 #adma202102802-bib-0038 #adma202102802-bib-0039 #adma202102802-bib-0040"><sup>36–40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a consequence, the reported energy density of SMBs is limited to 60 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup> (Table <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information), even much lower than that of the prototype SIBs mentioned above.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For a practical SMB, the Na‐metal anode should support stable cycling at a high capacity with a controlled N/P ratio.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Up to now, it remains a significant challenge to realize a stable SMB with practical areal capacity.</p><p>Herein, we report a self‐regulating alloy interface, nickel antimony (NiSb), which enables the smooth deposition of Na metal during the discharge process. A thin layer of NiSb chemically deposited on the current collector regulates the uniform electrochemical plating of Na metal via a reactive formation of protruded Na‐metal nanoislands and a subsequent lateral connection and planarization of these islands with additional Na metal, which is different from the conventional “nuclei formation and growth” model of Na deposition on the sodiophilic substrate.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0034 #adma202102802-bib-0042 #adma202102802-bib-0043"><sup>34,42,43,</sup></link><sup>]</sup> We demonstrate that this self‐regulating mechanism enabled by the alloy interface effectively lowers the overpotential and offers dendrite‐free plating/stripping of Na metal over 1000 h at an exceedingly high areal capacity of 10 mAh cm<sup>–2</sup>. We further pair the stable Na‐metal anodes with thick cathodes made of sodium vanadium fluoride phosphate (NVPF). The full cell delivers an ultrahigh areal capacity of ≈7 mAh cm<sup>–2</sup> and reaches a remarkable energy density over 200 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup> with taking the total weight of the current collectors, electrodes, separator, and flooded electrolyte into account. Profiting from this unique feature of the smoothing deposition, the full cell could stably cycle over 230 cycles with a high capacity retention of 99.92% per cycle and ultrahigh energy efficiency of 95.8%. We further demonstrate that the energy density can overpass 230 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup> when using lean electrolyte. More importantly, we demonstrate that this self‐regulating mechanism is a universal strategy to protect other alkali‐metal anodes such as Li and K.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0030"><title type="main">Self‐Regulating Mechanism of NiSb</title><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, the self‐regulating mechanism lies on the electrochemical reaction between Na<sup>+</sup> ions and NiSb interface during plating, to form chemically sodiophilic yet electron‐hindered semiconducting Na<sub>3</sub>Sb nanoislands and the sodiophobic yet electron‐favored conductive Ni matrix.</p><figure xml:id="adma202102802-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202102802:adma202102802-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202102802-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Self‐regulating mechanism of Na metal using the alloy interface. a) Schematic illustration of the smoothing deposition by the self‐regulating mechanism. NiSb first reacts with Na ions to form the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb/Ni heterostructure. Na favorably nucleates on the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb due to the sodiophilicity. The energy barriers built at the interface of Na/Na<sub>3</sub>Sb block transportation of the electrons from the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb to Na seeds and thus hinder the growth of Na seeds. The following deposition connects Na seeds and further enables a smooth film of Na. b) TEM image with low magnification for the sodiated NiSb at 0.01 V. The areas within the red dot line correspond to Na<sub>3</sub>Sb, confirming the size of Na<sub>3</sub>Sb nanoislands is between 30 and 50 nm. c) High‐resolution TEM image of the area in the red box in (b). d) The SAED pattern of the sodiated NiSb. The circled spots in the pattern correspond to the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb. The diffuse ring near the transmission spots proves the amorphous materials, which could be ascribed to the amorphous Ni matrix around the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb after the complete sodiation of NiSb. e) SEM image for the deposited Na at the capacity of 0.05 mAh cm<sup>–2</sup>. The uniform Na seeds with the size of ≈30 nm can be observed. f) SEM image of the deposited Na at the capacity of 0.1 mAh cm<sup>–2</sup>. The size of the Na seeds does not become larger and the gaps between the Na seeds are partially filled. g) SEM image of the deposited Na at the capacity of 0.5 mAh cm<sup>–2</sup>. h) COMSOL simulation of the self‐regulating deposition process on the alloy interface. The pink, gray, and white areas represent the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb nanoislands, amorphous Ni matrix, and Na, respectively. Gradient color in electrolyte corresponds to the local concentration of Na ions. The M in the figure represents the molarity.</caption></figure><p>As a proof of concept, a thin and uniform layer of NiSb was deposited on the upper surface of the current collector made of metal foil by a galvanic displacement reaction (see the Experimental Section for the detailed description of the experiment). The NiSb‐coated current collector showed a smooth topography (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Na metal was then electrochemically plated onto the NiSb‐coated substrate by discharging to 0 V. Due to the excellent conductivity of the current collector, the whole electrode was shared with same voltage, which could guarantee the even sodiation of NiSb. During the initial stage of the electrochemical deposition, Na<sup>+</sup> ions first reacted with NiSb completely according to Equation (<link href="#adma202102802-disp-0001">1</link>),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0044 #adma202102802-bib-0045 #adma202102802-bib-0046"><sup>44–46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which formed uniform structure where islands of Na<sub>3</sub>Sb embedded in the Ni matrix (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1b</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) at the interface. The size of the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb islands was ≈20–40 nm (transmission electronic microscopy (TEM) (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1c</link>)), and the Ni matrix was amorphous according to the measurement with selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED, Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1d</link>)<displayedItem xml:id="adma202102802-disp-0001" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202102802:adma202102802-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma202102802-math-0001.png"><mrow><mtext>NiSb</mtext><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mn>3</mn><msup><mtext>Na</mtext><mo>+</mo></msup><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mn>3</mn><msup><mi mathvariant="normal">e</mi><mo>−</mo></msup><mtext> </mtext><mo>→</mo><mtext> </mtext><msub><mtext>Na</mtext><mn>3</mn></msub><mtext>Sb</mtext><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mtext>Ni</mtext></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><p>In the subsequent plating process, Na metal was preferably deposited on the surface of the Na<sub>3</sub>Sb islands due to the higher affinity of Na to Na<sub>3</sub>Sb than to Ni. These Na‐metal seeds showed a typical size of ≈30 nm, which matched well with the size of the underlying Na<sub>3</sub>Sb islands (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1e</link>). The heterojunction of Na/Na<sub>3</sub>Sb created a built‐in electric barrier blocking the electron transport from Na<sub>3</sub>Sb to Na,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0047"><sup>47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which prevented the further growth of the Na‐metal seeds (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1f</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). At this point, Na would deposit on the surrounding Ni surface, which eventually connected the isolated Na‐metal seeds and smoothened the whole surface of the deposited Na‐metal anode (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1g</link>). In comparison, depositing Na metal on the pristine or Sb‐coated current collector resulted in conventional heterogenous nucleation and growth of Na metal, such that the surfaces of as‐deposited Na metal became very rough and were rich in dendritic Na or mossy pores (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>We simulated the deposition of Na metal on the unmodified and NiSb‐coated current collector using COMSOL Multiphysics (see the Experimental Section for detailed parameters of the simulation). For the NiSb‐coated substrate, small Na<sub>3</sub>Sb domains were separated by Ni matrix in the simulation. It can be seen from the simulation results that one Na nuclei was appeared on each Na<sub>3</sub>Sb domain at the initial stage, and then Na metal was deposited on the Ni domains, which eventually formed a flat Na‐metal film (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-fig-0001">1h</link>). In sharp contrast, a Na apex first appeared on the pristine current collector and then immediately grew to a large needle (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). These simulation results are in good agreements with the experimental observations.</p><p>Such a unique self‐regulating mechanism enables the effective suppression of dendrite growth. As a proof‐of‐concept demonstration, we recorded the in situ morphological evolution of Na metal plated on the pristine current collector, NiSb‐modified current collector, and a graphite film by operando optical microscopy (Figure <link href="#adma202102802-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). As a proof of concept, we fabricated Li‐metal and K‐metal anodes on NiSb modified current collectors. The NiSb, which was deposited on the surface of the current collector, reacted with Na<sup>+</sup> ions during the plating process and formed isolated Na<sub>3</sub>Sb nanoislands and a continuous Ni matrix. Nanosized Na‐metal seeds are first deposited on the sodiophilic Na<sub>3</sub>Sb domains. The built‐in electric field of the Na/Na<sub>3</sub>Sb heterostructure hindered the subsequent growth of the Na seed. Instead, Na metal was deposited on the surface of the electron‐favored conductive Ni matrix, which connected the nanoseeds and flattened the Na‐metal layer.</p><p>We have demonstrated that applying this self‐regulating NiSb interface on the 3D CC could yield high‐capacity, dendrite‐free, and long‐cycle‐life Na‐metal anodes. Importantly, this strategy could yield stable SMBs with remarkable energy densities over 200 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup> with flooded electrolyte and over 230 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup> with lean electrolyte, when all the cell weights were taken into consideration. Note that this is the first report which reaches the milestone of 200 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup>, where Na batteries are more economical than Li batteries. We further demonstrate that this self‐regulating mechanism is a universal strategy to protect other alkali‐metal anodes. Our findings show a great leap toward the realization of high‐energy‐density and sustainable Na batteries in grid storage.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0090"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0110" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Cu‐ or Ni‐Coated Carbon Cloth (CC)/Carbon Felt (CF) and Amorphous Ni Coated CC (aNi‐CC)</title><p>All the chemicals were purchased from Sigma‐Aldrich without further purification. Commercially available hydrophobic CC (W0S1009, Taiwan CeTech Co. Ltd.) was first soaked and sonicated in a mixed solution of concentrated sulfuric acid and nitric acid (<i>V</i><sub>H2SO4</sub>/<i>V</i><sub>HNO3</sub> = 3:1) for 2 h at 80 °C and then rinsed with deionized (DI) water. After this process, the hydrophobic CC became hydrophilic. The hydrophilic CC was further coated with Ni through a PAMD process. In a typical PAMD process, the CC was first silanized by being immersed into a solution containing 4%, v/v [3‐(methacryloyloxy)propyl]trimethoxysilane in a mixed solvent (<i>V</i><sub>ethanol</sub>:<i>V</i><sub>acetic acid</sub>:<i>V</i><sub>DI water</sub> = 95:1:4) for 1 h at room temperature. After rinsing in the DI water for several times, the silanized CC was further immersed in an aqueous solution of [2‐(methacryloyloxy)ethyl]trimethyl ammonium chloride (METAC, 20 wt%). Potassium persulfate (2 g L<sup>−1</sup>) was added to initialize the polymerization of METAC at 80 °C. After 1 h, PMETAC‐coated CC was further washed with DI water and then dipped into an aqueous solution of (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>PdCl<sub>4</sub> (5 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>) in dark environment to load the catalyst on the surface. The residual [PdCl<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup> was removed by washing in the DI water. Finally, the [PdCl<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>‐loaded CC were immersed into a mixed aqueous solution of A and B (pH = 7.5) for 30 min, where solution A was comprised of NiSO<sub>4</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O (80 g L<sup>−1</sup>), sodium citrate (40 g L<sup>−1</sup>), and lactic acid (20 g L<sup>−1</sup>) and solution B was dimethylamine borane (1.5 g L<sup>−1</sup>) in water. Ni‐coated CF (CF, SQ‐2000, Kunshan ShunDuoShun Electronic Materials) was made through the same procedure as the preparation of Ni‐coated CC (Ni‐CC). For Cu deposition, solution A was an aqueous solution comprised of NaOH (12 g L<sup>−1</sup>), CuSO<sub>4</sub>·5H<sub>2</sub>O (13 g L<sup>−1</sup>), and KNaC<sub>4</sub>H<sub>4</sub>O<sub>6</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O (29 g L<sup>−1</sup>), and solution B was an aqueous solution of formaldehyde with a concentration of 9.5 mL L<sup>−1</sup>. The preparation of aNi‐CC was conducted according to a previous report.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202102802-bib-0053"><sup>53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Typically, the [PdCl<sub>4</sub>]<sup>2−</sup>‐loaded CC was immersed into a solution containing NiSO<sub>4</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O (25 g L<sup>−1</sup>), NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub> (20 g L<sup>−1</sup>), CH<sub>3</sub>COONa·3H<sub>2</sub>O (10 g L<sup>−1</sup>), and C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>Na<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>·2H<sub>2</sub>O (10 g L<sup>−1</sup>). The solution was heated to 90 °C under the stirring for 1 h.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of NiSb‐Coated CC (NiSb‐CC) and NiSb‐Coated Ni Foil (NiSb‐Ni)</title><p>The Ni‐CC was immersed into an ethanol solution containing antimony trichloride (SbCl<sub>3</sub>, 2 g L<sup>–1</sup>) and sealed in an autoclave, which was then heated to 85 °C for 4 h. The sample was then washed with ethanol three times. The NiSb‐Ni foil was prepared through the same procedure.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Sb‐Coated Ni Foil and CC(Sb‐CC)</title><p>The Sb‐coated Ni foil was prepared via thermal evaporation (JSD‐600). To prepare Sb‐CC, SbCl<sub>3</sub> (10 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>) and tetrabutylammonium tetrafluoroborate (0.1 <sc>m</sc>) were dissolved in the dimethyl sulfoxide in the Ar environment as the bath solution. And then, the Cu‐CC were immersed in the above solution for 24 h for the complete replacement of the Sb coatings.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0150" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Na‐CC, Na‐Ni‐CC, Na‐NiSb‐CC, Li‐CC, Li‐NiSb‐CC, K‐CC, and K‐NiSb‐CC</title><p>The NiSb‐CC was placed at the edge of the molten Na droplets at 300 °C and the Na could infuse into the NiSb‐CC to obtain Na‐NiSb‐CC. The Na‐CC and Na‐Ni‐CC were prepared by immersing the CC and Ni‐CC in the molten Na. The preparation of Li‐CC, Li‐NiSb‐CC, K‐CC, and K‐NiSb‐CC was following the similar procedures.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0160" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of NVPF Cathode</title><p>Stoichiometric amounts of NH<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, NaF, and NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub> (2:3:2, mole ratio) were dissolved in distilled water to form a pale‐yellow solution. An aqueous citric acid (10% wt) was dipped into the pale solution under the stirring until the color turned orange. The mixed solution was evaporated at 85 °C to form the precursor gel. The Ni‐CF were immersed into the gel to load the precursors and then dried. The Ni‐CF‐supported NVPF precursor was annealed in the Ar at 650 °C for 8 h to produce the binder‐free NVPF‐Ni‐CF cathode. The loading of the NVPF on the Ni‐CF can be adjusted by controlling the concentration of precursor gel.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202102802-sec-0170" numbered="no"><title type="main">Structural Characterization</title><p>The morphology and the energy‐dispersive X‐ray mapping of the electrode were observed by the field emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM, TESCAN MAIA3 and TESCAN VEGA3, Brno, Czech Republic).
