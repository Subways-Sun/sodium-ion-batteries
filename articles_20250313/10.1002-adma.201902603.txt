However, poor activity and instable cycling of common sodium anode materials represent a huge barrier for practical deployment. A smart design of ordered nanotube arrays of iron oxide (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) is presented as efficient sodium anode, simply enabled by surface sulfurization. The resulted heterostructure of oxide and sulfide spontaneously develops a built‐in electric field, which reduces the activation energy and accelerates charge transport significantly. Benefiting from the synergy of ordered architecture and built‐in electric field, such arrays exhibit a large reversible capacity, a superior rate capability, and a high retention of 91% up to 200 cycles at a high rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, outperforming most reported iron oxide electrodes. Furthermore, full cells based on the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> array anode and the Na<sub>0.67</sub>(Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>)O<sub>2</sub> cathode deliver a specific energy of 142 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at a power density of 330 W kg<sup>−1</sup> (based on both active electrodes), demonstrating a great potential in practical application. This material design may open a new door in engineering efficient anode based on earth‐abundant materials.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201902603-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>A smart design consisting of an Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube array</b> with a built‐in electric field is demonstrated. Benefiting from the synergy of the ordered nanoarchitecture and built‐in electric field, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> arrays exhibit highly efficient sodium storage with a large reversible capacity, a superior rate capability, and a capacity retention of 367 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> up to 200 cycles at a high rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201902603:adma201902603-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201902603-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201902603-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201902603-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>As an emerging energy storage technology, sodium–ion batteries (SIBs) have received excessive attention in recent years, due to their abundant resources, low‐cost, and appealing electrochemical performance.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0001"/> A primary challenge facing their commercial deployment is to identify efficient anode materials capable of delivering Na<sup>+</sup> ions robustly and durably.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0002"/> Among various anodes developed for sodium technology, transition metal oxides and in particular iron oxide (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) might be a potential choice for low‐cost and scale‐up battery application.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0003"/> Iron is the fourth abundant element in the earth's crust and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is a huge waste product from the steel industry. Most impressively, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> offer the promise of 1007 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> through a six‐electron reaction with Na<sup>+</sup> ion, which might result in a leap in the energy of sodium full cells. However, the storage of Na<sup>+</sup> in Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> suffers from much lower capacities and more sluggish kinetics compared to Li<sup>+</sup>. This poor activity could be directly correlated with a thicker Na<sub>2</sub>O layer formed during sodiation compared to lithiation, which impedes the further transport and insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0004"/> In addition, the substantial volume expansion upon sodiation and the low conductivity of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> also present a huge difficulty for the electrochemical stability upon Na cycling.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0005"/></p><p>To mitigate these challenges, several materials engineering strategies have been applied in the designs of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> anode and proven profitable. One general strategy to boost its activity involves the design of ultrafine Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0006"/> Nonetheless, the fabrication of fine particles ≈5 nm in size remains formidably challenging. While the incorporation of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> particles into carbon matrix is another efficient strategy, the carbon would considerably decrease the initial current efficiency.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0007"/></p><p>Recently, 3D self‐organized electrode architectures have captured tremendous attention in battery researches.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0008"/> This design offers remarkable features that conventional film electrodes may not have, such as reduced transport length, efficient electrolyte penetration, and superior structural integrity. However, to the best of our knowledge, 3D architectures of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> have not been demonstrated for Na<sup>+</sup> storage to date. On the other hand, the built‐in electric field (BEF) effect has been widely acknowledged in the modulation of battery kinetics.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0009"/> When two semiconductors, one n‐type and the other p‐type, are coupled, a BEF will generate as soon as their Fermi level reaches the balance through spontaneous migration of carriers near the interface.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0010"/> If properly designed, the BEF inside a heterostructure will boost carrier transport and electrode activity for efficient energy storage. It is noted that previous research on BEF focuses on heterostructure powder which, when used as electrode, needs the addition of binder and conductive agent. The presence of additives would disturb the BEF inside the electrode, resulting in the ambiguity in the assignment and explanation of the BEF effect.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0011"/></p><p>In this work, we initiated a material design of 3D Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube array with BEF enabled by the formation of p–n heterostructure. The construction of 3D ordered Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> architecture is implemented through electrochemical anodization of iron substrate and the p–n heterostructure is rendered by subsequent sulfurization. The resultant sulfur‐doped Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> (S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) nanotube arrays can be used as additive‐free anode, taking profit of unique nanoarchitecture, enhanced charge transport, and high Na<sup>+</sup> ion reactivity. As a consequence, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> arrays afford a reversible capacity 667 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and superior rate capability of 390 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a retention of 91% over 200 cycles. In addition, sodium full cell composed of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> anode and Na<sub>0.67</sub>(Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>)O<sub>2</sub> cathode delivers a specific energy of 142 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at a specific power of 330 W kg<sup>−1</sup> (based on both active electrodes). Considering its facile synthesis and excellent electrochemical performance, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> would be an anode of choice for SIBs.</p><p>Ordered S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotubes were prepared by a two‐step process, i.e., electrochemical anodization of Fe foil and subsequent sulfurization in sulfur vapor. This overall process is schematically presented in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a. In the first step of synthesis, a cleaned and polished Fe substrate was immersed in NH<sub>4</sub>F solution of glycol. When connected with an electric field, the growth of nanotube occurs immediately as a result of Fe<sup>2+</sup> ion dissolution into electrolyte and the diffusion of OH<sup>−</sup> and O<sup>2−</sup> ions into Fe bulk.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0012"/> This synthesis is very rapid and will finished within several minutes. After anodization, the color of Fe substrate transforms from light grey to yellow, characteristic of the formation of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O, as shown in Figure S1 in the Supporting Information. In the second step, the anodized nanotube film was annealed at 300 °C for 20 min in Ar flow with or without sulfur vapor, and accordingly denoted as S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, respectively. A distinct color change to black for S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> indicates the formation of Fe<sup>2+</sup> species caused by sulfur treatment.</p><figure xml:id="adma201902603-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201902603:adma201902603-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201902603-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Synthesis and morphology of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube array. a) Schematic of synthesizing S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. SEM images of b) Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and c) S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> show ordered nanotube structure. Insets are magnified pictures. d) SEM section view and e) high‐resolution TEM images of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. f) TEM image and element mappings of Fe, O, and S of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> by EDS.</caption></figure><p>Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) were employed to characterize the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube samples (Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0001"/>b–e). Both as‐anodized and sulfurized Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> samples show self‐ordered nanotube architecture, with an inner diameter of 70 nm and a wall thickness of ≈13 nm. The average length of nanotube is ≈1.4 µm and the mass loading is about 0.4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Their similar SEM profiles confirm that the thermal annealing has a negligible effect on the shape and order of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotubes. Naturally, increasing the annealing temperature and duration would convert more oxide to sulfide and ultimately destroys the ordered nanotube architecture. The heterostructure of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is evidently revealed by high‐resolution TEM imaging. The interplanar spacing of 0.252 nm corresponds to the (110) plane of hematite Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>,<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0013"/> while the spacings of 0.270 and 0.221 nm correlate to the (200) and (211) planes of FeS<sub>2</sub>, respectively.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0014"/> As Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is a well‐recognized n‐type semiconductor while FeS<sub>2</sub> is a p‐type one, their intimate contact would develop a BEF in the interphase zone spontaneously. In addition, X‐ray energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) in Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0001"/>f shows the contours of Fe and O well matching that of S, suggesting the presence of heterostructure throughout the nanotubes. The amount of sulfur in S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> was estimated to be ≈10% of that of oxygen, as analyzed by EDS and inductive coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy.</p><p>The structure of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube samples was probed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and Raman spectroscopy. The as‐anodized Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotubes appear amorphous and crystalize into α‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> hematite (powder diffraction file (PDF) No. 33‐0664) after thermal annealing in Ar atmosphere (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). In contrast, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is composed of trace FeS<sub>2</sub> (PDF No. 42‐1340) phase in addition to the primary phase of hematite. The segregation of FeS<sub>2</sub> phase might be due to the rapid accumulation of sulfur species on the nanotube surface rather than diffusion into bulk because sulfurization is a kinetically controlled process.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0008" style="qv">8b</link> In the Raman spectra shown in Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0002"/>b, the scattering bands at 228, 297, and 407 cm<sup>−1</sup> are characteristic of hematite, while the bands at around 670 cm<sup>−1</sup> reflects the existence of Fe<sup>2+</sup> species caused by the oxygen‐deficient condition.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0015"/> Specifically, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> reveals prominent bands at 332, 376, and 418 cm<sup>−1</sup>, again confirming the presence of FeS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0014"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201902603-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201902603:adma201902603-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201902603-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structure and chemical composition of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube array. a) XRD patterns and b) Raman spectra of Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube arrays. Core‐level XPS spectra of c) Fe 2p, d) O 1s, and e) S 2p for S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. No S 2p signal is detected in Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. f) Mott−Schottky plots for Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> derived from impedance measurements.</caption></figure><p>To probe the chemical state and constitution of nanoarrays, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Figure S2, Supporting Information) was recorded. Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0002"/>c–e displays the core‐level spectra of Fe 2p, O 1s, and S 2p of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, respectively. The main part of Fe 2p spectrum can be deconvoluted into two doublets (2p<sub>1/2</sub> and 2p<sub>3/2</sub>) located at 726.4/724.7 and 712.3/711.0 eV, correspond to Fe<sup>3+</sup> and Fe<sup>2+</sup>, respectively. A similar phenomenon is also for Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> (Figure S3, Supporting Information), due to the annealing in Ar flow. Another prominent peak at 707.5 eV that is absent for Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> can be assigned to Fe–S. Accordingly, the O 1s spectrum consists of three peaks at 533.8, 531.9, and 530.3 eV, which can be assigned to defect oxygen, O–S, and O–Fe, respectively.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0013"/> Meanwhile, the S 2p spectrum shows dominant S–Fe and S–O doublets without peaks due to elemental S, signifying the complete removal of sulfur by CS<sub>2</sub> soaking. A weak doublet at 164.8/163.4 eV might be attributed to S–Fe–O, as a direct result of partial substitution of oxygen by sulfur. To validate the idea of p–n heterostructure, we conducted the electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) test in the dark to examine the semiconductor type of array products. The Mott–Schottky plot is presented in Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0002"/>f. The “Λ” shape in the plot of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with both positive and negative slopes signals that p‐ and n‐type semiconductors coexist in S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, corroborating the formation of a p–n heterojunction.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0016"/> In contrast, Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> only shows one positive slope in the plot, indicative of a pure n‐type semiconductor.</p><p>Electrochemical Na<sup>+</sup> ion storage in S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube arrays was investigated in 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> diglyme solution using metallic sodium as the counter electrode. Galvanostatic test and cyclic voltammetry (CV) results are presented in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>. In the potential range of 0.1–2.5 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na), S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> affords an initial reversible capacity 667 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current rate of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, superior to Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> powder (Figure S4, Supporting Information). Remarkably, unlike most conversion anodes with a large irreversible loss, the purposely designed S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> electrode exhibits a record‐high initial Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 83%. Such a high CE may eliminate the necessity of presodiation, and thus is significant for practical application.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0017"/> In the first cycle of CV profile (Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0003"/>b), a major cathodic peak at ≈0.9 V represents the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into surface FeS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0018"/> while the minor peak at 0.7 V might be attributed to the conversion from Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>FeS<sub>2</sub> to Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>S, accompanying with the formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI). The following cathodic peaks below 0.5 V reflect the conversion of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>O.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0007" style="qv">7a</link> In the reverse process, two anodic peaks at 1.1 and 1.3 V can be assigned to the reoxidation of Fe to Fe<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>3+</sup> species. Notably, the cathodic peak at ≈0.9 V disappears in the following cycles, as a result of loss of structural ordering of FeS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0018" style="qv">18a</link> Besides a high capacity and CE, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> demonstrates a superiority cycling. A capacity of 511 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> or a retention of ≈99% is achieved upon 50 continuous cycles at a rate of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, in contrast to Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with a low capacity retention of 94 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="adma201902603-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201902603:adma201902603-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201902603-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical Na<sup>+</sup> storage characterization of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube array. a) Voltage–capacity and b) CV profiles of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> upon initial cycles. c) Rate performance comparison of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Capacity values for desodiation process are adopted. d) Long‐term cycling of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at a fixed rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. e) CV curves of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> at various scan rates. f) Rate capability comparison of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with other iron oxide electrodes for Na<sup>+</sup> storage.</caption></figure><p>The superior activity is exemplified by kinetic test carried out at various currents. As Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0003"/>c and Figure S5 in the Supporting Information present, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> affords capacities of 641, 580, 528, 489, 455, and 390 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at rates of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Under identical condition, Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> delivers consistently lower capacities. More importantly, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> demonstrates a long‐term cyclability at a high current of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> by retaining 367 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (91% retention) over 200 cycles, which is among the best results reported for iron oxides (Table S1, Supporting Information). It is speculated that the robust electrical contact with substrate and rich space allows the relaxation of accumulated stress and strain, significantly enhancing the Na cycling. In addition, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> was subject to CV at scan rates ranging from 0.1 to 2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The similar profiles under various rates as well as a liner relationship between the peak current and the rate (Figure S6, Supporting Information) suggest a somehow surface‐capacitive rather than diffusion‐controlled behavior.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0017" style="qv">17a</link> This unique character might be the key to the kinetic superiority of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> versus other iron based electrodes.</p><p>In order to understand the structural evolution of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> upon Na<sup>+</sup> storage, XRD measurement was carried out at various (de)sodiation stages of the first cycle (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a,b). Upon initial discharge (sodiation) to 1.0 V, a weak peak at 31.9° appears, characteristic of NaFeS<sub>2</sub>, which was further reduced to Na<sub>2</sub>S upon discharge to 0.5 V.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0018" style="qv">18b</link> Peaks due to Na<sub>2</sub>O (PDF No. 12‐0288) appear at the end of discharge, confirming the conversion reaction. In the inverse process, the peaks of Na<sub>2</sub>S and Na<sub>2</sub>O gradually vanish, while the peaks from NaFeS<sub>2</sub> and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> reappear, in consistent with previous reports.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0006 #adma201902603-bib-0007" style="qv">6a,7c</link> Note that the Na‐driven phase amorphization and the dense coverage of SEI membrane might prevent from the precise analysis of structural evolution of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0005"/> A similar conclusion can be drawn for the structural probing of sulfurized Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> powder electrode (Figure S7, Supporting Information). Therefore, we further implemented Raman characterization to unveil the reaction process (Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0004"/>c). Most prominently, the scattering peaks at 395 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>) and 462 cm<sup>−1</sup> (FeO) gradually vanish upon sodiation and only partially recover upon subsequent desodiation.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0006" style="qv">6a</link> This result again suggests a significant amorphization of the original crystalline structure. To explain such a high activity of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, a BEF‐related mechanism is tentatively developed and schematically described in Figure <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0004"/>d. As Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is an n‐type semiconductor while FeS<sub>2</sub> (or FeO<i><sub>x</sub></i>S<i><sub>y</sub></i>) is a p‐type one, a BEF will be spontaneously developed in the heterogeneous interface of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and FeS<sub>2</sub>. To validate the concept of BEF, we carried out Kelvin probe force microscopy measurement on a purposely designed planar Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>/FeS<sub>2</sub> heterojunction. The result clearly verifies the establishment of an electric field (potential difference) across the heterointerface of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and FeS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S8, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201902603-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201902603:adma201902603-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201902603-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Mechanism of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube array upon electrochemical Na<sup>+</sup> storage. a) Galvanostatic curves, b) XRD patterns, and c) Raman spectra collected at the indicated stages during the initial cycle. The electrode was charged and discharged at a current rate of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> between 0.1 and 2.5 V. d) Schematic of the formation of BEF and Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism. Upon discharge, a BEF is spontaneously developed in the interphase of n‐type Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and p‐type FeS<sub>2</sub>. On recharge, FeS<sub>2</sub> domain with more Na<sup>+</sup> ions is a relative n‐type semiconductor to Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> with less Na<sup>+</sup> ions. Again this forms a new BEF that facilitates the transport of ions.</caption></figure><p>Upon discharge (sodiation), the BEF that has an n→p direction will push Na<sup>+</sup> ions from Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> to neighboring FeS<sub>2</sub>, leading to enhanced transport kinetics.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0011" style="qv">11b</link> As for recharge, desodiation preferentially initiates in the Na<sub>2</sub>S zone due to its high reactivity compared with Na<sub>2</sub>O. This situation will give rise to relatively Na<sup>+</sup>‐rich FeS<sub>2</sub> domains and Na<sup>+</sup>‐deficient Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> domains, developing a new BEF with a direction from FeS<sub>2</sub> to Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> due to their the potential difference.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0009 #adma201902603-bib-0011" style="qv">9,11a</link> As a result, Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics can be boosted for S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> in both sodiation and desodiation states. This assumption can be corroborated by the EIS spectra and the measurement of the apparent chemical diffusion coefficients of lithium (<i>D</i><sub>Li</sub>) at sodiation and desodiation states,<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0019"/> as shown in Figure S9 in the Supporting Information. In both states, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> exhibits smaller charge‐transfer resistances and consistently much greater <i>D</i><sub>Li</sub> values than Ar‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. These results unambiguously explain the excellent sodium performance of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotubes. Furthermore, we compare the electrochemical performance of the powder electrodes of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and the mixture of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> and sulfurized Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. The results again confirm the validation of BEF in modulation of electrode activity (Figure S10, Supporting Information).</p><p>To meet practical requirement, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>//Na<sub>0.67</sub>(Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>)O<sub>2</sub> full cells were assembled to evaluate the compatibility of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> electrodes in battery. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201902603-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>. The full cell affords a reversible capacity of ≈500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (on the basis of anode mass) and an average voltage of 2.5 V, leading to a specific energy of 160 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> (based on both active materials) at a power density of 60 W kg<sup>−1</sup>. The energy still reaches 142 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> under a specific power of 330 W kg<sup>−1</sup>, which is comparable to that of commercial LiFePO<sub>4</sub> batteries and outperforms many recently reported sodium systems.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0020"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201902603-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201902603:adma201902603-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201902603-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Full cell of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>//Na<sub>0.67</sub>(Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>)O<sub>2</sub>. a) Schematic of coin‐type full cell. Initial galvanostatic profiles of b) Na<sub>0.67</sub>(Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>)O<sub>2</sub> cathode at a rate of 35 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and c) S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>//Na<sub>0.67</sub>(Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>)O<sub>2</sub> full cell at a rate of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> (based on the anode mass). d) Cycling performance of full cell at various current rates. e) Photograph of a charged full cell lighting light‐emitting diode series.</caption></figure><p>To summarize, we have explored a heteroarchitecture design of S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanotube as a highly efficient additive‐free Na anode. The 3D nanotube array renders a highway for rapid carrier transport, a large accessible area for electrochemical reaction, and sufficient space for volume swelling. Meanwhile, the heterostructure spontaneously develops an electric field in the interphase zone that drastically accelerates the transport of charge carriers. Benefiting from the dual strategies, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> exhibits a highly active and efficient Na<sup>+</sup> storage, outperforming most iron‐oxide based electrodes. More critically, S‐Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> exhibits a high initial current efficiency of 83%, which is a bottleneck breakthrough of most conversion anodes.<link href="#adma201902603-bib-0021"/> We argue that the synergetic strategy might enable design of earth‐abundant sodium electrode materials with unprecedented activity and efficiency. Such a design also serves as a perfect plateau to explore the underlying correlation of BEF and electrochemical performance of electrodes.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201902603-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge the financial support of the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 51872192, 51772197, and 51672182), the Thousand Young Talents Plan, the Jiangsu Natural Science Foundation (Grant No. BK20180002), the Key University Science Research Project of Jiangsu Province (Grant Nos. 19KJA170001, 17KJA430013), the 333 High‐Level Talents Project in Jiangsu Province, the Six Talent Peaks Project in Jiangsu Province, and of the Priority Academic Program Development (PAPD) of Jiangsu Higher Education Institutions.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201902603-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma201902603-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma201902603-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma201902603-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>N.</givenNames> <familyName>Yabuuchi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Kubota</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Dahbi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Komaba</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
