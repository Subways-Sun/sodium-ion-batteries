A lack of efficient anode materials with high reversible capacity is one main challenge facing the development of SIBs. Antimony‐ and bismuth‐based chalcogenides materials can store large amounts of Na<sup>+</sup> ions, owing to the alloying/dealloying reaction mechanism within a low potential range, and thus, are regarded as promising anodes for SIBs. However, these materials face great challenges of poor ion diffusion rate, multiple phase transformations, and severe morphology pulverization. Herein, recent developments in antimony‐ and bismuth‐based chalcogenides materials, mainly rational structural design strategies used and the electrochemical reaction mechanisms involved, are summarized. Perspectives for further improving antimony‐ and bismuth‐based chalcogenides anodes are also provided.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Storage space</b>: Owing to high theoretical capacities and volumetric energy densities, antimony and bismuth‐based chalcogenides are regarded as promising anodes for sodium‐ion batteries (see figure). In this review, recent advances in this field are summarized from multiple perspectives, including material design strategies and sodium‐storage mechanisms.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900784-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0001"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Currently, various energy‐storage devices, including lithium‐, sodium‐, and potassium‐ion batteries;<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0001 #asia201900784-bib-0002 #asia201900784-bib-0003"/> lithium–sulfur batteries;<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0004"/> metal–air batteries;<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0005 #asia201900784-bib-0006 #asia201900784-bib-0007"/> lithium‐metal batteries;<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0008"/> as well as zinc‐<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0009 #asia201900784-bib-0010"/> magnesium‐,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0011"/> calcium‐,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0012"/> and aluminum‐ion batteries,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0013"/> have been developed to store sustainable energy, namely, wind power, solar energy, tidal power, and so forth. Among these devices, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have achieved great commercial success.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0014 #asia201900784-bib-0015"/> However, limited lithium resources hinder the development of LIBs for large‐scale applications.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0016"/> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have recently attracted great attention, owing to the abundance of sodium resources and similar operating mechanism to that of LIBs.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0017 #asia201900784-bib-0018"/></p><p>One of the critical challenges in developing suitable anode materials for SIBs results from the large ionic radius of the Na<sup>+</sup> ion. To date, commonly used materials include carbon‐based compounds,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0019 #asia201900784-bib-0020"/> oxides and sulfides,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0021 #asia201900784-bib-0022 #asia201900784-bib-0023 #asia201900784-bib-0024 #asia201900784-bib-0025"/> alloy metals,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0026"/> and organic compounds;<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0027"/> however, most of these materials can barely achieve a reversible capacity of more than 500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Antimony and bismuth chalcogenides have high theoretical capacities (e.g., 946 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, 625 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>) and show high reversible capacities.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0028 #asia201900784-bib-0029 #asia201900784-bib-0030"/> Most importantly, they also exhibit ultrahigh volumetric capacities, owing to a high density. However, most bismuth and antimony chalcogenide based anodes suffer from huge volume changes and sluggish reaction kinetics during sodiation/desodiation processes.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0031"/> To alleviate these problems, several strategies, including morphological control, layer coating, employing conductive substrates, and building heterostructures, have been developed.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0024"/></p><p>In the past few years, some reviews have summarized antimony‐ and bismuth‐based anode materials for energy storage.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0028 #asia201900784-bib-0029 #asia201900784-bib-0032"/> However, most of them focused on antimony and bismuth metals and their oxides. They rarely deal with selenides, especially tellurides. In addition, no specific review has yet focused on the sodium storage mechanism, structural design, and sodium storage properties of antimony and bismuth chalcogenide anodes for SIBs. In particular, some important studies over the past two years have not yet been summarized. Herein, we present a minireview of antimony and bismuth chalcogenide anodes for SIBs, covering electrochemical reaction mechanisms, rational structural design strategies, and the most recent advances. The aim of this review is to provide a comprehensive insight into this field.</p></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0002"><title type="main">Material Design Strategy</title><p>In recent years, antimony and bismuth chalcogenides with various morphologies and structures have been reported. Based on strategies used to enhance electrochemical performance, these studies can be divided into four categories: nanocrystallization, layer coating, employing conductive substrates, and building heterostructures. In this section, we discuss these four typical strategies.</p><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0003"><title type="main">Morphology Control</title><p>Owing to ultrasmall dimensions, nanocrystalline materials have several advantages over their bulk counterparts.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0033"/> This can significantly improve the energy efficiency of the electrode material because the induced large surface area can provide increased adsorption sites and shorten the ion diffusion pathway. This is one of the most important factors in determining the power characteristics of a battery. Moreover, nanocrystalline materials can stand larger expansion stresses during intercalation/deintercalation of alkali ions.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0034"/></p><p>To enhance the electrochemical performance of bismuth and antimony chalcogenides, different morphologies have been obtained. For example, Manthiram and Zhao solvothermally prepared amorphous Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanowires (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0001"/> b);<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0035"/> crystal growth along the [001] direction was confirmed by means of TEM (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0001"/> c). Kim and co‐workers employed a Li<sup>+</sup>‐ion intercalation and exfoliation method to obtain Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanosheets (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0001"/> d),<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0036"/> with only 3–4 unit cells of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> across the [010] plane stack to form nanosheets, as confirmed by means of tapping‐mode AFM (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0001"/> e and f). Low‐dimensional bismuth and antimony chalcogenides can be assembled into 3D morphology. Liu et al. hydrothermally synthesized chrysanthemum‐like Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> that consisted of many crystalline Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanowires (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0001"/> g–i).<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0037"/> Such 3D morphology is beneficial for contact with electrolyte to allow ion diffusion.</p><figure xml:id="asia201900784-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900784-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Atomic arrangement of the orthorhombic Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> crystal. b) SEM and high‐resolution (HR) TEM images of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0035"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2015, Royal Society of Chemistry.) d) High‐magnification SEM image, e) AFM image, and f) the corresponding height profiles of the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanosheet.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0036"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2018, Wiley.) g) SEM image, h) TEM image, and HRTEM image of chrysanthemum‐like Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0037"/> (Reproduced with permission, Copyright 2018, Wiley.)</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0004"><title type="main">Coating</title><p>To enhance the conductivity and structural stability of bismuth and antimony chalcogenide anodes, amorphous carbon and conductive polymers, such as polypyrrole (PPy), polyethylene oxide (PEO), and poly(3,4‐ethylenedioxythiophene):poly(styrene sulfonate) (PEDOT:PSS), are usually employed as coating materials. The group of Huang fabricated Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@C hollow spheres with glucose as the carbon source in an in situ manner under hydrothermal conditions (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0002"/> a and b).<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0038"/> A thin amorphous carbon layer, with a thickness of about 5 nm, was found to be uniformly distributed on the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> hollow spheres in Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0002"/> c. Because of the carbon layer and hollow structure, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@C hollow spheres showed better cycling stability than that of pure Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> spheres. Owing to advantages of high electronic conductivity, flexibility, and facile synthesis, conductive polymers have been regarded as ideal additives for electrodes. Lou et al. synthesized a clip‐like PPy‐coated Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> composite through a ZnSe(DETA)<sub>0.5</sub> microbelt‐templated method by using the ion‐exchange technique,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0039"/> as illustrated in Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0002"/> d–g. The clip skeleton had a diameter of about 60 nm and was coated with a thin PPy layer. The coating was found to improve the electrochemical performance of the designed materials.</p><figure xml:id="asia201900784-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900784-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Low‐ and b) high‐magnification SEM images and c) a HRTEM image of hollow sphere Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0038"/> (Reproduced with permission, Copyright 2018, Elsevier.) d) Schematic representations to illustrate the structural evolution process of a ZnSe(DETA)<sub>0.5</sub> microbelt (DETA=diethylenetriamine) to a Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> microclip. e) TEM image of ZnSe(DETA)<sub>0.5</sub>. f) SEM image and g) HRTEM image of Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub>@PPy microclips.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0039"/> (Reproduced with permission, Copyright 2018, Wiley.)</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0005"><title type="main">Employing Conductive Substrates</title><p>Growing active materials on conductive supports is another common route to fabricate high‐performance metal chalcogenide electrodes. Carbonaceous materials have widely been used, for example, graphene, carbon nanotubes, carbon nanofibers, and carbon cloth. Generally, there are some advantages to the use of conductive supports. First, the existence of these supports may change the crystal growth kinetics of active materials, in some cases. This probably occurs because the support materials often have a nonpolar body with some polar surface functional groups, for example, graphene oxides. Second, a conductive substrate can serve as a highway to rapidly transport electrons to active material. Lastly, binder‐free electrodes can be fabricated if the substrate has sufficient mechanical strength and flexibility.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0044"/></p><p>The active material can be ex situ or in situ bonded onto the substrate. For example, Liu and co‐workers prepared Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/sulfur‐doped graphene sheets (Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/SGS) by means of ex situ bonding of commercial Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> particles on SGS in an aqueous mixture of H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0003"/> a and b).<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0040"/> A study by the group of Xu is a good example of crystal growth kinetics being changed by the substrate.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0041"/> As mentioned above, generally, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and other antimony chalcogenides grow with a 1D morphology. However, Xu and co‐workers prepared a Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticle/rGO composite (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0003"/> c and d). The reason why the size of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> decreased was mainly attributed to the incorporated graphene sheets suppressing stacking of the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanocrystals formed. Nevertheless, the presence of rGO did not suppress this stacking for Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods prepared by means of a hydrothermal method, reported by Li and Zhao,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0042"/> as shown in Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0003"/> e and f. Pan et al. prepared a Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MWCNTs composite through the reaction of S<sup>2−</sup> with Sb<sup>3+</sup> cations, which were attracted to the negatively charged groups on functional MWCNTs (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0003"/> g and h).<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0043"/></p><figure xml:id="asia201900784-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900784-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) SEM and b) TEM images of the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/SGS composite.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0040"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2016, American Chemical Society.) c) SEM and d) TEM images of the as‐prepared Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/reduced graphene oxide (rGO) composite.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0041"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2017, Elsevier.) e) SEM and f) TEM images of a Sb<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>3</sub> rods/rGO composite.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0042"/> g) SEM and H) TEM images of a Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/multiwalled carbon nanotube (MWCNT) composite.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0043"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2017, Elsevier.)</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0006"><title type="main">Building Heterostructures</title><p>Owing to unique interface effects, building heterostructures has been regarded as a promising anode design technique. An internal electric field can be constructed at heterointerfaces to enhance the surface reaction kinetics and facilitate charge transport by creating a heterostructure through coupling crystals with different bands gaps.</p><p>To improve ion diffusion kinetics and boost electronic conductivity, the group of Zhang reported a core–shell structure with a Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> core and MoS<sub>2</sub> shell prepared through a two‐step hydrothermal method.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0045"/> The MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, with a wrinkled structure, are coated on the surface of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0004"/> a–c). Such a Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructure showed a better electrochemical performance than those of both pure Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and pure MoS<sub>2</sub>. Yin and co‐workers used zeolitic imidazolate framework‐8 (ZIF‐8) as a precursor to obtain ZnS@resorcinol‐formaldehyde (ZnS@RF) composite, and further converted it into the ZnS−Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@C core–shell heterostructure through an ion‐exchange reaction and annealing process,<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0046"/> as displayed in Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0004"/> e–h.</p><figure xml:id="asia201900784-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900784-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) SEM images of Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods. b) SEM image, c) TEM image, and d) HRTEM image of the Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructure.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0045"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2018, IOP Publishing.) e)–f) TEM images, g) HRTEM lattice image, and h) line scanning curves and elemental mapping of the ZnS−Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@C core–shell heterostructure.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0046"/> (Reproduced with permission; copyright 2017, American Chemical Society.)</p></caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0007"><title type="main">Sodium Storage Mechanism</title><p>The sodium storage reactions of bismuth and antimony chalcogenides mainly involve two steps: conversion and alloying. Different from intercalation‐type electrodes, the crystal phase conversion and structural evolution take place during both steps. Thus, bismuth and antimony chalcogenide electrodes always suffer from large volume expansions, leading to an unsatisfactory cycling life.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0028"/></p><p>Generally, for bismuth and antimony chalcogenides, the possible sodium storage reaction can be represented by Equations <link href="#asia201900784-disp-0001 #asia201900784-disp-0002"/>:<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0028 #asia201900784-bib-0029"/><displayedItem xml:id="asia201900784-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/asia201900784-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-math-0001"/></displayedItem><displayedItem xml:id="asia201900784-disp-0002" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/asia201900784-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-math-0002"/></displayedItem></p><p>Sodium storage in each step can offer a high capacity. Compared with the conversion step, the alloy step occurs at a lower potential range.</p><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0008"><title type="main">Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub></title><p>Sun et al. utilized Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorods to study the sodium storage mechanism by means of cyclic voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic tests, and ex situ XRD measurements.<link href="#asia201900784-bib-0047"/> Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0005"/> a shows CV cycles over five scans, with a range of oxidation and reduction peaks. The cathodic peak located at about 1.15 V originates from the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> structure to form Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>. The cathodic peak present at 0.94 V is due to the conversion reaction, with the formation of both metallic Bi and Na<sub>2</sub>S. The peaks at 0.48 and 0.24 V correspond to the reaction between sodium and metallic bismuth. In the anodic sweep, the oxidation peaks at 0.7 and 0.8 V correspond to the reversible desodiation reaction of Na−Bi phases; the peak at 1.75 V is due to the formation of NaBiS<sub>2</sub>. The specific capacity fades rapidly after the first initial scan; this is assumed to be induced by huge volume changes during the sodiation/desodiation reaction (Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0005"/> b). The ex situ charge/discharge XRD analysis shown in Figure <link href="#asia201900784-fig-0005"/> c demonstrates that diffraction peaks corresponding to Bi and NaBiS<sub>2</sub> appear upon discharging to 1.1 V (stage C), which match well with previous CV analysis that the crystal structure of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> undergoes a morphological deterioration through a conversion reaction on the electrode surface.</p><figure xml:id="asia201900784-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900784:asia201900784-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900784-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) CV curves of the cell with a Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorod electrode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> within a potential range of 0.001–3.0 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). b) Charge/discharge voltage profiles of a Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanorod full cell at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Ex situ XRD patterns of the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode collected at various points, as indicated in the first discharge–charge profile: A) fresh electrode, B) after discharge to 1.2 V, C) after discharge to 1.1 V, D) after discharge to 0.8 V, E) after discharge to 0.4 V, F) after discharge to 0.005 V, G) after charge to 0.8 V, H) after charge to 1.8 V, and I) after charge to 3.0 V. Some strategies to enhance the electrochemical performance of these materials include morphology control, coating techniques, employing conductive substrates, and building heterostructures. In detail, nanocrystallization can shorten the ion‐diffusion distance in electrode materials to enhance the kinetics. Layer coating and employing conductive substrates not only improves the conductivity of composites, but also buffers volume changes during sodiation/desodiation. Additionally, through the inner electric field, the antimony and bismuth chalcogenide based heterostructures have high electronic conductivity. Some recent studies have used more than one strategy to cooperatively enhance the sodium‐storage properties.</p><p>Nevertheless, it should be noted that these strategies also have disadvantages. The nanocrystal has a large surface area, which leads to more side reactions. The introduction of a low‐density coating layer and substrates will weaken the advantages of a high volumetric capacity for antimony and bismuth chalcogenides. Also, significant gaps in understanding of the mechanism for related heterostructures still remain. Thus, greater efforts should be focused on the following two main research directions: 1) modifying the surface of antimony and bismuth chalcogenides nanocrystals with an artificial SEI film to reduce side reactions, and 2) further research into the operating mechanism for related heterostructures through advanced characterization technology and theoretical investigations.</p></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Natural Science Foundation of Hunan Province (grant no. 2017JJ1008) and the Key Research and Development Program of Hunan Province of China (grant no. 2018GK2031).</p></section><section xml:id="asia201900784-sec-0021" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="asia201900784-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="asia201900784-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Huang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Wei</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Liao</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>W.</givenNames> <familyName>Ni</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>C.</givenNames> <familyName>Wang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Ma</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>J.
