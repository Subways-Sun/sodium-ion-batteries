type="main">Abstract</title><p>Rechargeable sodium‐metal batteries (RSMBs) with high energy density and low cost are attracting extensive attention as promising energy‐storage technologies. However, the poor cyclability and safety issues caused by unstable solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) structure and dendrite issues limit their practical application. Herein, it is theoretically predicted that constructing the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure with high work function can lower the Fermi energy level, and therefore effectively suppressing continuous electrolyte decomposition derived from the electron‐tunneling effect after long‐term sodiation process. Furthermore, the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure on 3D porous nickel foam (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF) is experimentally fabricated as an advanced Na‐anode current collector. The seamless Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure not only offers abundant active sites to induce uniform Na<sup>+</sup> deposition and enhance ion‐transport kinetics, but also facilitates the formation of stable SEI for dendrite‐free sodium anode, which are confirmed by cryogenic components transmission electron microscopy tests and in situ spectroscopy characterization. As a result, the Na‐composite anode (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na) delivers stable plating/stripping process of 5000 h and high average Coulombic efficiency of 99.7% over 2500 cycles. More impressively, the assembled sodium‐ion full cell displays ultralong cycle life of 10 000 cycles at 20 C. The strategy of stabilizing the sodium‐metal anode gives fundamental insight into the potential construction of advanced RSMBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202210826-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Based on theoretical simulation, the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure on 3D porous nickel foam (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF) with high work function is developed as a current collector for dendrite‐free sodium‐metal anodes. The excellent electrochemical performance of Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na can be attributed to enhanced sodiophilicity, fast ion‐transport kinetics, and uniform solid‐electrolyte xml:id="adma202210826-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202210826-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>High‐energy rechargeable batteries built with inexpensive sodium (Na)‐metal anodes are widely considered as potential successors to lithium batteries for renewable energy markets, on account of high theoretical capacity of metallic Na (1166 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and abundant Na resources in the earth's crust (2.3 wt%).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0001 #adma202210826-bib-0002 #adma202210826-bib-0003 #adma202210826-bib-0004 #adma202210826-bib-0005 #adma202210826-bib-0006"><sup>1–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the practical utilization of sodium‐metal batteries is impeded by their low Columbic efficiency (CE) and poor lifespan, which can be attributed to the following intractable issues: i) the uncontrollable Na deposition behavior on the current collector would cause severe dendrite growth, and further triggering separator pierce and serious short circuit;<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0007 #adma202210826-bib-0008"><sup>7,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> ii) infinite volume expansion of Na‐metal anode during the plating/stripping process would damage solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) and cause continuous side reactions;<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0009 #adma202210826-bib-0010"><sup>9,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and iii) uneven charge accumulation and blocked ion transport would lead to the loss of active metallic Na and production of inactive “dead sodium.”<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0011 #adma202210826-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is very urgent to adopt effective strategies for developing stable sodium‐metal anodes.</p><p>Considerable efforts in terms of constructing conductive scaffold as current collector, optimizing electrolyte components, engineering functional separator, and establishing artificial SEI have recently been devoted to solving the above issues. Among them, construction of current collector skeletons (i.e., 3D copper current collector,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> graphene scaffolds,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0014 #adma202210826-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon cloth,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na‐alloy architecture,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and coherent lattice<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) has been proposed as one of the most effective strategies to inhibit dramatically volumetric change and induce uniform current accumulation during the Na plating/stripping process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0018 #adma202210826-bib-0019 #adma202210826-bib-0020"><sup>18–20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Even so, the electrochemical performance of sodium‐metal batteries, especially the long cyclability at high current density, is still unsatisfactory because of the lack of effective regulation on SEI structure and ion‐transport kinetics of current collector during the repeated sodiation cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In general, continuous electron transfer from the current collector to electrolytes would cause the unremitting decomposition of electrolyte and uncontrollable growth of SEI layer on the current collector surface, thereby leading to uneven Na nucleation/growth.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0022 #adma202210826-bib-0023 #adma202210826-bib-0024"><sup>22–24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this regard, we consider it will be highly challenging but very profitable to optimize the current collector surface at the molecular level to induce the formation of stable SEI structure and uniform Na<sup>+</sup> deposition behavior, which may significantly impact the long‐term cycle stability of sodium‐metal batteries.</p><p>Fundamentally, an ideal SEI layer should be dense and thick enough for blocking continuous electrolyte decomposition rooted in the electron tunneling effect which is closely related to the Fermi level (<i>E</i><sub>f</sub>) of current collector surface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0025 #adma202210826-bib-0026"><sup>25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It should be noted that the <i>E</i><sub>f</sub> is the difference between the work function (<i>W</i><sub>f</sub>) representing the minimum energy required for electrons to escape from the <i>E</i><sub>f</sub> into the vacuum and the vacuum level (<i>E</i><sub>vacuum</sub> = 0 eV).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, rational molecular and electronic structure design to lift the work function of the current collector is capable of lowering the Fermi level and terminating the interfacial electronic tunneling, eventually achieving a stable SEI construction and corrosion inhibition of electrode for dendrite‐free sodium‐metal batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> With 3D porous conductive skeleton structure, the nickel foam is identified as potential candidate for pre‐storing metallic sodium.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0029 #adma202210826-bib-0030"><sup>29,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On top of that, the nickel foam derived sulfide (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>) and phosphide (Ni<sub>3</sub>P) possess higher work function compared to other nickel compounds, which are beneficial to modifying the SEI structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0031 #adma202210826-bib-0032"><sup>31,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, compared with individual components, the heterostructures with unique interfacial electronic structures may be able to more effectively modulate the interfacial electron tunneling. In consequence, it is desirable to design the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure on nickel foam to optimize the Fermi level of the current collector surface for realizing stable SEI structure and dendrite‐free Na deposition.</p><p>In this work, we theoretically predict that the 3D current collector with Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure possesses higher work function than individual components after the repeated sodiation process, which can lower the Fermi level of current collector surface and further restrain continuous electrolyte decomposition originating from the electron tunneling. Based on the theoretical calculation results, we design and synthesize the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure on porous nickel foam (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF) as 3D current collector scaffold for high‐efficiency and dendrite‐free Na‐metal anodes. The enhanced Na<sup>+</sup>‐transport kinetics and uniform ion flow of the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF are demonstrated via the finite element simulation (FES). And the highly reversible Na plating/stripping process and the fine structure and composition of uniform SEI are revealed by in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD), the cryogenic components transmission electron microscopy (cryo‐TEM), and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectral analysis. The as‐obtained Na and Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF composite anode (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na) exhibits ultralong cycle life and high Coulombic efficiency in both carbonate electrolyte and ether electrolyte. More impressively, the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na||Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> full cell delivers an ultralong lifespan over 10 000 cycles at 20 C with nearly 100% CE.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202210826-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Density functional theory (DFT) calculations were carried out to investigate the sodiophilic feature and SEI formation mechanism of the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF, Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub> on porous nickel foam (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>@NF) and Ni<sub>3</sub>P on porous nickel foam (Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF) in the sodiation process. After the long‐term sodiation process, the work function value with the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure (3.71 eV) becomes higher than those of the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub> (3.51 eV) and Ni<sub>3</sub>P (3.59 eV), indicating the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na has lower Fermi level compared to the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>@NF@Na and Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na, which can significantly prevent the further electron tunneling and solvent decomposition occurring at the interface between the initial SEI and electrolyte according to the frontier molecular orbital theory (Figure <link href="#adma202210826-fig-0001">1b</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202210826-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202210826-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The unique advantage of the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure was investigated by the FES, because FES technique can qualitatively describe the change of ion flow and current density on homogeneous interface and heterogeneous interface at a macroscopic scale. In Figure <link href="#adma202210826-fig-0001">1c</link>, the homogeneous substrate (blue) represents Ni<sub>3</sub>P or Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>, and the top boundary line represents the metallic Na. The transition of colors indicates the differences in current density under an applied electric field. The arrow represents the direction of the voltage drop between the poles, indicating the consistency of the applied voltage in two systems. The tip induction of ion flow is observed on homogeneous substrate (Figure <link href="#adma202210826-fig-0001">1c</link>), which would cause nonuniform distribution of current and accelerate dendritic growth. And the FES reveals that the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure can decrease the density of ion flux and suppress the dendrite growth. Based on these theories, the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure on porous nickel foam (Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF) is prepared as 3D conductive skeleton for dendrite‐free Na‐metal anodes. We elucidate that the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P heterostructure has improved spatial electronic concentration and enhanced adsorption energy toward Na, and the Cryo‐TEM, TGC measurement, and XPS analysis reveal the uniform “plum‐pudding” SEI structure of the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na electrode. As a result, the Ni<sub>3</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Ni<sub>3</sub>P@NF@Na can achieve high Coulombic efficiency of over 99% in both carbonate and ether electrolytes, as well as excellent reversibility (5000 h at 5.0 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> and 5.0 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>). The full cell with NVP cathode delivers ultrastable capacity retention of 81% even at the high current density of 20 C after 10 000 cycles.
