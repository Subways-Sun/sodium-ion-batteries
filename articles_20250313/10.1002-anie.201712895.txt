However, the intrinsic mismatch between low anodic decomposition potential of superionic sulfide electrolytes and high operating potentials of sodium‐ion cathodes leads to a volatile cathode–electrolyte interface and undesirable cell performance. Here we report a high‐capacity organic cathode, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, that is chemically and electrochemically compatible with sulfide electrolytes. A bulk‐type ASSSB shows high specific capacity (184 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and one of the highest specific energies (395 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>) among intercalation compound‐based ASSSBs. The capacity retentions of 76 % after 100 cycles at 0.1 C and 70 % after 400 cycles at 0.2 C represent the record stability for ASSSBs. Additionally, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> functions as a capable anode material, enabling a symmetric all‐organic ASSSB with Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> as both cathode and anode materials.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>An organic electrode material</b> (Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) has been successfully developed and used as both cathode and anode in an all‐solid‐state sodium battery. The material is chemically and electrochemically compatible with sulfide solid‐state electrolytes. A high specific capacity, record energy density, and stable cycling are shown for Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>‐based all‐solid‐state sodium batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201712895:anie201712895-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201712895-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="anie201712895-ntgp-0001"><note xml:id="anie201712895-note-1001"><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201712895-sec-0001"><p>In contrast to traditional liquid batteries, all‐solid‐state batteries use non‐flammable, thermally and mechanically stable solid‐state electrolytes (SSEs) that could potentially block metal dendrites and enable direct‐series‐stacking of individual cells, therefore offering better safety and potentially higher energy density.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0001"/> For large‐scale energy storage, all‐solid‐state sodium batteries (ASSSBs) are especially attractive thanks to the vast abundance of sodium element on the Earth. Table S1 in the Supporting Information summarizes all reported ASSSBs with key performance parameters. ASSSBs using oxide electrolytes show low reversible capacity and fast capacity decay (&lt;60 % retention in 10 cycles) due to poor contact at the cathode–electrolyte interface,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0002"/> while those with polymer electrolytes show large polarization and low active material utilization as the result of low ionic conductivity.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0003"/> Sulfide SSEs feature superior formability and high Na‐ion conductivities,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0004"/> but have challenges in their narrow electrochemical stability window.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005"/> The anodic decomposition potential is reported to be as low as 2.3 V (theoretical)/2.7 V (experimental) versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na for the representative electrolyte Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4.</sub><link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005b"/> This potential is well below the operating potential of most intercalation‐type cathode materials previously reported for ASSSBs such as NaFePO<sub>4</sub>,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0002a"/> Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0002b"/> Na<sub>2/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0003a"/> and NaCrO<sub>2</sub><link href="#anie201712895-bib-0006"/> (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0001"/>). The chemical reactions between sulfide electrolytes and cathode materials result in resistive interfacial layers that deteriorate cell performance.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005b #anie201712895-bib-0006 #anie201712895-bib-0007"/> Such electrochemical and chemical incompatibility in all‐solid‐state lithium batteries (ASSLBs) can be alleviated by pre‐coating cathode materials with inert Li‐ion conductors (LiNbO<sub>3</sub>, Li<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, etc.).<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0008"/> The Na‐ion‐conducting counterpart of coating materials have been theoretically predicted only recently<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0009"/> and are yet to be implemented experimentally. Cathode materials with lower operating potentials such as TiS<sub>2</sub> suffer from intrinsically low reaction reversibility.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005a #anie201712895-bib-0010"/> Conversion‐type cathode materials such as S and Na<sub>2</sub>S operate at sufficiently low potentials, but it remains challenging to manage the large volume change during cycling.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0011"/> Therefore, new Na‐intercalation cathode materials with chemical and electrochemical compatibility with sulfide electrolytes are necessary for building high‐performance sulfide‐based ASSSBs.</p><figure xml:id="anie201712895-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201712895:anie201712895-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201712895-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Potential–capacity plot for previously reported intercalation‐type cathode materials and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> for ASSSBs. Shadow and blank bars represent the observed and theoretical specific capacities, respectively. Specific energy is calculated considering a sodium anode.</p></caption></figure><p>Organic carbonyl compounds (OCCs) with controllable molecular design and sustainable resource are promising electrode materials.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0012"/> Most OCCs operate within 1.5–2.5 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0012b"/> which falls within the stable window of sulfide SSEs (yellow region of Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0001"/>). OCCs also deliver much higher specific capacities (200–500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) than previously reported sodium intercalation cathode materials (100–200 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0012a #anie201712895-bib-0013"/> Therefore, we expect OCCs to avoid the aforementioned incompatibility issues but still deliver competitive specific energy. Herein we report for the first time on a tailored OCC, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, with an average voltage of 2.15 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na and a theoretical specific capacity of 205 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> based on a two‐electron two‐sodium reaction between Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. ASSSBs were built using Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> SSE<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0010a"/> which has a Na‐ion conductivity of &gt;10<sup>−4</sup> S cm at room temperature and an anodic decomposition potential of 2.7 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. Both the chemical and electrochemical compatibilities between Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> were investigated. Furthermore, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> can also function as a capable anode material with a high theoretical specific capacity of 205 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> based on a two‐electron two‐sodium reaction between Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>6</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, adding to the limited choices of anode materials for sulfide‐based ASSSBs where only NaSn alloys (Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>Sn, etc.) are known to work to certain degree.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005b #anie201712895-bib-0010a"/> We have demonstrated a symmetric all‐organic Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>/Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>/Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> full cell.</p><p>Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> was synthesized from commercially available Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (<i>comm</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) by reduction with sodium naphthalenide (NaC<sub>10</sub>H<sub>8</sub>) in tetrahydrofuran (THF) (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0002"/> a). The molar ratio of NaC<sub>10</sub>H<sub>8</sub> to Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is the stoichiometric 2:1. Pristine <i>comm</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> has a particle size of ca. 50 μm in diameter (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0002"/> b), which was ball‐milled to less than 1 μm to facilitate reduction. Subsequent reduction further downsized Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> to nanoparticles (200–300 nm). The small particle size will increase the contact area between electrolyte and electrode particle and facilitate charge transfer.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0014"/> The thermal stability of the OCCs was analyzed under nitrogen. <i>Comm</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is stable up to 246 °C, and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> shows no obvious weight loss up to 500 °C (Figure S1).</p><figure xml:id="anie201712895-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201712895:anie201712895-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201712895-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Synthesis and characterization of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. a) Chemical reduction/sodiation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (green powders) into Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (purple powders) by reaction with NaC<sub>10</sub>H<sub>8</sub>. b) SEM images of pristine Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, ball‐milled Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. c) XRD patterns and d) FT‐IR spectra of <i>comm</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, <i>milled</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, respectively.</p></caption></figure><p>The phase structure and purity of the products were confirmed with X‐ray diffraction (XRD; Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0002"/> c). <i>Milled</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> exhibits the same diffraction peaks as those for <i>comm</i>‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (space group <i>Fddd</i>).<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0015"/> The peak width largely increased confirming the decrease of particle size. After sodiation, the color of powders changed from green to dark purple (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0002"/> a). Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> shows a different diffraction pattern, which coincides with Yamashita's prediction using first‐principles calculations that Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> has a different space group (<i>C</i>2/<i>m</i>).<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0015"/> Although no standard XRD pattern could be used for indexing, four characteristic peaks at 2theta of 29.4°, 32.2°, 39.6°, and 46.0° are the same as those of discharged product of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> at 1.6 V, where Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> formed, in a liquid cell reported by Wang et al.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0014"/> This comparison confirms that our chemically sodiated product is indeed Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. The change of chemical bond within the molecule was probed by FT‐IR spectroscopy (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0002"/> d). Pristine and milled Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> show identical spectra. All the peaks are characteristic of the C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub><sup>2−</sup> dianions.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0016"/> The sharp peak at 1060 cm<sup>−1</sup> is assigned to C=C stretching. The broad and strong peak centering at 1443 cm<sup>−1</sup> represents the vibration of C=O stretching. After sodiation, the broad peak shifts to a smaller wavenumber of 1337 cm<sup>−1</sup>. Similar shift was observed in the lithiation of Li<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (ca. 1450 cm<sup>−1</sup>)<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0017"/> to Li<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (ca. 1400 cm<sup>−1</sup>),<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0018"/> thus confirming successful reduction of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. The small peak at 973 cm<sup>−1</sup> is indicative of a small amount of further reduced C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> anions,<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0019"/> which likely resulted from inhomogeneous reduction.</p><p>The sodium‐ion conducting SSE, Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>, was synthesized through one‐step high‐energy ball‐milling of Na<sub>2</sub>S and P<sub>2</sub>S<sub>5</sub> without subsequent heat treatment. The XRD pattern and Raman spectrum of only PS<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> unit in the product (Figure S2) suggest the formation of pure Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> SSE.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0010a #anie201712895-bib-0020"/></p><p>The electrochemical properties of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> were measured using pellets sandwiched between ion‐blocking electrodes by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> shows no tail in its EIS spectrum, indicating predominant electronic conduction in the material (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0003"/> a).<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0021"/> Equivalent circuits and fitting results are shown in the inset of Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0003"/> a and Table S2, respectively. The conductivity of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> at 60 °C is 3.9×10<sup>−4</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>, which is comparable to that of Hayashi's Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> prepared through a two‐step ball‐milling/annealing method.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0010a"/> The conductivity of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is 1.0×10<sup>−4</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>, which is comparable to that of LiCoO<sub>2</sub> and higher than those of LiMn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and LiFePO<sub>4</sub>.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0022"/> Calculation from Arrhenius curves shows similar activation energy for Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0003"/> b). From EIS spectra and polarization curves (Figure S3), we can derive the Na<sup>+</sup> and electron transference numbers for Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> as 0.999 and 0.969, respectively. Therefore, Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> is an ionic conductor and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is a mixed electronic‐ionic conductor.</p><figure xml:id="anie201712895-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201712895:anie201712895-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201712895-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Conductivities of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and their chemical and electrochemical compatibility. a) EIS spectra of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> pellets at 60 °C sandwiched between ionically blocking electrodes (inset: cell schematic for EIS test and equivalent circuit); b) Arrhenius plot and activation energies of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> conductivities; c) S 2p XPS spectra of pristine Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>/Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>/Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> mixture after heating at 60 °C; d) Cyclic voltammograms for electrochemical window test of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and operating window test of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (left inset: schematic cell set‐up for CV test; right inset: electrochemical impedance spectra for Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> cell before and after the CV test). Scan rate: 0.05 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>We subsequently evaluated the chemical and electrochemical compatibility of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> with Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and its charged form Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. After mixing and heating Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> with Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, XRD patterns for all materials were unchanged (Figure S4). The S 2p peaks for Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> at 161.9 and 160.7 eV in X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra do not exhibit noticeable change either (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0003"/> c),<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0023"/> and no signals from oxidized species, for example, polysulfide and S with higher binding energies of &gt;163 eV, were found.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0024"/> Therefore, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is chemically compatible with Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>.</p><p>Electrochemical compatibility between Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> was subsequently investigated by cyclic voltammetry (CV). Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> begins to decompose above 2.6 V versus Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> (potential of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> is 0.1 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0025"/>) and continues to show an irreversible oxidation peak (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0003"/> d). EIS spectra show dramatic increase in resistance after the CV scan (inset in Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0003"/> d), indicating very low ionic conductivities of the decomposition products (e.g. S, P<sub>2</sub>S<sub>5</sub> and PS<sub>3</sub><sup>n−</sup>).<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005b"/> Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> shows four pairs of redox peaks at 1.60–2.54 V versus Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, all below the decomposition potential of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>. Therefore, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> are electrochemically compatible.</p><p>The schematic of a Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> ASSSB is displayed in Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0004"/> a. Cold‐pressing results in intimate contact between cathode and electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0004"/> b). The uniformity of the cathode composite (mass ratio of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>:Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>:carbon 4:5:1, active material loading ca. 1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>) was examined with energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX) spectroscopy (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0004"/> b bottom): O from Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is well surrounded by S from Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>. The galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles for the ASSSB operated at 0.1 C (1 C=200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) at 60 °C show four distinct voltage plateaus averaging at 2.05 V versus Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> (or 2.15 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na), reflecting multiple reversible phase transitions between Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0004"/> c). With an upper cutoff potential of 2.6 V versus Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> delivers a reversible capacity of 182 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, or 90 % of the theoretical capacity, and a 1st‐cycle coulombic efficiency as high as 99 %. Charging to even higher potentials is not desirable due to considerable irreversible capacity and significantly increased impedance attributable to the decomposition of Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> (Figure S5). After 100 cycles at 0.1 C and 400 cycles at 0.2 C, the cells deliver charge capacities of 140 and 107 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0004"/> c,e, and Figure S6). The corresponding capacity retentions are 76 % @100 cycles and 70 % @400 cycles, which are the record cycling performance reported to date for ASSSBs (Table S1). XRD pattern (Figure S4a) and XPS spectrum (Figure S7) of the cycled cathode composite revealed no formation of new species during battery cycling, again proving excellent compatibility between Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub> and Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. The capacity decay may be attributed to the continuous phase transitions between Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub><link href="#anie201712895-bib-0026"/> as well as the gradual degradation of the SSE–anode interface.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005b"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201712895-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201712895:anie201712895-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201712895-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performance of a Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> ASSSB. a) Schematic of the ASSSB; b) SEM image of cathode/electrolyte cross‐section (left top) and cathode surface (right top) and corresponding EDX mapping of O and S (bottom); c) Charge/discharge voltage profiles at different cycle numbers at 0.1 C at 60 °C (Inset: Capacity and coulombic efficiency versus cycle number at 0.1 C); d) Representative charge/discharge voltage profiles at different current rates (Inset: Rate capabilities and cycling of the battery from 0.1 C to 1 C); e) Capacity and coulombic efficiency versus cycle number at 0.2 C at 60 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>An ASSSB with Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> cathode and sodium anode offers a specific energy of up to 395 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at the active‐material level, the highest for intercalation compound‐based ASSSBs. The battery delivers specific charge capacities of 164, 138, 117, and 94 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at cycling rates of 0.1 C, 0.2 C, 0.3 C, and 0.5 C, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0004"/> d).</p><p>The above studies concern the cathode performance of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> involving two phenolate groups. Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> also has two carbonyl groups that can store two additional sodium ions at low potentials through Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>+2 Na<sup>+</sup>+2 e<sup>−</sup>⇔Na<sub>6</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. Considering the lack of anode materials for ASSSBs besides NaSn alloys, we are motivated to investigate the possibility of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> as an anode material. Like the cathode studies, we first examined the chemical compatibility of the fully reduced Na<sub>6</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, also synthesized via chemical reduction (for characterization, see Figures S1 and S8), with Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>. XPS spectrum of a heated mixture of the two again revealed sufficient chemical compatibility (Figure S9). The cyclic voltammogram of a Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> battery at low potentials shows only one pair of redox peaks (0.73/1.29 V versus Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>; Figure S10), in sharp contrast with the multi‐peak pattern at high potentials. Correspondingly, discharge profiles show one plateau at ca. 0.8 V versus Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> (inset of Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0005"/> a). Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> delivers a high specific capacity of 172 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C and close to 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 C (Figure S11). Capacity retentions after 50 and 400 cycles at 0.1 and 0.2 C are 77 % and 68 %, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0005"/> a,c and Figure S12). Microcracks are found in the cycled cathode (Figure S13), which may originate from the volumetric change of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> during cycling and be responsible for the capacity fading. For comparison, a Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> battery with an ester‐based liquid electrolyte cycled within the same potential range shows only 45 % capacity retention after 15 cycles due to material dissolution (Figure S14). Lee et al.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0026"/> recently reported stable cycling between Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>6</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> in an ethereal liquid electrolyte after a high‐potential (ca. 3.2 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) activation process, which potential can be detrimental to sulfide SSEs.<link href="#anie201712895-bib-0005b"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201712895-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201712895:anie201712895-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201712895-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Anode and symmetric full cell performance of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> at 60 °C. a) Cycling performance for Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> ASSSB cycled within 0.4–1.5 V (Inset: charge/discharge voltage profile at different cycles); b) Cycling of a symmetric Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> full cell (Inset: cell schematic); c) Capacity and coulombic efficiency versus cycle number of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> ASSSB at 0.2 C.</p></caption></figure><p>The simultaneous superior cathode and anode performance of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> makes a single‐material cell feasible. In a symmetric Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>|Na<sub>3</sub>PS<sub>4</sub>|Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> cell, the Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> at the cathode side is desodiated to Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> during initial charging, while that at the anode side is sodiated to Na<sub>6</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. The reverse process takes place during discharging. The initial discharge/charge capacity of the battery is 167/187 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> based on the mass of Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> at the cathode (Figure S15). The symmetric cell shows stable cycling performance with a capacity retention of 66 % after 50 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie201712895-fig-0005"/> b). The output voltage of the battery is relatively low at ca. 0.65 V, therefore in‐serial connection of several unit cells is necessary for higher voltages, which configuration all‐solid‐state battery uniquely allows for. A single‐material cell will simplify cell assembly, reduce cost, and allow for charging in either direction.</p><p>In summary, we have successfully developed an organic electrode material (Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>) as both a cathode and an anode material that is chemically and electrochemically compatible with sulfide SSEs. High specific capacity, energy density, and especially stable cycling were demonstrated for Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>‐based ASSSBs compared with previously reported ASSSBs. A symmetric all‐organic Na<sub>4</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub>‐based ASSSB was finally prototyped.</p><section xml:id="anie201712895-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>We thank the Solid Sodium team led by Prof. Steve Martin for technical discussion and thank Dr. Zhuan Zhu for assistance in Raman mesurements.
