However, its poor electrical conductivity, combined with its degraded cycling performance, restricts its practical application. Herein, Bi microsphere coated with nitrogen‐doped carbon (Bi@NC) was synthesized. Owing to the unique Bi crystals and nitrogen‐doped carbon layer, the obtained Bi@NC anode exhibited satisfactory cycling stability and superior rate capability. Moreover, after assembling Bi@NC anode with Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode to full battery, excellent sodium storage performance was obtained (57 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>).</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Bi microsphere coated with nitrogen‐doped carbon (Bi@NC)</b> has been synthesized. All results showed that Bi@NC composite can work as a superior anode for SIBs.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100519-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202100519-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>In recent years, large‐scale combustion of coal and oil have caused problems such as smog and acid rain.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> The development of renewable energy is an effective way to deal with current environmental problems.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are widely used in electronic equipment and electric cars.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> But lithium resource is unevenly distributed on the earth and limited. Besides, with the increasing demand for LIBs, the available lithium resources are decreasing, and the price of lithium has become expensive.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Therefore, people urgently need to develop new generation of energy storage systems without lithium.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0014"><sup>[5]</sup></link> It′s worth noting that sodium is in the same main group as lithium, which has similar properties to lithium. More importantly, there are abundant reserves in nature.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0018"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Hence, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have aroused great interest in energy storage.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0023"><sup>[7]</sup></link> However, on account of the difference in ion radius between sodium and lithium, finding the appropriate anode material for SIBs is more difficult than LIBs.</p><p>As the common anode of LIBs, graphite is difficult to accommodate sodium ions. Thus, graphite‐based materials cannot be an appropriate anode for SIBs.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0026"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Some alloy‐type anodes, such as tin (Sn), germanium (Ge), antimony (Sb), bismuth (Bi), silicon (Si) and phosphorus (P) are the candidates for anodes of the SIBs. They have low operating potential and high specific capacity.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0030"><sup>[9]</sup></link> However, the alloy‐type anodes have thus far suffered from huge volume expansion during sodium ion storage processes. For example, the volume expansion of the alloying reaction between Sn and sodium ions can reach up to 420%.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0033"><sup>[10]</sup></link> The severe volume expansion can cause electrode material pulverization and electrical disconnection, and forming unstable solid electrolyte interface (SEI), thus resulting in poor sodium ion storage performance of anode.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0034"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Among these alloy‐based materials, Bi is chemical stable and with relatively small volume expansion (240%).<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> It is noteworthy that Bi is a typical two‐dimensional material which has large crystal layer spacing which benefits the accommodation of sodium ions.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0036"><sup>[13]</sup></link> It is a good choice of anode material for SIBs.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0039"><sup>[14]</sup></link> But Bi as the anode of sodium battery also undergoes volume expansion and cannot form stable SEI.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0042"><sup>[15]</sup></link></p><p>People are trying to solve the problems of Bi in sodium ion battery. The designing of core‐shell and three‐dimensional (3D) structures are good strategies to enhance the sodium ion storage capacity of Bi anode.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0042"><sup>[15]</sup></link> In addition, benefiting from the stable SEI formed in ether electrolyte, the sodium ion storage performance of Bi was further improved.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0046"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Although there are lots of reports on improving sodium storage performance of Bi, the sodium storage performance of Bi in full battery still needs to be explored. Moreover, the reaction processes and mechanism between Bi and sodium ion is still unclear. Herein, we synthesized the Bi microspheres coated by nitrogen‐doped carbon (Bi@NC). The resultant Bi@NC composite with high specific surface area and mesopores which can reduce sodium ion diffusion distances and enhance electrical conductivity of the electrode. Owing to the unique Bi crystals and porous nitrogen‐doped carbon, the synthesized Bi@NC electrode performed a superior electrochemical property in the Na//Bi@NC half cell. After 4000 cycles at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity of 363 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was obtained. Assembling with Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode to form a full battery, which can stably cycle for 2000 cycles, and remained capacity of 57 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (84.5% of its initial capacity, calculated based on the mass of cathode material). Such superior sodium storage performance of Bi@NC anodes can be assigned to the protective effect of nitrogen‐doped carbon layer on Bi microspheres as well as the large interlayer spacing (d (003): 0.395 nm) provides enough sites for the insertion of sodium ions.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100519-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The synthesis processes of Bi@NC microspheres composite were depicted in Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0001">1</link>a. Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was synthesized by solvothermal reaction which has a unique microstructure. Then, after polymerization of dopamine on the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3,</sub> the polydopamine coated Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA) was obtained. After thermal reduction by hydrogen, Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was reduced to Bi and the polydopamine coating was pyrolyzed into nitrogen‐doped carbon. Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0001">1</link>b shows the SEM image of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> precursor, the as‐prepared sea urchin‐like Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> microspheres have a diameter of about 2–3 μm and comprise numerous ultrafine nanowires. After coating with polydopamine (PDA), the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA (Figure S1, Supporting Information) preserves the original morphology. After hydrogen reduction and pyrolysis of PDA, the prepared Bi@NC microspheres maintains the sea urchin‐like morphology (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0001">1</link>c and d). The unique morphology exposed more active sites for sodium and ensured Bi in the electrode fully contact with the electrolyte. The derived carbon layer can be seen in highly magnified TEM images and HRTEM (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0001">1</link>e and f). Moreover, in HRTEM image, the (012) crystal plane of Bi with 0.328 nm lattice spacing can be identified, which demonstrated that the Bi in the composite is highly crystalline. Additionally, the elements distribution in the composite were identified by High‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0001">1</link>g). All the elements of Bi, C and N were identified.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100519-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100519-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Synthetic mechanism diagram of the Bi@NC; SEM images of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> microspheres (b) and Bi@NC (c); (d) TEM image of Bi@NC microspheres; (e) Partial enlarged detail TEM image of Bi@NC microspheres; (f) HRTEM image of Bi@NC; (g) HAADF‐STEM image and corresponding position of elements mapping of Bi, C and N of Bi@NC.</p></caption></figure><p>XRD patterns of the synthesized Bi@NC microspheres were shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0002">2</link>a. All diffraction peaks were corresponding to the characteristic crystal plane of Bi (JCPDS PDF#85‐1329), which indicates that the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> in Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA composite (Figure S2, Supporting Information) is completely transformed to Bi. Although the characteristic peak of C in the composite was not clearly detected by XRD, two peaks at 1300 and 1580 cm<sup>−1</sup> should be indexed from Raman spectrum (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0002">2</link>b). Those correspond to the D and G bands of carbon material.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0047"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The high intensity of D band (I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub>=0.85) reflected that the carbon in the composite has a large degree of defects.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0048"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Those defects in the carbon layer of composite could enhance sodium‐ion battery performance.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0049"><sup>[19]</sup></link> In addition, mesoporous generally produced during the cracking process of carbon materials and the specific surface area also changed.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0050"><sup>[20]</sup></link> In order to identify the transformation in specific surface area and pore distribution during thermal reduction, Bi@NC and Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA composite were tested for absorption and desorption in nitrogen atmosphere (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0002">2</link>c). It was calculated from the isotherms that Bi@NC composite has larger specific surface area (135 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) than Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA (32.9 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>). After reduction at high temperature, the hysteresis loops suggested that Bi@NC provided a typical mesoporous structure, with a pore size mainly distributed in 4 nm (see Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0002">2</link>d). The larger specific surface area and mesoporosity can enhance the electrons and ions transfer in the cyclic process. In addition, the mesoporous carbon also provided a protective matrix during the reaction.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0029"><sup>[8c]</sup></link> Therefore, the rich mesoporous and larger specific surface areas can enhance the performance of Bi@NC anode in sodium‐ion battery. In order to get the ratio of Bi and the thermal stability of the composite, a thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) test was performed. The mass ratio of the sample was reduced 13.68 wt% in 150 °C–450 °C (Figure S3 Supporting Information). Hence, the weight ratio of Bi in Bi@NC was calculated about 77.42 wt%. The high weight ratio of Bi and less carbon can increase the capacity of the anode and beneficial to enhance the energy density. The binding energy of Bi@NC composite were analyzed by XPS. Three elements of Bi, C and N were accurately found in XPS spectrum (Figure S4, Supporting Information). The S element was not detected, indicating that Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> is completely reduced to Bi. The N 1s spectrum was divided into three peaks corresponding to pyridine nitrogen, pyrrole nitrogen, and graphitic nitrogen, respectively (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0002">2</link>e).<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0049"><sup>[19]</sup></link> These peaks demonstrate that N heteroatoms have been doped into the carbon framework.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0051"><sup>[21]</sup></link> At the same time, Bi 4f orbits (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0002">2</link>f) was found which were assigned to Bi 4f<sub>7/2</sub> (157.1 eV) and Bi 4f<sub>5/2</sub> (162.3 eV).<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202100519-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100519-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>XRD diffraction patterns (a) and Raman spectrum (b) of Bi@NC composite; N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms (c) and pore size distribution curves (d) of Bi@NC and Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA; N 1s (e) and Bi 4f (f) XPS spectra of Bi@NC.</p></caption></figure><p>The redox reaction of Bi@NC electrode was verified by cyclic voltammetry (CV). In Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0003">3</link>a, typical redox peaks were readily obtained. In the initial cathodic process of Bi@NC, four distinct peaks at 0.44, 0.47, 0.59 and 0.67 V can be observed, but the peaks at 0.44 and 0.59 V gradually disappear as the cycle going on, which is attributed to the reaction of SEI formation.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0052"><sup>[22]</sup></link> Therefore, the peaks at 0.44 V and 0.67 V should be atrributed to the reactions between sodium ions and Bi (Bi→NaBi→Na<sub>3</sub>Bi). Meanwhile, two oxidation peaks at 0.61 V and 0.76 V were identified, which attributed to the reduction of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi→NaBi, and NaBi→Bi. Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0003">3</link>b shows the specific capacity‐voltage curves of Bi@NC anode, the discharge specific capacity reached up to 552 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> in the first cycle. For the charging processes, the charge specific capacity relaxes to 466 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, it could be calculated that the coulombic efficiency is about 84.42%. After 100 cycles, the electrode still exhibited obvious charging and discharging plateaus, indicating that the composite had superior reversibility. For verifying the advantage of microstructure and nitrogen‐doped carbon of Bi@NC anode, bismuth nanoparticles (Bi NP) without carbon coating were synthesized by hydrothermal method (Figure S5, Supporting Information). It has the same crystal structure as the Bi@NC composite (Figure S6a, Supporting Information). Compared with the Bi@NC, Bi NP shows similar charging and discharging plateaus (Figure S6b, Supporting Information). After 100 cycles, the Bi NP anode exhibited a capacity of 97.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0003">3</link>c). In addition, the N‐doped carbon (NC) anode was synthesis and the cyclic performance was also evaluated (Figure S7). NC anode has a superior cyclic stability. After cycling 100 cycles, it exhibited a capacity of 100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. After Bi compounding with NC, Bi@NC anode exhibited a reversible specific capacity of 371 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. These results show that Bi in the Bi@NC composite has higher reactivity and utilization, and the nitrogen‐doped carbon has great effects to enhance the cycle stability of Bi. In order to verify the superior cycle ability of the Bi@NC anode, a long‐term cycling test was performed. Surprisingly, after 4000 cycles at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the Bi@NC anode exhibited a capacity of 363 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0003">3</link>d). In the initial cycle, the electrolyte couldn't completely diffuse into the mesoporous structure at a high current density, there are capacity drops in the cycle. But as the cycle goes on, electrolyte immerses into the mesoporous structure, the sodium ions in the electrolyte diffused into the internal mesopores of the Bi@NC, resulting in an enhancement of capacity.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0053"><sup>[23]</sup></link> Furthermore, Bi@NC anode also exhibited superior rate capability (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0003">3</link>e). It exhibited capacities of 373, 370, 368, 366, 363, 360, 354 and 340 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at current density of 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 5.0 and 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> respectively. As a contrast, the Bi NP is poor. More surprising, the capacity of Bi@NC anode increased to 370 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> as the current density was changed to 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The superior sodium ion storage capacity should be attributed to the unique mesoporous of Bi@NC and the protecting effect of the nitrogen doped carbon layer as well as the large interplanar spacing which provides enough space for the embedding of sodium ions.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0056"><sup>[24]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202100519-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100519-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of Bi@NC electrode at 0.1 mV/s; (b) Specific‐capacity/voltage curves of Bi@NC electrode for first three and 100th cycles at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>; (c) Cycling performance of Bi@NC and Bi NP anodes at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>; (d) Long‐term cycling performance of Bi@NC electrode at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>; (e) Rate performance of Bi@NC and Bi NP anodes.</p></caption></figure><p>It is important to explore the reaction processes between Bi and sodium ions. The corresponding morphological changes of composite materials during cycling processes were observed by TEM. Figure S8 (a–d) shows the TEM images of Bi@NC at different voltages, and they presented similar morphologies to the original composite material, indicating that the morphological structure is maintained after charging and discharging. It should be attributed to the protection of the nitrogen doped carbon layer. In addtion, the kinetics process of the Bi@NC electrodes in cycle was identified. CV scans were carried out at sweep speed of 0.5–2.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (in Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0004">4</link>a). As the scanning speed increased, the positions of the peaks changed slightly due to polarization, but still exhibited strong redox peak with similar shapes. As reported elsewhere, the current (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>v</i>) of the CV curves follow the relationship:<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0057"><sup>[25]</sup></link><displayedItem xml:id="asia202100519-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><mediaResource href="graphic/asia202100519-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="asia202100519-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100519-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV profiles of Bi@NC anode at different scan rates; (b) Linear fitting plots of log (<i>v</i>)‐log (<i>i</i>), calculated from CV curves.</p></caption></figure><p>In the formula, a and b are parametric constants.which can be calculated by fitting log (<i>v</i>)‐log (<i>i</i>) plots. The values of b exactly 0.5 or exactly 1 indicate a purely diffusion‐controlled process or purely interface‐controlled behavior, respectively. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0004">4</link>b, two pairs of redox peaks are linearly fitted, and b values are calculated to 0.51/0.51 and 0.52/0.51, indicating that the sodium ion storage behavior was mainly a diffusion‐controlled process. This typical diffusion‐limited behavior indicated the sodium ions could rapidly sodiation and desodiation in Bi@NC anode.</p><p>In view of the excellent sodium storage activity in Na//Bi@NC half‐cell, it is necessary to evaluate the performance of Bi@NC anode in full battery. Hence, the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode was successfully synthesized. As shown in Figure S9 (Supporting Information), the synthesized Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C shows typical crystal structure, and all the characteristic crystals of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> can be well indexed in the patterns. SEM images show that it has a uniform morphology (Figure S10, Supporting Information). In the Na//Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> half‐cell, the cathode material displays characteristic discharge/charge plateaus of 3.34 V/3.4 V, (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0005">5</link>a), which is in line with previous studies.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0058"><sup>[26]</sup></link> Notably, in the cyclic test, a specific capacity of 101 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was obtained after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0005">5</link>b), thus showing high sodium storage activity. Besides, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C also exhibited impressive rate capability (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0005">5</link>c). All results verified that Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode is a suitable electrode to assemble full cells. After coupled with the Bi@NC anode, the electrochemical performance of the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C//Bi@NC battery was tested. Figure S11 (Supporting Information) shows CV curves of the full battery. Obviously, two pairs of redox peaks corresponding to the reduction/oxidation of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C can be detected. From the second cycle onwards, the curves of the second and third scans overlap, thus indicating its excellent reversibility. Besides, the mismatch between first and second scan originates from the generation of SEI. For the corresponding discharge/charge curves between 1.5 V and 3.4 V, two pairs of plateaus can be observed (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0005">5</link>d), which is consistent with the results of CV. The charging plateaus of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C//Bi@NC battery are 2.89 V and 2.70 V, and corresponding discharging plateaus are 2.76 V and 2.60 V. In particular, for the first cycle, the charge capacity reached to 94 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and discharge capacity is 90 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (calculated from the mass of cathode material). The coulombic efficiency reached to 95.7%. After the first cycle, both charge and discharge plateaus legible, hence indicating the stable electrochemical properties of the full battery. Notably, the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C//Bi@NC full battery exhibits superior cycling stability. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0005">5</link>e, after 2000 cycles (1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>), a specific capacity of 57 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> is obtained, which is 84.55% of its initial discharge capacity. Moreover, rate capability of the full battery was also evaluated (Figure <link href="#asia202100519-fig-0005">5</link>f). The specific capacities of 91,81, 73, 69, 67 and 65 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> were obtained at current density of 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8,and 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Surprisingly, when the current was turned back to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a high specific capacity of 90 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained, thereby showing superior reversible capacity. Such superior sodium ion storage performance of full battery should be ascribed to the appropriate matching of Bi@NC anode and cathode as well as the effective optimization of anode materials.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100519-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100519:asia202100519-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100519-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) Specific capacity‐voltage curves for first three cycles of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode; (b) Cycle performance of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode; (c) Rate capability of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode; (d) Specific capacity‐voltage curves for first three cycles of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C/Bi@NC full battery; (e) Cycle performance of full battery;(f) Rate capability of full battery.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia202100519-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In short, we thermally reduced polydopamine‐coated Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> to obtain a Bi@NC composite. Benefit from the mesoporous structure derived from the carbon layer and nitrogen doped carbon, Bi@NC as anodes in Bi@NC//Na half‐battery showed superior cycle performance and rate capability. Moreover, the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C//Bi@NC full battery was assembled and it exhibited long‐term cycling performance with reversible cycle up to 2000 cycles. All results show that the Bi@NC composite is a superior anode for SIBs which has great practical application potential for energy storage field.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100519-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p>Synthesis process of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>: The Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> precursor was synthesized according to a previous report.<link href="#asia202100519-bib-0062"><sup>[27]</sup></link> In typical processes, polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP) (0.5 g) and 0.3 g thiourea were dispersed in 60 mL ethylene glycol (EG) with continuous stirring at 65 °C. Then, 0.485 g Bi (NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> ⋅ 5H<sub>2</sub>O dissolved in 10 mL EG was added dropwise to solution. After stirring in thermostat water bath at 65 °C for 30 min, the mixed suspension turned black. Then, transferred suspension to a polytetrafluoroethylene autoclave for solvothermal reaction, the temperature was kept at 140 °C for 12 h. Then, black product was collected by centrifuge. After washing with deionized water and ethanol, the black Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was transferred to a vacuum oven dried overnight.</p><p>Synthesis of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA: 121 mg trishydroxymethyl aminomethane was added to 100 mL of deionized water and stirred for a while (the pH of the solution is about 8.5). Then, 100 mg of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was added to the solution. After 10 min of ultrasound dispersion, 50 mg dopamine hydrochloride was dispersed in the suspension, and the mixed suspension was continuously stirred for 12 h. Black product (Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA) was then centrifuged and dried in a vacuum oven.</p><p>Synthesis of Bi@NC: The resultant Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA was put in a tube furnace with hydrogen/argon flow (5% H<sub>2</sub> and 95% Ar). Then, the temperature was raised to 550 °C. After heating for 3 h, Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@PDA was reduced to Bi@NC.</p><p>Synthesis of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode: 0.8 g glucose, 1 g NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub>, 2 g NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> ⋅ 2H<sub>2</sub>O, and an appropriate amount of absolute ethanol were put into a ball mill and milled at a speed of 800 rpm/min for 24 h. Then the mixed slurry was transferred to an oven to be dried. The obtained precursor was put into a tube furnace with nitrogen as the protective gas, and raised the temperature to 850 °C for 4 h.</p><p>Electrochemical tests: The half cells performance of Bi@NC anode and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C cathode was evaluated after assembling CR2032‐type coin cells. The counter electrode was sodium metal sheet. For Bi@NC half cells, the electrode consists of Bi@NC, super‐P carbon, and CMC binder coated on copper foil with a weight proportion of 7 : 2 : 1. For Na//Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C half cells, the electrode consists of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C, super‐P carbon, and PVDF binder coated on aluminum foil with a weight proportion of 7 : 2 : 1. 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in diglyme was used as electrolyte. For the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>@C//Bi@NC full battery, the anode was pre‐cycled 3 cycles in half cells to eliminate irreversible reactions. The mass loading ratio between the anode material and cathode material is about 1: 3.5. The mass loading of the anode is about 1.0 mg cm<sup>2</sup>. Battery performance was evaluated on a NEWARE BTS battery test system. The cyclic voltammetry (CV) data was obtained by a CHI 660D electrochemical workstation.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100519-sec-0005" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title
