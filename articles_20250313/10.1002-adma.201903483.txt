type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐based layered oxides are among the leading cathode candidates for sodium‐ion batteries, toward potential grid energy storage, having large specific capacity, good ionic conductivity, and feasible synthesis. Despite their excellent prospects, the performance of layered intercalation materials is affected by both a phase transition induced by the gliding of the transition metal slabs and air‐exposure degradation within the Na layers. Here, this problem is significantly mitigated by selecting two ions with very different MO bond energies to construct a highly ordered Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring superstructure within the transition metal layers in a model compound (NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>). By virtue of substitution of 1/3 nickel with antimony in NaNiO<sub>2</sub>, the existence of these ordered Ni<sub>6</sub>‐rings with super‐exchange interaction to form a symmetric atomic configuration and degenerate electronic orbital in layered oxides can not only largely enhance their air stability and thermal stability, but also increase the redox potential and simplify the phase‐transition process during battery cycling. The findings reveal that the ordered Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring superstructure is beneficial for constructing highly stable layered cathodes and calls for new paradigms for better design of layered materials.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201903483-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Two ions with very different M</b><b>O bond energy</b> are selected to construct a highly ordered Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring superstructure within transition metal layers in a model compound (NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>). The formed Ni<sub>6</sub>‐rings with super‐exchange interaction by Ni/Sb ordering can greatly enhance the air stability and thermal stability of layered cathodes, increase the redox potential, and simplify the phase‐transition process during battery cycling.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903483:adma201903483-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903483-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201903483-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201903483-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Layered sodium transition‐metal oxides Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MO<sub>2</sub> have been extensively investigated as the most promising cathode candidates in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) because of their superior specific capacity and the simple analogies from the successful application of LiMO<sub>2</sub> in Li‐ion battery systems.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0001 #adma201903483-bib-0002 #adma201903483-bib-0003 #adma201903483-bib-0004 #adma201903483-bib-0005 #adma201903483-bib-0006 #adma201903483-bib-0007 #adma201903483-bib-0008"/> In general, O3‐type Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MO<sub>2</sub> (Na occupies octahedral sites) outperform their P2 analogs (Na occupies prismatic sites) in terms of the specific capacity due to the sufficient Na content.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0009 #adma201903483-bib-0010 #adma201903483-bib-0011"/> However, two main challenges might hinder O3‐type Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MO<sub>2</sub> cathodes from practical application. On one hand, most of O3‐Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MO<sub>2</sub> compounds are very hygroscopic against moist atmosphere, leading to the formation of electrochemically inactive NaOH, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, or Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> by partial Na extraction. These alkaline species would accelerate the defluorination of polyvinylidene fluoride binder and corrosion of current collector due to their dissolution into <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone solvent, drastically impeding their practical viability.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0012 #adma201903483-bib-0013 #adma201903483-bib-0014 #adma201903483-bib-0015 #adma201903483-bib-0016"/> On the other hand, extraction and insertion of Na ions from layered compounds inevitably leads to several first‐order phase transformations sequences accompanied by multiple voltage plateaus and steps in the electrochemical curves,<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0017 #adma201903483-bib-0018"/> incurring low diffusion coefficients and complicated cation diffusion mechanisms as additional energy barrier is needed in moving the Na ions from neighboring phase boundaries. So stepwise voltage profile and poor rate performance are often observed for O3‐type cathodes because the asymmetries between the required multiple kinetic processes are inclined to impose an abrupt change in crystal structure and composition.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0019"/></p><p>Such poor structural stability with regard to multiple phase evolution has been found to be related to cationic ordering/disordering and the gliding of M oxide layers.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0020 #adma201903483-bib-0021"/> It is well established that three types of ordering exists in binary Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[M1,M2]O<sub>2</sub>‐layered oxides: cationic ordering, Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy ordering and charge ordering. Cationic ordering, related to the phase transition and structural stability, is commonly determined by the difference of M1 and M2 within transition metal layers. Large disparity in ionic radii and MO bond energy tends to form ordered arrangement while small difference is favorable for forming disordered arrangement. Cationic ordering/disordering linked to complicated phase transformations and instable air exposure often accompanies stresses changes in structure and possible particles crack, which instead affect the shape of the voltage curve and cause battery performance degradation.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0022 #adma201903483-bib-0023 #adma201903483-bib-0024"/> In this context, it is of great importance to tailor the structural stability to facilitate better material design. Of particular urgency is the study of cationic ordering tendencies between M chemistries and its driving force within the O3‐type layered lattice structure.</p><p>Herein, for the first time, we present a combined experimental and theoretical study to elucidate the effects of intercalation cationic ordering on structural stability in layered O3 oxides using NaNiO<sub>2</sub> as a model system. By virtue of substitution of 1/3 nickel with antimony in NaNiO<sub>2</sub>, a highly stabilized phase with honeycomb‐type cationic ordered superlattice in the Na[Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> compound is established. The formed Ni<sub>6</sub>‐rings with super‐exchange interaction by Ni/Sb ordering largely increases the air stability, thermal stability, and phase stability of pristine NaNiO<sub>2</sub>. Moreover, sequential O3–P3–O1 phase transition process undergoes a symmetrical–asymmetrical–symmetrical electronic structural evolution during Na ions deintercalation/intercalation in Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. And unique valence disproportionation is surprisingly detected in O1 phase supported by soft X‐ray absorption spectra, magnetic moment, and radial distribution function. This study demonstrates the relationship between intercalating cationic ordering and structural stability in the O3 model structures, providing a firm platform to design high‐stable and high‐voltage cathodes for advanced SIBs.</p><p>The PXRD pattern of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a) synthesized at 700 °C can be indexed to a monoclinic unit cell (<i>C</i>2/<i>m</i>, <i>a</i> = 5.3226(9) Å, <i>b</i> = 2.8501(1) Å, <i>c</i> = 5.5885(5) Å, and β = 110.448(4)°), suggesting a distorted O3‐type structure owing to the <i>a</i>/<i>b</i> ratio (1.87) deviated from that of the ideal hexagonal system (√3 ≈ 1.73). The pattern of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>b) were indexed to the monoclinic space group <i>C</i>2/<i>m</i> (<i>a</i> = 5.3067(7) Å, <i>b</i> = 9.1879(2) Å, <i>c</i> = 5.6313(1) Å, and β = 108.246(1)°). The specific atomic positions and cell parameters are listed in Tables S1 and S2 (Supporting Information). Examination of the 2θ region between 17° and 30° reveals superlattice reflections based on the <i>C</i>2/<i>m</i> model, as marked by asterisks in Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>b. In honeycomb NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compounds, the 2:1 ordering of Ni/Sb atoms in the <i>a</i>–<i>b</i> plane generates a unit cell larger by √3<i>a</i><sub>0</sub> that yields superlattice reflections not described by the <i>R</i><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201903483:adma201903483-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma201903483-math-0001.png"><mover accent="true"><mn>3</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover></math><i>m α</i>‐NaFeO<sub>2</sub> subcell (where <i>a</i><sub>0</sub> is basal plane cell parameter for α‐NaFeO<sub>2</sub>).<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0025"/> Reflections of typical superlattices at 2θ values of 19.1°, 19.9°, 22.1°, 25.3°, 29.3°, and 38.6° (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>c and Figure S1, Supporting Information) are well indexed by corresponding crystallographic planes in this cell, indicative of a large degree of ordering within the layered structure. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations further demonstrate this honeycomb configuration has the lowest thermodynamic energy per formula unit (Figure S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201903483-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903483:adma201903483-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903483-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) The XRD and Rietveld plots of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> (a) and NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (b) samples. c) The (020) and (110) crystallographic planes of typical superlattice peaks. d) ABF and e) HAADF‐STEM images of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at the [001] zone axis. f) ABF and g) HAADF‐STEM image of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at the [100] zone axis. h–j) The crystal structure of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> viewed along different directions.</caption></figure><p>Detailed honeycomb‐ordered structural information on NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compounds was experimentally confirmed by atomic‐scale aberration‐corrected annular‐bright field (ABF) and high‐angle annular‐dark‐field (HAADF) scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM). The positions of the Sb and Ni columns was marked by larger and smaller dark‐dot contrast in the ABF‐STEM images and bright‐dot contrast in the HAADF‐STEM images, respectively.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0026"/> The light element Na and O columns in the layered structure are revealed by the faint dark‐dot contrast with the interlayer positions in the ABF‐STEM images. Ni/Sb atoms arrange in a hexagonal symmetry from the ABF‐STEM images (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>d) at the [001] zone axis. Corresponding HAADF‐STEM images reveal the projected distance between the <i>M</i> atomic columns is 1.7 Å (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>e). The dots of the O columns on both sides of the Ni/Sb layers demonstrate different darkness or sometimes vanish completely in the [100] ABF‐STEM and HAADF‐STEM images, which suggest some stacking faults as lateral displacements of the dot patterns in the Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> layers (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>f). The sequence Sb–Ni–Ni–Sb associated with the cationic honeycomb ordering in the TM layers is highlighted in HAADF‐STEM images (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>g) by the size change of the bright spots. Corresponding crystal structures of typical NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with different perspectives are plotted in Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0001"/>h–j. Each SbO<sub>6</sub> octahedron is surrounded by six NiO<sub>6</sub> edge‐sharing octahedra forming the Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring honeycomb lattice. Ni<sub>2</sub>SbO<sub>6</sub> layers and Na layers alternate with ABCABC oxygen stacking patterns. The Na<sup>+</sup> ions are intercalated within the Ni<sub>2</sub>SbO<sub>6</sub> slabs and occupy an octahedral NaO<sub>6</sub> site.</p><p>To explore the effect of honeycomb superstructure on its structural stability, aging experiments (exposing materials to air for 10 days or soaking in water for 10 min followed by drying at 100 °C overnight) were performed. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a, the characteristic (111) peak of the O3‐phase NaNiO<sub>2</sub> gradually weakens and vanishes during the air exposure process. In addition, a gradual shift of the (001) peak toward lower angles is observed as the formation of P3‐type Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>H<i><sub>x</sub></i>NiO<sub>2</sub> upon exposure to air, originating from a migration of Na<sup>+</sup> ions out from the lattice to form impurities. Further IR spectra analysis shows the wavenumber located at 3636 and 868 cm<sup>−1</sup> for the soaked sample can be assigned to the OH and CO bond vibration, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0002"/>b). When NaNiO<sub>2</sub> samples are exposed to moist atmosphere, Na<sup>+</sup> ions are readily exchanged with H<sup>+</sup> ions by uptaking H<sub>2</sub>O (Figure S3, Supporting Information) from atmosphere as the following process: NaNiO<sub>2</sub> + <i>x</i>H<sub>2</sub>O = Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>H<i><sub>x</sub></i>NiO<sub>2</sub> + <i>x</i>NaOH, NaOH + CO<sub>2</sub> = NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, 2NaOH + CO<sub>2</sub> = Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> + H<sub>2</sub>O.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0027 #adma201903483-bib-0028"/> Even worse, the formed NaOH, NaHCO<sub>3</sub>, and Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> are electrical insulators, which leads to the much decreased crystallinity (Figure S4, Supporting Information). In contrast, the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are almost the identical with that of as‐prepared sample upon exposing to air or soaking in water (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0002"/>c). That is to say 1/3 Sb substitution obtains the Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring superstructure, which is sufficient to endow NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> water‐resistant to inhibit the spontaneous structure evolution and oxidation to higher valence state (Figure S5, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201903483-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903483:adma201903483-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903483-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of as‐synthesized and aged NaNiO<sub>2</sub> sample. b) IR spectra of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> before and after being soaked in water. c) XRD patterns of as‐synthesized and aged NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sample. d) In situ XRD patterns of the NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> material during the thermal treatment process. e) Corresponding heating program chosen for XRD tests. f,g) Contour plot of the evolution of the characteristic diffraction peaks during the thermal treatment process. h) Calculated energy barrier before and after O extraction for NaNiO<sub>2</sub> (blue background) and NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (red background).</caption></figure><p>In situ X‐ray diffraction patterns during the heat treatment process were collected to better understand thermal stability of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0002"/>d,e). The change of peak intensity and position of several main characteristic peaks located at 50°–65° and 66°–76.5° with a function of temperature are presented as 2D contour plots in Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0002"/>f,g. Note that the intensity of the superlattice peak characteristic of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> centered at 17°–30° shows no obvious change, suggesting the honeycomb‐ordered superstructure is very stable during the annealing treatment. Furthermore, all diffraction lines only show peak position shift without any shape change and no extra peaks beyond the O3 structure are detected. And an exact opposite evolution is clearly observed for the XRD pattern and the well‐defined diffraction lines belonging to the monoclinic phase were completely recovered to the original positions upon cooling down to the room temperature, indicating superior thermal stability. Calculated total DFT energies (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0002"/>h) demonstrate that ordered Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring superstructure in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> significantly enlarges the energy difference (4.356 vs 3.254 eV) between Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>SbO<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>SbO<sub>5.5</sub> when one atom was removed from the O3 structure, consequently breaking the MO bond become more difficult, which could refrain the loss of crystal oxygen in the framework and promote higher structure stability over a wider temperature range.</p><p>By virtue of further DFT analysis, two characteristics in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are preferred to make its honeycomb structure more energy favorable. First, Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring configuration has the minimum distorted Ni–O and Sb–O local octahedron. The specific bond length and bond angle of each octahedron in different configuration are listed in Tables S3–S13 (Supporting Information). This peculiarity in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> guarantees a D<sub>3d</sub> symmetry in electronic structure (Figures S6–S8), thus d<i><sub>xy</sub></i> orbital degenerates with d<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>2‐</sub><i><sub>y</sub></i><sub>2</sub> orbital and d<i><sub>xz</sub></i> orbital degenerates with d<i><sub>yz</sub></i> orbital in Ni atoms as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a,b.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0029"/> Second, Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring configuration promises highly symmetric atomic structures within transition metal layers. The distance between neighboring Ni atoms in Jahn–Teller distorted NaNiO<sub>2</sub> is 2.860 Å (white dashed line) and 3.089 Å (yellow dashed line), respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0003"/>c). In NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, all neighboring NiNi bonds indicate a length of 3.101 Å (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0003"/>d), thus a high symmetry atomic structure within MO<sub>2</sub> slabs is established.</p><figure xml:id="adma201903483-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903483:adma201903483-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903483-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) The partial density of states (pDOS) of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> (a) and NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (b). c,d) The comparison of bond length of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> (c) and NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> structure (d). e,f) The spin difference charge density of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> (e) and NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (f).</caption></figure><p>The two characteristics might originate from the elimination of magnetic frustration and the existence of a 90° Ni–O–Ni super‐exchange interaction. When Sb replaces a Ni atom, every oxygen atom connects one Sb atom and two Ni atoms within MO<sub>2</sub> slabs. Considering Sb atom does not have any spin electrons, thus no magnetic frustration can be observed.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0030"/> Similar with LiNiO<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0031"/> NaNiO<sub>2</sub> can be considered as a prominent example of strong geometrically frustrated material as it shows neither spin nor orbital ordering, of which the frustration parameter value is estimated to be 1.8. However in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, magnetic measurement indicated that onset of antiferromagnetic takes place at 16 K and the frustration parameter approaches 0.75, much smaller than that of NaNiO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0032"/> As illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0003"/>e, the electronic spin difference charge density of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> on oxygen atoms shows a shape pointing to a sodium atom and a nickel atom. Whereas, no charge density pointing to two different Ni atoms can be found. In comparison, the spin difference charge density on oxygen atoms exhibits a shape pointing to two neighboring nickel atoms in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, manifesting a strong Ni–O–Ni 90° super‐exchange interaction,<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0033"/> which is indicated by a blue arrow (Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0003"/>f) and promotes its structural stability.</p><p>The electrochemistry of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> and NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was then incorporated in half Na cells (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a). Various voltage plateaus and steps are obviously observed in the charge/discharge curves of the NaNiO<sub>2</sub>, indicating several phase transitions of O3–P′3–P′′3–O′3–O′′3 occurring along with NiO<sub>2</sub> slab gliding.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0034"/> When 1/3 Sb is introduced into the crystal framework, the multiple voltage plateaus are simplified to two main plateaus at 3.26 V (O3–P3 transition) and 3.64 V (P3–O1 transition) without affecting the capacity.<link href="#adma201903483-bib-0035"/> The calculated voltages of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are consistent with experimental voltage from P3 to O1 upon charge and P3 to O3 during discharge. Notably, slight voltage polarization are observed for O3–P3 and O1–P3 phase transition voltage, which might be caused by the energy loss from a symmetrical electronic structure (O1 or O3) to an asymmetrical electronic structure (P3) as discussed later. Several P3 and O1 structures are chosen to obtain the most energy favorable structure in order to match the experimental voltage. Corresponding convex hull is plotted in Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0004"/>b and Figure S9 (Supporting Information). Intermediate P3 phase can be finally written as Na<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> due to the lowest energy and the suited calculated voltage. The detailed structural evolution of O3–P3–O1 accompanied by transition metal layers glides is illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0004"/>c. Obviously, from a stepwise voltage profile observed for NaNiO<sub>2</sub>, unsatisfactory battery performance is achieved as the phase boundary movement for more biphasic regions is required. In contrast, both original and aged NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrodes show an initial charge capacity of 120 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 82% after 50 cycles owing to the simplified phase transition behavior (Figures S10–S12).</p><figure xml:id="adma201903483-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903483:adma201903483-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903483-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Calculated voltage plot (DFT) compared with experimentally obtained charge/discharge profile of NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. b) Formation energies of O3, P3, and O1 structures for Na<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Ni<sub>2</sub>SbO<sub>2</sub> as a function of Na content (<i>x</i>) obtained using ab initio DFT calculations. c) The Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring structure evolution of Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: red, black, yellow, and brown indicates oxygen, nickel, sodium and antimony, respectively. d) Contour plot of the (001) diffraction peaks of in situ XRD patterns evolution collected during the first charge of the Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cells. e–g) SAED patterns of typical O3, P3, and O1 phase.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#adma201903483-fig-0004"/>d shows the contour maps of experimental operando XRD patterns for the phase stability of Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> during initial Na extraction. Ni ions in O3 phase have a magnetic moment around 1.76 µ<sub>B</sub>, referring to divalent Ni<sup>2+</sup>. In P3‐Na<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, three types of magnetic moment exists: half of Ni have a magnetic moment of 1.74 µ<sub>B</sub>, a quarter is 1.15 µ<sub>B</sub>, and the rest is 0.19 µ<sub>B</sub>, corresponding to Ni<sup>2+</sup>, Ni<sup>3+</sup> and Ni<sup>4+</sup>, respectively. Two types of magnetic moment of Ni ions are found in O1‐Na<sub>1/3</sub>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>: half is 1.72 µ<sub>B</sub> for Ni<sup>2+</sup>, and half is 0.19 µ<sub>B</sub> for Ni<sup>4+</sup> (agrees with the sXAS results). As for O1‐Na<sub>1/3</sub>Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, both Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Ni<sup>4+</sup> ions under symmetrical environment have equal bonds length free from Jahn–Taller effect. In short, an interesting electronic structural evolution of symmetry–asymmetry–symmetry during O3–P3–O1 phase transformation process is proposed, accompanied by three valence disproportionation of nickel in P3 phase, divalent and tetravalent Ni coexisting in O1 phase.</p><p>In summary, a comprehensive study of structural stability, ordering tendencies, and reaction mechanisms has been performed on typical honeycomb ordered NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and prototype NaNiO<sub>2</sub> materials through both experimental characterizations and calculated strategies. Ordering on the honeycomb in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> not only allows this phase to be more stable than square and zigzag structures at the same Na concentrations, but also significantly enhance its whole structural stability in terms of air, thermal and phase stability. Detailed atomic and electronic structure analysis reveals that the dramatically enhanced stability is associated with averaged bonds length and degenerated orbital by the super‐exchange interaction of Ni<sub>6</sub>‐ring structure unit in NaNi<sub>2/3</sub>Sb<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which provides a rigid framework upon sodium‐ion transport, simultaneously simplifies multiple phase evolution, and critically influences the battery cycle stability of both capacity and voltage. Therefore, reasonable ionic incorporation with very different bond energy and compatible ionic radii in layered materials, such as V<sup>5+</sup>, Bi<sup>5+</sup>, Sb<sup>5+</sup>, As<sup>5+</sup>, and Te<sup>6+</sup>, might be a sensible strategy to modulate the ordering of the metal lattice in order to enhance their structural stability (Figure S16, Supporting Information). The present work sheds light on how in‐plane ordered superstructure can affect the structural stability and battery performance, which could provide new guidelines for designing highly stable electrode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201903483-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>P.‐F.W., M.W., and Y.X. contributed equally to this work.
