Herein, a new strategy of introducing Mg ions into the hinging sites of Mn‐based tunnel‐structured cathode material is designed. Highly reversible electrochemical reaction and phase transition in this cathode are realized. The resulted Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.95</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with Mg<sup>2+</sup> in the hinging Mn‐O<sub>5</sub> square pyramidal exhibits promising cycle stability and rate capability. At a current density of 2 C, 67% of the initial discharge capacity is retained after 800 cycles (70% at 20 C), much improved than the undoped Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>. The improvement is attribute to the enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics and the lowered desodiation energy after Mg doping. Highly reversible charge compensation and structure evolution are proved by synchrotron‐based X‐ray techniques. Differential charge density and atom population analysis of the average electron number of Mn indicate that Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.95</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is more electron‐abundant in Mn 3d orbits near the Fermi level than that in Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, leading to higher redox participation of Mn ions.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs2433-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.95</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as a novel cathode material for sodium‐ion batteries is designed and synthesized aiming to effectively facilitate Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and lower desodiation energy. Structure evolution of tunnel‐type cathode during high rate charging/discharging is revealed for the first time, enabling high rate capability and cycle stability with the help of Mg.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2433:advs2433-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs2433-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs2433-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered as the most appealing alternative to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) regarding the limited natural abundance and increasing consumption of Li resources. Similar to LIBs, Na<sup>+</sup> can be the charge carrier to achieve electrochemical energy storage in SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0001 #advs2433-bib-0002 #advs2433-bib-0003 #advs2433-bib-0004 #advs2433-bib-0005"><sup>1–5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, sluggish mobility of Na ions result in relative lower energy density and poorer kinetics than LIBs. Therefore, developing high‐performance cathode materials is essential for pushing the practical application of SIBs.</p><p>Over the past years, several kinds of cathode materials have been developed for SIBs, including the layer‐structured metal oxides, tunnel‐structured metal oxides, polyanion compounds, and organic materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0006 #advs2433-bib-0007 #advs2433-bib-0008 #advs2433-bib-0009"><sup>6–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, tunnel‐structured Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> cathode is a promising cathode material, first developed by Hagenmuller's group.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na ions in Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> can shuttle through the large “S shape” tunnels reversibly. However, it suffers from structural degradation during longtime Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction. To solve the existing problems, plenty of works have been done in the recent years. Wang et al. designed Ti‐substituted Na<sub>0.44</sub>[Mn<sub>0.66</sub>Ti<sub>0.34</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>0.66</sub>[Mn<sub>0.66</sub>Ti<sub>0.34</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> cathodes for aqueous sodium‐ion batteries. They revealed that Ti substitution could tune the charge ordering property and reaction pathway, significantly smooth the charge/discharge curves and improve cycle stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0011 #advs2433-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Xu et al. reported that Fe substitution in the tunnel Na<sub>0.61</sub>[Mn<sub>0.61−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i>Ti<sub>0.39</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> could realize Fe<sup>3+</sup>/Fe<sup>4+</sup> redox couple in tunnel‐structured cathodes, achieving a high working voltage of 3.56 V with a usable capacity of ≈90 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Latter, Wang et al. successfully introduced F<sup>–</sup> to enlarge the sodium diffusion paths of Na<sub>0.66</sub>[Mn<sub>0.66</sub>Ti<sub>0.34</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, effectively inhibiting structural degradation (85 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is maintained at 2 C after 1000 cycles) and promoting electrochemical kinetics,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> especially enhancing the low temperature performance at −20 °C. Even so, there are still some unstable structure transformation for Mn‐based cathode materials due to the unavoidable Jahn–Teller distortion, which limits their further applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0015 #advs2433-bib-0016 #advs2433-bib-0017"><sup>15–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>As a kind of environmental‐friendly alkaline earth metal, Mg<sup>2+</sup> has similar ionic radius as Mn<sup>3+</sup> but are more stable in electronic structure. Mg<sup>2+</sup> has been widely used for doping or substituting in cathode materials to improve their performance in electrochemical sodium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0018 #advs2433-bib-0019"><sup>18,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>y</sub></i>Mg<i><sub>y</sub></i>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>y</i> = 0, 0.05, 0.1),<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> increasing the amount of Mg<sup>2+</sup> could smooth the electrochemical curve and mitigate Jahn–Teller effect. Due to the similar electronic structure but smaller ionic radius properties than Na<sup>+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup> can substitute into both Na sites and transition metal sites in P2‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>[Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Ni<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.15</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> compound. Mg ions in the prismatic sodium site play a role like “pillar” to stabilize the layered structure along <i>c</i>‐axis during Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/extraction. Although the electrochemical inert Mg<sup>2+</sup> makes no contribution to the charge compensation for redox reaction, the formation of “Na‐O‐Mg” configuration can ionize the MgO bond, placing O 2<i>p</i> states relatively high in energy and inducing the oxygen redox without oxygen release.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0021 #advs2433-bib-0022"><sup>21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Herein, we introduced Mg<sup>2+</sup> into the hinging site allocated between small tunnel and “S shape” tunnel in tunnel‐structured Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> to tune the structure evolution during sodiation/desodiation process and successfully relieve the lattice strain. The strategy of Mg doped NMM‐0.05 largely enhanced the kinetics of sodium storage, leading to superior long‐cycle stability and rate capability. Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.95</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub> keeps 67% capacity retention rate after 800 cycles at 2 C. Even at 20 C, a capacity of 60 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> can still be obtained. X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) demonstrated the high reversibility of structure evolution and charge compensation during charge and discharge. The immediate structure evolution during high‐rate cycling was also revealed by using synchrotron‐based time‐resolved XRD technique. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations illustrated the desodiation energy is decreased and the average charge density in Mn 3<i>d</i> orbits near the Fermi level is increased. Mg<sup>2+</sup> doping could be an effective strategy for optimizing tunnel‐type cathode materials for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2433-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Various ratios of Mg‐doped samples were successfully synthesized by solid‐state reaction (Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.95</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.85</sub>Mg<sub>0.15</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, denoted as NMM‐0, NMM‐0.05, NMM‐0.1, NMM‐0.15, NMM‐0.2). The XRD patterns of synthesized samples with different doping ratios are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2433-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> and Figure S1, Supporting Information. NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 exhibit an orthorhombic tunnel structure with space group of <i>Pbam</i>. As the increase of Mg content over 0.05, the P2 phase begins to appear, mixed with the tunnel phase, as seen in NMM‐0.1. When the Mg content over 0.15, pure P2 phase can be observed in NMM‐0.15 and NMM‐0.2. The XRD refinements based on Rietveld method<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0023 #advs2433-bib-0024 #advs2433-bib-0025"><sup>23–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> for NMM‐0.05 are shown in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0001">1b</link>. The refined lattice constants are <i>a</i> = 9.082(3) Å, <i>b</i> = 26.432(5) Å, <i>c</i> = 2.824(8) Å. The increase of Mg content makes <i>b</i> expand while <i>a</i> and <i>c</i> slightly shrink, compared with those of NMM‐0 in Figure S2 and Table S1, S2, Supporting Information.</p><figure xml:id="advs2433-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2433:advs2433-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs2433-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Mg<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>2</sub> with <i>x</i> from 0 to 0.2. b) XRD patterns and Rietveld refinement of as‐prepared NMM‐0.05. c) XANES spectra of Mn K‐edge in NMM‐0.05 and standard manganese‐based metal oxide references (Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, LiMn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, and MnO<sub>2</sub>). d) Schematic illustration of NMM‐0.05 in the <i>a–b</i> plane with hinging site of Mn5 substituted by Mg<sup>2+</sup>. SEM images of NMM‐0.05 with scale bar of e) 10 and f) 1 µm.</caption></figure><p>XAS was carried out to detect the valence state of Mn in NMM‐0.05 by comparing with relative Mn‐based metal oxide references (Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, LiMn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, and MnO<sub>2</sub>). As shown in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0001">1c</link>, the average valance of Mn is about +3.6, which is consistent well with the expected stoichiometric ratio. Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0001">1d</link> shows the schematic illustration of the tunnel structure. The “Mn2‐Mn1‐Mn2‐Mn3‐Mn4‐Mn5” constructs the “S shape” channels, in which Na2 and Na1 are approximately half‐filled. While “Mn2‐Mn3‐Mn4‐Mn5‐Mn4‐Mn5” forms small channels with full‐filled Na1. The micro‐morphology of NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 obtained by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) are shown in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0001">1e,f</link>; Figures S3 and S4, Supporting Information. It can be found that both NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 consist of rod‐shaped particles in the size range of 7–10 µm. Energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mappings indicate that Na, Mn, and Mg elements are distributed in the sample homogeneously. The atomic stoichiometry results are shown in Tables S3 and S4, Supporting Information, respectively, which agree well with the designed composition. To precisely confirm the actual ratios of metal ions in NMM‐0.05, ICP‐OES test was also conducted and the results are shown in Table S5, Supporting Information. The ratio of 0.441: 0.952: 0.048 for Na: Mn: Mg is very close to the expected stoichiometry. According to the results shown in Tables S1 and S2, Supporting Information, Mn1, Mn3, and Mn4 sites are occupied by Mn<sup>4+</sup>. While the Mn2‐O<sub>6</sub> octahedral and Mn5‐O<sub>5</sub> square pyramidal are occupied by Mn<sup>3+</sup>, which contributes to the charge center for Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction. According to the XRD refinement in Table S2, Supporting Information, Mg<sup>2+</sup> is doped into the Mn5 sites, which is the only one Mn<sup>3+</sup>‐O<sub>5</sub> square pyramidal. The Mn5‐O<sub>5</sub> square pyramidal forms edge‐linked chains linking to two double and a triple octahedral chains, which leads to the formation of two types of tunnels. Thus, Mn5‐O<sub>5</sub> plays as an important hinging role to tune lattice strains induced by insertion/extraction of Na ions. In NMM‐0.05, about 0.45 Mg ions substitute the Mn5 sites, and the formation of Mg 3s‐O 2p configuration introduces much flexible Mg‐O<sub>5</sub> square pyramidal. The Mg<sub>0.45</sub>Mn<sub>0.55</sub>‐O<sub>5</sub> square pyramidal enhances the hinge of small tunnel and “S shape” tunnel, suggesting the enlargement along <i>b</i> axis and the shrinkage of <i>a</i> and <i>c</i> parameters. In addition, this hinge can effectively tune the lattice strains and enhance the structure stability during repeated insertion/extraction of Na<sup>+</sup>. As for sodium ions shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2433-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>, Na1 occupied in the small tunnel. Sodium ions in Na2 and Na3 sites have a trigonal prismatic coordination with a NaO bond in distance of 2.3–2.4 Å. During sodiation/desodiation process, Na ions transport sequentially along the <i>c</i> direction, following the arrow direction in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0002">2</link>.</p><figure xml:id="advs2433-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2433:advs2433-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs2433-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Sodium configuration in the tunnel structure and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusing direction in different planes, a) <i>a</i>–<i>c</i> plane, b) <i>b</i>–<i>c</i> plane, c) <i>a</i>–<i>b</i> plane.</caption></figure><p>The electrochemical behaviors for NMM‐0.05 and NMM‐0 were evaluated by galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) and cyclic voltammogram (CV). <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link> shows the GCD curves of NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 between 2.0–3.8 V at 0.2 C (20 mA g<sup>–1</sup>). In Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003">3a</link>, NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 exhibit specific capacities of 46 and 47.2 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> during the initial charge, respectively, extracted about half of the whole Na<sup>+</sup> (0.22) from the tunnel host. In the following charge and discharge process, NMM‐0.05 delivers a reversible capacity of 105 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>, slightly higher than NMN‐0 (101 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>). Both the NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 electrodes exhibit similar six‐plateau characteristics, due to the complex phase evolution and sequential Na<sup>+</sup> transferring for partial occupancy in tunnel host. To better understand the sequence of Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation, the differential capacity versus voltage plot for NMM‐0.05 at 0.2 C in the first discharge (black) and second charge (red) is shown in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003">3b</link>. Six pairs of redox peaks suggest the sequential insertion/extraction of sodium ions, which are indexed as Na1, Na2, Na3 transferring from the small and “S shape” tunnel at different states of charge. The overall sequence of Na<sup>+</sup> was quantitatively inserted or extracted due to the electrostatic repulsion among sodium ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003">3c</link> shows the typical CV curves of NMM‐0.05 in the first ten cycles, which is similar to the d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> plot in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003">3b</link>. Six pairs of redox peaks in Figure 3c are quite symmetric and perfect overlapped for the first ten cycles, suggesting the effect of Mg doping on enhancing kinetics and lowering polarization. The enhanced kinetics and ionic diffusion of NMM‐0.05 were investigated by measuring the CV curves at different scanning rates. Figure S5, Supporting Information and Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003">3d</link> are the CV curves of NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 at various scanning rates from 0.2 to 1.0 mV s<sup>–1</sup>. With the increase of scanning rates (<i>v</i>), the peak currents (<i>I</i><sub>p</sub>) of the oxidation and reduction peaks also increase. Plotting <i>I</i><sub>p</sub> and the square root of scanning rate <i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> as a function in Figure <link href="#advs2433-fig-0003">3e, a</link> good linear character of <i>I</i><sub>p</sub> versus <i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> suggests the typical diffusion‐controlled behavior in sodiation/desodiation process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2433-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The diffusion coefficients of Na<sup>+</sup> (<i>D</i><sub>Na+</sub>) for NMM‐0 and NMM‐0.05 were calculated to be 2.897 × 10<sup>–10</sup> and 3.496 × 10<sup>–10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup> based on Equation S1, Supporting Information, respectively. Synchrotron‐based time‐resolved in situ XRD confirms that NMM‐0.05 undergoes single phase evolution during fast charge and discharge processes without the existence of intermediate phases, which is quite different from those observed in layer‐structured cathode materials. As a result, NMM‐0.05 cathode exhibits superior long‐cycle stability and rate capability. This work not only provides a promising strategy for improving electrochemical performance of tunnel‐structured cathode materials, but also gives a hint that the fast‐charging ability of cathode materials could be improved by constructing Li or Na ordering sequence in crystal channels, which can effectively restrict the inhomogeneity of Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> during ion transportation and suppress the irreversible phase transition.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2433-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs2433-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs2433-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs2433-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material Synthesis</title><p>A series of Mg<sup>2+</sup> doped Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Mg<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>2</sub> (Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.98</sub>Mg<sub>0.02</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.95</sub>Mg<sub>0.05</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.925</sub>Mg<sub>0.075</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.85</sub>Mg<sub>0.15</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.44</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, denoted as NMM‐0, NMM‐0.02, NMM‐0.05, NMM‐0.075, NMM‐0.1, NMM‐0.15, NMM‐0.2) were synthesized by traditional solid‐reaction method. The theoretical calculations were conducted in Cambridge Sequential Total Energy Package (CASTEP) of Materials Studio (Accelrys Inc.). The desodiation energy was defined as: <i>E</i> = (<i>E</i><sub>NMM‐0/0.05</sub> + <i>nE</i><sub>Na</sub> − <i>E</i><sub>NMM‐0/0.05*</sub>)/<i>n</i>, (<i>E</i><sub>NMM‐0/0.05</sub> means the energy after desodiation; <i>E</i><sub>NMM‐0/0.05*</sub> means the energy before desodiation; <i>E</i><sub>Na</sub> means the energy of Na and <i>n</i> is the number of Na that deintercalated). Geometry optimizations of the above structures were conducted separately. The calculation quality was set to fine and the GGA‐PBE functional was employed. The convergence tolerance was set as: 1 × 10<sup>–5</sup> eV atom<sup>–1</sup> for energy, 0.03 eV Å<sup>–1</sup> for max force, 0.05 GPa for max stress, 0.001 Å for max displacement. The cut‐off energy was 598.7 eV and ultra‐soft pseudopotential was employed. The <i>k</i>‐point was set to 2 × 1 × 5. Koelling–Hamon relativistic treatment was chosen to deal with heavy atoms.
