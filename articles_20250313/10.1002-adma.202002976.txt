xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0010"><label>1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are being considered as one of the most appealing alternatives to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) for grid‐scale energy storage with intriguing features of infinite abundance and low cost of sodium resources and similar electrochemical reaction mechanisms to LIBs.<sup>[1–</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, because of the larger ionic radius, higher reduction potential, and slower reaction kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> ions compared to Li<sup>+</sup> ions, exploring electrodes with high reversible capacities and facile reaction processes remains challenging.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0005 #adma202002976-bib-0006 #adma202002976-bib-0007 #adma202002976-bib-0008"><sup>5–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the past few years, a wide range of promising materials has been developed as efficient electrode materials for SIBs, including cathode materials (e.g., polyanions,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0009 #adma202002976-bib-0010 #adma202002976-bib-0011 #adma202002976-bib-0012"><sup>9–12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> transition metal oxides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0013 #adma202002976-bib-0014 #adma202002976-bib-0015 #adma202002976-bib-0016 #adma202002976-bib-0017"><sup>13–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> hexacyanoferrates<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0018 #adma202002976-bib-0019"><sup>18,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) and anode materials (e.g., alloys,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0020 #adma202002976-bib-0021 #adma202002976-bib-0022 #adma202002976-bib-0023 #adma202002976-bib-0024 #adma202002976-bib-0025 #adma202002976-bib-0026"><sup>20–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> metal chalcogenides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0027 #adma202002976-bib-0028 #adma202002976-bib-0029 #adma202002976-bib-0030 #adma202002976-bib-0031 #adma202002976-bib-0032"><sup>27–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbonaceous materials<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0033 #adma202002976-bib-0034 #adma202002976-bib-0035"><sup>33–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup>). It is noteworthy that the electrodes with two/multiple‐electron reactions are more favored because of the high theoretical capacity for energy storage. Among these materials, metal sulfide anodes come into the spotlight due to the dazzling superiorities of high capacity and remarkable electrochemical reversibility.</p><p>By virtue of the advantageous features of diverse material species, compositions, crystalline phases, valence states, architectures, and controllable nanostructures/morphologies, metal sulfides manifest high electrochemical activity for sodium storage. Numerous metal sulfides have been applied for sodium storage and show promising electrochemical results.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0036 #adma202002976-bib-0037 #adma202002976-bib-0038"><sup>36–38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Metal sulfides usually have higher electronic conductivity compared to metal oxide counterparts.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0039 #adma202002976-bib-0040"><sup>39,40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> And the bond energy of M–S in metal sulfides is weaker than the M–O in metal oxides, leading to faster kinetics during the conversion reactions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the metal sulfides based electrode materials, mixed metal sulfides (MMSs) with mixture phases of different metal sulfides demonstrate richer redox reactions and higher electronic conductivity compared to the single‐component metal sulfides, showing intrinsic advantages for sodium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For MMSs, it is verified that the phase boundaries at the heterointerfaces can provide abundant lattice mismatches, distortions, and defects, which have great influences on the charge carrier transport behavior by regulating the reaction kinetics and long‐range disorder.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, driven by the internal electric field at the heterointerfaces due to the dissimilar coupling components with different bandgaps, the interfacial reaction kinetics and electron/ion transport can be greatly promoted.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0043"><sup>43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Additionally, owing to the synergetic effect of different components, the uniformly dispersed intermediate nanocrystals during electrochemical reactions can avoid the aggregation of generated metal nanoparticles thus obtaining good cyclability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0044"><sup>44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> And the diverse redox potentials and out‐of‐step electrochemical reactions of different components would mitigate the strain during sodium uptake/extraction processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is inferred that MMSs with suitable compositions could achieve excellent electrochemical performance.</p><p>In general, the sodium storage mechanisms of metal sulfides are attributed to the combination of insertion, conversion, and/or alloying reactions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0037 #adma202002976-bib-0046 #adma202002976-bib-0047"><sup>37,46,47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During these reactions, the electrodes inevitably undergo severe volume change, which causes the collapse and pulverization of the structures and fading of capacity during repeated cycling. To address these issues, nanostructure engineering and compositing with conductive carbonaceous materials are reliable strategies for boosting the sodium storage properties of MMSs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The nanostructured electrodes have several structure‐dependent merits, resulting from the nanosized building units, large surface areas, and robust secondary configurations.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0048 #adma202002976-bib-0049 #adma202002976-bib-0050"><sup>48–50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Specifically, the nanoscale permeable particles provide decreased diffusion energy barrier for electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions thus facilitating the reaction kinetics. The large surface area endows accessible electroactive sites and enables the full infiltration of electrolytes. And the elaborate architecture with tailored size and composition helps to retain the integrity of the electrode to achieve long‐term cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0051 #adma202002976-bib-0052"><sup>51,52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the above promotive effects could be further facilitated through the integration of active materials with conductive carbonaceous materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0053 #adma202002976-bib-0054 #adma202002976-bib-0055"><sup>53–55</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The carbonaceous materials not only provide convenient electron/ion transportability for the hybrids but also exhibit excellent mechanical stability to effectively accommodate the severe volume variation during charge/discharge processes. Besides, it has been reported that metal doping in metal sulfides can introduce some lattice change (such as distortion, expansion, and contraction), vacancies, and electron deficiencies, which provide a facile path for sodium penetration into the host materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0056"><sup>56</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, nanostructure engineering and rational composition design on MMSs are believed to promote outstanding electrochemical properties from the considerations of improved electronic conductivity, accelerated ion transport, buffered volume change, and strengthened structural stability.</p><p>Although several review articles have provided comprehensive discussions on the application of metal sulfides for SIBs, they mainly focus on single‐component metal sulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0037 #adma202002976-bib-0038 #adma202002976-bib-0041 #adma202002976-bib-0046 #adma202002976-bib-0057"><sup>37,38,41,46,57</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Research efforts on the design and synthesis of MMSs for sodium storage have not been systemically surveyed. In this progress report, we summarize the recent advances of rationally designed MMSs with tailorable architectures, structural/compositional complexity, controllable morphologies, and enhanced sodium storage properties (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>). First, we highlight the progress in the application of MMSs with simple compositions as anode materials in SIBs. Then, MMSs with complex compositions are thoroughly discussed, including integrating with carbonaceous materials and metal doping in MMSs. Finally, we will provide some brief conclusions and perspectives on the development of MMSs to inspire more innovative research on the design and fabrication of complex and rational MMSs for energy storage.</p><figure xml:id="adma202002976-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202002976:adma202002976-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202002976-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Overview of mixed metal sulfides for sodium‐ion batteries.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0020"><label>2</label><title type="main">MMSs with Simple Compositions for SIBs</title><p>It has been demonstrated that mixed metal sulfides with synergistic effects of dissimilar components have shown better electrochemical properties compared to the single‐component metal sulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0044 #adma202002976-bib-0058"><sup>44,58</sup></link><sup>]</sup> More specifically, the heterostructures can lead to a promoted electron/ion transport pathway, and the diverse redox potentials and out‐of‐step electrochemical reactions of different components endow strengthened structural stability. For example, Veerasubramani et al. reported the fabrication of the Fe<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>S/MnS nanocomposites through sulfidation of the Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>/MnO<sub>2</sub> precursors.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0058"><sup>58</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The obtained Fe<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>S/MnS nanocomposites show a longer lifespan and better rate capability compared to the individual Fe<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>S and MnS electrodes. These results may originate from the synergistic effects of different components, controlled structural degradation, and enhanced pseudocapacitive contribution of the Fe<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>S/MnS hybrids. Nanostructuring has been adopted to construct MMSs for boosted sodium storage performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0042 #adma202002976-bib-0059 #adma202002976-bib-0060"><sup>42,59,60</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Notably, yolk‐shelled structures with distinctive hollow configurations can help to accelerate the reaction kinetics and buffer the volume variation of electrodes. Choi et al. reported the preparation of yolk–shelled SnS‐MoS<sub>2</sub> microspheres through a spray pyrolysis process (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>,<link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002">b</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0044"><sup>44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The yolk–shelled SnS‐MoS<sub>2</sub> microspheres exhibit much‐improved cycling stability compared to the yolk–shelled SnS and MoS<sub>2</sub> and dense SnS‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. The yolk–shelled configuration offers a decreased Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion length and interspace for volume change, and uniformly dispersed SnS and MoS<sub>2</sub> nanocrystals avoid the metal aggregation during discharge thus to obtain good cyclability. Gu and co‐workers demonstrated the fabrication of yolk–shelled Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> spheres by a self‐templating method (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002">2c,d</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0061"><sup>61</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The as‐formed yolk–shelled Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> spheres exhibit superior sodium storage performance with a remarkable capacity of 300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1200 cycles. The in‐depth investigation of phase boundaries on improved electrochemical properties was conducted by Liu and co‐workers.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During the charge/discharge processes of yolk–shelled Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructure, the abundant phase boundaries generated from the recrystallization/redistribution of MoS<sub>2</sub> phase can alleviate the volume change and stabilize the Bi/Na<sub>2</sub>S interface with uniform dispersion. As also confirmed by theoretical analysis, the abundant phase boundaries can induce a self‐built‐in electric field, resulting in boosted ion/electron transport pathway and improved reaction kinetics. As a result, the electrode exhibits high electrochemical reversibility with endurable Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructures. The Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub> composites exhibit excellent cycling stability of 323 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1200 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much superior to the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and MoS<sub>2</sub> electrodes.</p><figure xml:id="adma202002976-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource with ultrathin 2D nanosheet subunits have shown remarkable superiority toward sodium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0062 #adma202002976-bib-0063 #adma202002976-bib-0064 #adma202002976-bib-0065"><sup>62–65</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In general, the thin permeable nanosheets can promote transport of the electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions and facilitate the full infiltration of electrolyte, and the hollow structures guarantee the structural stability with effective accommodation of the stress during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0066 #adma202002976-bib-0067"><sup>66,67</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a typical example, we recently have demonstrated the synthesis of hierarchical CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> double‐shelled nanoboxes (denoted as CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> DSNBs) by using Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes as the precursor and template.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0068"><sup>68</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The multi‐step synthesis procedure of the heterostructures is schematically shown in Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002">2e</link>. Initially, a shell of Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanosheets is grown on Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes through the coordinating etching and precipitating reactions. Then, the obtained Cu<sub>2</sub>O@Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanocubes are transferred to Cu<sub>2</sub>O@CuS@Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanocubes through a selective sulfidation reaction. Afterward, the Cu<sub>2</sub>O core in the Cu<sub>2</sub>O@CuS@Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanocubes is selectively removed by an etching treatment to form the CuS@Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes. After the final solvothermal sulfidation reaction, the CuS@Co(OH)<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes are converted to CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> DSNBs (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002">2f–h</link>). The CoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are strongly anchored on the CuS inner shell from the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observation (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002">2i</link>). With the unique structure of CoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets on the CuS nanobox, the CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> DSNBs exhibit good sodium storage performance in terms of high reversible capacity (625 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>), good rate capability (304 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and enhanced cycling stability (79% capacity retention after 500 cycles). The cyclability is much superior than the single‐shelled CoS<sub>2</sub> and CuS nanoboxes and double‐shelled CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes with separated‐layer structure (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0002">2j</link>), indicating the vital effect of heterointerfaces on the electrochemical durability. A kinetics analysis based on the cyclic voltammogram test further indicates the characteristics of a capacitance‐controlled behavior of the CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> DSNBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0030"><label>3</label><title type="main">MMSs with Complex Compositions for SIBs</title><p>To further enhance the structural stability and sodium storage performance of MMSs, numerous efforts have been devoted to tune the structures of MMSs. Efficient strategies, including compositing with carbonaceous materials and metal doping in the structures, have been introduced to construct complex MMSs with boosted electrochemical properties.</p><section xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0040"><label>3.1</label><title type="main">MMSs with Carbonaceous Materials for SIBs</title><p>In general, integrating electrode materials with carbonaceous materials is an effective way to enhance sodium storage performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0069"><sup>69</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On the one hand, the superior electronic conductivity of carbonaceous materials endows the composites an efficient electron transport network to compensate for the insufficient electronic conductivity of metal sulfides. On the other hand, carbonaceous materials are ideal additives for relieving the large strain generated during repeated (de)sodiation processes and possibly suppress the shuttle effect of intermediate polysulfides in the electrolyte. Various carbonaceous materials with diversified structures, including pyrolytic carbon, carbon nanotubes, carbon cloth, and graphene, have been adopted to decorate the MMSs for efficient sodium storage.</p><p>The pyrolysis of polymers is a simple and efficient approach for preparing carbonaceous materials, which has been widely used to in situ decorate the MMSs. Various polymers, such as poly(styrene‐acrylic),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0070"><sup>70</sup></link><sup>]</sup> polydopamine (PDA),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0071 #adma202002976-bib-0072 #adma202002976-bib-0073 #adma202002976-bib-0074 #adma202002976-bib-0075"><sup>71–75</sup></link><sup>]</sup> poly(3,4‐ethylenedioxythiophene),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0076"><sup>76</sup></link><sup>]</sup> polyacrylonitrile,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0077"><sup>77</sup></link><sup>]</sup> dextrin,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0078"><sup>78</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and polypyrrole<sup>[79,80]</sup> have been used as precursors to generate highly conductive carbon materials. For example, a carbon‐coated Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub>@MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructure was developed by Liu and co‐workers.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0081"><sup>81</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub> core with higher electronic conductivity could improve the electronic conductivity of the MoS<sub>2</sub> and facilitate the Na<sup>+</sup> ion transport due to the different reaction reversibility. Besides, the electric field at the heterointerface is strong because of the large bandgap difference of the Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub> (0 eV) and MoS<sub>2</sub> (1.17 eV), which can enhance electron/ion transport kinetics between the MoS<sub>2</sub> layer and the Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub> core (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>). X‐ray absorption near‐edge spectra of Fe L‐edges and Mo M‐edges confirm the increased interfacial structural disorder and improved charge‐carrier mobility of the Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub>@MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructure. DFT calculations suggest that the energy barrier of Na<sup>+</sup> ions in the Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub>‐MoS<sub>2</sub> heterostructure is much smaller than that in bare Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub> and MoS<sub>2</sub>.With decreased ion diffusion energy barrier and reinforced geometry architecture, the heterointerfaces endow the hybrids fast charging/discharging capability and improved cycling stability. The Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub>@MoS<sub>2</sub>@C electrode exhibits remarkable cyclability up to 1000 cycles with a high capacity retention of 93.4%. Kinetic analyses indicate that the Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub>@MoS<sub>2</sub>@C exhibits a capacitive process with the largest Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion coefficient compared with the MoS<sub>2</sub>@C and Fe<sub>9</sub>S<sub>10</sub>@C.</p><figure xml:id="adma202002976-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource layer sandwiched carbon between the two metal sulfides may be an elaborate structure to obtain satisfying electrochemical performance. However, the synthesis of sandwiched carbon modified MMSs for sodium storage is rarely reported. Recently, we have designed and synthesized hierarchical three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0082"><sup>82</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The overall synthetic route for the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hierarchical nanoboxes is schematically depicted in Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003">3b</link>. Uniform Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes are used as self‐templates to prepare CuS nanoboxes through the sequential sulfidation and etching processes. Next, Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes are formed by coating a thin layer of PDA on the CuS nanoboxes followed by thermal annealing in N<sub>2</sub>. Lastly, a layer of MoS<sub>2</sub> is grown on the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes through a solvothermal reaction to form the three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hierarchical nanoboxes (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003">3c</link>–<link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003">f</link>). The rational design can efficiently promote the transport of electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions, buffer the volume variation of active materials, enhance the electronic conductivity of the composites, and maintain the structural stability for extended cycling. More specifically, compared to the normal carbon‐integrated MMSs, the thin layer sandwiched carbon between two metal sulfides has some unique advantages. First, the sandwiched carbon can facilitate the electron transfer along the two metal sulfides, ensuring the high electronic conductivity of the hybrids. Second, the middle nitrogen‐doped carbon could effectively separate the intermediates of different metal sulfides during electrochemical reactions, suppressing the agglomeration of nanoparticles. Third, the flexible sandwiched carbon could well accommodate the volume change of the electrode materials, which helps to maintain the nanobox structures with enhanced structural stability. As a result, the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hierarchical microboxes demonstrate enhanced electrochemical properties in terms of high reversible capacity of 442 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and good rate capability of 297 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 3 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>Thanks to their tunable compositions and diverse structures, metal‐organic frameworks (MOFs) have been widely utilized as precursors to prepare carbon‐decorated MMSs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0045 #adma202002976-bib-0083 #adma202002976-bib-0084 #adma202002976-bib-0085 #adma202002976-bib-0086 #adma202002976-bib-0087 #adma202002976-bib-0088"><sup>45,83–88</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Specifically, the porous configuration and nanosized subunits of MOF‐derived MMSs can provide high surface area and reduced diffusion length for Na<sup>+</sup> ions/electrons. And the pyrolysis of the heteroatom‐containing ligand could generate heteroatom‐doped carbon, guaranteeing high electronic conductivity of the hybrids. For example, Xiang et al. reported the formation of MoS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/C nanoboxes by combining the solvothermal method and calcination process using the ZIF‐67 (ZIF = zeolitic imidazolate framework) template.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0084"><sup>84</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Due to the well‐designed hybrid structures (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003">3 g</link>,<link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003">h</link>), MoS<sub>2</sub>/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/C composites manifest a high capacity of 546 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a good rate capability of 222 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Using CoZn‐MOFs as templates, nitrogen‐doped carbon decorated Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/ZnS nanocrystals were fabricated by Liang and co‐workers.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A large amount of phase boundaries between ZnS and Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> particles is observed from the HRTEM images. The abundant phase boundaries of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/ZnS provide numerous crystal defects and active sites for fast transport of electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions. The Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/ZnS exhibits a larger capacitive contribution and a higher Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient compared to the monometal sulfides, suggesting the enhancement of ion diffusion and conductivity by the two mixed metal sulfides. The diverse redox potentials and out‐of‐step reactions of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> and ZnS nanocrystals would mitigate the strain during the charging/discharging processes, and the nitrogen‐doped carbon with high conductivity can largely contribute to the pseudocapacitance effect. With these structural advantages, the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>/ZnS exhibits a good rate capability of 258.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> and excellent stability up to 500 cycles. By coupling with polymer‐derived pyrolytic carbon, the electrochemical performance of MOF‐derived MMSs can be greatly boosted. For example, ZnS‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@C double‐shelled polyhedrons synthesized via a sulfurization method combining a subsequent cation‐exchange reaction are reported by Yin and co‐workers (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0003">3i,j</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0089"><sup>89</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Owing to the high electronic conductivity and hollow configurations, the ZnS‐Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@C polyhedrons can keep a high reversible capacity of 1029 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and retain a capacity of 630 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 120 cycles.</p><p>Owing to the excellent conductivity, strong mechanical flexibility, and easy accessibility, different carbon matrixes including carbon cloth,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0090 #adma202002976-bib-0091"><sup>90,91</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0092"><sup>92</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and graphene,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0093 #adma202002976-bib-0094 #adma202002976-bib-0095 #adma202002976-bib-0096 #adma202002976-bib-0097"><sup>93–97</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been adopted to decorate the MMSs. In this way, the conductivity of these MMSs could be greatly improved, the severe volume change could be efficiently accommodated, the structural integrity of active materials could be stabilized from the multicomponent synergy, and the ion/electron transfer is boosted via the built‐in electric field. For example, Guan et al. fabricated coherent SnS<sub>2</sub>/NiS<sub>2</sub> hetero‐nanosheet arrays on carbon cloth (denoted as SnS<sub>2</sub>/NiS<sub>2</sub>@CC) (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>–<link href="#adma202002976-fig-0004">d</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0091"><sup>91</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Because of the strong coupling effect of heterogeneous SnS<sub>2</sub>/NiS<sub>2</sub> with a promoted transfer rate of electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions, reduced aggregation and volume variation, and 3D hierarchical nanostructures with accessible channels and highly conductive networks, the SnS<sub>2</sub>/NiS<sub>2</sub>@CC electrode demonstrates a high reversible capacity (857 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and good cycling stability (343.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles). Besides, combining MMSs with graphene has been widely reported to promote the electrochemical performance of MMSs. SnS<sub>2</sub>/Co<i><sub>x</sub></i>S‐rGO composites have been synthesized via the sulfidation of CoSn(OH)<sub>6</sub> precursors (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0004">4e–h</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0098 #adma202002976-bib-0099"><sup>98,99</sup></link><sup>]</sup> TEM investigations reveal that the (SnCo)S<sub>2</sub> heterostructures interlaced by highly elastic graphene nanosheets are highly stable. Sn<sup>0</sup>/Na<sub>2</sub>S interface thus resulting in better reversibility of continuous conversion/alloying reactions (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0004">4i</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0099"><sup>99</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a result, the (SnCo)S<sub>2</sub>/SG nanocubes manifest a high reversible capacity (758 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>) type="main">Metal‐Doped MMSs for SIBs</title><p>Metal doping is an effective approach for tuning the electrical properties of metal chalcogenide semiconductors and it has been widely used in the semiconductor industry.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0100 #adma202002976-bib-0101"><sup>100,101</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Doping a small amount of metal ions into the metal chalcogenides can lead to the minor lattice expansion/contraction of the structures, which results in higher mobility and more defect structures.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0030 #adma202002976-bib-0056 #adma202002976-bib-0102"><sup>30,56,102</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, the metal doping can increase electronic conductivity and facilitate the Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion by decreasing the diffusion barrier. For example, Re‐doped MoS<sub>2</sub> was reported to show improved electronic conductivity and more diffusion channels owing to the increased number of charge carriers and defect structures, leading to improved sodium storage properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0103"><sup>103</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the research on the synthesis of metal‐doped MMSs for sodium storage is quite limited. Bera et al. reported the synthesis of dual‐layered and doped heterostructures composed of SnS and Sn‐doped Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (denoted as SnS‐Sn:Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>) (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>,<link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005">b</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0104"><sup>104</sup></link><sup>]</sup> With the improved kinetics, the SnS‐Sn:Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> heterostructures maintain a high reversible capacity of 500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles, which is much superior compared to the SnS and Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> samples (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005">5c</link>,<link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005">d</link>). Considering the distinctive merits of hollow architectures, the fabrication of metal‐doped MMSs with hollow structures is expected to achieve decent sodium storage performance. We recently reported the fabrication of copper‐doped CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S double‐shelled nanoboxes (denoted as Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs) via a sequential ion‐exchange strategy (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005">5e</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0105"><sup>105</sup></link><sup>]</sup> First, through a seeded epitaxial growth process, a layer of ZIF‐8 is successfully grown on ZIF‐67 nanocubes to obtain core–shelled ZIF‐67@ZIF‐8 polyhedrons. Then, the ZIF‐67@ZIF‐8 particles are transformed into CoS<sub>2</sub>@ZnS DSNBs by a facile anion‐exchange reaction. At last, the CoS<sub>2</sub>@ZnS DSNBs are further converted to Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs by a cation‐exchange process with copper ions. The Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs exhibit a well‐defined polyhedral structure with hollow configurations (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005">5f–i</link>). Benefiting from the unique heterostructure and distinct hollow nanostructures, these Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs deliver a high capacity of 535 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even at a high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a high capacity of 333 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be retained. Besides, a long cycle life with 76% capacity retention over 300 cycles is achieved, which is much superior compared to the single‐component metal sulfides (Figure <link href="#adma202002976-fig-0005">5j</link>). The kinetic study based on CV offers further insight into the sodium storage behavior. The Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs exhibit capacitance‐controlled characteristics, which is responsible for the superior rate performance. The high capacitive contribution may result from the heterostructures with nanoparticle‐composed hollow configuration, which offers a large surface for the adsorption/desorption of Na<sup>+</sup> ions during repeated charge/discharge processes.</p><figure xml:id="adma202002976-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202002976:adma202002976-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202002976-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) HAADF‐STEM and b) TEM images of the SnS‐Sn:Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> composite. c) Galvanostatic discharge–charge voltage profiles of the SnS‐Sn:Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. d) Comparison of cycling performances of the SnS‐Sn:Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>, and SnS electrodes. e) Synthetic process for the preparation of the Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs. f,g) FESEM and h,i) TEM images of Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs. j) Cycling performance of the Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub>@Cu<i><sub>x</sub></i>S DSNBs, Cu‐CoS<sub>2</sub> SSNBs, and CoS<sub>2</sub> SSNBs at a current density of 0.3 A g<sup>−1</sup>. a–d) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0104"><sup>104</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2019, American Chemical Society. e–j) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202002976-bib-0105"><sup>105</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2020, Wiley‐VCH.</caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0060"><label>4</label><title type="main">Summary and Outlook</title><p>In summary, we provide an overview of the recent developments on the rational design and synthesis of mixed metal sulfides as high‐performance electrode materials for sodium‐ion batteries. It shows that great progress has been achieved in developing various types of mixed metal sulfides as anode materials for sodium storage, as summarized in <b>Table</b> <link href="#adma202002976-tbl-0001"><b>1</b></link>. The advantageous effects of mixed metal sulfides and nanostructure engineering on their electrochemical properties are comprehensively discussed. The interfaces in mixed metal sulfides with a strong electric field enable fast charge carrier transport, leading to improved rate capability and cycling stability. Meanwhile, the rich phase boundaries could offer abundant defects and active sites for fast electron/ion transport. Besides, incorporation of carbonaceous materials with mixed metal sulfides can further enhance the electronic conductivity of the hybrids, provide an efficient buffering matrix/shell to mitigate large volume variation of electrode materials during the long‐term cycling, and reduce the aggregation of active materials during synthesis and charge/discharge processes. Moreover, metal doping in metal sulfides could increase electronic conductivity and facilitate the Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion by decreasing the diffusion barrier. Of note, nanostructure engineering on mixed metal sulfides is an efficient way to boost the sodium storage performance, owing to decreased particle size to reduce the diffusion length of Na<sup>+</sup> ions/electrons, and exquisite architecture design in micro/nanoscale to improve the geometrical stability and accommodate volume expansion. Additionally, a wide variety of metal sulfides can be incorporated into mixed metal sulfide combinations to satisfy a wide variety of design needs. Therefore, high reversible capacity, enhanced rate capability, and long‐term cyclability can be achieved through the rationally designed mixed metal sulfide nanostructures.</p><tabular sulfides to be optimized for practical sodium‐ion batteries. While mixed metal sulfides show improved electrochemical properties compared to the single‐component metal sulfides, how to choose different metal sulfides and integrate them into one united configuration remains challenging. The in‐depth reaction mechanisms of mixed metal sulfides during (de)sodiation remain to be further investigated, especially how these heterostructures determine the charge transport and electrochemical kinetics is not yet fully understood. Some side reactions between electrolytes and intermediates and dissolution of polysulfides in electrolytes need to be suppressed. Developing feasible and cost‐effective synthetic methods for high‐performance mixed metal sulfide electrodes remains a big challenge. In addition, more complex structures, such as ternary/quaternary metal sulfides might be further studied. Furthermore, the intrinsic relationships between nanostructured mixed metal sulfide materials and their electrochemical properties await extensive investigation via advanced in‐situ/ex‐situ characterization and theoretical calculations. Although many mixed metal sulfides show excellent sodium storage performance in half cells, it is expected to see more studies about their application in full cells/prototype batteries. For prototype batteries, the higher voltage behavior of MMSs based anodes would make a safer battery than one with alloy or carbonaceous anodes, but at the same time it becomes necessary to find high‐voltage cathode materials that can match with MMSs to achieve full batteries with high output energy density. Besides, the adoption of earth‐abundant and non‐toxic raw materials (e.g., Mn‐based and Fe‐based MMSs) and green manufacturing processes are more favored considering the cost‐effectiveness and environmental issues.</p><p>With continuous studies on the mixed metal sulfides for sodium‐ion batteries and growing interest from battery communities, we anticipate that mixed metal sulfides will play a vital role in energy storage applications in view of the unique structural and compositional features. It is optimistic that more advanced mixed metal sulfide electrode materials will be designed toward grid‐scale energy storage applications with high energy/power density in the future.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0070" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>X.W.L. acknowledges the funding support from the National Research Foundation (NRF) of Singapore via the NRF investigatorship (NRF‐NRFI2016‐04) and Ministry of Education of Singapore via the Academic Research Fund (AcRF) Tier‐1 Funding (RG116/18).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202002976-sec-0080" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202002976-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202002976-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202002976-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>C.</givenNames> <familyName>Vaalma</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>D.</givenNames> <familyName>Buchholz</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Weil</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Passerini</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Nat.
