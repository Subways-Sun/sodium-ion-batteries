Formed in situ via electropolymerization of functional imidazolium‐type ionic liquid monomers, the polymer membrane protects the metal against parasitic reactions with electrolyte and, for fundamental reasons, inhibits dendrite formation and growth. The effectiveness of the membrane is demonstrated using direct visualization of sodium electrodeposition.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605512:adma201605512-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605512-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201605512-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201605512-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Secondary sodium–ion and sodium–metal batteries offer multiple advantages over their lithium counterparts for portable storage in electric vehicles and locomotives as well as for stationary storage in power stations.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001"/> Among the motivations for interest in these batteries include the low cost and high natural abundance of sodium, the high theoretical capacity (1166 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), moderate redox potential (−2.71 V vs the standard hydrogen potential) of the sodium anode, and the fact that high‐temperature sodium–sulfur (Na–S) and Na–metal chloride (Na/MeCl<sub>2</sub>) batteries have already been demonstrated to be commercially viable technologies for grid storage and for electrification of rail transportation. On‐field accidents and difficult maintenance of these batteries due to their high operating temperature are a concern for broader deployment in portable devices, robotics, and electric vehicles.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0002"/> Room‐temperature sodium batteries based on high‐voltage intercalation cathodes[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001" style="qv">1d,e</link>] or energetic conversion cathodes (e.g., Na–S or Na–O<sub>2</sub>/CO<sub>2</sub> cells[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001 #adma201605512-bib-0003" style="qv">1b,3</link>]) have already been reported to overcome some of these difficulties;[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001" style="qv">1d</link>] however, the focus on improvements afforded by ambient‐temperature operation typically glosses over fundamental challenges associated with long term stability of a sodium metal anode in liquid electrolytes.</p><p>Recharge of any battery utilizing a sodium metal anode requires repetitive stripping and platting of the metal onto itself. The size difference between sodium ions and sodium atoms subjects the anode to large volumetric and morphological changes during these cycles, which makes the batteries unstable and failure by multiple processes can occur.[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0003 #adma201605512-bib-0004" style="qv">3c,4</link>] First, because of its high reactivity, metallic sodium rarely forms stable solid electrolyte interphases (SEIs); rather it is corroded continuously by aprotic liquid electrolytes upon cycling, which lowers the Coulombic efficiency (CE) and battery lifetime.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0005"/> Second, the uncontrolled nature of the SEI formation reactions leads to significant spatial variations in SEI conductivity, which drives spatial variations in Na deposition during battery recharge, making the metal particularly unstable toward the morphological instability known as dendritic electrodeposition.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0006"/> Finally, due to the softness of the metal and low melting point, dendrite induced short circuits either end in thermal runaway or in sodium dendrites breaking away from the metal electrode to become electrochemically disconnected/orphaned from the metal mass, which also shortens battery lifetime.</p><p>We herein report on the synthesis, chemistry, and interfacial properties of ionic liquid membranes created as SEI films formed directly on metallic sodium through electropolymerization of functional ionic liquid monomers in liquid electrolytes. It is found that such membranes markedly increase the stability of sodium anodes against failure. We also find that unlike Li anodes, which can fail by any of the processes discussed above, Na anodes almost always fail as result of electrolyte degradation and runaway of the cell impedance. Our focus on artificial SEI membranes based on ionic liquids (ILs) is motivated by several considerations, including the large number of previous works which show that some IL chemistries exhibit exceptional chemical and electrochemical stability in contact with reactive metals.[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001 #adma201605512-bib-0006 #adma201605512-bib-0007" style="qv">1b,6,7</link>] Additional motivation comes from recent studies which report that electrolytes in which ILs are used as additives in conventional aprotic liquids are able to stabilize lithium metal anodes against dendrite formation.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0006 #adma201605512-bib-0007"/> Theoretical work suggest that the tethered anions in an IL can improve the stability of Li plating during the battery recharge by acting as a supporting electrolyte.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0008"/> This has been verified by the experimental studies which show that dendrite free electrodeposition can be achieved by uniform ion distribution achieved by contacting lithium to functional glass fibers or solid polymer electrolytes.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0009"/> In this context, the IL additive is thought to reduce the magnitude of destabilizing electric fields near the anode<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0008 #adma201605512-bib-0010"/> by providing a localized supply of anions able to support a high cation flux at the electrode when the cell is polarized. This effect is quite general and is not limited to Li. Carbonate based electrolyte containing around 5–10 vol% 1‐methyl‐3‐propylimidazolium‐chlorate tethered to SiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles have been reported to enhance the stability and cycle life for both Na–S and Na–O<sub>2</sub>/CO<sub>2</sub> cells.[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001 #adma201605512-bib-0003" style="qv">1b,3a</link>].</p><p>Electrochemical polymerization of reactive monomers on metallic surfaces is a well‐established method to fabricate functional polymer thin films for protecting metals<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0010"/> and electronic devices, including sensors.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0011"/> The approach offers several advantages over other polymerization techniques that may also be used to create coatings on metals: (i) it combines polymer synthesis with thin film formation; (ii) it eliminates the need for exogenous oxidants to initiate the polymerization; and (iii) important properties of the membrane, including its thickness, morphology, and porosity are controlled by transport processes in the film itself.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0012"/> An additional benefit is that protective membranes based on co‐polymers can be created in‐situ using combinations of polymerizable molecular addives in a liquid electrolyte, which would make it possible to engineer membranes/artificial SEIs on reactive metals with specific, desired properties. Although the method has not been previously studied to protect reactive alkali metals for battery applications, it is well suited to this application because the transport processes that control film thickness and morphology are related to those that control access of ions to/from the metal surface during charge/discharge processes in a battery. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>A is a schematic representation of the process, showing how it can be used to create an ionic polymer membrane on the surface of a sodium anode. The polymerization reaction is believed to progress via the usual steps (initiation, propagation, and termination) that govern radical and ionic polymerization processes. Initiation occurs during charging, wherein the unsaturated ionic liquid monomers accept electrons to form reactive species. These species react with monomers to increase the size of the active center, propagating the polymerization process and creating a thin, porous film in conformal contact with the electrode.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605512-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605512:adma201605512-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605512-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Formation of polymeric ionic liquid film. A) A schematic drawing of the polymeric ionic liquid film formation on the electrode. The cations bearing the unsaturated compound accept electrons from the electrode during charging and form free radicals. These radicals will initiate and propagate the polymerization process and form a polymer film on the electrode surface. B) SEM, EDS mapping, and C) AFM height image of the stainless steel electrode covered by the polymeric film formed by 1,3‐diallyl imidazolium perchlorate (DAIM) under a constant current of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Scale bar: 20 µm). D,E) Amplitude and the relevant 3D height AFM images of the same film covered on the stainless steel electrode in (B and C).</caption></figure><p>The molecular structure of the IL monomer plays an important role in regulating the structure and morphology of the polymer membrane as well as in controlling the degree of polymerization achieved.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0013"/> Ohno has argued that polymerization of IL molecules may result in degradation of properties of the monomers, including ionic conductivity, due to elevation of the glass transition temperature and a reduced number of mobile ions after covalent bonding of the component ions.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0014"/> In order to achieve optimized adhesion and ionic conductivity, different functional imidazolium cation‐based ionic liquid monomers bearing allyl or vinyl group and perchlorate anion, namely 1‐allyl‐3‐methylimidazolium perchlorate (AMIM), 1,3‐diallyl imidazolium perchlorate (DAIM), 1‐allyl‐3‐vinyl imidazolium perchlorate (AVIM) were synthesized, and their electroinitiated polymerization process on the electrode surface investigated. To characterize the polymeric film formed on the electrode, the pure monomers (ie. bulk polymerization) were dropped onto polypropylene separators sandwiched by two stainless steel electrodes in a coin cell. A constant current of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> was applied to the cells to initiate polymerization and the current maintained until the voltage is observed to begin diverging (see Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). At this point the electrodes are thought to be covered by a protective film that limits further reaction with monomer in the IL electrolyte. The morphology of the IL polymer film was examined by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0001"/>B and Figure S2 in the Supporting Information. Both DAIM and AVIM monomers are able to polymerize with good adhesion and conformal contact to the stainless steel electrode. In contrast, polymerization AMIM is sluggish and the material barely forms a film on the surface due to limited unsaturated component. In the case of DAIM, electropolymerization produced a uniform thin membrane able to cover the rough and scratched surface of the stainless steel electrode. The surface topography of the film was characterized by atomic force microscopy (AFM) in tapping mode. Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0001"/>C shows that on stainless steel the films provide a smooth and uniform height/topography image, which is consistent with what is observed using SEM analysis of the membrane. To assess the membrane thickness, a scratch was made to expose the stainless steel and allow measurement of the height from the stainless steel surface to the upper polymer interface. This approach yielded a membrane thickness of approximately 80 nm (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0001"/>D and Figure S3, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0015"/></p><p>The molecular weight (<i>M</i><sub>w</sub>) and polydispersity index of the electropolymerized IL were determined by means of gel permeation chromatography (GPC) and the results are reported in Table S1 in the Supporting Information. It is seen that whereas AMIM forms oligomers, DAIM and AVIM monomers form high molecular weight polymer. The existence of sp<sup>2</sup> hybridized vinyl group in AVIM makes it more reactive and self‐polymeriable in the presence of an initiating moiety,<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0016"/> leading to much larger <i>M</i><sub>w</sub> and denser membrane films than produced by electropolymerization of DAIM. Thus, while membranes formed by DAIM are rubber‐like with softer consistency and more open morphologies, those based on AVIM have molecular weight about twice as large and less open morphologies. Based on the morphology and stability of the film formed, DAIM was selected for more in‐depth studies. To create membranes suitable for electrochemical studies, DAIM monomer were investigated as additives in liquid electrolytes to form optimized passivation membranes in a variety of configurations on sodium metal anodes. For brevity, we here focus on membranes created in situ on metallic sodium using 20 wt% of the IL monomer as an additive in an electrolyte comprised of 1 <sc>m</sc> sodium perchlorate in ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate (EC/PC‐NaClO<sub>4</sub>).</p><p>To evaluate the stability of the sodium metal anodes passivated with our artificial polymer IL membrane, the Coulombic efficiency (CE) for sodium stripping and platting processes was determined from galvanostatic experiments in a Na/stainless steel cell. Initially, a predetermined amount of sodium (1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) was plated on the stainless steel electrode at a constant current of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>. In the following cycle, a fraction (1/6) of the sodium was striped and plated from the stainless steel repeatedly at the same current density (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>A). Under these conditions, the CE can be calculated based on the simple formula proposed by Aurbach et al.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0017"/> Control cells that do not contain IL monomer in the electrolyte, or which contain the unpolymerizable IL monomer 1‐methyl‐3‐propylimidizolium chlorate (MPIM), were evaluated in the same manner and found to yield very low CE values around 16.7% and, for the cells with no IL in the electrolyte, to fail within the first cycle. This result is expected as the unpassivated sodium metal reacts vigorously and irreversibly with the aprotic liquid electrolyte during electrochemical cycling. The low CE is also consistent with previous reports by Yui et al.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0018"/> and Seh et al.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0005"/> in cycling experiments in carbonate electrolytes in which all of the plated sodium was stripped from the stainless steel electrode each cycle. Consistent with the discussion in the introduction, the authors explain the low CE for sodium metal anodes in terms of formation of nonuniform SEI as well as dendritic growth.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605512-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605512:adma201605512-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605512-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical characterization of the stable SEI on the sodium metal anode. A) Voltage–time profiles of Na/Stainless Steel cells with different IL additives for the cycling efficiency calculation (the cells with DAIM can run stably for over 121 cycles). B) Cell failure time at various current densities derived from constant current polarization test of Na/Na symmetric cell with and without 20 wt% DAIM in the electrolyte. C,D) Nyquist plots for a Na symmetric cell after every 2 h charging at 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> in the electrolyte without and with DAIM.</caption></figure><p>In contrast, electrolytes containing DAIM as an additive exhibit markedly improved Coulombic efficiency with values as high as 95.0% measured at a current density of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>. Additionally, cells containing DAIM exhibit vastly improved stability in long‐term cycling measurements and lower overpotential (Figure S4A, Supporting Information) compared with those containing MPIM. It is important here to note that while MPIM additives in carbonate electrolytes were reported previously to prevent dendrite formation in lithium metal batteries,<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0006"/> the material only has a limited effect in improving the CE of the sodium metal anode, with only a 60% CE being achieved. As the surface of the neat stainless steel electrode is relatively rough, this experiment tentatively confirms our previous argument that DAIM monomer helps form a uniform ion conducting polymeric membrane that not only protects the sodium metal surface, but facilitates transport at the anode.</p><p>A more detailed assessment of the membranes was performed using an aggressive galvanostatic polarization experiment in Na/Na symmetric cells in order to study the role played by the as prepared membranes over a wider range of current densities. Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0002"/>B reports the cell lifetimes at various current densities in the two cases —with and without DAIM additive in the electrolyte. It is seen that the cell lifetime doubles at low current density and improves by at least a factor of three at higher current densities (≥1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>). As important is our observation that unlike typical lithium metal anodes, wherein cell failure by internal short‐circuit is evidenced by a large voltage drop during galvanostatic polarization,[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0007" style="qv">7d</link>] failure of sodium anodes is almost always evidenced by a diverging voltage (Figure S4B, Supporting Information), indicative of electrolyte degradation. Comparison of the voltage profiles at 0.1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure S4B, Supporting Information) shows a higher polarization voltage with fluctuations for the cells in the neat electrolyte, indicative of severe side reactions between sodium and electrolyte. The results reported here are to our knowledge the first to show that sodium metal cells are more likely to fail by electrolyte depletion than by dendrite‐induced short circuits. This observation is thought to derive from the relative softness of metallic sodium (room‐temperature hardness: 0.5 MPa and shear modulus: 3.3 GPa) relative to Li;[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0008 #adma201605512-bib-0019" style="qv">8a,19</link>]. It indicates that dendrite induced short‐circuiting and associated phenomena such as thermal runaway are likely not as important for sodium metal battery technology as they are for Li metal cells, and provides insights into the role the IL membranes may play in stabilizing the sodium anode.</p><p>The stability of the Na/Na symmetric cell containing DAIM monomer additives in the electrolyte was also investigated by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy experiments. To evaluate the interfacial reactivity of the sodium metal surface during polarization, impedance of the cells containing 0 or 20 wt% DAIM was measured as a function of time following intervals of 2 h charging at 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0002"/>C,D). The interfacial resistance was found to be unstable for the control cell, displaying erratic values and transients from measurement to measurement. In contrast, in the 20 wt% DAIM case, the interfacial resistance steadily increases, indicative the formation of a stable SEI layer. The electrochemical initiated polymerization process in the presence of sodium anode and electrolyte was further investigated by linear‐sweep voltammetry (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The oxidation currents after the breakdown voltage are seen to be much lower for IL containing electrolytes than for the control electrolyte, indicating that the IL containing electrolytes have better electrochemical stability than the neat liquid electrolytes.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0020"/> It is also noted that small oxidization peaks were observed between 2 and 4 V for the electrolytes containing DAIM and AVIM, possibly indicative of an electron‐transfer‐induced polymerization process. The peak current increases linearly with the increase of the root mean square of the scan rate, indicating a diffusion‐limited controlled polymerization process.[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0001" style="qv">1b</link>]</p><p>To verify our hypothesis that the polymeric IL film helps stabilize sodium metal and prevent side reaction between electrode and aprotic electrolyte, an in situ optical microscopy technique was applied to directly visualize how sodium deposits in a quartz cuvette optical Na/Na symmetric cell (Figure S6A, Supporting Information). A constant current of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> was applied to polarize one electrode and the deposition of Na visualized as a function of time. We choose a fixed current density of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for these experimets to make the study consistent with our previous gross analyses of coin cell performance improvements provided by the IL membranes. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>A shows light microscopic images of the sodium electrode at different deposition times in a 1 <sc>m</sc> EC/PC‐NaClO<sub>4</sub> electrolyte. Due to the softness of sodium metal, the pristine electrode surface is never smooth. As the deposition progresses, the electrode is seen to become gradually covered by two types of deposits—thin, shiny fiber‐like and bulky, dark mushroom‐like structures, which form in a dynamic swinging movement manner, indicating that the electrolyte is in motion. Both types of deposits grow not in a specific direction but rather appear to choose their direction randomly, which is probably a reflection of the unevenness of the electrode surface. We note that similar shiny, fiber‐like deposits were reported previously on sodium metal electrodes at a very low current density of 57 µA cm<sup>−2</sup><link href="#adma201605512-bib-0018"/> and on lithium electrodeposition as well.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0021"/> The bulky, darker sodium deposit observed here are to our knowledge the first time such deposits have been reported for sodium. We tentatively attribute their formation to the uncontrolled side reaction of the metal with liquid electrolyte. These deposits, however, appear to play a much more significant role in the stability of a sodium electrode. They grow faster, break easily and are observed to quickly withdraw from the electrode mass and float away randomly in the electrolyte. This so‐called “dead sodium” is responsible for the low Coulombic efficiency of sodium electrodes in liquid electrolytes.[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0005 #adma201605512-bib-0008" style="qv">5,8b</link>] As seen in Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0003"/>B), addition of DAIM to the electrolyte produces large improvement in the stability of sodium deposition at the same current density. It is obvious for example that the large, dark mushroom‐like deposits are completely eliminated during the whole process of deposition, which probably indicates that the in situ formed polymeric ionic film can protect sodium and eliminate the side reaction with liquid electrolyte. The needle‐like deposits nonetheless continue to exist, but it is seen that the quantity of this type of dendrite is much smaller than that of the control and the growth rate is less than half that of the bulky dendrites (Figure S6B, Supporting Information). The grows of the needle deposits may be related to the effective pore size (≈1 µm) of the polymer film, which was approximated from the storage modulus (4.05 × 10<sup>−3</sup> Pa) of the polymeric DAIM. As the effective pore is in the similar length scale to that of the needles and much smaller than mushroom deposits, it is possible that the membranes function by selectively blocking the growth pathways for the mushroom‐like deposits, while offering little resistance to the needles, which may penetrate through the film and continue to grow.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605512-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605512:adma201605512-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605512-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Visualization of the electrochemical sodium deposition. B) Light microscopy images of the sodium deposition in the same conditions with (A) in 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO4 in EC/PC with 20 wt% DAIM as an additive (scale bar: 50 µm). C,D) SEM images of the sodium metal surface after 10 h electrodeposition in a symmetric coin cell without and with DAIM additive in the electrolyte (scale bar: 20 µm). E,F) C 1s XPS analysis of the sodium metal surface in the same condition with C and D, respectively.</caption></figure><p>The condition and environment are quite different between coin cells and optical cells due to the assembly pressure and existence of separators in the coin cells. Based on a recent theoretical study of dendrite growth in elastic solid electrolyte media, we hypothesize that with the help of mechanical pressure and constraints imposed by a separator that prevents execessive growth and breakage of the bulky sodium electrodeposits, metallic sodium can be an effective anode once the side reaction with liquid electrolytes are eliminated by protecting sodium.[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0008" style="qv">8a</link>] To verify this hypothesis, the morphology of the sodium metal anode after galvonastatic polarization and after ten cycles of stripping and platting in coin cells were examined by SEM (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0003"/>C,D, and Figure S7, Supporting Information). As expected, sodium anodes polarized in liquid electrolytes containing DAIM exhibit uniform and flat surface morphology with a film formed on top, indicative of dendrite free electrode deposition with the help of pressure and separator. In the absence of the DAIM additive, the sodium electrode was quickly oxidized when transferring it to the SEM sample holder, indicating that in addition to protecting sodium from parasitic reactions with liquid electrolytes, the film provides some protection against reaction with species such as O<sub>2</sub> in air. Though the MPIM and AVIM additives in the electrolyte seem to be beneficial compared with the additive‐free electrolytes, these additives are not as effective as DAIM. X‐Ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis of the C 1s spectrom for the control sodium metal and sodium electrode deposited in electrolytes containing DAIM confirms the existence of IL components on the sodium surface (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0003"/>D,E).<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0022"/></p><p>In order to evaluate the suitability of the protected sodium anode for room‐temperature sodium metal batteries, the sodium anode created after 10 h deposition in the presence of 20 wt% DAIM in the electrolyte in a Na/Na symmetric cell was transfered to a Na–Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and Na–polyacrylonitrile–sulfur (SPAN) composite half‐cells and its stability investigated under galvanostatic conditions. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> is attractive because it exhibits a high intercalation potential of around 3.4 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na), which makes Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> a good candidate for next generation cathode materials for electrical energy storage, however, due to the larger ionic radius of sodium, it is a challenge to achieve stable Na ion extraction and insertion in the material over long‐term cycling.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0023"/> Previous reports have shown that fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) electrolyte additives are effective in creating a stable passivation layer between electrode and electrolyte interphases[<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0023" style="qv">23b,c</link>], however FEC decomposition products include hazardous gases such as hydrogen fluoride (HF).<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0024"/> Therefore, it is urgent to develop alternative methods to stabilized this type of cell. SPAN composite cathode are of interest, because these anodes have been reported to deliver high storage capacity and stable cycling in lithium/sulfur batteries employing aprotic carbonate electrolytes.<link href="#adma201605512-bib-0025"/> For these reasons, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and SPAN serve as ideal candidates for investigating the electrochemical stability of the protected sodium anode in rechargeable high‐voltage and high‐energy sodium batteries.</p><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>A–C report the electrochemical characteristics of Na–Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cells based on the DAIM treated Na metal anodes. A floating point test (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0004"/>A) from 3 to 5 V provides the time‐dependent current response for the cells. It is seen that cells based on the protected sodium electrode show no evidence of chemical reaction up to at least 4.8 V, while an irregular and noisy current response is apparent for the control cell over almost the entire voltage range. The improved cell stability directly relates to the reduced side reaction between sodium anode and aprotic electrolyte; it also reduces the overpotential during galvanostatic cycling (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0004"/>B), which results in higher cycling stability and Coulombic efficiency (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0004"/>C). When the anode is protected by a polymerized IL membrane, both the capacity and efficiency rise, with the Coulombic efficiency exceeding 96% and capacity maintaining at 97 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 160 cycles. In contrast, the control cell exhibits lower, variable Coulombic efficiency and a quick decrease of discharge capacity to below 20 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in 30 cycles. We believe these observations are direct benefits of the tethered and uniform polymeric film that continuous supply ions near the electrode and prevent sodium metal from reacting with liquid electrolyte.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605512-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605512:adma201605512-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605512-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Full cell application for a high voltage Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> sodium ion battery and high energy SPAN sodium metal battery. A) Constant voltage–charge profile of the Na–Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cells with pristine sodium anode and protected sodium anode in a carbonate electrolyte maintained at 3, 3.2, 3.4 and 5 V for 2 h at room temperature. B) The voltage profile and C) Cycling performance of the Na–Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cells with and without protected sodium metal anode (inset) at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> of the active material in the cathode. D) Cycling performance of the Na–SPAN cells with and without protected sodium metal anode (inset) at 0.2 C (1 C = 1675 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) based on sulfur in the cathode.</caption></figure><p>We also evaluated application of the IL‐membrane‐protected sodium anode in Na–SPAN batteries. As SPAN has a high theoretical capacity, more coulombs will be applied to the cell during cycling. Therefore, it is important to evaluate the effect of the sodium anode protection in these cells. As expected, the protected cell gives rise to an almost 100% Coulombic efficiency and a reversible discharge capacity for over 100 cycles. In contrast, the control cell exhibits random Coulombic efficiency and the capacity drops to 200 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201605512-fig-0004"/>D and Figure S8, Supporting Information). The failure in the Na–SPAN cell could be attributed to the side reaction with the electrolyte during cell charging, as the voltage profile is noisy and the efficiency is very low. These results underscore the importance of protecting the sodium anode in sodium metal batteries.</p><p>In summary, we report ionic membranes created on a sodium metal electrode by in‐situ electroinitiated polymerization of imidazolium‐type ionic liquids containing unsaturated functional groups. We show that such membranes can be used to protect sodium metal anodes against parasitic reactions with liquid carbonate electrolytes without compromising ion transport in the SEI. The protected anodes exhibit high Coulombic efficiency and stable long‐term cycling even at relatively high current densities. We use spectroscopic and analytical tools to show that the membranes form as conformal coatings on sodium that are optimally approximately 50nm thick. Additionally, direct visualization of sodium deposition by means of optical microscopy and indirect studies using electrochemical analysis show that the membranes stabilize deposition of sodium against dendrite formation. It is argued that the IL membranes achieve the latter function by serving as a tethered supporting electrolyte to reduce the electric field at the anode and stabilize deposition of Na. Application of the protected anodes in inexpensive rechargeable sodium metal batteries based on high voltage or high specific capacity cathodes is also demonstrated. Our findings underscore the synergistic benefits of protecting reactive metal anodes using ionic coatings. Further investigations are needed to fully understand the effect of different tethered polymeric ions, including co‐polymers formed in‐situ by electropolymerization of mixtures, on stabilizing parasitic chemical reactions and electrodeposition processes at reactive metal anodes.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201605512-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>S.W. and S.C. contributed equally to this work.
