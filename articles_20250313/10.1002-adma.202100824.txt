Herein, the electrochemical behavior of three organosulfur compounds, i.e., 4,4′‐thiobisbenzenethiol (TBBT), 1,4‐benzenedithiol (1,4‐BDT), and diphenyl disulfide (DPDS), is revealed in room‐temperature rechargeable sodium (Na) batteries, which show significantly improved performances when sodiated Nafion membranes are used. Large oligomers of organosulfur can be formed during charging, and they are readily blocked by the nanosized ion‐conducting clusters in the Nafion membrane. In addition, large organosulfur monomers can also be blocked. Only 5.4% of TBBT diffuses through the Nafion membrane after 800 h. The Na|TBBT cell sustains 77% of the theoretical capacity after 300 cycles (2420 h). Moreover, the Na|TBBT redox flow cell shows promising rechargeability. Due to the medium molecular size, the organosulfur oligomers are expected to provide a new avenue to develop high‐capacity chalcogen cathodes, besides inorganic S and S‐containing polymers.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202100824-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>4,4′‐Thiobisbenzenethiol (TBBT) and its oligomers formed in situ in charging have large size, and therefore can be blocked by the nanosized ion‐conducting clusters in sodiated Nafion membranes, leading to enhanced performance of sodium batteries. The Na|TBBT cell retains 77% of the theoretical capacity of TBBT after 300 cycles (2420 h). In addition, the Na|TBBT flow cell also shows promising rechargeability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100824-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202100824-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202100824-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have made a great contribution to the development of portable devices and electric vehicles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Li ions are intercalated in graphite or transition metal oxides accompanied by the electron gaining and losing to accomplish the storage of electric energy. However, the existence of these “host” materials sets a low limit of energy density. It is urgent to develop high‐capacity electrodes based on multi‐electron redox chemistries to meet the challenge brought by the rapidly developing mobile phones and electric vehicles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Organosulfur is a class of promising high‐capacity electrode materials having diverse structures and functional groups.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As early as 1980s, the organosulfur compounds were introduced in energy storage systems,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> but underwent a slowly developing process because of the inferior energy density to that of transition metal oxides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In 2016, Wu et al. investigated the electrochemical behavior of dimethyl trisulfide in Li batteries, and obtained relatively stable cycling performance using carbon nanotube paper current collector.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the following work, the author group found that the organosulfur molecules containing multiple sulfur groups could promote the generation of intermolecular cyclic organosulfur compounds by the linkage of SS bonds in the charge process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The oligomers will be decomposed into lithium organosulfides by the breaking of SS bonds in the discharge process and the charge/discharge processes are quite reversible. The examples include the three isomers of benzenedithiolates (BDT), i.e., 1,2‐BDT, 1,3‐BDT, and 1,4‐BDT. Dimers, trimers, or tetramers are formed in the charge process.</p><p>Sodium (Na) batteries are an attractive candidate for large‐scale energy storage because of the abundant resources and low cost of the Na element.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the theoretical specific energy of the room temperature (RT) Na–S batteries can reach 1274 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, which is much higher than that of LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the shuttle effect is severe, inducing the violent fade of the capacity, due to the active chemistry of metallic Na. Various strategies have been proposed to suppress the shuttle effect. The adsorption and catalysis effect of the host materials, construction of functional interphase on metallic Na showed efficacy on ameliorating the cycling performance of Na–S batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The S‐containing polymers can avoid the shuttle effect because of the low solubility, but suffer from the slow kinetics due to the solid‐phase conversion reaction and relatively low content of S.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, minimizing the shuttle effect by separator modification or using ion‐conducting membranes is also a promising approach.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nafion membrane composes of inert poly tetra fluoroethylene (PTFE) skeleton and ion‐clusters with sulfonate groups, which can conduct ions. The nanosized ion‐clusters enable Nafion membranes to be effective in blocking the shuttle effect of polysulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0011 #adma202100824-bib-0012"><sup>11a,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To the best of our knowledge, there is no promising rechargeable Na–organosulfur batteries at RT reported in literatures to date.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0004 #adma202100824-bib-0013"><sup>4,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Visco et al. investigated the electrochemical behavior of tetraethylthiuram disulfide in Na batteries at high temperature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Very recently, Bhargav and Manthiram showed the voltage profile of the first cycle of a Na battery with diisopropyl xanthogen polysulfide as the cathode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Herein, we propose to prompt Na–organosulfur batteries with three different organosulfur compounds, i.e., 4,4′‐thiobisbenzenethiol (TBBT), 1,4‐benzenedithiol (1,4‐BDT), and diphenyl disulfide (DPDS), through the inhibition effect of Nafion membrane. Dimer or trimer of these organosulfur molecules can be formed during charging through the formation of SS bonds. The enlarged molecular and the pristine big organosulfur are difficult to diffuse through the Nafion membrane. Therefore, the Na|TBBT cell shows stable cycling performance of over 2400 h, with 77% of the theoretical capacity sustained. The utility of organosulfur is remarkably higher than those of inorganic sulfur cathodes in literature. Due to the medium molecular size, the organosulfur oligomers are expected to supply a new avenue to develop high‐capacity chalcogen cathodes, besides inorganic S and S‐containing polymers. We believe that this work can provide inspiration for the shuttle suppression of soluble organic electrode materials, leading to high performance of rechargeable alkali‐metal batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100824-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>First, we compare the electrochemical behavior of the organosulfur compounds in Na half cells with Nafion or polypropylene (PP) separator by cyclic voltammetry. TBBT is selected as the organosulfur representative. As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, there are two pairs of redox peaks between 1.8 and 2.3 V for TBBT with the PP separator. The area of oxidation peaks is much bigger than that of reduction peaks and a stable current of 0.7 A g<sup>−1</sup> is sustained after 2.3 V in the positive scanning process, which is assigned to the shuttle current of TBBT.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0011"><sup>11c</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It demonstrates that the severe shuttle phenomenon occurs for the Na–organosulfur cell with the PP separator. Then a sodiated Nafion membrane was used. The optical photo and IR spectra of the prepared Nafion membrane are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S1</link> in the Supporting Information. A broad peak at 1710 cm<sup>−1</sup> shifts to 1662 cm<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating the exchange of H<sup>+</sup> with Na<sup>+</sup> and confirming the Nafion membrane endowed with the conductivity of Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0011 #adma202100824-bib-0016"><sup>11d,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> When the Nafion membrane is used, there are two redox peaks very close to each other and the peak areas are also comparable. Meanwhile, the shuttle current in the positive scan is negligible. It indicates that the Nafion membrane can significantly prohibit the shuttle effect of organosulfur. Simultaneously, 1,4‐BDT and DPDS are also checked (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). The Nafion membrane shows efficacy on the shuttle suppression for these two molecules as well.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100824-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100824-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) The cyclic voltammograms of Na–TBBT cells with the PP separator or Nafion membrane. b,c) The voltage profiles of the three Na–organosulfur cells with the PP separator (b) and Nafion membrane (c). d) The cycling performance of the three Na–organosulfur cells with Nafion membranes.</caption></figure><p>Then, we investigated the cycling performance of Na–organosulfur cells using the PP separator. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0001">1b</link>, the Na|TBBT and Na|DPDS cells show the initial discharge capacity of only about 50 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much lower than the theoretical ones. For the Na|1,4‐BDT cell, the first discharge capacity reaches 180 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, but still less than half of the theoretical capacity (377 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). It indicates that the organosulfur molecules may have shuttled to the Na anode before and during the cell operation. The three Na–organosulfur cells, unsurprisingly, all show severe overcharging in the first cycle. The cells cannot be charged to the upper cutoff voltage even when the charging capacity reaches 1000 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. It can be deduced that the severe shuttle effect prevents the batteries to be rechargeable. In sharp contrast, the overcharging phenomena were immediately inhibited when the Nafion membrane was used (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0001">1c</link>). Both Na|TBBT and Na|DPDS cells show a pair of platforms at 2.1 V in the charging/discharging processes, respectively. The reversible capacity of TBBT reaches above 210 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is very close to the theoretical value (214 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). However, the discharge capacity of DPDS is only about 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The Na|1,4‐BDT cell shows two plateaus in the charge/discharge processes. It is similar to that in lithium batteries, but with a decrease of the output voltage about 0.3 V which is mainly ascribed to the equilibrium potential difference between Li‐ and Na‐metal anodes. Moreover, the Na|TBBT cell illustrates very stable cycling performance (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0001">1d</link>). The discharge capacity gradually increases to the theoretical capacity and then keeps no change in the first 100 cycles. After 300 cycles, the reversible capacity of 165 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, equivalent of 77% of the theoretical one, is retained and the total cycling time reaches 2420 h. In addition, the Na|1,4‐BDT and Na|DPDS cells display inferior cycling stability with lower initial capacities and faster fading rate compared with the Na|TBBT cell. However, they are still greatly improved as those cells with PP separators cannot even be cycled.</p><p>It is worthwhile to mention that the utility and stability of TBBT in this study is superior to the typical inorganic sulfur cathode in Na batteries. As summarized in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S3</link> in the Supporting Information, the RT Na–S battery can generally deliver the capacity less than half of the theoretical value after 100 cycles. Moreover, the Na–S batteries with Nafion membrane can only deliver the capacity less than 50% of theoretical one with the longest lifespan of 100 cycles, further emphasizing the advantage of organosulfur compounds.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0011"><sup>11a,c,d</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The utility of organosulfur is even superior to those of Li–S batteries (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). Although the present specific capacity of TBBT is lower than that of inorganic S, the high utility and stability of TBBT during cycling provides an alternative direction to design stable high‐capacity chalcogen electrodes.</p><p>The cycled cells were dissembled to intuitively check the degree of shuttle effect. It can be seen that the surface of the Na‐metal anode looks yellow with PP separator, which is the common color of organosulfides (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> and Figures <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S4–S6</link>, Supporting Information). The energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectra also show that the overall S content is high. On the contrary, the surfaces of metallic Na with the Nafion membrane look lighter (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0002">2b</link>–<link href="#adma202100824-fig-0002">d</link>). It indicates that Nafion membrane can help minimize the shuttle of organosulfides. Moreover, TBBT with Nafion membrane exhibits the lowest S content and DPDS shares the highest S content, which is consistent with the cycling performances of the three organosulfur compounds. Through these electrochemical characterizations, it can be found that, compared with PP membrane, Nafion membrane shows irreplaceable advantages.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100824-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100824-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image and optical photo (inset) of the Na‐metal anode in a Na|TBBT cell with the PP separator. b–d) SEM images and optical photos (insets) of the Na‐metal anode in Na|TBBT (b), Na|1,4‐BDT (c), and Na|DPDS (d) cells with Nafion membranes. For the cells with PP separator, the cells were dissembled at the charging capacity of 1000 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the first charging process. The cells with Nafion membranes were dissembled at fully charged state after 20 cycles. The scale bar in the four images is 2 µm.</caption></figure><p>To explore the reaction mechanism of organosulfur in Na batteries and elucidate the difference of cycling stability and shuttle effect between the three compounds, mass spectroscopy was introduced to identify the charge/discharge products. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>–<link href="#adma202100824-fig-0003">c</link>, it is found that the most intense peaks of the mass/charge fraction (<i>m</i>/<i>z</i>) of the charged products for TBBT, 1,4‐BDT, and DPDS are 495.9552, 419.9191, and 218.0189, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100824-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100824-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) The mass spectra of the charged products of Na|TBBT (a), Na|1,4‐BDT (b), and Na|DPDS (c) cells after the first cycle. Their van der Waals sizes are indicated in the corresponding insets. d) The schematic of the Na–organosulfur battery with Nafion membrane. The inset is the TEM image of Pb‐stained Nafion membrane. The relative size of the organosulfur compounds and the Nafion membrane is exaggerated to emphasize the size effect.</caption></figure><p>Besides, it was found that organosulfur molecules can form oligomers through SS bonds in the charging process for Li batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, combined with their own molecular structures, we can determine the corresponding charged product of TBBT to be the cyclic dimer 2, 4, 5, 7, 9, 10‐hexathia‐1, 3, 6, 8(1, 4)‐tetrabenzenacyclodecaphane. The one for 1,4‐BDT is the cyclic trimer 2,3,5,6,8,9‐hexathia‐1,4,7(1,3)‐tribenzenacyclononaphane. For DPDS, the charge product is still DPDS. In the discharge processes, the sodium organosulfides are formed through the breakage of SS bonds, which is confirmed by the results of mass spectra (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information). Therefore, the overall charge/discharge processes of TBBT and the other two can be descried in Equation (1) and Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S8</link> in the Supporting Information, respectively. It should be noted that there are two charge/discharge plateaus in the voltage profiles of the Na|1,4‐BDT cell while only one charge/discharge plateau appears for Na|TBBT and Na|DPDS. The intermediate with one S radical and one thiol group is found at the end of the first plateau during charging (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information). It indicates that the two plateaus correspond to the two‐step sodiation/desodiation of the two sulfur sites of the 1,4‐BDT, as the length of the two platforms are almost equal.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0006"><sup>6a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the reversibility of the oligomer formation is confirmed from the mass spectrum of the charged product of the Na|TBBT cell after 50 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S10</link>, Supporting Information)</p><p><inlineGraphic href="urn:x-wiley:23667486:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-gra-0002" alt="image" location="graphic/adma202100824-gra-0002.png"/></p><p>After the clarity of the charge/discharge processes of the organosulfur molecules in Na batteries, we can see that the molecular sizes of the charge products for TBBT and 1,4‐BDT are remarkably enlarged. The S radicals, more than one in a single molecule ((SRS)<i><sub>n</sub></i>, R = alkyl or aromatic groups), can link the organosulfur compounds together, forming much larger dimers or trimers. Based on calculation, the van der Waals sizes of the corresponding oligomers of TBBT, 1,4‐BDT, and DPDS during charging are on the order of TBBT (13.16 Å) &gt; 1,4‐BDT (11.46 Å) &gt; DPDS (11.18 Å), as shown in the insets of Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0003">3a</link>–<link href="#adma202100824-fig-0003">c</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S11a–c</link> in the Supporting Information. The sizes of the discharged products also follow the same order (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S11d–f</link>, Supporting Information). The size order is consistent with the performance stability order shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0001">1d</link>. Therefore, it is believed that large molecules can be blocked by the Nafion membrane. To have a view of the microstructure of the Nafion membrane, transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed on the Pb‐stained sample, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S12</link> in the Supporting Information and the inset of Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0003">3d</link>. The dark dots, i.e., Pb‐stained ion‐clusters, can be seen. The diameters of these ion‐clusters vary between 4.4 and 12.5 nm. However, it is known that they are connected by ion channels with a diameter of 1–2 nm. Therefore, considering that the compounds that can form larger oligomers during charging have the weaker shuttle phenomena, we anticipate that bigger molecules are harder to penetrate the Nafion film due to the size effect (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0003">3d</link>). Consequently, the shuttle effect is more significantly suppressed as the molecular size becomes bigger. It preliminarily confirms that the enlarged size of the in situ formed oligomer during charging is of great importance to the cycling performance.</p><p>Furthermore, an H‐type tank was designed to check the shuttle effect of pristine organosulfur compounds with the PP separator or Nafion membrane. As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>, the tank was filled with organosulfur‐containing electrolytes on one side while the blank electrolyte was injected into the tank on the other side. Then, the shuttle effect of organosulfur molecules can be evaluated by the spectroscopic intensity of organosulfur in the control electrolyte. The strong peaks at 1574 and 1578 cm<sup>−1</sup> correspond to the fundamental benzene ring vibration of TBBT and 1,4‐BDT, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S13</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It was adopted as the characteristic peak as no benzene ring exists in the control electrolytes. The intensity is normalized according to the peak at 1470 cm<sup>−1</sup>, assigned as bending mode of –CH<sub>2</sub>– in the solvent of tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0004">4b</link>, the peak intensities (1574 or 1578 cm<sup>−1</sup>) of TBBT and 1,4‐BDT with the PP separator increase sharply within 48 h. However, only very weak peaks can be seen over 2 weeks with the Nafion membrane and the peaks are not strong after 30 days. The shuttle effect was quantitatively expressed by the relative intensity of fundamental benzene ring vibration (1574 or 1578 cm<sup>−1</sup>). The level of shuttle increases with the relative peak intensity and the fully shuttle state corresponds to the half of the peak intensity at initial state as the electrolyte was added into the both sides of the tank with the same volume. The shuttle degree of TBBT and 1,4‐BDT reaches 100% and 50% after 48 h with PP separator while only 10.8% and 44.4% after more than 30 days with Nafion membrane. It confirms the efficacy of Nafion membrane on the suppression of shuttle of the organosulfur molecules. The same trends can be seen in the UV–vis spectra (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S14</link>, Supporting Information). Moreover, the lower shuttle degree of TBBT than 1,4‐BDT with Nafion membrane confirms the size of the organosulfur monomers is also of great importance on the cycling performance, as the size of TBBT is obviously larger than 1,4‐BDT (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S15</link>, Supporting Information). It is consistent with that TBBT shares superior cycling stability to 1,4‐BDT (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0001">1d</link>), the sizes of whose charge products are comparable while the discharge product of TBBT is much larger than that of 1,4‐BDT (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information). In other words, both the size of organosulfur monomers (discharge products) and oligomers (charge products) are influencing the suppression of the shuttle effect. It is well known that, for inorganic sulfur cathode, smaller sulfur molecules can promise better cycling performance because of the absence of the polysulfides (S<i><sub>n</sub></i><sup>2−</sup>, 4 &lt; <i>n</i> &lt; 8) with high solubility.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Herein, we propose that bigger organosulfur compounds with the structure that can be enlarged during charging ((SRS)<i><sub>n</sub></i>, R = alkyl or aromatic groups), foreshadow better cycling performance.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100824-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100824-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) The schematic diagram of the H‐type tank for the Raman test. b,c) The Raman spectra of the control electrolyte in the H‐type tank with PP separator (b) or Nafion membrane (c) at different holding times. d) The shuttle degree and relative Raman intensity of TBBT and 1,4‐BDT.</caption></figure><p>In light of the superior electrochemical behavior of TBBT in Na batteries with Nafion membrane, we investigated its potential application in redox flow batteries, which are promising in large‐scale energy storage for solar and wind energy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Na|TBBT cells were first operated at enhanced current densities. As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>, the specific capacity first increases and then stabilizes at 203 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for over 100 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Afterward, the specific capacity slowly fades to 151 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 450th cycle. When the current density was set to 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity of 188 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be achieved at the 50th cycle, and the capacity of 140 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is attained at 450th cycle. The total cycling time reaches 1750 and 740 h, respectively. It illustrates the excellent cycling stability. Besides, the high solubility of organic molecules in the solvents is of great importance, determining the overall energy density of the resultant organic redox flow batteries. The TBBT shows the solubility of 1.2 and 1.3 <sc>m</sc> in DME and TEGDME at RT, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S16</link>, Supporting Information). Therefore, a high theoretical capacity of 69.5 Ah L<sup>−1</sup> could be reached. Following that, the voltage profiles of the Na|TBBT cells were compared at different concentrations. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0005">5b</link>, the discharge capacities of 10.5, 16.2, and 28.6 Ah L<sup>−1</sup> are obtained with the concentrations of 0.2, 0.4, and 0.8 <sc>m</sc> at the 10th cycle, respectively. The voltage hysteresis between the charge/discharge process is just slightly increased, indicating no severe polarization occurs. Moreover, the cycling stability at high concentrations is also encouraging. The reversible capacity is above 15.0 Ah L<sup>−1</sup> (150 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) after 200 cycles with 0.4 <sc>m</sc> TBBT and the capacity of 24.0 Ah L<sup>−1</sup> (120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) is attained after 50 cycles with 0.8 <sc>m</sc> TBBT (Figures <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S17</link> and <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S18</link>, Supporting Information). The cycling time exceeds 650 and 570 h, respectively. Besides, the good rate performance of the Na|TBBT cell is achieved (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information). It exhibits the reversible capacity of 145 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the high current of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and then the reversible capacity of 207 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is obtained when the current is set back to 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100824-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100824:adma202100824-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100824-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) The capacity retention of the Na|TBBT cells with the Nafion membranes at high rates. b) The voltage profiles of the Na|TBBT cells with different concentrations of TBBT. c) The comparison of the organosulfur compounds with inorganic S and S‐containing polymers as cathodes of rechargeable batteries.</caption></figure><p>Lastly, as a proof of concept, the RT Na–organosulfur flow cell was also evaluated in a continuous flow mode. The schematic and optical photo of the flow cell are displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S20a</link>,<link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">b</link>, respectively, in the Supporting Information. A peristaltic pump was adopted to exchange the electrolyte between the electrochemical cell and the catholyte reservoir at 12 mL min<sup>−1</sup>. With 0.8 <sc>m</sc> TBBT, the flow cell can stably work for 40 h (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S20c</link>, Supporting Information). To the best of our knowledge, this is the first demonstration of redox flow battery using the organosulfur molecules as the cathode and metallic Na as the anode. The cycling performance is also among the best ones of the Na‐ and Li‐based organic flow cells in literatures (Table <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). It unravels the possibility of designing and exploiting the diverse S‐containing organic molecules for the low‐cost large‐scale energy storage.</p><p>It is known that the inorganic S<sub>8</sub> compounds have the highest S content, but suffer from the severe shuttle phenomenon. Even though the Nafion membrane was adopted, the capacity retention is still not satisfying.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0011 #adma202100824-bib-0012 #adma202100824-bib-0021"><sup>11a,12,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The S‐containing polymers can avoid the shuttle effect, but the low S content in the polymer skeleton limits the specific capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0010"><sup>10a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, the reaction kinetics are also sluggish, deteriorating the rate performance. It should be highlighted that the organosulfur compounds have the medium molecular sizes compared to the inorganic S and S‐containing polymers, determining that they have the balanced properties. The solubility of organosulfur oligomers in liquid electrolyte may improve the reaction kinetics<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and the S content is also tunable in a wide range (Figure <link href="#adma202100824-fig-0005">5c</link>). Moreover, because of the significantly increased molecular size, the notorious shuttle effect can be readily suppressed by a membrane with nanometer‐scale channels, not limited to Nafion membrane. A nanoporous polybenzimidazole (PBI) membrane was tried in the organosulfur cells, which has already been adopted in fuel and flow cells.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100824-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100824-supitem-0001">S21</link> in the Supporting Information, the Na|TBBT cell with the PBI membrane can deliver the reversible capacity of 150 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 80 cycles. Future work should be focused on the high‐capacity organosulfur compounds and low‐cost membranes with nanoscale ionic channels.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100824-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We have adopted organosulfur molecules as the cathode in rechargeable Na‐metal batteries at RT. The fundamental electrochemistry of these three organosulfur compounds (TBBT, 1,4‐BDT, and DPDS) is unraveled and accordingly their electrochemical behaviors are understood. It is uncovered that the organosulfur molecules that form large‐size charged products can have better cycling stability, which is due to the blockage of the nanosized ion‐clusters in the Nafion membrane. The large TBBT and enlarged dimer during charging eventually enable the stable and high‐utilization cycling performance. After 300 cycles (≈2420 h), 77% of the theoretical capacity of TBBT remained. As a proof of concept, we demonstrate the rechargeability of a Na|TBBT redox flow cell, supplying alternatives for large‐scale energy storage. We believe that the nature of large‐size organosulfur and the size‐enlarging strategy by the in situ formation of organosulfur oligomers supply a new avenue to develop high‐capacity chalcogen cathodes, besides inorganic S and S‐containing polymers. This work can inspire more interest in designing and exploiting alternative electrode materials for alkali‐metal and redox flow batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100824-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>S.T. and Q.C. contributed equally to this work.
