As a typical redox‐active organic electrode materials, pyrene‐4, 5, 9, 10‐tetraone (PT) shows high theoretical capacity due to the rich carbonyl active sites. But its electrochemical behavior in secondary batteries still needs further exploration. Herein, PT‐based linear polymers (PPTS) is synthesized with thioether bond as bridging group and then employed as an anode material for lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) and sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). As expected, PPTS shows improved conductivity and insolubility in the non‐aqueous electrolyte. When used as an anode material for LIBs, PPTS delivers a high reversible specific capacity of 697.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and good rate performance (335.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>). Moreover, a reversible specific capacity of 205.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> could be obtained as an anode material for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>An aromatic carbonyl compound‐linked linear polymer (PPTS)</b> has been synthesized and applied as an organic anode material for lithium‐ion batteries and sodium‐ion batteries, which exhibits better reversible capacities, good cycling stability and outstanding rate performance compared with the monomer (PT).<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have dominated the portable electronic device market and are favored by electric vehicles (EVs), smart grids and large‐scale energy storage applications.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> The rapid evolution of EVs has prompted the pursuit of high‐performance LIBs, especially with environmentally friendly, high energy density and low cost. As a response, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have become a promising energy storage system (ESS) due to their rich sodium resources, environmental friendliness and the similar electrochemical storage mechanism with LIBs.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> However, the current electrode materials of commercial LIBs/SIBs are mainly use metal oxides and graphite, which faces problems such as low specific capacity, limited resources, environmental pollution, and high cost.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0007"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Therefore, there is an urgent need to exploit environmentally sound and superior electrochemistry performance materials for ESSs.</p><p>Organic materials have gained much attention for ESSs in terms of the beneficial characteristics: natural abundance, environmental friendliness, produces in minimal waste, high specific capacity and system security.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Among these organic materials, carbonyl‐containing conjugated compounds have been developed by researchers for ESSs due to their high theoretical capacity and multiple active sites.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0019"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Unfortunately, the inevitable existence solubility and weak conductivity of small‐molecule organic compounds limit their further development in ESSs.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0023"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Extensive knowledge of organic reaction processing has prompted researchers to design reasonable molecular structures to achieve high capacity, high conductivity, rapid reaction kinetics and strong mechanical properties, which is an effective strategy for organic electrodes to improve electrochemical performance.</p><p>Recently, some reports indicate that the design of linear polymers, multimers or adjusting molecular polarity can solve the problem of solubility,<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0026"><sup>[7]</sup></link> and the introduction of electron withdrawing groups or heteroatoms (N, S) can solve the problem of poor conductivity.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0027"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Linear polymers, an emerging class of organic electrode material, have been widely researched for ESSs particularly if the frameworks possess lots of carbonyl groups,<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0032"><sup>[9]</sup></link> which show wide array of characteristics such as strong covalent bond, insolubility in non‐aqueous solvent, flexible designability.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0035"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Firstly, strong skeleton and high chemical stability can keep the polymer skeleton intact, thus ensuring good cycle stability of the electrode material.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0036"><sup>[11]</sup></link> In particular, the designability of the framework allows various active sites to be introduced into the polymer framework, thereby adapting to the requirements of various ESSs.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0037"><sup>[12]</sup></link> To sum up, the design of multiple carbonyls polymers have stimulated research interest as electrode material for ESSs and the introduction of multiple active sites and heteroatoms are expected to solve these problems.</p><p>In this work, we synthesized poly(pyrenetetralone sulfide) (PPTS), a linear polymer on the strength of pyrene‐4, 5, 9, 10‐tetraone (PT) containing abundant carbonyl units linked by thioether bonds through one‐step synthesis. The PT unit has abundant redox active carbonyl groups and supports a reversible redox reaction. The introduction of abound S atoms can effectively improve the conductivity of the material. Due to fast reaction kinetics, accelerated lithium ion and electron transport and stable amorphous framework, PPTS shows improved conductivity and insolubility in the non‐aqueous electrolyte. When employed as an anode material for LIBs, PPTS enable uptake Li‐ions during the battery process, thus delivers an initial reversible capacity of 697.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Meanwhile, outstanding high‐rate performance (335.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>) could be observed. Moreover, when used as an anode material for SIBs, an initial reversible capacity of 205.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and a long‐term cycling stability (100 cycles with 66.8% capacity retention) at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> also could be obtained.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The PPTS is successfully synthesized via linking electroactive organic units with thioether bonds between 2, 7‐dibromo‐4, 5, 9, 10‐pyrenetetrone (PT‐2Br) using only one step as displayed in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>a. The bridging of multiple active organic units through sulfide bonds is a simple and effective method for preparing polymer electrode materials. Firstly, the chemical structure of PPTS is verified through the Fourier transform infrared reflection (FT‐IR). Obviously, the characteristic vibration peaks located at 728 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to C−Br and two new characteristic peaks around 1381 and 1107 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponding to the bonds of sulfur‐disubstituted aromatic ring (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>b).<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0040"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The absence C−Br and presence of ring sulfur prove the successfully synthesis of the polymer using thioether bond as a bridging bond between the PT units. In addition, a strong characteristic vibration peak around 1680 cm<sup>−1</sup> (for C=O) could be observed after the polymerization, indicating the presence of PT units in the polymer chain. Obviously, the broad characteristic peak near 3500 cm<sup>−1</sup> belongs to the water present in the material. Furthermore, X‐ray diffraction (XRD) is conducted to investigate the crystalline state of PPTS. Obviously, compared with the PT with obvious sharp characteristic diffraction peaks (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>c), there is no characteristic peak of PPTS, revealing the amorphous state of PPTS. The results of XRD further suggest the successfully preparation of PPTS. After that, scanning electron microscope (SEM) is performed, as can be seen in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>d, PT (inset) is composed of relatively uniform submicron rods, while PPTS presents an irregular block structure. And the surface area of PPTS is estimated to 73.77 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> from the nitrogen absorption/desorption experiment (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>e). The N<sub>2</sub> isotherm adsorption curves present the characteristics of a typical II type adsorption curve, indicating the characteristics of the mesoporous for PPTS. The pore size distribution diagram shows that the main pore size distribution can be found in the 1.9–2.8 nm spacing (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>f), which also reveals the obvious mesoporous structure. In order to understand the different solubility between monomer (PT) and PPTS in electrolyte, a dissolution experiment is carried out. By comparing the color of the solution in the vial, we can qualitatively understand the difference in solubility (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0001">1</link>g). Obviously, PPTS is not soluble in electrolyte, while PT can dissolve well after immersion.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) The synthesis route of PPTS; (b) FT‐IR spectra, (c) XRD patterns, (d) SEM images of PT (inset) and PPTS, (e) N<sub>2</sub> isotherm adsorption/desorption curve of PPTS, (f) the pore size distribution diagram of PPTS, (g) photographs of PT and PPTS immersed in electrolyte after 7 days.</p></caption></figure><p>To further evaluate the stability of the material, the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) of PPTS, PT and PT‐2Br are carried out under nitrogen (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0002">2</link>a), compared with a rapid mass loss of PT and PT‐2Br at around 320 °C, after the evaporation of absorbed water and solvents below 200 °C, PPTS shows excellent thermal stability until 600 °C. The accelerated weight loss from 600 °C is due to the collapse of the polymer skeleton. Furthermore, to study the chemical composition of PPTS, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is employed. For C1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0002">2</link>b), the three peaks at binding energy of 284.9, 285.8, and 288.1 eV can be assigned to the bonds of C−C/C−H, C−S and C=O, respectively. For S2p spectrum (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0002">2</link>c), the divided two peaks at 168.5 and 169.4 eV corresponds to S2p<sub>1/2</sub> and S2p<sub>3/2</sub>, respectively. For O1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0002">2</link>d), two peaks located at 532.2 and 531.5 eV corresponds to the bonds of O−H/O−C and O=C, respectively.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0040"><sup>[13]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) TGA curves of PPTS and PT, PT‐2Br; (b) C1s XPS spectrum, (c) S2p XPS spectrum and (d) O1s XPS spectrum of PPTS.</p></caption></figure><p>Coin type half‐cells are prepared for further estimate the lithium storage performance of PPTS as an anode material, in which using metal Li‐foils as counter electrode, 1 M lithium bis‐trifluoromethanesulfonimide (Li‐TFSI) in 1,3‐dioxolane (DOL): dimethoxyethane (DME) (1 : 1, v : v) as the electrolyte. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>a, the illustration is enlarged for CV curve of PPTS at the range of 3.0‐1.5 V) is first investigated in potential window of 0.01–3 V at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The CV curve of PPTS during first cycle is significantly diverse with the other cycles, in particular during cathodic scan, the weaker oxidation peak around 2.1 V is attributed to the evolution of carbonyl to lithium‐enol. Subsequently, a broad peak around 0.76 V can be observed, which can be ascribed to the evolution of carbonyl to lithium‐enol and the generation of the SEI film between the electrode and electrolyte. In the anodic process, two weak asymmetric oxidation peaks around 0.91 V and 2.8 V corresponds to the delithiation process of lithium‐enol and formation the carbonyl. Subsequent overlapping CV curves prove good electrochemical stability. Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>b shows the GCD curves of PPTS at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In the first cycle of the discharge profile, a gentle discharge platform appears between 1.10–0.25 V that corresponds to the continuous reduction peak in the CV curve. However, the plateau below 0.50 V can be ascribed to the generation of the SEI phenomen. In addition, the initial discharge and charge capacities of PPTS at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> are 1737.6 and 697.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, with a coulombic efficiency (CE) is only 40.12%. The decomposition of part of the electrolyte and the occurrence of the SEI phenomenon lead to a small reversible capacity at the beginning,<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0016"><sup>[4e]</sup></link> which is more common for the anode materials. In addition, PPTS shows a sloping GCD curves and no obvious platform on the subsequent GCD curves. Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>c compares the cycling stability of PPTS and PT at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. PT shows rapid capacity decay when employed as an anode material for LIBs, which may be ascribed to its serious solubility in the electrolyte. In contrast, the capacity of PPTS decreases slightly during the first 20 cycles, and the capacity remains stable in the subsequent continuous charge and discharge cycles. After 100 cycles, PPTS can still provide a reversible capacity of 406.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The long cycle performance under high current density (1 A g<sup>−1</sup>) is shown in the Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>d, obviously, PPTS can also provide a reversible discharge capacity of 198.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles, with a stable CE of ∼99%. In the first 100 cycles, the rapidly decayed capacity may be due to the decomposition of part of the electrolyte at high current densities. To further evaluate the potential application of PPTS, the rate performance under a stepped current density is also evaluated by successive GCD tests, which is an important indicator of electrode materials to achieve rapid charge and discharge. It can be seen that PPTS shows good rate capacities including 552.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, 457.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, 390.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 335.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, when the current densities change from 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> to 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>e). An impressive reversible capacity of 451.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> could be obtained when the current density is adjusted back to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, elucidating the good electrochemical reversibility and fast ion diffusion coefficient. In order to further demonstrate the good electrochemical performance of PPTS, the electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) of PPTS and PT are tested (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>f). We can clearly find that the charge transfer resistance (Rct) value of PPTS is much smaller than PT, which proves that the conductivity of the electrode material has been improved after the introduction of S atoms and the formation of polymer to a certain extent.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performance of PPTS for LIBs. (a) CV curves of PPTS at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, (b) galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles of PPTS, (c) cycling performance at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, (d) long‐term cycling performance at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, (e) rate performance, (f) nyquist plots of different samples.</p></caption></figure><p>To further understand the charge/discharge reaction kinetics of PPTS, the CV measurement at various scan rate are tested (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0004">4</link>a). Current density (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>v</i>) follow the power law relationship: <i>i</i>=<i>aν</i><sup>b</sup>, where a and b are natural constant. Generally speaking, the dynamic characteristics of battery electrode materials can be judged according to the b value, b=0.5 indicates an electrochemical system dominated by a semi‐infinite diffusion process. When the b value is closer to 1, the electrochemical process is dominated by capacitive behavior.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0043"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Plot the peak current (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>ν</i>) logarithmic graph can get a slope, this is the b value (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0004">4</link>b). The b values calculated according to the formula are 0.79 and 0.88, suggesting the capacity contribution is not dominated by diffusion. Furthermore, the formula: <i>i</i>(V)=<i>k<sub>1</sub>v+k<sub>2</sub>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> is used to quantitatively analyze the capacity contribution value. Typically, at a specific potential, the <i>k<sub>1</sub>v</i> on behalf of the capacitive effect and <i>k<sub>2</sub>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> represents the diffusion‐controlled insertion.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0044"><sup>[15]</sup></link> As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0004">4</link>c, the capacity contribution rate of capacitive effect at various scan rates are calculated to be 46.32%, 52.55%, 55.88%, 59.61% and 62.49%, respectively. Typically, Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0004">4</link>d shows the capacity contribution value of capacitance behavior when the scan rate is 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical reaction kinetics of PPTS. (a) CV curves of at various scan rates from 0.2 to 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, (b) the relationship between log(<i>i</i>) and log(<i>v</i>), (c) the pseudocapacitive and diffusion‐controlled charge storage contributions at different scan rates, (d) the capacitive contribution (the shaded region) at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>In order to further study the structural change of the anode materials under the charge and discharge processes, the ex‐situ FT‐IR of PPTS in the fully charged/discharged state in the electrochemical reaction are tested. The carbonyl groups on the PPTS structure have reversible redox activity during the electrochemical reaction processes. During the discharging process, the carbonyl groups can react with lithium ions to form an enol‐structure. On the contrary, during the charging process, the lithium ions are removed and re‐form the carbonyl groups. The FT‐IR results of the electrode material in the fully charged and discharged state after 1st and 5th cycles are displayed in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0005">5</link>. Obviously, the characteristic absorption peak of C=O at 1660 cm<sup>−1</sup> disappears after 1st deeply discharge, which indicates that the active site gradually decreases and disappears completely as the potential decreases. In addition, it resumes after the re‐charging process. It is impressive that after 5th cycles of charge and discharge, the carbonyl peak can still be maintained well, demonstrating the good reversible oxidation‐reduction reaction of the PT units. At the same time, during the charging and discharging processes, the peaks at 1412 and 1116 cm<sup>−1</sup> belong to the C−S bond unchanged, which proves the good stability of the C−S bond.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p><i>E</i>x‐situ FT‐IR spectra of PPTS after lithiation process and delithiation process.</p></caption></figure><p>Moreover, the lithiation process is further certified by XPS spectra analysis. As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0006">6</link>a, after the deeply discharge to 0.01 V, the C1s spectrum can be divided into five peaks, located at 284.3, 284.9, 285.8, 287.2 and 289.9 eV, ascribing the C=C, C−C, C−O/C−S, C=O and C−Li bonds, respectively.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0020 #asia202100455-bib-0045"><sup>[5a,16]</sup></link> The C=O bond almost disappears and the appearance of C−O bond is attributed to the evolution of C=O to C−O−Li for the Li‐ion insertion. In addition, a new peak at 289.9 eV assigned to C−Li bond emerges, which may be originated from the lithiation reaction or Li<sup>+</sup> ion insertion in condensed aromatic structures. Meanwhile, three peaks are also observed in the O1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0006">6</link>b), corresponding to O−Li (531.1 eV), C−O (532.2 eV) and C=O (533.5 eV), respectively,<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0020"><sup>[5a]</sup></link> further suggesting the formation of lithium enol structure.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) C1s and (b) O1s XPS spectra for PPTS discharged at 0.01 V.</p></caption></figure><p>Compared with PT, PPTS shows better electrochemistry performance and improved conductivity due to the introduction of S atoms and the occurrence of polymerization. In order to demonstrate this, the higher highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy level and the lower lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy level of PT and PPTS are calculated using density functional theory (DFT). As we know, the energy difference Eg between HOMO and LUMO orbitals is often regarded as an indicator of electronic conductivity.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0046"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The smaller the Eg value, the better the conductivity of the material. Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0007">7</link>a gives the molecules structure of PT and PPTS unit. From the calculation results in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0007">7</link>b, it can be clearly found that the Eg value of PPTS (2.91 eV) is smaller than that of PT (3.50 eV), which proves that the introduction of S atoms can effectively reduce its energy gap, thereby exhibiting improved electronic conductivity. This result corresponds to the result of electrochemical impedance in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>f.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0007"><label>7</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) The molecules structure of PT and PPTS, (b) HOMO/LUMO energy level and orbits distribution of PT and PPTS.</p></caption></figure><p>In recent years, due to abundant sodium resources, similar chemical properties to lithium and low cost, SIBs are considered to be an ideal substitute for LIBs. However, due to the large difference in ionic radius between Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup> (radius: 1.02 Å (Na<sup>+</sup>) vs. 0.76 Å (Li<sup>+</sup>)), the inorganic Li intercalation compound as the Na intercalation electrode materials is not ideal as expected. In this regard, organic electrode materials show their special advantages (diversity, designability, structural flexibility to adapt to large sodium ions, et.al) and can be directly applied to rechargeable SIBs. In order to verify this, the electrochemical performance of PPTS as an anode material for SIBs is evaluated. Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0008">8</link>a gives the CV profiles of PPTS at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, which are similar to those CV curves of LIBs (Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0003">3</link>a). Similarly, the following CV curves almost overlapped, which proves the good electrochemical reversibility of PPTS as an anode material for SIBs. Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0008">8</link>b presents the GCD curves for the PPTS based SIBs at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Because of the generation of the SEI film in the first cycle of discharge, PPTS can provide a high discharge specific capacity of 588.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and only a reversible charge specific capacity of 205.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> could be observed. This phenomenon can be found on the irreversible reduction peak under 1.0 V in the CV curve. PPTS shows similar GCD curves in the subsequent discharge/charge processes, which coincides well with the CV curves. The long cycling stability of PPTS at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> is presented in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0008">8</link>c. The capacity gradually become stable after 10th consecutive charge/discharge cycles and a reversible capacity of 137 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> could be hold after 100 cycles, with a stable CE of ∼98%. As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202100455-fig-0008">8</link>d, PPTS exhibits good rate performance at various current densities from 0.05 to 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The capacity attenuation is relatively small at different current densities, varying from 174.5 to 165, 127.7, 100.6, and 75.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at every current densities, respectively. Outstandingly, a reversible capacity of 111.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be gained once the current density returns to 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100455-fig-0008"><label>8</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100455:asia202100455-fig-0008"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100455-fig-0008-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performance of PPTS for SIBs. (a) CV curves, (b) galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles, (c) cycling performance at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, (d) rate performance with different current densities from 0.05 to 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we synthesized a carbonyl‐rich linear polymer connected by thioether bonds, which not only solves the problem of monomer solubility, but also improves the conductivity. Abundant carbonyl groups can be used as active sites to reversibly deintercalate Li/Na‐ions. At the same time, the introduction of S atoms can effectively improve the conductivity of the material. PPTS shows improved electrochemical performance compared with monomer when employed as an anode material for LIBs/SIBs, which is demonstrated through the density functional theory. We believe that our preliminary research on PPTS is expected to encourage researchers to explore more similar high‐energy organic anode materials and further develop high‐performance, environmental friendliness and sustainable ESSs.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of PPTS</title><p>2,7‐dibromopyrene‐4,5,9,10‐tetraone (PT‐2Br) was prepared refer to the reported work.<link href="#asia202100455-bib-0049"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Subsequently, a solution of 100 mL anhydrous N‐methyl pyrrolidone was poured into a single‐neck round bottom flask, then added 2,7‐dibromopyrene‐4,5,9,10‐tetraone (420 mg, 1 mmol) and Na<sub>2</sub>S (78.4 mg, 1 mmol) under nitrogen, respectively. The solution was stirred and heated under nitrogen at 190 °C for 24 h. Subsequently, the product was collected by centrifugation and washed several times with NMP, water and acetone until colorless. Finally, the product was dried at 80 °C under vacuum for over 24 h.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material characterization</title><p>PerkinElmer Frontier equipment was applied to determine FT‐IR spectra via the KBr pellet technique. SEM studies were carried out on a JEM‐6701F (JEOL Ltd.). Rigaku Ultima VI Powder X‐ray diffractometer was used to test the crystalline state using Cu Kα radiation (40 kV, 20 mA). N<sub>2</sub> (77 K) adsorption‐desorption measurements were carried out on a Micromeritics Tristar 3000. Before analysis, the sample was pre‐treated at 100 °C for 2 h to get rid of adsorbed gas/moisture. XPS analysis was performed on a VG Theta Probe spectrometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA) with Al Kα X‐ray source. TG analysis was conducted on a 2020S TG‐DTA Analyzer (MAC Science, Japan) in temperature range of 20–800 °C at a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurements</title><p>30 wt% PPTS powder, 60 wt% acetylene black and 10 wt% PVDF were uniformly mixed and NMP was used as a solvent to prepare PPTS electrodes. After grinded thoroughly in a mortar for 30 min, the mixture was pasted on copper foil and then dried at 70 °C overnight before cut into circle disc. The 2032 type coin half‐cells were prepared in Ar glove box. For LIBs electrochemical test, the PPTS as the working electrode, the counter electrode is Li‐foil, the separator is Celgard 2400 membrane. Meanwhile, a mixture of DOL and DME (1 : 1 volume) contain 1 M LiTFSI is used as the electrolyte. For SIBs electrochemical test, Na‐foil was applied as the counter electrode, the separator is microfiber film (Celgard M825) and 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> dissolved in EC/DMC (1 : 1 vol %) was emloyed as the electrolyte. Test the constant current charge and discharge experiment on the LAND CT2001 A battery test equipment (CT2001A), the potential window is 0.01 to 3 V (vs. Li<sup>+</sup>/Li). CHI660 electrochemical workstation was employed to test CV measurements and EIS measurements.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100455-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Theory Calculation</title><p>All the calculations were conducted with the Gaussian 09 package.
