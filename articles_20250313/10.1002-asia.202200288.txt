However, their poor intrinsic electronic conductivity and sluggish sodium ions diffusion kinetics severely hinder their practical applicability. Here, SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C was successfully prepared by a simple solid‐state reaction technique coupled with carbon coating. HRTEM images show that the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C particles are covered with uniformly ultrathin amorphous carbon layer of about 1.8 nm, thus improving the electronic conductivity and diffusion coefficient of sodium ions. As anode for SIBs, the as‐obtained SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C material exhibits excellent specific capacity (369 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) and remarkable rate performance (177 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), which indicates its good prospect in practical application.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite</b> with ultrathin amorphous carbon was prepared by high‐temperature pyrolysis using glucose as carbon source. The amorphous carbon layer can not only enhances the electrical conductivity of the material, but also delivers excellent rate capability and long‐term cycling stability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202200288:asia202200288-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202200288-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0001"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered to be prospective alternatives to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) in portable and wearable electronic devices, electric vehicles and large‐scale energy storage due to their relatively low cost and abundant element storage.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> However, the large ionic radius leads to significant volume expansion of electrode materials during Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and delamination, as well as slow electrochemical kinetics, so SIBs always suffered from poor energy storage performance such as low capacity, inferior rate performance and short cycle life. Therefore, efforts have been made to develop sodium storage electrode materials with sufficiently high electrochemical capacity and outstanding cycling stability.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> At present, the reported anode materials for SIBs mainly include carbon base, titanium base, alloy, organic and other anode materials.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0011"><sup>[3]</sup></link> It is worth noting that niobates with sheared ReO<sub>3</sub> crystal structure (M−Nb−O, M stands for metal ions) are considered to be very prospective anode materials for SIBs due to their merits of high theoretical capacity and outstanding safety.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0016"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, the inferior intrinsic electronic conductivity and sodium ions diffusion coefficient of M−Nb−O anode material limit its application in high‐rate energy storage devices. To address the above problems, effective strategies were designed to develop novel electrode materials with larger lattice spacing and nanoscale structures, which can provide abundant available channels for the rapid diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup>. For instance, Shen et al.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0022"><sup>[5]</sup></link> successfully fabricated Ti<sub>2</sub>Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>9</sub> nanosheets with abundant nanovoids by liquid exfoliation coupled with topological dehydration, which provided a good specific capacity of 250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and a medium average voltage of ≈0.7 V. The excellent electrochemical performance was attributed to the low diffusion barrier and increased interlayer distance. In addition, compounding with carbon materials can not only improve the electronic conductivity of M−Nb−O but also enhance the electrochemical performance of the electrode materials.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0023"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Shang et al.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0028"><sup>[7]</sup></link> successfully synthesized TiNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>/carbon nanotube composites by ultrasonic dispersion coupled with a simple solvothermal method, which can be maintained at 110 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> specific capacity after more than 1000 cycles at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The addition of carbon nanotubes not only enhances the rate capability, but also increases the sodium storage capacity. However, the electrochemical performance cannot meet the practical applications. Therefore, further development of novel M−Nb−O compounds with high safety, large capacity, outstanding cycling stability and remarkable rate capacity is a potential direction for anode materials.</p><p>As a typical niobate semiconductor material with narrow band gap, SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> has been widely studied in the fields of photodegradation of organic pollutants and water splitting due to its appropriate energy band structure, special crystal structure and excellent chemical stability.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0029"><sup>[8]</sup></link> In addition, SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> materials were also used as electrode materials in the field of energy storage. For example, Kong et al.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0034"><sup>[9]</sup></link> successfully synthesized SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> nanosheets by facile microwave‐assisted hydrothermal method using K<sub>8</sub>Nb<sub>6</sub>O<sub>19</sub> ⋅ 10H<sub>2</sub>O and SnCl<sub>2</sub> ⋅ 2H<sub>2</sub>O as precursors. SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> nanosheets were innovatively used as anode materials for LIBs, showing favorable cycling stability (498 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and satisfactory rate performance (306 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>). Meanwhile, Huang et al.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0035"><sup>[10]</sup></link> successfully fabricated the two‐dimensional SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>/amino‐functionalized graphene nanocomposite (EN‐RGO) by uniformly anchoring SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> nanosheets on amino‐functionalized graphene via electrostatic attraction, resulting in a reversible capacity of 300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Such superior electrochemical performance is mainly ascribed to the admirable electronic conductivity of the En‐RGO nanosheets and the interfacial effect between SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and En‐RGO. However, complicated synthesis steps, relatively low capacity, and poor cycling stability limit the application of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> in SIBs.</p><p>In this work, we successfully prepared micrometer‐sized SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite materials via a facile solid‐state reaction and high temperature pyrolysis using glucose as carbon source. It is noteworthy that a uniformly ultrathin amorphous carbon layer was successfully coated on the surface of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particles via high temperature pyrolysis, which can increase the electrical conductivity of the material. As a result, both the cycling performance and rate capability of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C are significantly better than those of pristine SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. In detail, it delivered a satisfactory capacity of 203.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and a stable reversible capacity of 102.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, the simple carbon coating strategy to prepare SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite is also applicable to other niobium‐based oxides, which can greatly promote the development of niobium‐based oxides in the field of sodium ions energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0002"><title type="main">Results and discussion</title><p>The schematic illustration of the synthesis approach of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite material is shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0001">1</link>. The SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> powder was firstly prepared by a classical solid‐state synthesis method with stoichiometric amount of SnO and Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> as precursors. After that, the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite was synthesized by high temperature carbonization using glucose as carbon source.</p><figure xml:id="asia202200288-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202200288:asia202200288-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202200288-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of the preparation process for SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C.</p></caption></figure><p>The morphology and microstructure of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite materials were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM). As illustrated in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0002">2</link>a and 2b, it can be seen that the size of the as‐obtained SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C particles ranges from 0.3 μm to 1.5 μm. Moreover, compared with SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particles, the surface of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C particles becomes slightly rough, which is mainly due to the amorphous carbon layer covering the surface of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particles. In order to further clearly observe the surface morphology of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C particles, Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) observations were performed. Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0002">2</link>c and 2d illustrate the TEM images of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite at different magnifications, it can be seen that the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particle was well wrapped by a carbon layer. Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0002">2</link>e shows the HRTEM image of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite, notably, the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particles were uniformly covered by a ultrathin amorphous carbon layer with a thickness of ∼1.8 nm. The amorphous carbon layer can provide long‐range conductivity and effectively improve the electronic conductivity of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particles, resulting in excellent electrochemical performance.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0036"><sup>[11]</sup></link> In addition, the surface shows clear lattice fringes with a lattice spacing of 0.30 nm, corresponding to the (311) crystallographic plane of foordite SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0002">2</link>f presents the energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) elemental mapping for Sn, Nb, O, C of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C. It can be seen that these elements are uniformly distributed in the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C.</p><figure xml:id="asia202200288-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202200288:asia202200288-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202200288-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Morphological characterizations of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C. (a, b) SEM images of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> sample and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C sample. (c‐e) TEM and HRTEM images of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C sample. (f) EDS mapping patterns of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C sample.</p></caption></figure><p>To study the structural evolution of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C, the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>a. It is apparent that the whole diffraction peaks of both powder samples can be properly matched with the foordite structured SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (JCPDS 84‐1810). In addition, the characteristic peak intensities of the two samples are sharp and strong, indicating that the as‐prepared materials possess high crystallinity. Nevertheless, there is no obvious diffraction peak of carbon in SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C because of its small amount and amorphous state.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0037"><sup>[12]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="asia202200288-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202200288:asia202200288-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202200288-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD pattern of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C. (b, c) Raman spectra of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C. (d) XPS survey spectra of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C. The XPS spectra of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C for (e) Sn 3d, (f) Nb 3d, (g) C 1s and (h) O 1s. (i) TGA curves of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C material.</p></caption></figure><p>Raman spectra of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite materials are presented in Figure S1 and Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>b, respectively. It can be observed that the peaks at 131, 222, 574 and 794 cm<sup>−1</sup> belong to the distinctive peaks of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, which is consistent with previous report.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0040"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The strongest peak at 794 cm<sup>−1</sup> is attributed to the typical stretching mode of the Nb−O bond.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0041"><sup>[14]</sup></link> It is worth noting that the characteristic peaks of graphitic carbon (G‐band) and disordered carbon (D‐band) are detected at 1592 and 1345 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>c).<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0027 #asia202200288-bib-0044"><sup>[6d,15]</sup></link> These results demonstrate that carbon was successfully coated on the surface of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> particles. Besides, the peak intensity ratio of D‐band and G‐band (I<sub><i>D</i></sub>/I<sub><i>G</i></sub>) is 0.99, showing that the carbon is highly graphitized.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0045"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Due to the conjugation of electrons in the large π‐conjugated system, the π electrons flow easily, which makes the graphite have good electrical conductivity. Therefore, the graphitized carbon layer can improve the electrical conductivity of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode.</p><p>X‐ray photoelectron spectrometry (XPS) was performed in this study to characterize the chemical composition and valence state of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C. The integrated survey spectrum of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C is shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>d, containing of carbon, oxygen, niobium and stannum elements. The high‐resolution spectrum of Sn 3d splits into two similar peaks at 495.1 eV (Sn 3d<sub>3/2</sub>) and 486.7 eV (Sn 3d<sub>5/2</sub>) in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>e, which is coincident with the characteristic of Sn<sup>2+</sup> oxidation state. The two characteristic peaks of Nb 3d are located at 210.0 eV and 207.3 eV (Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>f), which belong to the spin‐orbit peaks of Nb 3d<sub>3/2</sub> and Nb 3d<sub>5/2</sub>, respectively, suggesting that niobium is completely incorporated in the form of Nb<sup>5+</sup>.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0048"><sup>[17]</sup></link> In the C 1s high‐resolution spectrum of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C (Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>g), the peaks at 286.2, 285.3, and 284.8 eV are attributed to C=O, C−O, and C−C groups, respectively.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0051"><sup>[18]</sup></link> As displayed in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>h, the O 1s spectrum of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C can be split into three peaks at around 533.4, 531.5, and 530.5 eV, corresponding to the C=O, C−O, and Nb−O/Sn−O, respectively, which is consistent with the C 1s peaks.</p><p>Thermo‐gravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed to evaluate the carbon coating mass fraction of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C materials. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0003">3</link>i, the curve of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> rises by 1.8%, indicating that the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> was oxidized to SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> at 500 °C. The curve of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C first drops by 4.6% and then rises by 1.3%, which is caused by the pyrolysis of carbon at high temperature and the oxidation of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> to SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. Therefore, it can be roughly calculated that the carbon content in SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C is about 5.1%.</p><p>In order to investigate the sodium ions storage mechanism of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C in SIBs. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> from 0.01–3.00 V are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>a and Figure S2, respectively. An irreversible cathodic peak at about 0.55 V can be observed during the first sweep and disappears in the subsequent cycles, corresponding to the decomposition of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> into metallic Sn and Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> and the formation of solid electrolyte interface (SEI) film, respectively. The sharp cathodic peak at 0.1 V corresponds to the formation of the Na−Sn alloy,<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0052"><sup>[19]</sup></link> and in the following cycles, this peak potential becomes smaller. In anodic scanning, four small peaks are found at 0.08, 0.27, 0.74 and 1.57 V. The sharp peak located around 0.08 V is caused by the reversible reaction of Na<sup>+</sup> with Super‐P.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0028"><sup>[7]</sup></link> The peaks located at 0.27 V and 0.74 V correspond to the dealloying process of Na<sub>x</sub>Sn.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0054"><sup>[19b]</sup></link> This broad peak at 1.57 V and 1.1 V in the second cathodic scanning are attributed to the intercalation and deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> in Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0046"><sup>[16a]</sup></link> The second and third cyclic voltammetry curves basically overlap, indicating that the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode has good cycle performance.</p><figure xml:id="asia202200288-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202200288:asia202200288-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202200288-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>b and Figure S3 show the charge/discharge curves of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> in the voltage range of 0.01–3.00 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Two potential plateaus with different slopes appeared in the first cycle, corresponding to the formation of SEI film and the reduction of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>, and the alloying of Sn and Na<sup>+</sup>, which is consistent with the results of cyclic voltammetry. The initial discharge and charge capacity of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C are 615 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 371 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and the Coulombic efficiency is 60.3%, which are higher than those of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (529, 310 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 58.6%). The low initial Coulombic efficiency can be due to the formation of SEI and irreversible reduction of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. The charge/discharge curves of the second cycle are basically coincidence with the third cycle, indicating that the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode has a good cycle reversibility. The cycling performance of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> was investigated. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>c, the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode exhibits relatively stable cycling performance from the second cycle. After 300 cycles, the discharge specific capacity of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode remains at 203.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. While the discharge specific capacity of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> electrode is only 154.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S4). The cycling performance enhancement of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C materials is determined by two factors: On the one hand, the carbon coating improves the overall conductivity of the material. On the other hand, the carbon layer can buffer the stress generated by the volume change during the intercalation and deintercalation of sodium ions and prevent the active material from falling off the current collector. Therefore, the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite materials possess outstanding cycling performance. To further investigate the cycling stability of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite electrode at high current density, the long cycling tests of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite electrodes were carried out at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>d, SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode has a stable reversible capacity of 102.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles. However, the reversible capacity of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> electrode is only 54.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 800 cycles (Figure S5). In addition, the charging/discharging curves of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C at different cycle stages at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup> are presented in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>e. In order to evaluate the rapid Na<sup>+</sup> transfer and capacity recovery of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode, the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> electrodes were further evaluated at different current densities from 0.05 to 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>f, the discharge specific capacity of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode are 369, 340, 283, 228, 177, 121, 73, and 67 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, which is higher than that of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (306, 256, 213, 154, 104, 67, 57, and 52 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively). Furthermore, when the current density was restored to 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge specific capacity of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode recovered to 349 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Obviously, the rate performance of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C is better than that of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>g and Figure S6 display the charge/discharge curves of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> electrodes at different current densities. As the current density increases, the discharge capacity decreases correspondingly, which is mainly attributed to the gradual enhancement of polarization at high rates.<link href="#asia202200288-bib-0055"><sup>[20]</sup></link> In particular, the rate performance of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C electrode is comparable or even better than other reported niobium‐based oxides (Figure <link href="#asia202200288-fig-0004">4</link>h), and more detailed data are presented in Table S1.</p><p>In order to further understand the mechanism for superior rate performance of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite anode. The uniformly amorphous carbon layer can improves the electrical conductivity of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite and the diffusion coefficient of sodium ions. In addition, the electrochemical results show that the as‐prepared SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C composite exhibits superior rate capability of 177 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and excellent discharge specific capacity of 369 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The outstanding electrochemical performance is mainly ascribed to the ultrathin amorphous carbon layer uniformly coated on the surface of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>. Therefore, we believe that SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C can be used as a prospective anode materials for high‐rate, high‐capacity and high‐safety SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C samples</title><p>Micrometer‐sized SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> bulks were fabricated via a classical solid‐state reaction of stannous oxide (SnO, 99.9%, Macklin) and niobium pentoxide (Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, 99.9%, Aladdin) powders within the stoichiometric proportion (SnO : Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> molar ratio of 1 : 1). In detail, 2 mmol SnO and 2 mmol Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> powders were well mixed in 10 mL absolute ethanol. The slurry was vigorously ground in an agate mortar for 30 min. Then, the prepared slurry was dried at 80 °C for 1 h. After ethanol was completely evaporated, the sample was sintered at 900 °C for 12 h in nitrogen atmosphere.</p><p>SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C was synthesized using glucose as carbon source. Firstly, the as‐prepared SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> (0.5 g) and glucose (0.1 g) were thoroughly ground in agate mortar for 1 hour. Secondly, the as‐obtained powder was calcined at 700 °C for 5 h in N<sub>2</sub>.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials characterization</title><p>The morphologies and microstructures of the SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C and SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub> materials were characterized by SEM (JEOL 6701F) and TEM (JEM 2100F). The crystal structure was determined by XRD (Bruker D8 Focus). The chemical compositions and the valence state information of the as‐prepared anode materials were recorded by XPS (PHI‐QUANTERA‐II‐SXM). The carbon coating layer content in SnNb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>6</sub>@C was determined using a TGA analyzer (NETZSCH STA 409 PC) in the range of 25∼900 °C with a heating rate of 5 °C/min. Raman spectra was recorded on a Confocal micro‐Raman spectrometer (Renishaw inVia Qontor).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical measurement</title><p>Firstly, the as‐obtained materials, Super‐P and PVDF were mixed in the mass ratio of 7 : 2 : 1, and then certain amount of NMP was added. After that, the as‐prepared slurry were uniformly pasted onto Cu foil, and heated at 80 °C for 12 h in a vacuum oven. Finally, the as‐obtained electrodes were punched into discs (diameter: 12 mm). CR2032 coin cells were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box using Na foil as counter electrode, 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme as the electrolyte, and GF/D glass microfiber filters (Whatman, USA) as the separator. Rate tests and galvanostatic cycling tests were performed at a voltage range from 0.01 to 3.00 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) on a LAND battery Test System (Wuhan Kingnuo Electronic Co., China). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) and cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements were conducted on a CHI 660 electrochemical workstation (CH Instruments Co., Shanghai). The CR2032 coin‐type cells were discharged/charged for 30 min at 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and then relaxed for 2 h for the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurement.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia202200288-sec-0008" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work is supported by the National
