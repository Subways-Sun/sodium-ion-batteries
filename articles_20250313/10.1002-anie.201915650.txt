A strategy based on engineering the crystal structure coupled with chemical substitution led to the design of the layered P2@P3 integrated spinel oxide cathode Na<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.1</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which shows excellent sodium‐ion half/full battery performance. Combined analyses involving scanning transmission electron microscopy with atomic resolution as well as in situ synchrotron‐based X‐ray absorption spectra and in situ synchrotron‐based X‐ray diffraction patterns led to visualization of the inherent layered P2@P3 integrated spinel structure, charge compensation mechanism, structural evolution, and phase transition. This study provides an in‐depth understanding of the structure‐performance relationship in this structure and opens up a novel field based on manipulating structural evolution for the design of high‐performance battery cathodes.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Crystal structure engineering</b> and element substitution has led to the stable layered P2@P3 integrated spinel cathode material Na<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.1</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The magnesium manipulates its structural evolution to afford reasonable phase ratios and suppresses unfavorable phase transformation as well as relieving the Jahn–Teller distortion, which results in xml:id="anie201915650-note-1001"><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201915650-sec-0001"><p>The ever‐increasing demand for sustainable energy resources has driven the development of large‐scale electrochemical energy storage systems (EESs).<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0001"/> Among the various alternative EESs, room‐temperature sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have drawn considerable attention because of their similar electrochemical storage mechanism to lithium‐ion batteries as well as the natural abundance of sodium.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0002"/></p><p>Layered transition metal oxide cathodes for SIBs have aroused much interest over recent years because of their large specific capacity, high ionic conductivity, environmental benignity, and feasible synthesis.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0003"/> Based on their Na occupation sites and the stacking sequence of the O layers, layered oxides can be classified into P2, P3, O2, and O3 structures.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0004"/> Most oxide cathodes which display a single crystalline structure, however, show limited electrochemical performance.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0005"/> Recently, the biphase synergy of P2‐Tunnel and P2‐P3 phases in layered transition metal cathode materials was utilized to combine the merits of different structures,<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0006"/> noting that the spinel phase in the cathode material could provide high electronic conductivity to coordinate in a timely manner with the Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation/intercalation.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0007"/> In addition, chemical substitutions, such as Ti‐substituted NaNi<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.2</sub>Ti<sub>0.3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as well as Cu and Mg cosubstituted Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Cu<sub>1/9</sub>Mg<sub>1/18</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode materials, could maintain structural stability by suppressing unfavorable multiphase transformations and relieving the Jahn–Teller distortion.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0008"/> An optimal strategy based on the idea of combining the bifunctional advantages of the triphase composite structure and chemical element substitution was proposed in this case. Deciphering the underlying reaction mechanism is also of great importance to provide significant guidelines for precisely designing battery materials.</p><p>Herein, we have designed a stable layered P2@P3 integrated spinel Na<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.1</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material through a strategy involving engineering of its crystal structure and element substitution. By virtue of the bifunctional advantages, the cathode delivers a high reversible specific capacity, an excellent rate performance, and a superior cycling stability in a Na half‐cell system, as well as an outstanding full‐cell electrochemical performance when matched with a hard carbon anode. The structural evolution of layered P2/P3 and spinel phases, atomic arrangements, charge compensation mechanisms, and phase transitions were comprehensively studied by a variety of techniques.</p><p>The layered‐layered‐spinel composite structures of Na<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.2</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.1</sub>Co<sub>0.15</sub>Mn<sub>0.65</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (hereafter denoted as LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM, respectively) cathode materials were analyzed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and the corresponding Rietveld refinement (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0001"/> a and Figure S1 in the Supporting Information). All the diffraction peaks of the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM cathode materials are well‐indexed to the <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i> space group of the hexagonal P2‐type phase, the <i>R</i>3<i>m</i> space group of the hexagonal P3‐type phase, and the <inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie201915650-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915650:anie201915650-math-0001"/> space group of the cubic spinel phase. The observed patterns of the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM cathode materials match well with the calculated patterns (see Tables S1 and S2). It was found that substitution with magnesium regulated the phase ratios of the layered P2@P3 and spinel structures. To achieve an analysis of the visualized microstructure of the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material, a combined characterization by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was conducted. The sample has a nanoflake structure (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0001"/> b, c, and Figure S2), which could contribute to promoting the contact between the active material and the electrolyte.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0009"/> Furthermore, the structural, chemical, and electronic characteristics of the layered P2@P3 and spinel structure were investigated by selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED; Figure S3) and advanced spherical aberration‐corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) combined with high‐angle annular dark field (HAADF) and annular bright field (ABF) imaging. The HAADF and ABF images clearly demonstrate that the obtained LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material exhibits three distinct crystal and atomic arrangements (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0001"/> d–k and Figure S4), which correspond to the cation stacking of the P2, P3, and spinel phases, respectively. In addition, STEM‐electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) was executed (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0001"/> l) to study the atomic composition and valence state of each atom. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was also applied to probe the valence states from the macroscopic perspective (Figure S5). STEM energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) elementary mapping and inductively coupled plasma‐mass spectrometry (ICP‐MS) were utilized to accurately analyze the elements in the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0001"/> m, Figures S6 and S7, and Table S3).</p><figure xml:id="anie201915650-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915650:anie201915650-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915650-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structure of the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material. a) Powder XRD pattern and Rietveld refinement plot. b) SEM image. c) TEM image. d,e) HAADF and ABF‐STEM images of the triphase composite structure. f–k) Enlarged HAADF and ABF‐STEM images of the P2, P3, and spinel structures, respectively. l) EELS spectra of Ni, Co, Mn L‐edges, and the O K‐edge. m) STEM‐EDS mapping results for selected elements.</p></caption></figure><p>The Na storage electrochemical performances of the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM electrodes were systematically tested (1 C=160 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). The LLS‐NaNCM electrode gives charge/discharge curves with two clear voltage plateaus, as well as delivering a discharge specific capacity of 153.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and a specific energy density of 376.4 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C between 1.5 V and 4.0 V (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0002"/> a). After substitution with magnesium, the electrochemical curves of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode became smoother without affecting the specific capacity (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0002"/> b). In particular, the clear voltage plateau at about 3.6 V turns into a smooth sloping line and the electrochemical behavior resembles the behavior of capacitors, which indicates that the phase transformation has been suppressed, as discussed later. In addition, similar conclusions were further confirmed by investigating the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM electrodes by cyclic voltammetry (CV; Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0002"/> c,d). To further investigate the rate properties, the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM electrodes were compared at different rates (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0002"/> e–h and Table S4). The LLS‐NaNCMM electrode shows a higher discharge capacity than the LLS‐NaNCM electrode at each current density. The LLS‐NaNCMM electrode exhibited a discharge capacity of 97.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at 5 C, with a stable midpoint voltage as well as superior energy efficiency and high Coulombic efficiency (Figure S8). It is worth noting that the integrated spinel phase in the layered oxide could accelerate fast electron transfer to coordinate in a timely manner with the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction because of the high electronic conductivity (10<sup>−3</sup>–10<sup>−4</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>) of the spinel structure at room temperature. As expected, the discharge capacity retention of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode could recover to about 99.6 % when the rate was reduced to 1 C, even after the high‐rate test (10 C=1.6 A g<sup>−1</sup>), thereby demonstrating the excellent reversibility and rate performance as well as Na<sup>+</sup> transport kinetics (Figure S9). In addition, the maximum specific capacity of the electrode could be deduced using the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurement process at equilibrium state, a reliable electrochemical technique to evaluate the transport kinetics. Under the same test conditions, the better transport kinetics of the electrode may deliver a higher specific capacity. Therefore, the obtained higher specific capacity of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode further confirms its enhanced transport kinetics after substitution with magnesium (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0002"/> i,j). The smooth charge/discharge curves as well as the continuous change in the equilibrium voltage of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode demonstrate the appearance of a solid‐solution reaction process within the potential window of 2.0 to 4.0 V. Furthermore, the average voltage polarization and ohmic polarization of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode are 34.5 mV and 24.6 mV, respectively (Figure S10). The CV tests at different scan rates with linear fitting and voltage polarization of the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM electrodes are also presented (Figure 2 k,l as well as Figures S11 and S12 and Tables S5 and S6). Moreover, a satisfactory capacity retention of 80.6 % of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode is obtained even after 200 cycles at 2 C, and the Coulombic efficiency over the entire cycling process is maintained at a high and stable level (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0002"/> m). On the one hand, the layered P2@P3 integrated spinel structure could not only provide stable prismatic crystalline frameworks, but also accelerated electron transfer to achieve fast Na‐ion insertion/extraction. On the other hand, the substituted magnesium can manipulate the structural evolution and phase ratios of the layered P2@P3 integrated spinel structure to a reasonable extent and suppress the unfavorable phase transformation in the high voltage region, as well as relieving the Jahn–Teller distortion in the low voltage region as discussed below.</p><figure xml:id="anie201915650-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915650:anie201915650-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915650-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performances of the LLS‐NaNCM and LLS‐NaNCMM electrodes. a,b) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves versus specific capacity. c,d) Cyclic voltammograms at 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. e–h) Rate performances at various rates and corresponding galvanostatic charge/discharge curves versus specific capacity. i,j) GITT curves at 0.1 C. k,l) Cyclic voltammograms at different scan rates. m) Cycling performance of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode over 200 cycles at 2 C.</p></caption></figure><p>To obtain an in‐depth understanding of the charge compensation mechanism in the complex chemical environment of the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material, advanced synchrotron‐based characterization, an increasingly widespread tool, was conducted.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0010"/> In situ synchrotron‐based hard X‐ray absorption spectra (XAS) were collected on the DESY beamline to precisely monitor the valence states and corresponding changes in the various elements. The Ni, Co, and Mn K‐edges of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode were analyzed by normalized in situ X‐ray absorption near‐edge spectroscopy (XANES; Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0003"/> and Figures S13) at different selected charge/discharge states. The peaks located at potentials lower than 2.5 V in the cyclic voltammograms are related to Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> redox processes, while the potentials higher than 3.0 V are mainly contributed from Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup>/Ni<sup>4+</sup> and Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>4+</sup> processes. The first galvanostatic charge/discharge curve versus the specific capacity of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode shows the open‐circuit voltage to be about 2.7 V (Figure S14). During the first charge process from 2.7 V to 4.0 V, the Mn may not participate in the charge compensation because of its lower redox potential, which accounts for no significant shift being observed in the Mn K‐edge. The Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> redox couple is involved in the charge compensation during the first discharge process, which is confirmed by the shift of the K‐edge to the lower‐energy side. In addition, the Ni K‐edge and Co K‐edge absorption spectra shift toward the higher‐energy side as the charge increases, which demonstrates oxidation of the Ni and Co elements. During the first discharge process, the K‐edges of Ni and Co shift to the lower‐energy side, thus indicating that they are reduced to lower valence states. Therefore, it can be rationally deduced that cationic nickel, cobalt, and manganese are simultaneously involved in the charge compensation mechanism. Moreover, the intensity of the pre‐edge region for the Ni, Co, and Mn K‐edges always remains very weak, which indicates that the transition‐metal ions remain in octahedral sites. Two peaks in the pre‐edge region of the XANES spectra for the Mn K‐edge are positioned at high energies at different discharge states, suggesting that the substituted magnesium may effectively alleviate the Jahn–Teller distortion to a certain degree.<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0011"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201915650-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915650:anie201915650-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915650-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Charge compensation mechanism of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion. a–f) In situ XANES spectra at the Ni K‐edge, Co K‐edge, and Mn K‐edge collected at different selected charge/discharge states.</p></caption></figure><p>The structural evolution of the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material during the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation was clearly evident and confirmed by in situ synchrotron‐based XRD experiments and the corresponding intensity contour maps (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0004"/> and Figures S15 and S16).<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0012"/> An in‐depth and thorough systematic understanding of the structure‐function‐property mechanisms are also discussed. When Na<sup>+</sup> was being extracted during the first charge process, the (002)/(004) peaks of the P2 phase and (003)/(006) peaks of the P3 phase shifted to lower angles. In contrast, the (101)/(102) peaks of the P2 phase and the (102) peak of the P3 phase shifted towards a larger angle with a gradual expansion of the interlayer spacing. As Na<sup>+</sup> was further extracted, the (105) peak of the P3 phase became slightly weaker but did not disappear, while the (105) peak of the P3′′ phase could not be generated. This observation suggests that the P3‐P3′′ phase transition was suppressed to a certain degree. During the first discharge process, the (002)/(004) peaks of the P2 phase and the (003)/(006) peaks of the P3 phase shifted to a higher angle while the (101)/(102) peaks of the P2 phase and the (102) peak of the P3 phase shifted toward a smaller angle. When Na<sup>+</sup> was further inserted, the (003)/(006) peaks of the P3 phase split into two, and the (105) peak of the P3′′ phase was generated, thereby demonstrating that the P3 phase was transformed into the P3′′ phase. Surprisingly, the P2‐P2′ phase transition was effectively suppressed, as evident from the observation of typical (101)/(102) peaks of the P2 phase after discharging to 1.5 V. In general, Mn‐based cathode materials usually suffer from the Jahn–Teller effect of the MnO<sub>6</sub> octahedron, which is one of the main reasons for structure instability. Suppression of the P2‐P2′ phase transition might be an effective way to alleviate the Jahn–Teller effect to a certain degree, which is very important to improve the reversibility of layered oxide cathode materials. The complex transformations (P2/P3/spinel→P2/P3′′/spinel→P2/P3/spinel→P2′/P3′′/spinel) are successfully restrained and can be simplified as the structural evolution (P2/P3/spinel→P2/P3′′/spinel) as a result of the suppression of TMO<sub>2</sub> gliding after uniform incorporation of substituted elements into the crystal lattice. It is worth mentioning that the P3 phase could be successfully recovered from the P3′′ phase during the second charge process, which accounts for the superior cycling stability (Figure S17). In addition, no apparent shift of the (220)/(311) peaks of the spinel phase occurs, thus demonstrating that the spinel structure stabilizes the overall structure.</p><figure xml:id="anie201915650-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915650:anie201915650-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915650-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Crystal structure evolution of the LLS‐NaNCMM electrode during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion. a) In situ synchrotron‐based XRD patterns collected during the first cycle at 0.1 C, with the corresponding charge/discharge curve on the right. b–d) Intensity contour map (b) and corresponding three‐dimensional (3D) maps (c,d) showing the evolution of the main characteristic diffraction peaks.</p></caption></figure><p>Furthermore, to investigate its practicability when applied in a sodium‐ion full battery, the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material was coupled with a hard carbon anode, which had been first pre‐sodiated. Before assembling the full‐cell battery, the pre‐sodiation process was conducted to facilitate the formation of the solid–electrolyte interphase (SEI) by an electrochemical process (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0005"/> a).<link href="#anie201915650-bib-0013"/> The electrochemical behavior of the hard carbon anode was investigated by galvanostatic charge/discharge and CV tests to obtain a better subsequent matching with the LLS‐NaNCMM cathode material (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0005"/> b). The full cell delivers a high specific capacity of 139.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C, and the corresponding energy density was estimated to be approximately 325.8 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201915650-fig-0005"/> c,d). The special triphase composite structure, electrochemical behavior, charge compensation mechanism, and multiphase transformations during Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation/intercalation of the layered P2@P3 integrated spinel structure were investigated systematically. The inherent beneficial synergistic effects could not only provide stable prismatic crystalline frameworks, but also accelerated electron transfer to coordinate with the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction in a timely manner. Furthermore, the substituted magnesium could effectively manipulate the structural evolution and phase ratios of the triphase composite structure as well as suppress unfavorable phase transformation and relieve the Jahn–Teller distortion.
