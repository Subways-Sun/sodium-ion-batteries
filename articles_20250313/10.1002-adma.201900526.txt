type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries are gradually regarded as a prospective alternative to lithium‐ion batteries due to the cost consideration. Here, three kinds of tin (IV) sulfide nanosheets are controllably designed with progressively exposed active facets, leading to beneficial influences on the Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics, resulting in gradient improvements of pseudocapacitive response and rate performance. Interestingly, different forms of kinetics results are generated accompanying with the morphology and structure evolution of the three nanosheets. Finally, detailed density functional theory simulations are also applied to analyze the above experimental achievements, proving that different exposed facets of crystalline anodes possess dissimilar Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics. The investigation experiences and conclusions shown in this work are meaningful to explore many other proper structure design routes toward the high‐rate and stable metal‐ions storage.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201900526-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Three kinds of tin (IV) sulfide nanosheets with progressively exposed active facets</b> are fabricated toward high‐rate and stable sodium storage. Due to the enhanced pseudocapacitive response generated from morphology and structure evolution, the small and few‐layered nanosheets show the best sodium‐storage performance.<blockFixed xml:id="adma201900526-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201900526-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Owing to the rapidly increasing consumption market of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), as well as the relative low abundance and uneven distribution of lithium resources, sodium‐ion batteries (NIBs) gradually become a kind of prospective alternative energy storage device in the near future.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0001 #adma201900526-bib-0002"/> In the academic field, the electrochemical performances of the reported cathode materials for NIBs are comparable to the corresponding counterparts for LIBs.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0003"/> Thus, the main challenge for the sodium‐storage issue is gradually pointing to relieve the inherent drawbacks of the appropriate anode materials.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0004"/> Up to now, different types of anode materials with alloying, conversion, and intercalation Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism are investigated toward realizing better performance.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0005 #adma201900526-bib-0006 #adma201900526-bib-0007 #adma201900526-bib-0008"/> However, the close‐packed crystal structures possessed by these anodes bring in considerable energy barrier for the Na<sup>+</sup> solid‐state diffusion, as well as leading to the severe volume change during the cyclic Na<sup>+</sup> storage processes, which cause the sluggish charging procedure and unavoidable capacity decay of NIBs.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0009"/> Along with the rapid development of nanotechnology in the past decades, porous and hierarchical materials with nanometre‐sized structure are often synthesized and applied for battery electrodes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0010 #adma201900526-bib-0011 #adma201900526-bib-0012 #adma201900526-bib-0013"/> The size‐decreased active material is able to increase the contact area with electrolyte, provide more surface for charge storage, and minimize the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion distance simultaneously, therefore resulting in high specific capacity and fast ion transportation to a certain extent.</p><p>On the other hand, the crystalline material with nanoscale structure can strengthen the capability of solvated Na<sup>+</sup> storage arising at its surface and/or near surface, which is called as pseudocapacitance or surface‐controlled process.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0014 #adma201900526-bib-0015"/> The related kinetics analysis based on the pseudocapacitance response also offers a relatively accurate quantitative method to obtain more information for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and storage, which can be used as guide to prepare more favorable nanostructures for high‐rate electrodes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0016 #adma201900526-bib-0017"/> Particularly, for a crystalline material, the nanoscale structure obtained by size‐decreasing route also represents the exposing intrinsic facets compared to the bulk material.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0018"/> Thus, it is believed that the enhanced pseudocapacitance response generated from surface‐increased nanoscale material is essentially associated with the exposed active facets. Due to the anisotropic lattice adopted by crystalline material, it is apparent that different exposed facets will give rise to dissimilar influence on the pseudocapacitive contribution. Very recently, in LIB field, several inspiring works indicate that appropriate material design with regard to the crystalline lattice is a promising direction to acquire high‐performance electrodes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0019 #adma201900526-bib-0020"/> As mentioned earlier, for a definite crystalline material, finding more beneficial active exposed facet with low energy barrier and stable ion‐binding ability is very meaningful to reversely conjecture the optimized nanoscale manufacture process. To the best of our knowledge, the detailed research about the relationship between the different exposed facets and their corresponding pseudocapacitance response, as well as the Na<sup>+</sup> storage properties, has rarely been noticed.</p><p>Tin (IV) sulfide (SnS<sub>2</sub>), a typical layered material with hexagonal unit cell, possesses high theoretical capacity of 1136 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> based on both of the conversion and alloying processes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0021"/> In this work, we employ a series of controllable and original programs, leading to three kinds of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets involved in respectively “thinner” and “smaller” 3D shape evolution, virtually progressively exposing (001) and (100) facets. Furthermore, a kind of nitrogen and phosphorus codoped porous carbon (NPC) first developed by our group is adopted as a skeleton for the SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets loading, aiming to strengthen the elecroconductivity and electrode stability of the anodes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0022"/> As a result, the SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with fully exposed active facets reveal the best Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance among them. In addition, when we progressively expose the different active facets of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics shows different variety regulation, generating various degrees of pseudocapacitance response and rate performance promotion. To explore the origin of this experimental fact, we also employ detailed density functional theory (DFT) calculations for the theoretical situations of Na storage and diffusion in SnS<sub>2</sub> crystalline structure.</p><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> shows the brief synthesis routes of the composites and overlooking scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of the as‐designed SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets. By smartly controlling the Sn sources, solvents, and reaction temperature, three kinds of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with stepwisely changed morphologies are obtained, which are named as D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>, F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>, and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>, respectively (D: dozens layered; F: few layered; SF: small few layered). The NPC employed in this work can be not only served as the 3D skeleton to enhance the electroconductivity and strengthen the structural stability of the anodes, but also provide additional Na<sup>+</sup> storage compared to common graphene and carbon nanotubes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0023"/> Besides, from the lower magnification SEM images, it can be seen obviously that the SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with “standing” feature are uniformly and fully anchored on the surface of NPC (Figure S1, Supporting Information). <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link> illustrates the detailed structure characterization and evolution of the three samples. As can be seen from Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0002"/>a–c, we draw three circles with radius of 100 nm in the TEM images, respectively, and the number of nanosheets situated in the circles shows an intuitively increasing trend in order, suggesting that the surface area density of the nanosheets on the NPC is improved through the reasonable synthetic adjustment. Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0002"/>d–f displays the high resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) investigations of the three SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, it can be clearly distinguished that the D‐SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are formed by more than 20 atom layers, as well as the F‐SnS<sub>2</sub> and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub> are of around 10 layers. Based on the SEM and TEM images, it can be found that the F‐SnS<sub>2</sub> and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub> become “thinner” and “smaller” compared to the D‐SnS<sub>2</sub> and F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0002"/>g). Considering the 2D layered crystalline structure of SnS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S2, Supporting Information) and formation regulation of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, the essence of this two‐step structure evolution is exposing the (001) and (100) facets of SnS<sub>2</sub> along the vertical directions. Furthermore, the HRTEM images of the three SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets display that the interplanar distances of (001) and (100) are around 0.59 and 0.32 nm, respectively (Figure S3, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0002"/>h shows the mapping test of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, all of the expected elements of the composite can be recognized clearly.</p><figure xml:id="adma201900526-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201900526:adma201900526-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201900526-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image of the NPC substrate. b–d) SEM images of the as‐designed D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, respectively. Scale bars: a) 500 nm; b–d) 100 nm.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="adma201900526-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201900526:adma201900526-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201900526-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) TEM and d–f) HRTEM images of the D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (a,d), F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (b,e), and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (c,f). g) Structure evolution of the three SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets based on the SEM and TEM investigations. h) Elemental mapping images of the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composite.</caption></figure><p>To obtain more crystal particulars of the different composite anodes, X‐ray diffraction examinations are performed with the results of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples shown in Figure S4 (Supporting Information), respectively. All of the apparent peaks are matched with the hexagonal structure (JCPDS card no. 23–0677), demonstrating the well crystallized 2T‐type SnS<sub>2</sub>. Furthermore, the (001) peaks of D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC are located at 14.96°, 14.80°, and 14.70°, respectively, which may be ascribed to the slight interlayer expansion trend of the different SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0024 #adma201900526-bib-0025 #adma201900526-bib-0026"/> Thermogravimetric analysis tests are also employed to determine the mass constitution of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composites. By assuming the final calcined product in air atmosphere is SnO<sub>2</sub>, the mass fraction of SnS<sub>2</sub> can be calculated to be 98.1%, 89.7%, and 90.0% for D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, respectively (Figure S5, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0027"/> To be more accurate, we investigate the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC sample by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy, and the results are revealed in Figure S6 (Supporting Information). All of the related photoelectric peaks for Sn, S, C, N, P, and O can be distinguished from the survey spectrum clearly (Figure S6a, Supporting Information). Moreover, two strong peaks at 494.6 eV (Sn 3d<sub>3/2</sub>) and 486.2 eV (Sn 3d<sub>5/2</sub>) suggest that Sn<sup>4+</sup> rather than Sn<sup>2+</sup> exists in the as‐prepared sample (Figure S6b, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0028"/></p><p>To investigate the detailed sodium‐storage properties, <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link> displays the rate capability and cycling performance of the as‐obtained SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples. Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"/>a presents the typical charge/discharge profiles of every SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC sample during the stable third loops at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In comparison, D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC shows the lowest discharge potential plateau and highest polarization among the three samples, indicating a relative higher energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion in the SnS<sub>2</sub> layers.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0011 #adma201900526-bib-0024"/> As a result, SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, and D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes reveal gradually decreased reversible specific capacities of 823, 803, and 767 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Further rate capabilities of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC electrodes are also investigated with current densities varying from 0.1 to 12.8 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"/>b). As can be seen, the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC shows the smallest downward trend in specific capacities, and the relative capacity differences of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes present increasing tendency one by one during the turning up of current densities. Even at the maximum rate, the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC can still maintain a discharge capacity of 378 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"/>c, the rate performance of the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC is also beneficial compared to most of the previously exhibited SnS<sub>2</sub> based anodes.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0027 #adma201900526-bib-0028 #adma201900526-bib-0029 #adma201900526-bib-0030 #adma201900526-bib-0031 #adma201900526-bib-0032 #adma201900526-bib-0033 #adma201900526-bib-0034 #adma201900526-bib-0035 #adma201900526-bib-0036 #adma201900526-bib-0037"/> Next, the capacity reversibility of three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composites and pure NPC skeleton is also tested at a constant current density of 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"/>d reveals the representative sodiation/desodiation voltage curves of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC at 1st, 2nd, and 20th. The first discharge capacity of the composite is 1052 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> while resulting in a low Coulomb efficiency (CE) of 74%, which is common for metal oxide/sulfide based Li/Na ion batteries.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0038 #adma201900526-bib-0039 #adma201900526-bib-0040"/> After the first several cycles, the capacity decay of the SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes turns to much sluggish, indicating a relative remarkable structural stability of the as‐prepared composites. Furthermore, the long term cycling performances of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples are illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"/>e. As a result, the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC is more robust compared to the other anodes and demonstrates the most stable cycling performance. Even after 200 cycles, a high discharge specific capacity of 701 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can still be preserved by SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anode, resulting in an average capacity decay of 0.07% every cycle compared to the second discharge specific capacity. This fact proves that decreasing the thickness and size of the active SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets is beneficial for strengthening the stability of electrode. In addition, the pure NPC is able to show a good discharge capacity of around 220 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which can also make a contribution for the as‐prepared SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composites. Furthermore, the cycling capability of our SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC is also better than the previously introduced SnS<sub>2</sub> based anodes (Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0003"/>f).<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0027 #adma201900526-bib-0028 #adma201900526-bib-0029 #adma201900526-bib-0030 #adma201900526-bib-0031 #adma201900526-bib-0032 #adma201900526-bib-0033 #adma201900526-bib-0034 #adma201900526-bib-0035 #adma201900526-bib-0036 #adma201900526-bib-0037"/> As a comparison, the pure SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoflakes display much more inferior cycling and rate performances than that of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composites (Figure S7, Supporting Information). Furthermore, the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples as well as pure SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoflakes are tested by Nyquist plots, the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC and F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC exhibit similar semicircle diameters, but much smaller than that of D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC and pure SnS<sub>2</sub> nanoflakes, which are coincident with their sodium‐storage performances (Figure S8, Supporting Information). The F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anode is also investigated after 50 cycles, as can be seen, the hierarchical morphology is able to be maintained, thus proving the structural stability of the as‐prepared SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets (Figure S9, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201900526-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201900526:adma201900526-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201900526-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical sodium‐storage properties of SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes. a) The third galvanostatic charge–discharge voltage profiles of the three samples at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Rate performances of SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes at various current densities from 0.1 to 12.8 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c) A comparison of rate capability with previously reported SnS<sub>2</sub> based anodes. d) Charge–discharge profiles of the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC electrode at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. e) Cycling performaces of the SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples together with pure NPC at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, including the Coulombic efficiency of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC. f) A comparison of cycling performace with previously reported SnS<sub>2</sub> based anodes.</caption></figure><p>According to the previous reported literatures, it is generally concluded that surface‐controlled pseudocapacitive behavior of electrode possesses significant benefit for charge storage under high current density.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0041"/> Thus, to deeply understand the relationship between morphology evolution (crystalline facet exposing) and rate performance improvement, detailed quantitative kinetics investigation of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes using cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements is employed. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a and Figure S11 (Supporting Information), CV curves at progressively increased scan rates of the SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composites illustrate obvious discrepancy in both of anodic and cathodic peaks. To seek for more particulars about the changes of redox peaks, CV curves of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples at the same scan rate of 0.6 mV s<sup>−1</sup> are compared in Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"/>b. It is notable that the anodic peak of F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC is broader than that of D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, and the peak current of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC becomes larger compared to F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC. In another point of view, the D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC and F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC show almost same peak currents during anodic process, while the F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC and SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC display similar shapes of anodic peaks. Due to the difference between the two shape change styles of the anodic peaks, it can be confirmed again that the two‐step evolution of the SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets introduced in Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0002"/>g, that is, the stepwisely exposed crystalline facets of SnS<sub>2</sub>, possesses different influences on Na<sup>+</sup> transport and storage kinetics. It is generally accepted that the relationship between current (<i>i</i>) and sweep rate (<i>v</i>) obeys the power‐law equation: <i>i</i> = <i>av<sup>b</sup></i>. When the value of <i>b</i> is equal to 0.5, a diffusion‐dominated Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation mechanism is resulted, while the <i>b</i> value of 1 represents an extreme surface‐controlled capacitive behavior. In particular, Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"/>c and Figure S12 (Supporting Information) display the comparison of calculated <i>b</i> values at intervals of 0.1 V, respectively, corresponding to anodic and cathodic reactions. Most of the <i>b</i> values are within the range of 0.5–1, thus proving that mixed Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanisms exist in these three kinds of anodes. Even more, the <i>b</i> values of D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC are evidently smaller than that of the other two samples, suggesting that increasing the number of exposed (001) facets is able to effectively improving the pseudocapacitance response of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets. Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"/>d and Figure S13 (Supporting Information) show the linear relationships between logarithm peak currents and sweep rates based on the abovementioned power law equation. After calculations, it can be obtained that the <i>b</i> values of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anode are closer to 1 than that of the other two composites (0.76 vs 0.69 vs 0.63 for the anodic peaks; 0.90 vs 0.81 vs 0.72 for the cathodic peaks). This fact further shows that increasing both of the exposed (001) and (100) facets of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets can lead to more surface‐controlled pseudocapacitive reactions with solvated Na<sup>+</sup>, which will make a considerable contribution to the energy storage at high rate.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0015 #adma201900526-bib-0020"/> To be more exact, the quantitative capacitive‐dominated (<i>i</i><sub>1</sub>) and diffusion‐controlled (<i>i</i><sub>2</sub>) currents can be separated at the fixed potential by the next equation: <i>i</i> = <i>i</i><sub>1</sub> + <i>i</i><sub>2</sub> = <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> + <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"/>e and Figure S14 (Supporting Information), the capacitive‐dominated areas (yellow part) are separated from the whole CV curves of the three anodes at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Notably, the capacitive contribution of the F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (≈68%; Figure S14a, Supporting Information) is closer to SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (≈74%; Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"/>e) but much larger than that of D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (≈55%; Figure S14c, Supporting Information). Furthermore, this phenomenon is also appearing in the other scan rates in view of the calculation results by the similar analysis method (Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0004"/>f; Figure S11b,d, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201900526-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201900526:adma201900526-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201900526-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Kinetics and quantitative analysis of the Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior for SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes. a) CV curves of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC at 0.4–1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) CV curves of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC samples at 0.6 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. c) Calculated <i>b</i> values of the three SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC anodes at different voltages during the anodic process. d) Logarithm peak current versus logarithm scan rate plots of SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC. e) Separation of capacitive (yellow part) and diffusion (gray part) controlled contribution at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> for SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC. f) Pseudocapacitive contribution ratio at a series of scan rates for SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC.</caption></figure><p>According to the previous data, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage performances of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are increased under varying degrees, accompanying with the turning into “thinner” and “smaller” processes, suggesting that exposing different facets generates dissimilar Na<sup>+</sup> storage and diffusion kinetics. To understand this phenomenon more deeply, we employ DFT methods for the further calculations based on the first principles. Considering the former‐mentioned structural evolution of the three SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets, we establish the optimized model structures of Na intercalated into the (001) interlayer and (100) lattice, as well as Na stored on the exposed (001) and (100) facets, which are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a–d. As a result, the calculated binding energies (Δ<i>E</i>) of the four models are −0.86, −1.11, 15.30, and −2.32 eV (Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0005"/>e). It is thus concluded that Na atom intercalated into the (100) lattice shows an unstable status, due to the too narrow lattice spacing resulting in large energy barrier for Na intercalation. On the other hand, the binding stabilities of the Na storage on the exposed (100) and (001) facets are more stable than that of Na intercalated into the (001) interlayer, which further proves the important role for the surface‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage compared to the diffusion‐controlled process. In addition, the exposed (100) facet is more favorable for Na storage than that of exposed (001) facet. This result preliminarily explains the reason for different pseudocapacitive response and rate performance varieties during the two‐step evolution of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets. Moreover, we investigate the Na atom diffusion paths in/on the (001) interlayer, exposed (001) facet and exposed (100) facet (Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0005"/>f–h), with the Na diffusion energy profiles shown in Figure <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0005"/>i. Similarly, the Na diffusion on the exposed (100) facet demonstrates the most ready route with the lowest energy barrier, which is consistent with the previous binding energy results. It is notable that the Na diffusion in the (001) interlayer generates much larger energy barrier than that of the other two diffusion styles, which further theoretically confirms that surface‐controlled pseudocapacitive behavior is more advantageous than the diffusion‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage process. From another point of view, stable binding status and low diffusion energy barrier are also beneficial to improve the structural stability of the electrode materials during the charge/discharge processes, leading to superior cycling performance. Considering the previous Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics analysis results, the capacitive contributions of F‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC are closer to SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC, but much larger than that of D‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC (for example, at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, 74% vs 68% vs 55%). This is because that the edge area of the nanosheets is much smaller than 2D face area, as a result, the total area of exposed (100) facets is also far smaller than that of exposed (001) facets. Thus, the above calculation results are well consistent with the previous experimental outcomes, and strongly certificate the correctness of our structural design.</p><figure xml:id="adma201900526-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201900526:adma201900526-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201900526-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Theoretical simulations of Na storage and diffusion in SnS<sub>2</sub> crystal structure. a–d) Typical models of Na atom intercalated/adsorbed into/on the (001) interlayer (a), the exposed (001) facet (b), the (100) lattice (c), and the exposed (100) facet (d). e) Binding energies (Δ<i>E</i>, in eV) of the four Na‐SnS<sub>2</sub> patterns. f–h) Typical models of Na atom diffusion paths in/on the (001) interlayer (f), the exposed (001) facet (g), and the exposed (100) facet (h). i) Na diffusion energy profiles through the different trajectories.</caption></figure><p>It is well known that during the low current density, the charging time is quite long and the anode materials are able to be fully charged by solvated Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#adma201900526-bib-0042 #adma201900526-bib-0043"/> Owing to the sluggish kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> solid state diffusion, during the increasing of the current rate, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage and transportation processes are gradually limited, resulting in quick decreasing of specific capacity. Thus, the key point to relieve this issue is that improving the Na<sup>+</sup> storage and diffusion kinetics by rational material structure design. To be more intuitional and based on the previous findings, as illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201900526-fig-0006"><b>6</b></link>, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage of crystalline SnS<sub>2</sub> at different charging situations is revealed. At the low rate, SnS<sub>2</sub> can be completely charged by deep Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation between the interlayer, as well as the pseudocapacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage on the exposed (001) and (100) facets. When the charging rate is increased, the amount of Na<sup>+</sup> stored in the interlayer shows a faster decreasing trend, compared to the surface‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage. Until under the ultrahigh rate, the capacitive‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage becomes more dominant but the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalated in the interlayer turns to be further limited. Therefore, decreasing both of the thickness and size of the crystalline SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets can not only lower the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion energy barrier, but also provide more stable active facets for capacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage.</p><figure xml:id="adma201900526-fig-0006"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201900526:adma201900526-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201900526-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustrating the Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism of SnS<sub>2</sub> crystal structure. According to the abovementioned kinetics analysis and DFT calculation, it can be concluded that the SnS<sub>2</sub> can be fully charged both of the interlayers and exposed active facets under low current densities, but the capacitive‐controlled Na<sup>+</sup> storage on the exposed (100) and (001) facets will dominate more under high rate process.</caption></figure><p>In summary, a series of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with progressively exposed (001) and (100) facets are successfully obtained by our reasonably designed synthesis processes, resulting in three kinds of SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC composites. After sodium‐storage performance testing, the SF‐SnS<sub>2</sub>@NPC demonstrates better rate and cycling performances than the other two anodes, as well as many ever reported SnS<sub>2</sub> based electrode materials. More interestingly, kinetics analysis indicates that different exposed facets of crystalline SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are able to generate dissimilar change styles of CV curves, as well as the corresponding pseudocapacitive contribution improvement trends. By means of the detailed DFT calculations about the Na storage and diffusion in SnS<sub>2</sub> crystal structure, we theoretically determine that the exposed facets possess more stable sites for Na storage, and lead to lower energy barriers during the Na diffusion. Furthermore, the exposed (100) facet is more favorable than exposed (001) facet for capacitive Na storage. In a word, our achievement demonstrates that exposed different crystalline facets are able to generate dissimilar Na<sup>+</sup> storage and diffusion mechanisms. This conclusion can also be expanded to many other crystalline electrode materials, and help to explore the most proper structure design route, thus leading to the high‐rate and stable metal‐ions storage.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201900526-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was mainly supported by MoE Tier 1 (Grant No. RG19/17).
