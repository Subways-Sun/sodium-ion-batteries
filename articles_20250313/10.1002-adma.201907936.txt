Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries are in high demand for large‐scale energy storage applications. Although it is the most prevalent cathode, layered oxide is associated with significant undesirable characteristics, such as multiple plateaus in the charge−discharge profiles, and cation migration during repeated cycling of Na‐ions insertion and extraction, which results in sluggish kinetics, capacity loss, and structural deterioration. Here, a new strategy, i.e., the manipulation of transition‐metal ordering in layered oxides, is proposed to show a prolonged charge−discharge plateau and cation‐migration‐free structural evolution. The results demonstrate that the transition‐metal ordering with a honeycomb‐type superlattice can adjust the crystal lattice and suppress cation migration by modifying the crystal strain to realize a large reversible capacity and excellent cycling performance, which are not characteristics of the widely used common layered oxides. These findings can provide new insight that can be used to improve the design of high‐performance electrode materials for secondary‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201907936-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>An effective strategy in layered oxides with binary transition metals (TMs), i.e., the ordered arrangement of TMs, is applied for the achievement of a relatively stable electrode material, which can relieve the multiple plateaus and cation migration. The designed Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> cathode with ordered superlattice structure delivers an ultralong potential platform, high capacity of 130 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and excellent cycling performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201907936:adma201907936-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201907936-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201907936-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Advances in the utilization of renewable energy and the demands of large‐scale electric markets have created a sense of urgency for the development of cost‐effective and high‐performance electrical energy storage systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0001 #adma201907936-bib-0002 #adma201907936-bib-0003 #adma201907936-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium‐ion batteries are becoming one of most widely used batteries, because of their favorable Na<sup>+</sup>/Na redox potential (−2.71 V vs. standard hydrogen electrode (SHE)), the available energy density, and the relatively low cost, considering the abundance of Na‐based raw materials and Al current collectors.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0005 #adma201907936-bib-0006 #adma201907936-bib-0007 #adma201907936-bib-0008"><sup>5–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is well known that the electrode materials are key factors determining the performance of sodium‐ion batteries. Thus, owing to their remarkable ability to accommodate the reversible intercalation−deintercalation of large Na‐ions, which provide large reversible capacity, layered transition‐metal (TM) oxides have attracted much attention. For example, layered oxides have been researched by Delmas et al. and categorized into two main groups of O3 type and P2 type, wherein the Na‐ions are, respectively, accommodated at octahedral and prismatic site.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0009 #adma201907936-bib-0010"><sup>9,10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As compared to P2 type oxides (i.e., Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>TMO<sub>2</sub>, 0.6 &lt; <i>x</i> &lt; 0.7), O3 type oxides (i.e., NaTMO<sub>2</sub>) have more intercalated Na‐ions and larger capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0011 #adma201907936-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the storage of Na‐ions with a large ionic radius (1.02 Å) makes the layered oxides face many challenges on the multiphase transition, air unstability, and fragile structure during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0013 #adma201907936-bib-0014 #adma201907936-bib-0015 #adma201907936-bib-0016"><sup>13–16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the development of highly stable and high‐performance layered oxides is necessary to effectively promote the applications of Na‐based electrodes materials.</p><p>Layered oxides mainly consist of stacked sheets with edge‐sharing TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedra, and the Na‐ions are accommodated at the octahedral or prismatic sites between the TM slabs. Some recent reports state that the TM‐ions migration from the TM slabs to the Na layers often result in the blocked Na<sup>+</sup> transmission and broken TM slabs as charged to the state of the deep desodiation, such as that of α‐NaFeO<sub>2</sub>, NaCrO<sub>2</sub>, and NaCoO<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0017 #adma201907936-bib-0018 #adma201907936-bib-0019 #adma201907936-bib-0020 #adma201907936-bib-0021"><sup>17–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, any level of TM‐ion migration suppression may be an effective approach to improve the electrochemical performance of layered oxides.</p><p>Herein, we propose the use of a TM/TM‐ordered superstructure in ordered Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> (hereafter referred to as ONNR) as a cathode for sodium‐ion batteries. The results clearly demonstrate that the TMs of the layered oxide have an ordered arrangement in the honeycomb superlattice, which consists of a RuO<sub>6</sub> octahedron surrounded by six NiO<sub>6</sub> edge‐sharing octahedrons. Comparison of the electrochemical performance to that of the disordered Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> (hereafter referred to as DNNR) reveals that the ONNR cathode has an ultralong voltage platform and a higher discharge capacity of 130 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In particular, the proposed TM/TM‐ordered arrangement yields reinforced TM slabs that facilitate the long‐term uptake and release of Na‐ions. The formation of TM/TM‐ordered superstructures via our proposed method may serve as an effective strategy to prevent the structural collapse of heavily cycled layers.</p><p>The ONNR was prepared by inducing a simple solid‐state reaction, and the corresponding crystalline structure was characterized by using the X‐ray diffraction (XRD). The results of XRD data Rietveld analysis of prepared sample (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>) using the GSAS+EXPGUI<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> reveals that the calculated pattern (red circles) is very similar to the experimental result (black line), demonstrating that the sample is assigned to the pure O3 phase and owns a typical layered structure, i.e., a hexagonal lattice with the space group Rm. Note that Table S1 (Supporting Information) presents the fitted lattice parameters for this sample with the low agreement factors. In addition, there is a characteristic peak at 2θ = ≈20°, which is associated with the typical diffraction of a superlattice phase. The composition of this sample was confirmed using a inductively coupled plasma analyzer (ICP), wherein the proportions of Na, Ni, and Ru elements are 0.996, 0.652, and 0.338, respectively. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of this sample (inset, Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0001">1a</link>) shows that this ordered sample is made up of finely divided particles with sizes in the approximate range of 1–2 µm. In addition, selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) and scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) have been employed for in‐depth characterization of sample structure. In the SAED pattern of this sample (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0001">1b</link>), the bright spots projected along the [010] direction are indicative of a typical diffraction pattern of an O3‐type lattice.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0023 #adma201907936-bib-0024 #adma201907936-bib-0025"><sup>23–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The STEM technique, which has sub‐angstrom level spatial resolution, was employed for further analysis of the superstructure of this ordered sample. In particular, the high‐angle annular dark‐field (HAADF) images have revealed a <i>Z</i><sup>1.7</sup> dependence relative to the atomic number <i>Z</i>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0026 #adma201907936-bib-0027 #adma201907936-bib-0028 #adma201907936-bib-0029"><sup>26–29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the HAADF images of ONNR, it can be observed from the perspective of the [310] aixs that the bulk materials have a fine layered O3‐type structure (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0001">1c</link>). When observed from the perspective of the [312] aixs, the ordered structure can be clearly distinguished, as the golden spots represent Ru atoms, and the red pots correspond to Ni atoms (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0001">1d</link>). A new generalized stoichiometric Na(Ni<sub>2/3</sub>Ru<sub>1/3</sub>)O<sub>2</sub>, which can be observed to have a honeycomb‐ordered structure when viewed from the perspective of the <i>c</i> axis, has been found to have an ordered TM arrangement (Figure S1a and S1b, Supporting Information). The simulation image of ONNR oriented in [312] axis has further confirmed the ordered arrangement of two types of atoms in the TM layer, and this arrangement is consistent with that of the atomic model along same axis (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0001">1d</link> inset and Figure S1c, Supporting Information). For comparison, the O3‐phase DNNR was prepared with minimal NiO impurity (Figure S2a and Table S2, Supporting Information). It also has a powder particle morphology with a particle size of 1–2 µm, which is similar with ONNR particles (Figure S2b and S2c, Supporting Information). The HAADF image of DNNR reveals no TM ordering, as the bright spots are randomly distributed (Figure S3a, Supporting Information). Additionally, in contrast to the obvious spots of superlattice patterns in the SAED image of ONNR along [001] axis, the typical O3 phase was observed to have no superlattice patterns in that of DNNR along [001] and other axis (Figure S3b and S3c, Supporting Information). The FIB (focused ion beam)–SAED image (Figure S3d, Supporting Information) of ONNR further confirms that the superlattice phenomenon occurs inside the ONNR particle as shown on the surface. Galvanostatic charge−discharge measurements were performed to examine the differences between the crystal structures of the ordered and disordered arrangements of the ONNR and DNNR cathodes, and consequently analyze their detailed electrochemical behaviors. The charge and discharge curves for ONNR following three cycles at 0.05 C are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>. The first discharge capacity reaches 130 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is close to the theoretical capacity value for the redox coupling of Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Ni<sup>3+</sup> in Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub>. The X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra of Ni 2p and Ru 3p (Figure S4, Supporting Information) further confirm the absence of valence evolution for Ru<sup>5+</sup> during the charge−discharge. Conversely, the redox reaction of Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Ni<sup>3+</sup> serves as the main source of charge transfer.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma201907936-bib-0030 #adma201907936-bib-0031"><sup>30,31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It should be noted that all of the charge−discharge curves are found to have ultralong voltage platforms, which are similar to that of the commercial LiFePO<sub>4</sub> cathode in a conventional Li‐ion battery. As compared to the ONNR cathode, the DNNR cathode delivers a relatively low discharge capacity of 75 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 C and less smooth voltage platforms (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002">2b</link>). Specifically, in the d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> versus voltage curves for the ONNR and DNNR cathodes (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002">2c</link> and Figure S5, Supporting Information), it can be clearly observed that the main peaks are an anodic peak (2.99 V) and a cathodic peak (2.92 V) that occurred at every charge−discharge curve of ONNR cathode (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002">2c</link>). Although there was minimal polarization during the first cycle, the ONNR cathode demonstrates good reversibility in response to Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation, with a voltage difference of only 0.07 V. Alternatively, an anodic peak at 2.98 V and a cathodic peak at 2.91 V, similar to that of the ordered cathode, were observed in the DNNR cathode results (Figure S5, Supporting Information). However, an anodic peak at 2.63 V and cathodic peaks at 2.98 and 2.58 V were also observed. The multiple anodic and cathodic peaks observed in the DNNR cathode results could be attributable to multiphase transformations, which provide the much energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup> transport, and limit the actual capacity of the DNNR cathode. The charge−discharge curves for the ONNR cathode were also obtained at different rates. The results indicate that this cathode is capable of excellent rate performance under the condition of the discharge capacity ranging from 130, 123, 116, 106, 95, 82, 71 to 57 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which corresponded to rates of 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 C, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002">2d</link>). The cycling performance and coulombic efficiency of the two types of cathodes were also measured (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002">2e</link>). It can be seen that the ONNR cathode delivers a more excellent capacity retention of 81% with the capacity ranging from 95 to 77 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C after 1000 cycles. However, regarding the cycling performance of the DNNR cathode, the discharge decreases from 48 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (1st cycle) to the 11 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (500th cycle), and, finally, to 3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (1000th cycle). The rapid capacity degradation of the disordered cathode could be caused by the structural collapse of DNNR during the long‐term desodiation and sodiation processes, whereas the ordered TM layers in ONNR could provide a firmer structure for the Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation process.</p><figure xml:id="adma201907936-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201907936:adma201907936-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201907936-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural characterizations for ONNR. a) X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern and Rietveld plots of Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub>; The charge−discharge curves obtained as a result of in situ measurement (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>, right) were found to agree well with those of the conventional coin cell (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0002">2a</link>). The in situ XRD patterns show that the intensities of the O3‐phase peaks gradually decrease, and those of the P3‐phase peaks increase upon charging to 3.1 V. The transformation from O3 to P3 is caused by the TMO<sub>2</sub> slabs gliding without breaking the TM−O bonds during the desodiation process, and the oxygen packing of the TM layers changes from “AB CA BC” to “AB BC CA”. Therefore, this phenomenon demonstrates that the coexistence of the two phases is the main reason for the ultralong voltage platform, and accounts for most of the charge capacity. When charging to the higher voltage, there is a little phase transform of P3‐ and P′3‐phases coexistence for providing the sequent capacity. Then, the discharge curve displays a reversible phase transition from P′3, P3 to O3 phase, suggesting that ordered cathode has the good reversibility. To precisely detect the evolution of the superlattice and the relative function during cycling, we performed the SAED characterization on the pristine, charged, and discharged ONNR electrodes. The diffraction images of three different states were all viewed from the perspective of the [1–11] zone axis. The big bright spots of (101), (011–), and (112–) etc. (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0003">3b</link>) in diffraction patterns of pristine are indexed to the typical reflections originating from the O3‐layered structure. Additionally, the small spots circled by white spots are assigned to the of superlattice phase, indicating that the pristine cathode has an ordered superstructure. Furthermore, the diffraction originated from the superlattice of ONNR was also observed to be well maintained after charge and discharge. More specifically, the <i>d</i>‐spacing of (101) in the pristine sample (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0003">3b</link>) is 0.374 Å<sup>−1</sup> in reciprocal space, and increases to 0.384 Å<sup>−1</sup> when charged to 3.8 V, while it returns to 0.373 Å<sup>−1</sup> after being discharged to 2 V, corresponding to the breadth of crystal lattice during sodium release and uptake. To compare the differences between the ordered and disordered samples, the in situ XRD patterns of disordered cathode have also been obtained (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The main difference could be that the O3 to P3 phase transition occurs as a gradual change process, where various intermediate phases such as O′3 and O′′3 phases coexist, rather than a simple two‐phases coexistence.</p><figure xml:id="adma201907936-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201907936:adma201907936-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201907936-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural characterizations of ONNR during the eletrochemical performance. a) In situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of ONNR cathode. Scar bar is 2 1/nm.</caption></figure><p>To investigate the influence of the TM arrangement on long‐term desodiation and sodiation during the electrochemical process, the crystal structures of cycled cathodes with ordered and disordered structures have been further explored and compared by performing XRD and STEM characterizations. The XRD patterns of the pristine ONNR, ONNR cathode after 1000 cycles, and DNNR after 1000 cycles have been obtained (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>). It can be observed that after 1000 cycles of Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction, the crystal phase of the most ordered cathode can recover the O3 phase with minimal P3 phase in the XRD pattern of ONNR. Regarding the DNNR cathode results, most of the P3 phase is unable to recover the initial O3 phase after 1000 cycles. For a confirmative comparison, the SAED patterns of cycled ONNR and DNNR cathodes along the same [201] axis (Figure S7, Supporting Information) have been obtained, and the results further have demonstrated that the superlattice structure is still retained in the ONNR cathode after long cycling. In order to probe mechanism of phase transformation in the cathodes after long‐term cycling, the cathodes have been further characterized by performing STEM measurements. The HAADF‐STEM image of the cycled cathode with an ordered superstructure was obtained (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4b</link>), from the perspective of the [310] direction, and the TM atomic columns (bright spots) appeared to form a typical layered structure. The corresponding fast Fourier transformation (FFT) image (inset, Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4b</link>) reveals the single crystalline nature of this sample. Additionally, the relevant line profile of this HAADF image (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4c</link>) clearly shows that the TM columns and Na<sup>+</sup> columns have different intensities. An apparent difference between these two columns is that the Na<sup>+</sup> columns had a relatively low intensity. However, regarding the results for the DNNR cathode after 1000 cycles, anomalous spots can be observed in the corresponding FFT images (inset, Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4d</link>), suggesting that the cycled disordered cathode has the characteristics of a multicrystalline structure. More importantly, several large bright‐spot columns were observed to exist between the TM layers in the HAADF image. These columns could be formed by the degraded TM that originated from the TM layers (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4d</link>). The line profile of this HAADF image also reveals the existence of high‐intensity metal atoms between the adjacent TM columns, indicating that some TMs could migrate from the metal‐oxide layers of the disordered cathode after long‐term cycling (Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4e</link>). On the basis of the above‐presented observations and analyses, the mechanisms of ordered and disordered cathodes have been illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201907936-fig-0004">4f</link>. In the ONNR, the TMs present the ordered honeycomb‐type arrangement, which afford a firm hexagonal structure in the planes of the TM layers. This regular arrangement could cause TM ions to generate an interactive force, which analogizes with the connected force of the “ordered puzzle,” in favor of the high‐stability of TM layers during the numerous desodiation and sodiation. However, the TM atoms are arranged in an irregular order in the DNNR. During the long‐term electrochemical cycling, the heavy Na‐ions (de)intercalation into interlayers could more easily cause the structural collapse for the DNNR, which resembles that of the “chaotic puzzle” without an ordered interactive force.</p><figure xml:id="adma201907936-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201907936:adma201907936-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201907936-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Comparison of cycling performances between ONNR and DNNR cathodes. a) X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of ordered pristine, ordered cathode after 1000 cycles, and disordered cathode after 1000 cycles (All tested samples were protected by Kapton film, and a broad halo at ≈20° is assigned to Kapton film in XRD patterns). b) HAADF‐STEM image of ordered cathode; scale bar, 1 nm. Inset, corresponding fast‐Fourier transformation (FFT) image of ordered cathode. c) Line profile of the STEM image in ordered cathode. d) HAADF‐STEM image of disordered cathode; scale bar, 1 nm. Inset, corresponding FFT image of disordered cathode. e) Line profile of the STEM image in disordered cathode. f) Schematic diagram showing that the ordered arrangement of transition metal (TM) in layered oxide could provide a more stable structure for long‐term (de)intercalations of Na<sup>+</sup> in comparison with that of the disordered arrangement.</caption></figure><p>In conclusion, using the example of layered metal oxides with binary TMs, we have demonstrated a feasible approach via constructing the ordered arrangement of TMs to achieve a relatively stable electrode material. The crucial function of TMs arrangement has been investigated by comparing the ONNR and DNNR cathodes. It is noteworthy that the ONNR cathode, which was designed to have an ordered superlattice structure, achieves a high capacity of 130 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, an ultralong voltage platform and the excellent cycling performance. Unlike the disordered arrangement of the DNNR structure, the regular arrangement of TMs in ONNR affords a firm layered structure, which enables the durable Na‐ion extraction and insertion to be sustained. Thus, ordered cathode materials may provide a promising idea for promoting the advanced sodium‐ion batteries in the extensively electrical markets, as well as promote the development of other electrode materials for the high‐stable and large‐scale energy storages.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0020"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0030"><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0040"><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0050"><title type="main">Preparation of ONNR and DNNR</title><p>Distinct calcinations were utilized to prepare the ordered and disordered samples. Prior to the calcinations, the precursors were obtained by mixing the Na, Ni, and Ru sources (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NiO, RuO<sub>2</sub>, &gt; 98%, Wako) with the stoichiometric amounts. Then, the precursors were ball‐milled with alcohol additions for 15 h at 200 rpm. After being dried at 80 °C for 12 h, the obtained powders were pressed into pellets. The ordered sample was formed by calcinating the precursors in a tube furnace at a temperature of 900 °C for 15 h in a flowing O<sub>2</sub> gas atmosphere. Alternatively, the disordered sample was prepared by preheating precursors in a muffle furnace at 650 °C for 10 h. The obtained sample was subsequently calcinated in a tube furnace at 900 °C for 15 h under flowing Ar gas. Finally, the obtained sample was sealed and stored in dried and vacuum‐sealed sacks.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0060"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The electrochemical performance was measured by assembling the Na/Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> half cells. These cells were assembled by using a CR2032 coin‐type cell. The sodium metal (Wako), glass fiber (Whatman), and Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> electrode were implemented as the anode, separator, and cathode, respectively. The process of Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> cathode preparation included the three steps. First, the Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> sample, Super P carbon, and poly(tetrafluoroethylene) (PTFE) were mixed at a weight ratio of 80:10:10. Second, the resulting slurry was cast on an aluminum foil (Al, Nilaco) at a loading of 2–3 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Third, the electrodes were dried in a vacuum drying oven for 12 h, and then stored for subsequent use. The cells were assembled in an Ar‐filled glove box (&lt;1 ppm of H<sub>2</sub>O and O<sub>2</sub>), and the electrolyte consisted of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC, LIPASTE‐P/S1, Tomiyama′s High Purity Chemicals) with 2 vol% fluorinated ethylene carbonate (FEC, C<sub>3</sub>H<sub>3</sub>FO<sub>3</sub>, &gt; 98%, TCL). The assembled Na/Na<sub>3</sub>Ni<sub>2</sub>RuO<sub>6</sub> cells were measured by using a Hokuto charging−discharging machine. Note that 1 C = 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0070"><title type="main">SEM and XRD Characterizations</title><p>SEM images were obtained by using a Hitachi S4800 machine. XRD patterns were obtained by using a Bruker D8 Advanced diffractometer with Cu Kα (λ = 1.5406 Å) radiation.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0080"><title type="main">ICP Characterizations</title><p>In this work, the composition was detected by using a SHIMADZU ICPS‐8100. The samples for ICP characterization were prepared by the pretreatment for substances with the nitrohydrochloric acid, which is formed by mixing concentrated nitric acid and hydrochloric acid (Sigma−Aldrich) in a volume ration of 1:3.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201907936-sec-0090"><title type="main">STEM and XPS Characterizations</title><p>XPS was performed by using a VG scientific ESCALAB 250 spectrometer with monochromic Al Kα excitation. The diffraction patterns and HAADF images were obtained by operating a Tecnai G2 F20 at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV, and a double aberration‐corrected Titan<sup>3</sup> Cubed 60–300 (FEI) STEM at an accelerating voltage of 300 kV. Note that the resolution of STEM was higher than 0.08 nm.</p><p>STEM sample preparation: After being charged to 3.8 V or discharged to 2.0 V, the coin cells were disassembled, and the cathode was immersed in a pure PC solution to remove the residual salts. Supersonic vibration was applied to ensure good dispersion of the powder samples within a small vial with pure DMC solution before they were dropped onto a carbon TEM grid.
