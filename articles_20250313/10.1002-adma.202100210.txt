xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>To meet the ever‐growing demand for advanced rechargeable batteries with light weight and compact size, much effort has been devoted to improving the volumetric capacity of electrodes. Herein, an effective strategy of polymorph engineering is proposed to boost the volumetric capacity of FeSe. Owing to the inherent metallic electronic conductivity of tetragonal‐FeSe, a conductive additive‐free electrode (hereafter denoted as CA‐free) can be assembled with an enhanced sodium storage volumetric capacity of 1011 mAh cm<sup>−3</sup>, significantly higher than semiconducting hexagonal‐FeSe. Impressively, the CA‐free electrode can achieve an extremely high active material utilization of 96.7 wt% and high initial Coulombic efficiency of 96%, superior to most of the anodes for Na‐ion storage. Moreover, the design methodology is branched out using tetragonal FeSe as the cathode for Li‐ion batteries. The CA‐free tetragonal‐FeSe electrode can achieve a high volumetric energy density of 1373 Wh L<sup>−1</sup> and power density of 7200 W L<sup>−1</sup>, outperforming most metal chalcogenides. Reversible conversion reactions are revealed by in situ XRD for both sodium and lithium systems. The proposed design strategy provides new insight and inspiration to aid in the ongoing quest for better electrode materials.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202100210-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Benefiting from the inherent high electronic conductivity of metallic tetragonal FeSe, a conducting additive‐free (CA‐free) electrode configuration is realized. xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Electrode materials, as the most vital components of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), have made great strides since the discovery of electrochemical lithium storage behavior of LiCoO<sub>2</sub> in the cathode side and graphite as the anode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0001 #adma202100210-bib-0002 #adma202100210-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> So far, most research pursue extremely high gravimetric capacity which, however, is not the only important metric for LIBs applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In areas of consumer electronics, microelectronics, military, aerospace, and even in electric vehicles, LIBs are needed not only to own light weight but compact size.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0005 #adma202100210-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, volumetric capacity should have been prioritized over gravimetric one, yet this crucial metric is often neglected.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Pitifully, the same situation also applies to the newly built sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), which is at the early stage of their development.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As for their potential application in the smart grid, the battery size is also decisive when being stored in the outdoors.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In comparison with the cathode, anode volumetric capacity is of particular importance because cathode materials usually offer much higher volumetric capacity than the state‐of‐the‐art anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Till now, the strategies used to enhance volumetric capacity mainly focus on the material‐level design, like the synthesis of Si@graphitic carbon nanowire array<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> or thick CNT‐Si film,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which increased the tap density of electrode and thus improved the volumetric capacity up to 1500 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup>. Another method is to composite the low‐volumetric‐capacity material with the ones owning high volume density. Taking advantage of high‐volume density of SnO<sub>2</sub> (6.5 g cm<sup>–3</sup>), several nanocomposites were reported to deliver an enhanced volumetric capacity, like hollow structured SnO<sub>2</sub>@Si nanospheres<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Si@SnO<sub>2</sub> core–shell particles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Besides the materials level, the electrode‐level performance is also crucial for volumetric capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As we all know, a working electrode generally consists of active material and a certain amount of electrochemical inactive components, such as binder and conducting additive (hereafter denoted as CA), which usually account for ca. 20–30% of the total electrode mass (Table <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Although much effort has been attempted to increase the energy density via electrode components optimization, like the binder‐free electrode,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the CA (Super P in most cases) seem to be indispensable since the electronic conductivity of most electrode candidates is inherently low.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Even worse, in most anodes, additional carbonaceous framework is highly needed for better rate capability and long‐term stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It has been demonstrated that, interestingly, Li<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub> electrode works well after removing CA out and the secret lies in the high electronic conductivity of the intercalated phases, i.e., Li<sub>4+δ</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub> and the interphase boundary equilibrated with Li<sub>7</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub>, which can help to accelerate the electron transportation during cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0018 #adma202100210-bib-0019"><sup>18,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, rational design of electrode materials with metallic conductivity could be a valid strategy toward high volumetric capacity, yet it remains challenging.</p><p>Encouraged by the discovery of high‐temperature superconductivity in layered LaFeAsO<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>F<i><sub>x</sub></i>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> iron selenide (FeSe) has attracted particular interest owing to the crystallographic simplicity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> FeSe crystallizes in two different polymorphs with different electronic structures: hexagonal NiAs‐type structure (hereafter denoted as <i>h</i>‐FeSe) is a semiconductor,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> while, tetragonal PbO‐type structure (<i>t</i>‐FeSe) is a metal with superconducting transition below 10 K,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> as displayed in the schematic crystal structure of <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001"><b>1b</b></link>,<link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">c</link>, respectively. Besides the exotic physical properties, FeSe shows great potential as the anode material for LIBs and SIBs owing to the high theoretical capacity. So far, most works have focused on the synthesis of nanocomposites with carbonaceous material to address their serious volume expansion during the conversion reaction.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0023 #adma202100210-bib-0024"><sup>23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite the great achievements, the merit of inherent metallic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe is usually being omitted. Since its electronic conductivity is comparable to or even higher than the carbonaceous materials, <i>t</i>‐FeSe is anticipated as the ideal material to assemble a CA‐free electrode without sacrificing its reversibility and high‐rate capability. On the other hand, ruling out the CA from the electrode complex could benefit to increase the density of working electrode; thus enhance the volumetric capacity output. The side reaction related to the decomposition of electrolyte to form SEI could also be largely suppressed due to the absence of high‐surface‐area CA, leading to a high initial Coulombic efficiency. In addition, <i>t</i>‐FeSe is thermodynamically stable with lower formation energy (−0.275 eV) as compared with <i>h</i>‐phase (−0.08 eV).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hence, it is meaningful to employ <i>t</i>‐FeSe as the electrode directly because it can avoid the irreversible phase transition from <i>h</i>‐ to <i>t</i>‐phase in the initial cycle and corresponding electrode reorganization.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100210-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100210:adma202100210-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100210-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,d) Synchrotron X‐ray diffraction patterns and Rietveld refinement of <i>h</i>‐FeSe with <i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> = 3.73% and <i>R</i><sub>p</sub> = 2.43% (a), and <i>t</i>‐FeSe with <i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> = 7.52% and <i>R</i><sub>p</sub> = 4.93% (d). b,c) Schematic crystal structure of <i>h</i>‐FeSe (b) and <i>t</i>‐FeSe (c). e,f) Temperature dependence of resistivity of <i>h</i>‐FeSe (e) and <i>t</i>‐FeSe (f). g,h) SEM images of <i>h</i>‐FeSe (g) and <i>t</i>‐FeSe (h).</caption></figure><p>Herein, we propose an effective strategy of polymorph engineering to boost the volumetric capacity of FeSe anode for Na<sup>+</sup> and Li<sup>+</sup> ions storage. Specifically, we directly employ <i>t</i>‐FeSe to realize a CA‐free electrode configuration with the result of an enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> storage volumetric capacity of 1011 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup>, much higher than the CA‐contained materials. More impressively, the proposed CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode raises the active material utilization from 70  to 96.7 wt% without sacrificing either the capacity or durability. The electrode also exhibits the highest initial Coulombic efficiency of 96% in the state‐of‐the‐art metal sulfides or selenides. Furthermore, <i>t</i>‐FeSe is investigated as the CA‐free cathode for lithium‐ion battery in a valid high working potential between 2.8 and 1.0 V with a superior volumetric energy and power densities. The enhanced volumetric performances can be understood in terms of the inherent metallic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe, which contributes to the construction of CA‐free electrode with compressed electrode density and fast electron compensation during electrochemical processes.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussions</title><section xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0030"><title type="main">Crystal Structure and Physical Property</title><p>The crystal structure of as‐prepared <i>h</i>‐ and <i>t</i>‐FeSe were examined via synchrotron X‐ray diffraction (XRD) together with the Rietveld refinement. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">1a</link>, all the reflections can be indexed based on <i>h</i>‐FeSe with the refined lattice parameters of <i>a = b =</i> 3.6183 Å and <i>c</i> = 5.8938 Å, consistent with the previous reported values.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In contrast, as displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">1d</link>, the reflections of <i>t</i>‐FeSe are indexed based on two different phases of FeSe: the main phase belongs to tetragonal symmetry with a space group of <i>p</i>4/<i>nmm</i>, while, the minor one corresponds to hexagonal structure with a space group of <i>P</i>63/<i>mmc</i>. Rietveld refinement suggests a molar ratio of 71:29 for <i>t</i>‐FeSe:<i>h</i>‐FeSe. It has been demonstrated that <i>t</i>‐FeSe exists on the Fe‐rich side, while <i>h</i>‐FeSe phases exists on the Se‐rich side according to the phase diagram of Fe‐Se system.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> When the annealing temperature is increased above the sublimation temperature of Se (685 °C), the poor Se environment benefit to the formation of tetragonal phase. However, it is really hard to achieve a uniform environment during solid phase reaction, so minor phase of <i>h</i>‐FeSe is accompanied. The high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information) also reveal a mixed phase formation that the interplanar spacing of 0.55 nm corresponds to (001) plane of tetragonal phase, while, 0.31 nm belongs to (100) plane of hexagonal phase. Moreover, the resistivity of <i>h</i>‐ and <i>t</i>‐phases were measured via a four‐point probe method. As displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">1e</link>, <i>h</i>‐FeSe shows a semiconducting behavior, in sharp contrast to the metallic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">1f</link>) whose resistivity decreases as the temperature lowers, despite the mixed phase characteristic. In particular, the room‐temperature electronic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe is achieved as 1003 S cm<sup>–1</sup>, more than 13 times higher than that of <i>h</i>‐FeSe (73 S cm<sup>–1</sup>). Noted that the electronic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe is also very competitive as compared with various carbonaceous materials (10–1000 S cm<sup>–1</sup>),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0027 #adma202100210-bib-0028"><sup>27,28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> seeing a strong possibility to construct a working electrode without Super P additive. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">1g</link>,<link href="#adma202100210-fig-0001">h</link>) shows that both <i>h</i>‐ and <i>t</i>‐FeSe crystallize into microsized particles. Energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy mapping (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) indicates a homogeneous distribution of Fe and Se in the as‐prepared <i>t</i>‐phase FeSe.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0040"><title type="main">Sodium Storage Property of <i>t</i>‐FeSe</title><p>The sodium storage property of <i>t</i>‐FeSe was first evaluated by assembling a coin‐type cell with normal mass ratio of active materials, Super P and binder as 7:2:1. The <i>h</i>‐phase electrode was prepared in the same way for comparison. To understand the redox behavior during Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction, cyclic voltammetry (CV) profiles were recorded at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>–1</sup> between 3.0 and 0.3 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). As displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0002"><b>2a</b></link>, three intense peaks appear in the first cathodic scan. The ones at 1.3 and 0.7 V correspond to the formation of intermediate phase of Na<sub>y</sub>Fe<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and further conversion reaction into Fe particles and Na<sub>2</sub>Se, respectively. Detailed phase evolution will be discussed in the subsequent in situ XRD discussion. The one at 1.1 V, which appears in the first cycle and vanishes in the subsequent cycles, can be attributed to the conversion reaction of impurity <i>h</i>‐FeSe (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). In the anodic scan, <i>t</i>‐FeSe shows one distinct broad peak at 1.5 V due to the reverse process from Fe and Na<sub>2</sub>Se into <i>t</i>‐FeSe. Additionally, there are several weak humps in the CV profiles, like 2.0 V in the anodic and 1.6 V in the cathodic scan, which can be attributed to the side redox reaction of Super P (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). After the initial cycle, <i>h</i>‐FeSe shows the same two cathodic peaks as <i>t</i>‐phase (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information), indicative of a phase transition from <i>h</i>‐ to <i>t</i>‐phase. And all the CV curves are superposed on each other, indicative of an excellent electrochemical reversibility. Furthermore, <i>t</i>‐FeSe anode delivers an initial discharge and charge capacities of 449 and 407 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>, respectively, with initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) of 91%, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0002">2b</link>. After 50 cycles, the electrode can still deliver a high capacity of 377 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> with a high capacity retention of 93% (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0002">2c</link>). Interestingly to note that there is no big difference between the cycle performance for <i>t</i>‐ and <i>h</i>‐phase FeSe in the normal electrode configuration (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). The reason lies in the positive effect of introduced conducting additive (Super P in our case) that facilitates fast electron transfer for the semiconducting <i>h</i>‐FeSe.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100210-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100210:adma202100210-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100210-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV profiles of <i>t</i>‐FeSe between 0.3 and 3.0 V at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles of <i>t</i>‐FeSe at 0.5 C. c) The corresponding cycling performance and Coulombic efficiency at 0.5 C. d) Nyquist plot of the <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode with carbon and without carbon. e) Cycling performance of CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe and <i>h</i>‐FeSe electrodes at 0.5 C and f) rate performance (1 C = 400 mA g<sup>–1</sup>).</caption></figure><p>To emphasize the efficiency of high electronic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe, the conductive additive is removed from the electrode complex and a CA‐free electrode is assembled for SIBs application. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0002">2d</link>, there is nearly no difference in the resistance at the OCV states for <i>t</i>‐FeSe before (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> = 11.7 ± 0.9 Ω) and after taking CA out (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> = 11.8 ± 0.8 Ω) owing to its inherent high conductivity. In sharp contrast, the CA‐free <i>h</i>‐FeSe electrode delivers much larger resistance than the CA‐contained electrode because of its semiconducting behavior (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). As compared in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0002">2e</link>, the CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode delivers a high reversible capacity of 400 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> with a capacity retention of 99% over 30 cycles, in sharp contrast to the extremely low capacity of the <i>h</i>‐phase. Even if the CA is moved out, the inherent high electronic conductivity of the <i>t</i>‐phase guarantees high utilization of the active materials, yet the <i>h</i>‐phase does not. Note that the experimental capacity is a little higher than the theoretical expected one (397 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>). This phenomenon could be attributed to the interface storage since the conversion‐<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> or alloying‐type<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> anode materials would decompose into nanoparticles with large specific area when being discharged to the low working potential. A recent study also suggested that the electrochemically reduced Fe nanoparticles could contribute to the spin‐polarized capacitance owing to the accumulation of spin‐polarized charges at the interface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the advance of metallic <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode is presented in the comparison of discharge capacities at various rates, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0002">2f</link>. At a high current rate of 12.5 C, <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode achieves a high reversible capacity of 284 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>, which recovers to 374 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> when the current rate turns back to 0.5 C, while, CA‐free <i>h</i>‐FeSe electrode can hardly deliver any capacity at 1.25 C‐rate due to the sluggish kinetic properties. Thus, <i>h</i>‐FeSe plays a negligible role in the electrochemical performance of the mixed <i>t</i>‐FeSe phases since the inherent semiconducting electronic conductivity of the <i>h</i>‐phase induces sluggish kinetic properties.</p><p>In addition, the electrochemical properties of CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe with different electrode components (active material:CA:binder = 7:2:1, 8:1:1, 9:0:1 and 29:0:1) were measured. The detailed calculation method for the volumetric capacity is given in the Experimental Section and Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S7</link> (Supporting Information). As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003"><b>3a</b></link>, four electrodes exhibit similar profiles, indicative of the similar Na<sup>+</sup> storage processes, yet slight distinction in voltage polarization. While the volumetric capacity reveals a significant difference in response to the conducting additive content. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">3b</link>, 9:0:1 electrode delivers volumetric capacity of 825 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup>, higher than the 8:1:1 (639 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup>) and 7:2:1 (408 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup>) samples. On account of the high density of FeSe, we further reduce the content of electrochemical inactive binder to achieve a high utilization of active material. Surprisingly, the highest volumetric capacity of 1011 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup> is delivered in the 29:0:1 sample, which is one of the highest values among the transition metal sulfides or selenides (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">3d</link>). Besides, the ICE of 29:0:1 sample is achieved as 96%, the highest value in the state‐of‐the‐art metal sulfides or selenides, as compared in Table <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information). This enhancement in the volumetric capacity and ICE is closely related to the proposed strategy of removing the puffy Super P from the electrode complex, which not only contributes to the increase of density of the electrode but also suppresses the side reactions between the electrolyte and electrode, since Super P has larger specific areas (65.5593 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, detailed nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information) than <i>t</i>‐FeSe (14.0772 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>). Furthermore, decreasing the binder content to 3% could help to increase the electrochemical reversibility and ICE, since the binders are known as insulating and electrochemically inactive.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The superiority in the CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode is also demonstrated in the high‐rate capability tests, as depicted in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">3c</link>. Though all four electrodes demonstrate strong tolerance to the applied current densities, the CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode delivers the highest reversible capacities owing to the high utilization of the active materials. In comparison with the reported volumetric capacities in the up‐to‐date SIBs anodes (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">3d</link>), the CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode is also very competitive, especially under higher current density.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100210-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100210:adma202100210-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100210-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Volumetric charge–discharge profiles of <i>t</i>‐FeSe at 0.5 C. b) Cycle stability at 0.5 C. c) Rate capability. d) Comparison of volumetric capacities with other reported values.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0008 #adma202100210-bib-0034 #adma202100210-bib-0035 #adma202100210-bib-0036 #adma202100210-bib-0037 #adma202100210-bib-0038 #adma202100210-bib-0039 #adma202100210-bib-0040"><sup>8,34–40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> e) Voltage versus time profile of the CA‐free electrode at FDSC mode. f) Cycling performance of CA‐free electrode at FDSC mode. g) Long‐term cycling test of CA‐free electrode at 0.5 and 2.5 C.</caption></figure><p>What is more, in circumstances of commercial application, such as electric vehicles and 3 C products, the strong ability of fast charging and slow discharging is highly desirable.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As for the half‐cell setup, whether the anode could afford fast discharge and slow charge scanning is especially essential since the charging processes of full cell corresponds to the inserted process of ions into the anode side. Thus, the CA‐free <i>t</i>‐FeSe electrode was further evaluated in a fast‐discharge–slow‐charge (FDSC) mode, under which various current rates are applied in discharge process, while recharged at 0.5 C rate. As demonstrated in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">3e</link>,<link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">f</link>, the electrode delivers a reversible volumetric capacity of 803 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup> at 5 C discharge rate (corresponding to 9 min). When being recharged at 0.5 C rate, all of the inserted sodium ions can be released. After cycled at FDSC mode for 100 cycles, the electrode exhibits decent cycling capability with almost no capacity decay. Moreover, the CA‐free electrode demonstrated outstanding long‐term cycle stability with a capacity retention of 94% and 92% after 500 cycles at 0.5 C and 2.5 C, respectively, as displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0003">3g</link>, which is attributable to the selected ether electrolyte in favor of cycling stability even for the micro‐sized material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, the higher cutting off voltage of 0.3 V could benefit to suppress the over‐reaction of conversion‐type materials with long‐lived cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, impressively to note that the proposed CA‐free electrode raised the active material utilization from 70% (CA‐contained) to 96.7% without sacrificing the durability and rate capability. As compared in Table <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information), the material utilization in our electrode configuration outperforms most of the reported anode materials or commercial LIBs level (92%–96% depending on various electrode materials), further demonstrating the superiority of our proposed design strategy.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0050"><title type="main">Sodium Storage Process</title><p>The phase evolution of <i>t</i>‐FeSe was then investigated via in situ XRD. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100210-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link> displays the contour map of <i>t</i>‐FeSe on a live SIB cell for the initial two cycles. At the OCV state, all the reflections belong to <i>t</i>‐FeSe phase, except the ones from Be window and <i>h</i>‐FeSe (at 32.3°). As the initial discharge to 1.36 V, there is no obvious peak change, indicative of Na<sup>+</sup> insertion process into <i>t</i>‐FeSe with the potential product of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>FeSe. Subsequently, the reflections of the <i>t</i>‐phase fade away rapidly; in the meanwhile, a series of new ones at 24.3°, 30.5°, and 36.2° emerge along with the flat voltage plateau at 1.35 V in the GCD profile, characteristics of a two‐phase transition. After careful indexing, these new reflections can be attributed to ThCr<sub>2</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>‐structured Na<i><sub>y</sub></i>Fe<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>2</sub> with space group of <i>I</i>4/<i>mmm</i>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0043"><sup>43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Also, the reflection of <i>h</i>‐FeSe at 32.3° disappears completely upon Na<sup>+</sup> insertion, consistent with the small plateau at 1.14 V. Further Na<sup>+</sup> insertion induces the flat voltage plateau at 0.75 V, where Na<i><sub>y</sub></i>Fe<sub>2</sub>Se<sub>2</sub> decomposes and converts to Na<sub>2</sub>Se and Fe, as evidenced by the vanish of these reflections and the simultaneous appearance of broad diffraction peaks at 22.5° (Na<sub>2</sub>Se), 37.6° (Na<sub>2</sub>Se), and 44.6° (Fe). Note that the hexagonal phase cannot be re‐formed after Na<sup>+</sup> extraction, which is consistent with the previously reported result,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and also verified by the ex situ XRD (Figure <link href="#adma202100210-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information). The irreversible phase transformation can be attributed to the higher formation energy of <i>h</i>‐FeSe (−0.08 eV) than that of the tetragonal phase (−0.275 eV).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100210-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the second cycle, <i>t</i>‐FeSe exhibits the same phase transition as the initial one, suggestive a high reversibility upon sodium insertion and extraction. xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0070"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>A new strategy of rational manufacturing of CA‐free electrode is proposed to boost the volumetric parameters of rechargeable battery. The merits of metallic <i>t</i>‐FeSe as the electrode material for SIBs can be summarized as follows: 1) the inherent metallic conductivity of <i>t</i>‐FeSe is essential to construct a CA‐free electrode, with an enhanced active materials utilization ratio from 70% to 96.7%; 2) the electrode could deliver an enhanced volumetric capacity of 1011 mAh cm<sup>–3</sup> after taking low‐density conducting additive out; 3) the CA‐electrode shows a high ICE of 96%, indicative of high utilization of the active material. Furthermore, the design methodology can be used for Li<sup>+</sup> storage with a high volumetric energy density of 1373 Wh L<sup>–1</sup>. The findings suggest that the metallic <i>t</i>‐FeSe is a promising candidate as the electrode for SIBs and LIBs toward high volumetric energy density. In particular, the present design principle can be extended to other functional electrode materials for building high‐efficiency energy storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100210-sec-0080" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>L.Z. and Z.W. contributed equally to this work.
