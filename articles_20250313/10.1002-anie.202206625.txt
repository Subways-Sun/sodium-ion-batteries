However, double‐high activity and reversibility is challenging due to the structural rearrangement and potential oxygen loss. Here, we propose a strategy for constructing a dual honeycomb‐superlattice structure in Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Li<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>][Mg<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> to simultaneously realize high activity and reversibility of lattice O redox. Theoretical simulation and electrochemical tests show that [Li<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>] superlattice units remarkably trigger the anion redox activity and enable the delivery of a record capacity of 285.9 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in layered sodium‐ion battery cathodes. Nuclear magnetic resonance and in situ X‐ray diffraction reveal that [Mg<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>] superlattice units are beneficial to the structure and anion redox reversibility, where Li<sup>+</sup> reversibly shuttles between Na layers and transition‐metal slabs in contrast to the absence of [Mg<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>] units. Our findings underline the importance of multifunctional units and provide a path to advanced battery materials.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A well‐designed sodium‐ion battery layered cathode has been constructed via dual honeycomb‐superlattice regulation, and this displayed double‐high anion redox activity and reversibility. Experimental and theoretical studies are carried out to explore the mechanism.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202206625:anie202206625-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202206625-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie202206625-sec-0001"><p>Low‐cost and high‐performance sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are strategically pivotal in large‐scale energy storage.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Cathodes are one of the most crucial components to determine the output voltage and energy density of SIBs, where layered oxides are intensively investigated due to its flexible composition, and high theoretical capacity.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Anion redox holds the promise of realizing excess high capacity beyond the theoretical limit of layered cathodes.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> However, the sufficient oxygen redox in layered structures is always accompanied by the structural rearrangement and redox irreversibility upon sodium release and uptake. Double‐high reactivity and reversibility remain a big challenge.</p><p>To trigger anionic redox activity in layered oxides, introduction of low‐electronegativity Li<sup>+</sup> into the TM slabs to generate the unhybridized oxygen 2p state by constituting Na−O−Li interactions is always necessary.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0011"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Na<sub>0.72</sub>[Li<sub>0.24</sub>Mn<sub>0.76</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> reported by Hu et al. delivers 271 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> during the first discharge between 1.5–4.5 V that is hitherto the highest capacity among layered transition metal cathodes of SIBs.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0014"><sup>[5]</sup></link> However, a series of findings indicated that the Li<sup>+</sup> might provoke superfluous O activity that the high‐activity oxygen ions are completely oxidized into O<sub>2</sub> escaping from the materials during charge.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0015"><sup>[6]</sup></link> The vacancy clusters and molecular O<sub>2</sub> appear at the charged state of the Na<sub>0.75</sub>[Li<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> and the Li<sup>+</sup> migrates to the Na layer,<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0018"><sup>[7]</sup></link> leading to the structure damage. Meanwhile, it has been reported that Mg<sup>2+</sup> existing in TM slabs could also stabilize the anion redox. Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions have relatively stronger ionic interactions with lattice O compared to Li<sup>+</sup>, which constrains the O loss and holds the superstructure to some extent.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0019"><sup>[8]</sup></link> The cyclicity meliorates with the increase of the Mg<sup>2+</sup> content in Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Mg<sub><i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0020"><sup>[9]</sup></link> The Mg doping in Na<sub>5/6</sub>Li<sub>1/4</sub>Mn<sub>3/4</sub>O<sub>2</sub> enhances the structure stability.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0021"><sup>[10]</sup></link> More recently, it is noteworthy that the regulation of the superlattice has a great influence on the anion redox during electrochemical processes. The superstructure such as one Li‐ion surrounded by six Mn‐ions trends to form in TM slabs due to the mismatch in the ionic valence and radius between the two kinds of metal ions.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0017 #anie202206625-bib-0022"><sup>[6b, 11]</sup></link> Bruce et al. have reported Na<sub>0.6</sub>[Li<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> as a material possessing a ribbon‐ordered superstructure, which holds back the vacancy cluster generation.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0018"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Pan et al. have demonstrated a strategy to regulate the superstructure motifs.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0017"><sup>[6b]</sup></link></p><p>The reported materials introducing the Li‐6Mn superlattice have made progress especially in terms of the superior capacity. The Mg‐6Mn superlattice introduction prevents oxygen loss while exhibiting an excess capacity. Herein, we combine these two elements and demonstrate a dual‐honeycomb superlattice in a layered cathode of Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Li<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>][Mg<sub>1/7</sub>Mn<sub>5/14</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (denoted as LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>) to simultaneously enhance the activity and reversibility of anion redox. The Li‐6Mn superlattice awakens the anionic redox activity, thus ensuring a record discharge capacity of 285.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The density functional theory (DFT) calculations, X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) tests reveal the participation of O redox. Meanwhile, the maintenance of the reversibility is attributed to the Mg‐6Mn superlattice, which served as pillars when Li<sup>+</sup> are removed from the TM layers at high degrees of desodiation, which prevents vacancy clusters, oxygen evolution and the deficiency of structure integrity, leading to a good cyclicity that maintains 85.5 % after 50 cycles at 1 C (100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). The in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) demonstrates favourable structural reversibility. The nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) illustrates the Li<sup>+</sup> manage to return to the pristine honeycomb sites in the TM layer, in contrast to Na<sub>0.78</sub>Li<sub>0.26</sub>Mn<sub>0.74</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (LiMn<sub>6</sub>).</p><p>The LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>, LiMn<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mg<sub>0.28</sub>Mn<sub>0.72</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (MgMn<sub>6</sub>) were prepared by a typical solid‐state method. The composition of LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> was confirmed by inductively coupled plasma optical emission spectrometry (ICP‐OES) as shown in Table S1, and the result is consistent with the designed component. The morphologies were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and all show flakelike particles (Figure S1 and S2). The high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images of LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> were obtained and displayed in Figure S1. The crystal structures of the three samples were examined by XRD, and all of them exhibit the P2 phase characterization (Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>a and S2). The crystal structure of LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>, shown in Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>a, is refined by using the GSAS+EXPGUI suite software, and the detailed lattice parameters are listed in Table S2.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0023"><sup>[12]</sup></link> A superstructure peak is visible, signifying the honeycomb ordering of 6 Mn ions centered with Li<sup>+</sup>/Mg<sup>2+</sup> exists in the TM layers, in accordance with the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) image shown in the Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>a inset. The big bright spots are indexed to the typical reflections of P2‐layered structure. The bright SAED spots are surrounded by an ordered array of dark ones which are assigned to the superlattice, indicating the presence of superstructures in the pristine materials. The structure model is shown in Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>d, e, where the Li‐6Mn, Mg‐6Mn superlattice units arrange alternatively. DFT calculations were performed to determine the appropriate superlattice structure through the comparison of the various structure formation energies (Figure S3, S4), and the configuration described here is substantially lower in energy.</p><figure xml:id="anie202206625-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202206625:anie202206625-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202206625-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) XRD pattern and Rietveld refinement of LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>. Note that the superstructure peak at ≈22° indicating honeycomb ordering. Structure model of LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> d) with c axis upwards and e) with c axis perpendicular outward to the plane. f) O oxidation onset potential and g) the partial density of states (PDOS) of O 2p‐orbital for LiMn<sub>6</sub>, LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> and MgMn<sub>6</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>The electrochemical properties were evaluated by galvanostatic tests in Na half cells. The voltage profile of the three electrodes within the voltage range of 1.5–4.5 V and at a current density of 0.1 C are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>b for the first charging process. The curve configuration of all three cathodes displays first a steep slope and then a long plateau. According to the literature reports, the plateau at ≈4.2 V corresponds to the redox of O.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0013 #anie202206625-bib-0019"><sup>[4b, 8]</sup></link> It is worth noting that a turning point between the slope and the plateau is observed in the curves, which could be defined as the O oxidation onset potential. The O redox in LiMn<sub>6</sub> gets involved earliest, due to the stronger O redox activation by Li<sup>+</sup>, as we expected. The O redox in MgMn<sub>6</sub> gets involved latest, and the O redox in LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> is located in the middle. Corresponding d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> curves (Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>c) are more pronounced, and the slope of all the three curves increased when d<i>Q</i>/d<i>V</i> was approximately 0.1. Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>f displays the O oxidation onset potential for the three cathodes. Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0001">1</link>g and the partially enlarged inset present the partial density of states (PDOS) of O 2p for the three materials calculated by density functional theory (DFT). The O 2p‐electron population distribution nearby the Fermi level (0 eV) determines the difficulty of charge compensation that O provided, illustrating the sequence of O activity is LiMn<sub>6</sub>, LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> and MgMn<sub>6</sub>. The theoretical results are in close alignment with the experimental data.</p><p>The XPS experiment was carried out to detect the oxygen species of the three samples at a charge state of 4.5 V after etching 20 nm (Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0002">2</link>a). The peak at 530.5 eV is assigned to the oxidized lattice oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup> (0&lt;<i>n</i>&lt;4).<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0016 #anie202206625-bib-0024"><sup>[6a, 13]</sup></link> The (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup> proportion of the LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> is dominant, in sharp contrast with the other two samples (Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0002">2</link>b), suggesting the excellent redox reversibility of lattice oxygen in LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>. The oxidized oxygen is still confined in the lattice and no O<sub>2</sub> is generated in the first charge process of LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> (Figure S6) via the characterization of in situ differential electrochemical mass spectroscopy (DEMS), which is consistent with the previous reports.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0017 #anie202206625-bib-0019 #anie202206625-bib-0027"><sup>[6b, 8, 14]</sup></link> Hence, for the subsequent discharge process (Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0002">2</link>c), the LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> cathode manifests an impressive specific discharge capacity of 285.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, owing to its outstanding structural reversibility. Due to the formation of O<sub>2</sub> and its subsequent escape from the cathode, LiMn<sub>6</sub> has the lowest capacity among the three samples.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0015 #anie202206625-bib-0028"><sup>[6, 15]</sup></link> Moreover, we monitored the variation of lattice parameters and the phase transformation of the LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> cathode by in situ XRD. The in situ XRD patterns collected during the initial two cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0002">2</link>d) show no phase transformation, despite the shifting of the peak position, which further verifies the reversible structure. Therefore, the superstructure transformation in the charged and discharged states of LiMn<sub>6</sub> and LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> cathodes (non‐magnetic Mg<sup>2+</sup> shows no nuclear magnetic signal) could be rationally deduced through this technology. Experimental <sup>7</sup>Li NMR spectra collected at different states of LiMn<sub>6</sub> and LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0004">4</link>. The pristine LiMn<sub>6</sub> and LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> powder samples present two distinct groups of peaks. The separated peaks at around 2000 and 1500 ppm (Li<sub>TM1</sub> and Li<sub>TM2</sub>) most probably originated from the difference in Li position, whereas both are located in the TM layer with a honeycomb‐like arrangement. On the basis of previous studies, we can assign the resonances at around 2000 ppm (Li<sub>TM1</sub>) to Li ions surrounded by 6 nearest Mn ions and that around 1500 ppm (Li<sub>TM2</sub>) to Li ions surrounded by less than 6 nearest Mn ions that are presumably due to the incomplete perfect lattice arrangement.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0032"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The Li‐ion sites situated in the TM layer are marked as a bluish violet shadow. The peaks centered around 750 ppm (Li<sub>AM1</sub> and Li<sub>AM2</sub> in the cycled states) are attributed to Li ions sitting in the Na layer,<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0016 #anie202206625-bib-0034 #anie202206625-bib-0035 #anie202206625-bib-0036 #anie202206625-bib-0037"><sup>[6a, 17b–d, 18]</sup></link> and are marked as a reseda shadow. The Li<sub>AM1</sub> peak in both LiMn<sub>6</sub> and LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> materials is nearly immobile in the Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation process, which is likely to exist as local defects or impurities through rational conjecture.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0036"><sup>[17d]</sup></link> At charged state, both materials display a new broad resonance (Li<sub>AM2</sub>) at the right side of the 750 ppm (Li<sub>AM1</sub>) peak at the expense of the Li<sub>TM1</sub> signal, which indicates the out‐of‐plane migration of Li<sup>+</sup> from the TM layer to the Na layer. Simultaneously, the migration results are manifested in the schematics in Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0004">4</link>. At discharged state, the Li<sup>+</sup> migrated to the Na layer (Li<sub>AM2</sub>) hardly return to TM layer in LiMn<sub>6</sub>, whereas the Li<sup>+</sup> in LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> is capable of returning to Li<sub>TM1</sub> sites in the TM layer, illustrating the reversible Li<sup>+</sup> migration in LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>. The Li‐ion distribution on the discharged LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> certifies the partial reversibility and preservation of the honeycomb structure in the TM layer. In addition, the peak at 0 ppm in the cycled samples can be assigned to diamagnetic Li‐containing impurities (such as Li<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> and LiF) formed on the surface due to the electrolyte decomposition.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0036"><sup>[17d]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202206625-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202206625:anie202206625-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202206625-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p><sup>7</sup>Li NMR spectra collected at different stages of the first cycle for a) LiMn<sub>6</sub> and c) LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub>. Layer stacking schematic at different states for b) LiMn<sub>6</sub> and d) LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> which displays chiefly reversible Li<sup>+</sup> migration.</p></caption></figure><p>It has been speculated that the Mg<sup>2+</sup> in the Mg‐6Mn honeycomb superstructure serve as pillars when Li<sup>+</sup> migrates to the Na layer during desodiation, and thus retains the honeycomb superstructure (even with the vacancies produced by Li<sup>+</sup> leaving) of Mg‐6Mn and □‐6Mn. In this case, the Li<sup>+</sup> migrated to the Na layer could move back to the pristine sites in the TM layer on discharging. The hypothesized schematic is shown in Figure <link href="#anie202206625-fig-0004">4</link>d. Nevertheless, for the LiMn<sub>6</sub> without Mg<sup>2+</sup>, the Li<sup>+</sup> removal from the TM layer to the Na layer triggers the clusters of vacancies left behind in the TM layer, which is related to the formation of unstable molecular O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie202206625-bib-0018"><sup>[7]</sup></link> The irreversible structural change in the TM layer goes against the Li<sup>+</sup> returning to the TM layer and the LiMn<sub>6</sub> material suffers from Li<sup>+</sup> loss on discharging. ICP experiments were performed to detect the metal ions dissolved in the electrolyte after a cycle, which verified the Li<sup>+</sup> dissolution in the LiMn<sub>6</sub> cathode. The results are shown in Table S4 and S5. Overall, the honeycomb ordering in LiMn<sub>6</sub> is not recovered after the electrochemical process, with the consequence that Li<sup>+</sup> escapes from the cathode. Oppositely, the honeycomb ordering in LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> is preserved, expressing the favourable reversibility and the consequent ultrahigh discharge capacity.</p><p>The LiMn<sub>6</sub>−MgMn<sub>6</sub> material with a dual honeycomb‐superlattice was designed and synthesized. The XAS and XPS results confirm the oxygen is activated by the Li‐6Mn superlattice, which is in agreement with the results of DFT calculations. Furthermore, NMR and in situ XRD experiments reveal the positive role of the Mg‐6Mn superlattice in stabilizing the crystal structure. The dual superlattice leads to a record first discharge capacity of 285.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in layered SIB cathodes and decent structure reversibility. It is likely that this method will be applicable to other ions, and provide a strategy to realize advanced battery materials.</p><section xml:id="anie202206625-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (No. 2021YFA1202300), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 22075132), the Natural Science Foundation of Jiangsu Province, China (No. BK20211556 and BE2021138), the Shenzhen Science and Technology Innovation Committee (Nos.
