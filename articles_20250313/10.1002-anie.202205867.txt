Their commercialization, however, still suffers from inferior stability, considerable [Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>] defects and interstitial water in the framework, which are related to the rapid crystal growth. Herein, a “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor is proposed to synthesize highly crystallized PBAs with decreased defects and water, which show both superior specific capacity and rate capability in SIBs. The air‐stability, all‐climate, and full‐cell properties of our PBA have also been evaluated, and it exhibits enhanced electrochemical performance and higher volume yield than its counterpart synthesized via the water‐based co‐precipitation method. Furthermore, their highly reversible sodium‐ion storage behavior has been measured and identified via multiple in situ techniques. This work could pave the way for the PBA‐based SIBs in grid‐scale energy‐storage systems.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A facile, cost‐efficient, and general programmable ball‐milling synthesis process without any additives was designed to prepare highly crystalline MnHCF‐S‐170. sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202205867-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The development and utilization of intermittent renewable energy have become urgent priorities in recent years due to the ever‐increasing concerns about the sustainability of fossil fuels.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0001 #anie202205867-bib-0002"><sup>[1, 2]</sup></link> For the efficient integration of intermittent renewable forms of energy into smart grids, rechargeable storage batteries have been regarded as one of the most effective technologies.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0003 #anie202205867-bib-0004"><sup>[3, 4]</sup></link> Compared to the well‐known lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) with limited and expensive resources, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are expected to be a better choice for grid‐scale energy storage, owing to the natural abundance of sodium and their low‐cost nature.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0005 #anie202205867-bib-0006"><sup>[5, 6]</sup></link> To date, the most promising cathode materials for commercial SIBs consist of four main kinds, including layered oxides,<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0007 #anie202205867-bib-0008 #anie202205867-bib-0009"><sup>[7–9]</sup></link> Prussian blue analogues (PBAs),<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0010 #anie202205867-bib-0014 #anie202205867-bib-0015"><sup>[10–12]</sup></link> polyanionic materials,<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0018 #anie202205867-bib-0019"><sup>[13, 14]</sup></link> and organic compounds.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0020 #anie202205867-bib-0021"><sup>[15, 16]</sup></link> PBAs, metal–organic frameworks with robust three‐dimensional open‐structures allowing reversible storage of Na ions, have been extensively investigated as cathode materials in the past decade. Unlike other cathodes that need high‐temperature calcination, the facile synthesis process for PBAs through water‐based co‐precipitation method at room temperature from abundant raw materials makes them promising candidates, which can be effectively mass produced and widely used as low‐cost cathode materials for SIBs in grid‐scale energy‐storage systems (GESS).<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0022 #anie202205867-bib-0023 #anie202205867-bib-0024"><sup>[17–19]</sup></link></p><p>The chemical formula of PBAs can be generally represented as <i>A<sub>x</sub>M<sub>1</sub></i>[<i>M<sub>2</sub></i>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>1−<i>y</i></sub>□<sub><i>y</i></sub>⋅<i>n</i> H<sub>2</sub>O, in which <i>A</i> represents alkali ions, <i>M</i> represents transition metals, and □ denotes vacancies occupied by interstitial water. Benefiting from low‐cost Fe/Mn resources and their electrochemical activity inside the PBA framework, Fe‐based and Mn‐based PBAs are two ideal candidates among the abundant PBA family, with high theoretical specific capacity up to ≈170 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Most PBAs, however, suffer from low capacity and short cycling lifespans due to their large content of vacancies and interstitial water molecules inside their crystal structure, which is caused by the rapid crystal growth.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0014 #anie202205867-bib-0025 #anie202205867-bib-0026"><sup>[11, 20, 21]</sup></link> To increase the crystallization of PBAs, several strategies have been designed. For instance, Guo and co‐workers developed a novel single‐iron source route to fabricate highly crystallized Fe‐based PBAs with impressive cycling stability and high specific capacity.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0027"><sup>[22]</sup></link> Yang's group and Huang's group reported that the presence of chelating agents (e.g., trisodium citrate) could slow down the crystal growth speed and form high‐quality PBAs with enhanced specific capacity and prolonged lifespan.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0028 #anie202205867-bib-0029"><sup>[23, 24]</sup></link> In addition, a lower precipitation temperature could also benefit the crystallization of PBAs.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0030"><sup>[25]</sup></link> Nevertheless, these strategies generally suffered from either the wasted water treatment issues caused by fatal side‐products or extra cost of the additives as well as low space utilization (6–7 kg per 100 L), which have hindered their practical large‐scale application. Therefore, exploration of facile synthesis strategies that could produce highly crystallized PBAs with low cost and high space utilization ratios is paramount importance.</p><p>Here, we report a “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor strategy that enables the fabrication of highly crystallized PBAs in a confined reaction zone and without the assistance of any additives. Our as‐prepared Mn‐based PBA (MnHCF‐S‐170) shows fast and stable electrochemical performance over the wide temperature range from −10 °C to 50 °C and enhanced air stability. Furthermore, full cells are demonstrated using MnHCF‐S‐170 as cathode and soft carbon as anode. In situ powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) and in situ Raman spectroscopy were further employed to reveal the high reversibility of the MnHCF‐S‐170 in details. Our results indicate that this “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor strategy is a promising route to achieve large‐scale production of PBAs with low cost and paves the way for their practical application in SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202205867-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Well‐crystallized Na<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub>MnFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> samples (MnHCF) were synthesized by creating a “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor that enables high‐yield production in a facile way (see Experiment section in the Supporting Information). The as‐prepared sample was heat‐treated at 170 °C to remove the absorbed water, with the product marked as MnHCF‐S‐170. A MnHCF sample synthesized via typical water‐based co‐precipitation was selected for comparison and denoted as MnHCF‐L.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0031 #anie202205867-bib-0032"><sup>[26, 27]</sup></link> Rietveld refinement was employed to explore the crystallographic information on the samples by GSAS software, and detailed illustrations of their crystal structures and refined PXRD patterns are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0001">1</link> and Supporting Information Tables S1 and S2. The results demonstrate that the MnHCF‐S‐170 has an unusual monoclinic structure, while the MnHCF‐L has a typical cubic structure (<i>Fm‐</i>3<i>m</i>, lattice parameters <i>a</i>=<i>b</i>=<i>c</i>=10.51899 Å). The structural difference may mainly be ascribed to their different defects and Na ion content inside the crystal frameworks as reported previously.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0033 #anie202205867-bib-0034"><sup>[28, 29]</sup></link> Supporting Information Table S1 presents the refinement results on MnHCF‐S‐170 (<i>R</i><sub>wp</sub>=3.047 %), which exhibits monoclinic phase with the space group <i>P21/n</i> and lattice parameters <i>a</i>=10.57750 Å, <i>b</i>=7.5284 Å, <i>c</i>=7.3378 Å, <i>α</i>=90.0000°, <i>β</i>=92.084°, <i>γ</i>=90.0000° and unit cell volume <i>V</i>=583.934668 Å<sup>3</sup>. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>a and Supporting Information Figure S1 show that the weight loss of the MnHCF‐S‐170 at 200 °C and 400 °C was 10.1 % and 13.0 %, respectively. Obviously, the weight loss of MnHCF‐L was higher than that of MnHCF‐S‐170 due to the large amounts of vacancies inside the crystal structure that were formed during the rapid co‐precipitation process. In particular, the weight loss before 400 °C can be ascribed to the extraction of the adsorbed water on the surface and the interstitial/coordinative water in the Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> vacancies, suggesting the intact nature of the crystals of MnHCF‐S‐170.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0031 #anie202205867-bib-0035 #anie202205867-bib-0036"><sup>[26, 30, 31]</sup></link> The chemical formulas of the two samples could be represented as Na<sub>1.66</sub>Mn[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>1.00</sub> for MnHCF‐S‐170 and Na<sub>1.19</sub>Mn[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>0.84</sub> for MnHCF‐L according to the result of inductively coupled plasma—optical emission spectroscopy (ICP‐OES). The significantly suppressed [Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>] vacancy further indicates the perfect crystallinity of the MnHCF‐S‐170. Remarkably, the yield and raw material utilization ratio were increased from 61.8 % to 83.5 % and 26.4 % to 33.2 %, respectively. More importantly, the water consumption of the as‐designed “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor was nearly 50 times higher than that of the typical co‐precipitation method in water (from 0.024 kg L<sup>−1</sup> to 1.188 kg L<sup>−1</sup>), indicating that this nanoreactor method could be applied for low‐cost and high‐efficiency synthesis of PBAs in large‐scale practical production (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0003">3</link>c). Furthermore, their air storage stability was investigated by carrying out PXRD on the fresh samples and the samples that were stored in air for 5 months. It can be seen that the highly crystallized MnHCF‐S‐170 shows no noticeable peak shift or intensity variation, while the peak intensity loss of the MnHCF‐L indicates obvious structural degradation (Supporting Information Figure S2). The Fourier transform infrared spectra (FTIR) of the two samples demonstrate typical feature of the PBAs, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>b and Supporting Information Figure S3a, in which the peaks at ≈3461 cm<sup>−1</sup> and 1617 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be ascribed to the O−H and H−O−H bending modes of the water molecules, while the peaks at 2060 cm<sup>−1</sup> and 586 cm<sup>−1</sup> are assigned to the −C≡N and Fe−CN bonds.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0037 #anie202205867-bib-0038"><sup>[32, 33]</sup></link> The porosity of the samples was probed via N<sub>2</sub> sorption measurements, and the surface areas were calculated to be 10.72 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for MnHCF‐L and 26.24 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for MnHCF‐S‐170 (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>c and Supporting Information Figure S3b).<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0039"><sup>[34]</sup></link> The XPS survey spectra confirmed the chemical compositions of all three samples (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>d, e and Supporting Information Figure S4). The survey spectra disclosed that Na, C, N, O, Fe, and Mn elements exist in MnHCF‐L, MnHCF‐S, and MnHCF‐S‐170 as shown in Figure S4a. The high‐resolution Fe 2p spectra in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0001">1</link>d and Figure S4b displayed binding energies of 708.1 eV for Fe<sup>II</sup> 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, 721.0 eV for Fe<sup>II</sup> 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, and 723.4 eV for Fe<sup>III</sup> 2p<sub>1/2</sub>. The smaller peak of Fe<sup>III</sup> 2p<sub>1/2</sub> in MnHCF‐S‐170 indicates the lower content of Fe<sup>III</sup> compared with MnHCF‐L. In Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>e and Figure S4c, the peaks at ≈641.5, 645.5 and ≈653.5 eV in the Mn 2p spectra indicate Mn<sup>II</sup> 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, Mn<sup>III</sup> 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, and Mn<sup>II</sup> 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively, suggesting that the Mn element exists in the single oxidation state of Mn<sup>3+</sup>.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0040"><sup>[35]</sup></link> The Raman spectra show three distinct peaks at 2081 cm<sup>−1</sup>, 2088 cm<sup>−1</sup>, and 2126 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which can be ascribed to the bonds of Fe<sup>2+</sup>‐CN‐Mn<sup>2+</sup> and Fe<sup>2+</sup>‐CN‐Mn<sup>3+</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>f).<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0041 #anie202205867-bib-0042"><sup>[36, 37]</sup></link> Scanning electron microscope images with the elemental mapping of the samples are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0002">2</link>g, h and Figures S5–S7, respectively. The as‐prepared MnHCF‐S‐170 exhibits a regular ellipsoidal morphology with a narrow particle size distribution (PSD) of 30 nm, while MnHCF‐L shows an uneven PSD ranging from 10 nm to 100 nm. All elements of MnHCF‐S‐170 are uniformly distributed in the particles, which further demonstrates its high purity and crystal integrity.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0043 the redox reactions of Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> and Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup>, respectively.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0045 #anie202205867-bib-0046"><sup>[40, 41]</sup></link> The discharge platform is merged into a single one by removing the interstitial H<sub>2</sub>O from the framework of MnHCF‐S (Supporting Information Figure S8), and similar phenomenon was previously observed and reported.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0005 #anie202205867-bib-0031"><sup>[5, 26]</sup></link> The MnHCF‐S‐170 and MnHCF‐L were subjected to galvanostatic cycling at a current density of 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> within the voltage window between 2.0 V to 4.2 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0003">3</link>b and Supporting Information Figure S9). MnHCF‐S‐170 delivered a discharge specific capacity of 164 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is close to its theoretical capacity. In contrast, the capacity of MnHCF‐L decreased to 135 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> due to the loss of the electrochemical redox‐active sites of [Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−/3−</sup> in the crystal structure. Investigation of the long‐term cycle stability was carried out at the current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after activation for 5 cycles (Figure 3d and Supporting Information Figure S10, S11 and S12). MnHCF‐S‐170 showed an initial specific capacity of 140 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and could maintain the capacity of up to 80 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles, with enhanced cycling stability compared with MnHCF‐L (Supporting Information Figures S10). The rate performances of the two samples are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0003">3</link>e–g and Supporting Information Figure S13, where the monoclinic MnHCF‐S‐170 shows better capacity retention at current densities ranging from 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> to 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> than that of MnHCF‐L. The MnHCF‐S‐170 could deliver 159, 146, 137, 131, 123, and 110 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10, 20, 50, 100, 200, and 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The MnHCF‐S‐170 could even exhibit a high specific capacity of 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, which means that this electrode material could be fully charged/discharged within 6 mins and maintain 62.9 % of its capacity at the low current density of 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. It can be seen in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0003">3</link>g that the defect‐suppressed MnHCF‐S‐170 shows the high capacity retention at each current density. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was carried out to study the charge transfer and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion process (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0003">3</link>h). The charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) of MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode (113.1 Ω) is much lower than that of MnHCF‐L (166.4 Ω). The decreased charge transfer resistance could be ascribed to the enhanced conductivity of the vacancy‐suppressed crystal structure of the PBA.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0027 #anie202205867-bib-0046"><sup>[22, 41]</sup></link> The galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was employed to analyze the kinetic properties of Na<sup>+</sup> in the two samples, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0003">3</link>i. As expected, the MnHCF‐S‐170 shows a higher sodium diffusion coefficient (<i>D</i><sub>Na</sub><sup>+</sup>) than that of MnHCF‐L because of its more stable crystal structure.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0022"><sup>[17]</sup></link></p><p>The CV curves at different scanning rates of the as‐prepared samples are shown in Supporting Information Figures S14, S15, which demonstrates that the Na storage behavior is mainly contributed by the diffusion process at low scanning rates, according to the formula <i>i</i>=<i>a</i> <i>v</i><sup><i>b</i></sup>, where <i>i</i> is a peak current, <i>v</i> is the scan rate, and <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are parameters (here, <i>b</i>=0.75). The Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior is gradually transformed to be dominated by the capacitive process at higher scan rates.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0047 #anie202205867-bib-0048"><sup>[42, 43]</sup></link> The results demonstrated that the Na<sup>+</sup> could migrate rapidly in the MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode during the charge and discharge processes, resulting in excellent rate performance. The comparisons between as‐prepared MnHCF‐S‐170 and the state‐of‐the‐art reported results in SIBs have been shown in summarized Table S3. According to the comparison, the MnHCF‐S‐170 synthesized via the ball‐milling shows higher specific capacity, relatively stable cyclability and less water consumption.</p><p>To reveal the Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism of the well‐crystallized MnHCF‐S‐170 cathode materials, in situ XRD and in situ Raman were employed and shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0004">4</link>. The (C≡N)<sup>−</sup> was sensitive to its surrounding chemical environment; therefore, in situ Raman spectra was applied to disclose the structural evolution of the electrode during the charging/discharging process (Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0004">4</link>a–c). At the fully charged state (charged to 4.2 V), the Raman spectra presented two strong peaks located at ≈2070, 2110 cm<sup>−1</sup> and three weak peaks at ≈2050, 2078, and 2090 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which can be assigned to the Fe<sup>III</sup>‐C≡N‐Mn<sup>III</sup> groups. During the following discharging process, the intensities of all peaks were gradually decreased, and the weak peaks at ≈2050, 2078, 2090 cm<sup>−1</sup> were gradually disappeared, demonstrating that the Fe<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> were reduced to Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>2+</sup> during the insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> into the lattice.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0049 #anie202205867-bib-0050 #anie202205867-bib-0051"><sup>[44–46]</sup></link> Interestingly, in the subsequent charging process from 2.0 to 4.2 V, the intensities of the peaks finally were almost recovered to their original states, and the weak peak groups also appeared, indicating that almost all Fe<sup>II</sup>‐C≡N‐Mn<sup>II</sup> groups were oxidized to Fe<sup>III</sup>‐C≡N‐Mn<sup>III</sup> groups. The slight difference of peak intensities during an entire charging/discharging process may be attributed to the irreversible (de)intercalation of partial sodium ions in the framework. Moreover, no obvious peak shift in Raman spectra further indicates that the structural change of MnHCF‐S‐170 is highly reversible during Na‐ion (de)intercalation.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0051"><sup>[46]</sup></link> Additionally, Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0004">4</link>d–f shows the in situ PXRD spectra of MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode during the first two cycles at the current density of 30 mA g<sup>−1</sup> between 2.0–4.2 V. During the charging process, with the Na<sup>+</sup> extraction from MnHCF‐S‐170, the XRD spectrum shows a merging process of doublets into single peaks at ≈25°, indicating a phase transformation from a monoclinic phase to a cubic.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0052 #anie202205867-bib-0053"><sup>[47, 48]</sup></link> In the initial stage of charging, no obvious shift is observed for the diffraction peak of the monoclinic phase. When the MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode is charged to 3.55 V, a new peak occurs at 16.8°, which can be attributed to the cubic phase with a space group of <i>Fm‐</i>3<i>m</i>, i.e., obtain a two‐phase mixed state. With additional charging, the intensity of the peak at 16.8° is increased, while the peak intensities of the monoclinic phase is continually decreased and finally disappeared when charged to 4.05 V, which means a complete two‐phase transition from monoclinic to cubic phase is at ≈4.05 V during the charging process.. No new peak appears when continually charged to 4.2 V, indicating that the subsequent insertion/extraction of sodium ions leads to no noticeable structure evolution for MnHCF‐S‐170, and the cubic phase has been maintained. On the discharging process, the structure of MnHCF‐S‐170 is reversibly transformed from cubic to monoclinic phase via a two‐phase mixture, which means the reversible extraction/insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions from/into MnHCF‐S‐170 through the spacious diffusion tunnels. The irreversible lattice shrink and expansion process may be attributed to the side reactions between the electrolyte and MnHCF‐S‐170, in which the interstitial water reacted with Na<sup>+</sup> and formed the [NaH<sub>2</sub>O]<sup>+</sup>. The monoclinic MnHCF‐S‐170 undergoes a highly reversible phase evolution process during the whole cycle (monoclinic↔cubic), which helps to explain the enhanced cycling stability of the MnHCF‐S‐170 and highlights the effectiveness of the proposed “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor.</p><figure xml:id="anie202205867-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202205867:anie202205867-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202205867-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Sodium storage mechanism. a) In situ Raman 3D colormap surface with projection, b) 2D contour plot, and c) line spectra (left) and corresponding galvanostatic charge/charge voltage profile at a current density of 30 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (right). d) In situ PXRD 3D colormap surface with projection, e) 2D contour plot, and f) line spectra (left) and galvanostatic charge/discharge voltage profile (right) at a current density of 30 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (right) of MnHCF‐S‐170.</p></caption></figure><p>Besides the air stability as discussed above, we further tested the all‐climate performance of MnHCF‐S‐170 to provide more practical details for their real applications. The air‐stability behavior could make the materials easier to be stored and transported, while the all‐climate suitability might pave the way for practical application in extreme environments.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0054"><sup>[49]</sup></link> Here, the all‐climate performance was investigated at both the low temperature of −10 °C and the high temperature of 50 °C, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0005">5</link>. The MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode delivered a highly reversible specific capacity of 132 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the first five cycles at −10 °C. The specific capacity was increased up to 153 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when the ambient temperature was increased to 50 °C. The rate performance measurements were carried out at current densities ranging from 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> to 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. In the cold environment of −10 °C, the MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode could retain capacities of 127, 123, 119, 112, and 96 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current density of 20, 50, 100, 200, and 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. This material even shows the reversible discharge capacity of 71 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the large current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a capacity retention of 53.8 %. It can be seen that the MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode shows much better rate performance in the hot environment, which may be due to the higher Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion ratio in the structural framework at high temperature.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0055 #anie202205867-bib-0056"><sup>[50, 51]</sup></link> The MnHCF‐S‐170 electrode delivered 141, 129, 122, 118, and 111 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current densities of 20, 50, 100, 200, and 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. More importantly, the MnHCF‐S‐170 exhibited a high‐capacity retention of 68.7 % at the high current density of 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 °C. The long‐term cycling performance was tested at −10 °C and 50 °C at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The MnHCF‐S‐170 showed the discharge capacity of 98 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles at −10 °C and 118 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 50 °C, respectively. The all‐climate performance of MnHCF‐S‐170 is better than those of MnHCF‐L and MnHCF‐S, which may mainly benefit from its intact crystal structure and suppress interstitial water content (Supporting Information Figures S16 and S17). The results indicate that neither low nor high temperature has any further influence on the crystal structure of the MnHCF‐S‐170 except for the kinetics factors.<link href="#anie202205867-bib-0057 #anie202205867-bib-0058 #anie202205867-bib-0059"><sup>[52–54]</sup></link> The reasons for the better all‐climate performance of the MnHCF‐S‐170 compare to those of other two samples may be as following: Compared to other two samples, the highly crystallized MnHCF‐S‐170 with unusual monoclinic phase possesses higher sodium‐ion contents, which means that the MnHCF‐S‐170 can provide sufficient Na ions during the charge and discharge process, and further achieve high specific capacity. The fewer Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> vacancies in the crystal structure of MnHCF‐S‐170 are beneficial to enhance the integrity of skeleton, obtain robust crystal structure, thereby proving smooth channels for Na‐ion transportation, and thus achieving fast Na<sup>+</sup> ions migration kinetics. In addition, the tiny amount of interstitial water in MnHCF‐S‐170 will be conducive to decrease the side reactions between the electrolyte and electrodes, resulting in superior electrochemical properties. Moreover, the MnHCF‐S‐170 with low resistance exhibits rapid electron transfer, which contributes its outstanding rate performances.</p><figure xml:id="anie202205867-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202205867:anie202205867-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202205867-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>All‐climate electrochemical performance of MnHCF‐S‐170. a), b) Charge/discharge curves at −10 °C and 50 °C at the current density of 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after activation. c)–e) Rate performances at −10 °C and 50 °C at current densities from 10–1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. f) Long‐term cycling performances at −10 °C and 50 °C at the current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>Furthermore, full cells based on MnHCF‐S‐170 as cathode material and soft carbon as anode material were evaluated. Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0006">6</link>a shows an illustration of the structure of the full cell, and the preparation details can be found in the experimental section. The voltage window was set at 1.7–3.8 V and the capacity ratio between the negative and positive electrodes (N/P ratio) was 1.2 to rationally make full use of both the cathode and anode, as displayed in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0006">6</link>b. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0006">6</link>c, the full cell achieved a specific capacity of 133 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (based on the mass of the cathode material) at the current density of 15 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. In Figure <link href="#anie202205867-fig-0006">6</link>d, the full cell achieved capacity retention of ≈81.5 % at 15 mA g<sup>−1</sup> after 70 cycles. Nevertheless, we will still be putting much effort into the fabrication of better practical full cells by optimizing the battery assembly technologies, N/P ratio, electrolytes, binder systems, etc. in our future work.</p><figure xml:id="anie202205867-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202205867:anie202205867-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202205867-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Pouch full‐cell electrochemical performance based on MnHCF‐S‐170 cathode and soft carbon anode. a) Schematic illustration of the full cell. b) Charge/discharge curves of the MnHCF‐S‐170 and soft carbon electrodes for determining the voltage range for full cells. c) Charge/discharge curves of the full cell for the first 5 cycles, and d) cycling performance of the full‐cell (inset: the lighting of a white light‐emitting diode (LED) panel driven by the full cell).</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202205867-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we have successfully designed a “water‐in‐salt” nanoreactor to prepare highly crystallized PBAs without any additives. The MnHCF‐S‐170 exhibited a high specific of 164 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and impressive all‐climate performance ranging from −10 °C to 50 °C for Na ion storage. Both in situ PXRD and in situ Raman spectroscopy results showed its highly reversible structural evolution process, and the GITT study revealed its impressive Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient. The MnHCF‐S‐170 and soft carbon based full‐cells were fabricated as a demonstration with a view towards their practical applications. The results suggest that the PBAs prepared via this low‐cost method with high space utilization are strong competitors among the various cathode materials for SIBs and should be received more comprehensive investigation towards their practical application in GESS.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202205867-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 51971124, 52102285, 52101265 and 52171217).
