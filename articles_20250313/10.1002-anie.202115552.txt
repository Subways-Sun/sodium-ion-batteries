Herein, we for the first time simultaneously tune bulk O<sub>2</sub> and nonbonding oxygen state for reversible oxygen redox chemistry in P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Fe<sub>0.5</sub>O<sub>2</sub> through a synergy of Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> coating and Li/Ti co‐doping. O<sup>2−</sup> is oxidized to molecular O<sub>2</sub> and peroxide (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup> (<i>n</i>&lt;2) during charging. Molecular O<sub>2</sub> derived from transition metal (TM) migration is related to the superstructure ordering induced by Li doping. The synergy mechanism of Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> coating and Li/Ti co‐doping on the two O‐redox modes is revealed. Firstly, Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> coating restrains the surface O<sub>2</sub> and inhibits O<sub>2</sub> loss. Secondly, nonbonding Li−O−Na enhances the reversibility of O<sup>2−</sup>→(O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup>. Thirdly, Ti doping strengthens the TM−O bond which fixes lattice oxygen. The cationic redox reversibility is also enhanced by Li/Ti co‐doping. numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted great interest due to the low cost and promising applications in large‐scale energy storage.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> A layered transition metal oxide cathode material is an important material for sodium‐ion batteries.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0002"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Oxygen redox in the layered cathode materials has been studied and proven to provide additional capacity during the charge and discharge process.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0005"><sup>[3]</sup></link> However, the oxygen redox process in SIBs can adversely affect the structure stability and reversible capacity of the cathode material.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0009"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Therefore, enhancing O‐redox activity and reversibility is still a major challenge.</p><p>The oxygen‐redox process has been extensively studied in Li‐rich cathode materials since 2000.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0012"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Oxygen has been reported to provide additional capacity due to the nonbonding Li 2s‐O 2p configuration.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0016"><sup>[6]</sup></link> A series of layered cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries with oxygen redox conditions, such as P2‐Na<sub>5/6</sub>[Li<sub>1/4</sub>Mn<sub>3/4</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> and P2‐Na<sub>0.6</sub>[Li<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, were discovered.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0017"><sup>[7]</sup></link> A similar Li−O−Na configuration is also found in the materials with Li doping in transition metal (TM) layers.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Zhou et al. reported the stable Li−O−Na configuration in the layered P2‐type Na<sub>0.66</sub>Li<sub>0.22</sub>Ru<sub>0.78</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode and achieved reversible anionic redox reactions.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0021"><sup>[9]</sup></link> The nonbonding Li−O−Na configuration has a band above the TM−O orbital bonding level.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0022"><sup>[10]</sup></link> The TM−O orbital in the bonding state is harmful to the structural stability of the cathode material in the charge compensation process. However, nonbonding oxygen ions in the Li−O−Na configuration can maintain the structure stability of the cathode material during the charging/discharging process, thus improving the reversibility of the oxygen‐redox chemistry in the cathode materials.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0025"><sup>[11]</sup></link></p><p>Recently, Bruce et al. discovered a new mechanism of the oxygen redox reaction in sodium‐ion and lithium‐ion batteries.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0030"><sup>[12]</sup></link> Superstructure ordering is found in the TM layer when Li/Mg ions enter the TM layer.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0027"><sup>[11b]</sup></link> The oxygen ions coordinated with one TM ion (O‐TM1) tend to bond with oxygen ions not coordinated with TM ions (O‐TM0), thus forming a Mn‐η<sup>1</sup>‐O<sub>2</sub> moiety.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020"><sup>[8]</sup></link> In this case, the TM vacancies attached to O<sub>2</sub> species are linked together in a spatial arrangement due to the migration of transition metals, thus forming vacancy clusters. The O<sub>2</sub> species are molecular O<sub>2</sub> trapped in vacancy clusters in the material. Molecular O<sub>2</sub> is also present in a variety of cathode materials, such as P2‐Na<sub>0.75</sub>Li<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2,</sub><link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020"><sup>[8]</sup></link> P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.72</sub>Mg<sub>0.28</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0034"><sup>[12d]</sup></link> Li<sub>1.2</sub>Ni<sub>0.13</sub>Co<sub>0.13</sub>Mn<sub>0.54</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0032"><sup>[12b]</sup></link> and Li<sub>2</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>F.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0033"><sup>[12c]</sup></link> Molecular O<sub>2</sub> can be generated in bulk or on the surface of the cathode material. Bulk O<sub>2</sub> is stably trapped in the vacancy cluster in the deep bulk of the material, while surface O<sub>2</sub> is easily lost in charge and discharge processes. It is well known that surface modification can inhibit the production of molecular O<sub>2</sub> on the material surface and further reduce gaseous O<sub>2</sub> release and oxygen loss, which improves the stability of the oxygen redox process.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0031"><sup>[12a]</sup></link> However, the molecular O<sub>2</sub> mechanism has not been clearly discussed in various sodium‐ion layered cathode materials, and oxygen release inhibition by the mechanism is not fully understood.</p><p>Herein, we simultaneously tune bulk O<sub>2</sub> and the nonbonding oxygen state for reversible anionic redox chemistry in a P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Fe<sub>0.5</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (MF) cathode material for the first time by Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> coating and Li/Ti co‐doping. The modified sample is abbreviated as LTO@MF. The O‐redox process and the modulation mechanism are revealed by a combination of some advanced analytical techniques (neutron diffraction, Resonant inelastic X‐ray scattering, high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy, O K‐edge soft X‐ray absorption spectroscopy and so on) and calculations. Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> coating restrains the surface O<sub>2</sub> and inhibits O<sub>2</sub> loss, and nonbonding Li−O−Na enhances the reversibility of O<sup>2−</sup>→(O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup>. Ti doping also strengthens the TM−O bond thus fixing lattice oxygen. This study offers some new insights into tuning the reversibility of oxygen anion redox chemistry.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202115552-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and discussion</title><section xml:id="anie202115552-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Role of Coating and Co‐Doping Synergy</title><p>XRD refinement results and neutron powder diffraction (NPD) refinement results of MF and LTO@MF samples (Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0001">1</link>a–d) present the <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i> space group (No 194).<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0035"><sup>[13]</sup></link> NPD results demonstrate that Li is doped into Nae and TM sites, and Ti is doped into TM sites in the LTO@MF sample, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0001">1</link>d and Table S5. From the XRD results in Figure S1a, compared with the MF sample, the (002) peak shift of TiO<sub>2</sub>@MF, NTO@MF, LTO@MF toward a lower angle (Figure S1b) represents an increase in lattice parameter <i>c</i> (Table S3, S6), which indicates the expansion of the (002) slab.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0036"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Ti may tend to enter the TM octahedral sites and bond with O. The Ti−O bonding energy (662 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) is larger than that of Mn−O (402 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) and Fe−O (409 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>), resulting in the shrinkage of the O−O and TM−O bond lengths and TMO<sub>2</sub> thickness, which benefits the stability of O.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0040"><sup>[15]</sup></link> The increase in Na−O and d indicates that the Na diffusion channel is enlarged in the LTO@MF sample, which enhances the rate capacity. <sup>7</sup>Li MAS ss NMR is sensitive to Li ions in different environments and is hence applied to determine the specific Li‐doping sites in LTO@MF samples.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0037 #anie202115552-bib-0041"><sup>[14a, 16]</sup></link> As presented in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0001">1</link>f, the <sup>7</sup>Li resonances presented in the green and purple regions are assigned to Li in TM and Na sites, respectively.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0037 #anie202115552-bib-0038 #anie202115552-bib-0045"><sup>[14a,b, 17]</sup></link> It can be confirmed that Li ions enter the TM sites, and there may be a small amount of Li occupying the Na position. For Li in TM sites, the low orbital overlapping of Li 2s and O 2p can form the Li−O−Na configuration with a higher Fermi level than other TM−O bonding levels, leading to higher anionic redox capacity and reversibility.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0016 #anie202115552-bib-0024 #anie202115552-bib-0048"><sup>[6, 10b, 18]</sup></link> The above discussion of the crystal structure is shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0001">1</link>e.</p><figure xml:id="anie202115552-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202115552:anie202115552-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202115552-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structure and morphology of MF and LTO@MF materials. HR‐TEM and SAED images of g) MF and h) LTO@MF.</p></caption></figure><p>The morphology of the MF and LTO@MF samples was characterized by HR‐TEM (Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0001">1</link>g and h), SEM (Figure S2d and e), EDS mapping (Figure S2b and c) and STEM (Figure S2a). The cationic and anionic element valence states of the MF and LTO@MF samples were characterized by XPS as shown in Figure S3. The lattice and surface O peaks were fitted as shown in Figure S3b.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0049"><sup>[19]</sup></link> From Table S8, the improvement of the lattice O ratio in LTO@MF was caused by the enhancement of the TM−O bonding energy due to Ti doping, which is beneficial for O‐redox reversibility and structural stability.</p><p>The rate performance of the bare MF and the samples coated with TiO<sub>2</sub>, Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> (LTO) and Na<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> samples in a voltage range of 1.5–4.2 V is shown in Figure S4a. Among all samples, the LTO@MF sample presents the best rate performance, revealing that the Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> (LTO) coating strategy can improve the rate performance more effectively. The improved rate performance of LTO@MF is due to the expansion of the Na layer, which is presented in Table S3. As shown in Figure S5, GITT results show that the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients of the LTO@MF sample are larger than those of the MF sample, demonstrating the faster sodium diffusion kinetics in the LTO@MF sample due to the expanded Na layer, leading to the improvement of the rate performance in the LTO@MF sample.</p><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0002">2</link>, the cycling performance is presented for MF and LTO@MF samples. The charge/discharge curves for the MF and LTO@MF samples of 1.5–4.3 V are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0002">2</link>a and b, respectively. In the 50<sup>th</sup> cycle, the voltage of the MF sample drops below 2.5 V at the beginning of the discharge, while it only drops to 3.5 V in the LTO@MF sample, indicating voltage decay is reduced in the LTO@MF sample, which is caused by the reversible O‐reduction process. Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0002">2</link>c shows the cycling results of both samples at 0.1 C in the voltage range of 1.5–4.2 V and 1.5–4.3 V. When the voltage increases from 4.2 V to 4.3 V, the first cycle capacity of the LTO@MF material rises from 182.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> to 193.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, while those of the MF sample are 142.8 and 140.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The increasing capacity of the LTO@MF sample is caused by the excitation of more O‐redox at higher voltage. The additional capacity for the LTO@MF material is due to the high‐energy Li 2s‐O 2p configuration,<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0016 #anie202115552-bib-0022"><sup>[6, 10]</sup></link> which is discussed in the following sections. The capacity retentions of the LTO@MF sample (81.3 % of 1.5–4.2 V and 63.3 % of 1.5–4.3 V) are increased compared with those of the MF sample (59.9 % of 1.5–4.2 V and 32.1 % of 1.5–4.3 V). In addition, the cyclic performance at 1 C and 0.5 C is also enhanced in the LTO@MF sample as shown in Figure S4b and c. Therefore, the synergistic modification strategy improves the electrochemical performance of the material.</p><figure xml:id="anie202115552-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202115552:anie202115552-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202115552-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Capacity–voltage curves from 1.5–4.3 V at 0.1 C of a) MF and b) LTO@MF for different cycle numbers. c) Cycling performance for the MF and LTO@MF samples performed in two voltage ranges of 1.5–4.3 V and 1.5–4.2 V.</p></caption></figure><p>Ex situ XPS Mn 2p results are presented in Figure S6. The change in Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> under various charge and discharge voltages is analyzed in Table S9. The relative content of Mn<sup>3+</sup> for the LTO@MF sample is lower than that of the MF sample, which proves the inhibition of Mn<sup>3+</sup> content and the harmful Jahn–Teller effect. For the LTO@MF sample, the reversible cationic redox process is proved by the change of the Mn valence states during charge and discharge. Ex situ<i>/</i>in situ XRD results show the change ratios of lattice parameters at different voltages as illustrated in Table S12 and Figure S7. In addition, the variation of the lattice parameters and HR‐TEM results for both samples before and after 100 cycles is presented in Table S11 and Figure S8, further confirming the enhancement of structural stability in the LTO@MF sample.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202115552-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Enhancement of O‐Redox Reversibility</title><p>The regulation of the O‐redox process plays a key role in improving the electrochemical performance in the LTO@MF material. Therefore, a series of experiments were applied to explore the O‐redox process in both cathodes. Ex situ XPS O 1s results were applied to reveal the relative content of different O species during the charge/discharge process, as shown in Figure S6c–f and Table S10.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0039 #anie202115552-bib-0049"><sup>[14c, 19]</sup></link> The existence of O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> reveals that the O ions are oxidized and contribute a capacity during charging.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0050"><sup>[20]</sup></link></p><p>DEMS can detect very small amounts of gaseous O<sub>2</sub> produced by the material during the charge/discharge process.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0027 #anie202115552-bib-0053"><sup>[11b, 21]</sup></link> The DEMS test is conducted from 1.5 V to 4.3 V at 0.5 C during the first cycle. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>a, O release is observed in the MF sample in the voltage range of 4.17–4.30 V, which is consistent with O being oxidized at high voltage.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0021"><sup>[9]</sup></link> The O release in the MF sample proves the irreversible O oxidation in the MF sample. However, no O release is observed in the LTO@MF sample as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>b. It can be confirmed that the irreversible O<sub>2</sub> release was prevented at high voltage.</p><figure xml:id="anie202115552-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202115552:anie202115552-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202115552-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>DEMS results and corresponding charge curves of a) MF sample and b) LTO@MF sample. 1.5–4.2 V CV curves: c) MF sample and d) LTO@MF sample. Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>c and d present the CV result of 1.5–4.2 V for MF and LTO@MF samples, respectively. The Mn oxidation peak (2.5836 V) and the Fe reduction peak (3.2989 V) of LTO@MF overlaps well after cycling, which reveals the improved cyclic stability. In addition, polarization was lowered and the cationic redox reversibility was enhanced in the LTO@MF sample. The oxidation peak above 4.0 V corresponds to the O‐oxidation process.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0056"><sup>[22]</sup></link> For the MF sample, the reduction in peak intensity involving O oxidation after 5 cycles is greater than that of the LTO@MF sample. The O‐oxidation peak of the 5<sup>th</sup> cycle for the MF sample is almost invisible, while it was clearly identified for the LTO@MF sample revealing the enhancement of the O‐redox reversibility in the LTO@MF sample. The O loss of MF at high voltage leads to a decrease in O ions, which results in a rapid O‐redox reduction in the following cycles.</p><p>An EPR test was performed to characterize the electronic states of O ions under perpendicular mode.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0057"><sup>[23]</sup></link> Figure S10 shows that the broad centrosymmetric peak shape corresponds to the coupling of tetravalent manganese and O ions.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0060"><sup>[24]</sup></link> After charging to high voltage, the Mn−O bonding becomes weak due to O‐oxidation. Therefore, the former broad peak disappeared, and a new sharp line shape from 3480–3540 G assigned to high valence O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup> (<i>n</i>≤2) species with single unpaired electrons appeared.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0038 #anie202115552-bib-0061"><sup>[14b, 25]</sup></link> The O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup> signals are enlarged in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>e and f for both MF and LTO@MF samples, respectively. For MF and LTO@MF samples, the resonance intensity increases from C4.2 to C4.5 V and decreases from C4.5 V to D1.5 V, which reveals that the amount of O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup> species increases during the charging process and decreases during the discharging process. However, the peak of D1.5 maintains a high level near the peak of C4.2 in the MF sample, while the D1.5 peak presents lower intensity than the C4.2 peak in the LTO@MF sample. This means that O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup> in the LTO@MF material is reduced to O<sup>2−</sup>, but not in the MF sample, which proves the improvement of O‐redox reversibility in the LTO@MF sample. Figure S11a and b show the EPR results of fully charged MF and LTO@MF after various cycles. The higher O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup> intensity in the LTO@MF sample demonstrates a reversible O‐redox process without O loss.</p><p>Raman spectroscopy can detect the O−O stretch information in the bulk structure of the materials (≈1 μm).<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0021"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Ex situ Raman results are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>g and h for MF and LTO@MF materials, respectively. The O−O stretch at 850 cm<sup>−1</sup> and 1150 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be ascribed to peroxide‐related O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> and superoxide‐related O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup>, respectively.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0062"><sup>[26]</sup></link> The signal of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> species derived from the electrolyte decomposition is also observed. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>g, O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> and O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> stretching modes are observed in the MF sample at the 4.2 V state, which reveals that O is oxidized to high valence. The O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> and O<sup>2−</sup> species disappear at 4.5 V because most O<sub>2</sub><sup>n−</sup> species are oxidized to higher valence and lost as O<sub>2</sub> gas. When discharging to 1.5 V, the residual O species at high valence is reduced to O<sub>2</sub><sup><i>n</i>−</sup> species. The O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> and O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> species present after discharge to 1.5 V can be oxidized easily to O<sub>2</sub> gas, which indicates the irreversible O‐redox process in the MF material. Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>h presents ex situ Raman results of the LTO@MF sample. When charging to 4.5 V, the existence of O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> and O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> species reveals the O‐oxidation process. Then, the O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> and O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> species disappear at discharging the 1.5 V state, which illustrates a reversible O‐redox process of the LTO@MF sample. The Raman results of the MF and LTO@MF samples after 30 and 50 cycles are shown in Figure S11c. O<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> or O<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> stretches are not observed in the MF sample but are clearly identified in the LTO@MF sample, revealing that the O loss leads to irreversible O‐redox in the MF sample. A detailed analysis of O species in O‐redox is given in the next section.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202115552-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Molecular O<sub>2</sub> and Peroxide/Superoxide (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup></title><p>According to previous work, with superstructure ordering from Li/Mg in TM layers, the migration of Li and Mn ions during charging forms the molecular O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020 #anie202115552-bib-0030 #anie202115552-bib-0065"><sup>[8, 12, 27]</sup></link> Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>a shows the STEM image of the pristine LTO@MF sample at a surface depth of 30 nm viewed along the [001] direction. Since the detecting depth is greater than the thickness of the coating layer, it can be confirmed that the observed lattice belongs to the P2‐layered oxide. By using a focused ion beam (FIB), a single layer of TM ions is shown in the image. The bright white spots with red circles are TM ions. The black holes with yellow circles in the center of six TM ions represent the Li in TM sites. The regular distribution of Li in the TM layer proves the superstructure ordering in the LTO@MF material. Since Li is only distributed in a certain range of surface depths, the superstructure ordering is not observed by STEM above a 100 nm depth, as shown in Figure S2a. The limited content of superstructure ordering makes it not detected by XRD. In addition, as shown in Figure S13, no superstructure ordering is observed viewed along [100], since the dark spots (Li) are covered by the bright spots (TM) in the next layer. The atomic arrangement is different from that in the previous research.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0031"><sup>[12a]</sup></link> Its formation mechanism needs further study.</p><figure xml:id="anie202115552-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202115552:anie202115552-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202115552-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) HAADF‐STEM image collected at a surface depth of 30 nm in the pristine LTO@MF sample viewing along [001] perpendicular to <i>ab</i> plane. b) Schematic of molecular O<sub>2</sub> formation in a TM vacancy cluster. Red shading represents electron holes on molecular O<sub>2</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>From the superstructure ordering, a model of the TM layer with a Li arrangement for the LTO@MF sample is constructed in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>b. According to previous work,<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020 #anie202115552-bib-0032"><sup>[8, 12b]</sup></link> we infer a possible TM migration mode during charging, leading to molecular O<sub>2</sub> bonding to TM in the vacancy cluster, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>b. By DFT, we calculated a stable model of a vacancy cluster containing four TM vacancies and two molecular O<sub>2</sub> for MF (−332.37 meV/f.u.) and LTO@MF (−329.91 meV/f.u.) charging to 4.3 V in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>b. The calculated O−O bonds in the O<sub>2</sub> configurations for the MF and LTO@MF samples are 1.21 and 1.22 Å, respectively, which is consistent with the molecular O<sub>2</sub> bond length of 1.20 Å.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0033 #anie202115552-bib-0066"><sup>[12c, 28]</sup></link> Therefore, the calculation results predict the molecular O<sub>2</sub> in charged materials.</p><p>The ex situ O K‐edge RIXS results for MF and LTO@MF samples are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>c and d, respectively. An inelastic peak at approximately 7.5 eV represents the O‐oxidation in charged MF and LTO@MF samples.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0069"><sup>[29]</sup></link> When discharging to 1.5 V, the inelastic peak was still observed in the MF sample but not in the LTO@MF sample. The blue rectangles highlight the region of low‐energy elastic peaks associated with O−O vibration for C4.3 V and D1.5 V states of the MF samples, while only present in C4.3 V state of the LTO@MF material. From an enlarged picture of the elastic peaks in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>e, the obtained vibrational frequencies for MF‐C4.3 V, MF‐D1.5 V and LTO‐C4.3 V materials are 1693, 1482, and 1565 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The larger fundamental vibrational frequency of the MF sample (1693 cm<sup>−1</sup>) than that of the LTO@MF sample (1565 cm<sup>−1</sup>) at the 4.3 V state, proves the bond between the molecular O<sub>2</sub> and TM in the lattice is weaker for the MF sample.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020 #anie202115552-bib-0067"><sup>[8, 28a]</sup></link> In the MF‐C4.3 V sample, the molecular O<sub>2</sub> is closer to the gaseous oxygen state. From the ex situ Raman result of the MF sample in Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>g, when charging to 4.5 V, most peroxide and superoxide species are oxidized to higher valence species, and ultimately form gaseous O<sub>2</sub>, resulting in an irreversible O‐redox process. However, the RIXS result for the LTO@MF‐C4.3 V sample shows a strengthened interaction between molecular O<sub>2</sub> and the lattice TM, and plenty of O still remains in peroxide and superoxide species at the C4.5 V state (Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>h) without O<sub>2</sub> gas release. The RIXS inelastic peak at 7.5 eV for the MF‐D1.5 V sample also demonstrates that high valence O species have not been completely reduced. For the LTO@MF‐D1.5 V sample, no signal of molecular O<sub>2</sub> and peroxide/superoxide species is observed in the RIXS results (Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0004">4</link>d and e) and Raman (Figure <link href="#anie202115552-fig-0003">3</link>h), and the RIXS inelastic peak is not observed, which reveals that the molecular O<sub>2</sub> in LTO@MF has been reduced to initial O<sup>2−</sup>, demonstrating the reversible O‐redox process in the LTO@MF sample.</p><p>Molecular O<sub>2</sub> in the bulk material is stable and can be reversibly reduced to O<sup>2−</sup>, while on the material surface it is easily lost in the charge and discharge processes.<link href="#anie202115552-bib-0020 #anie202115552-bib-0031"><sup>[8, 12a]</sup></link> From DEMS and RIXS results, some of the surface O<sub>2</sub> in the MF sample turned into O<sub>2</sub> gas during the first cycle. The oxygen redox mechanism has been revealed. Oxygen ions are oxidized to molecular O<sub>2</sub> and peroxide/superoxide (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup> species. Molecular O<sub>2</sub> is generated due to the superstructure derived from Li substitution. During the charging process, Li migrates from the TM layer to the Na layer, and other TM ions undergo migration to form molecular O<sub>2</sub> trapped in vacancy clusters. According to the above two oxygen redox modes, we unveiled the following synergy mechanism of Li<sub>2</sub>TiO<sub>3</sub> coating and Li and Ti co‐doping. 1) The coating modification inhibited the formation of molecular O<sub>2</sub> on the surface, and gaseous oxygen loss was prevented in the modified sample. 2) Li substitution in transition metal sites will form a nonbonding Li−O−Na configuration, which improves the reversibility of the oxygen redox between oxygen ions and peroxide (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup> species. 3) Ti substitution also increases the bonding energy between transition metal ions and oxygen ions, which is beneficial to improving the specific capacity and anionic redox reversibility. The reversible oxygen redox chemistry reduces the voltage decay and increases the reversible capacity of the material. In addition, the regulation of the lattice structure and the reversible cationic chemistry are also clarified. The modified sample delivers a highly reversible specific capacity of 147.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with 63.3 % capacity retention in a voltage window of 1.5–4.3 V after 50 cycles. More broadly, this study detects the coexisting molecular O<sub>2</sub> and peroxide/superoxide (O<sub>2</sub>)<sup><i>n</i>−</sup> species generated from oxygen oxidation in P2‐layered cathodes and first regulates both oxygen redox systems simultaneously. Further research is required to explore the specific mechanisms of different oxygen oxidation species in other cathode materials, which is critical for the precise modulation of oxygen redox chemistry. In addition, this proposed synergetic strategy can also provide ideas to improve the cyclic stability and specific capacity of layered cathode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202115552-sec-0007" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 11975238, 1575192 and 11675267), the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Grant No. ZDKYYQ20170001, 211211KYSB20170060 and 211211KYSB20180020) and Natural Science Foundation of Beijing Municipality (Grant No. 2182082).
