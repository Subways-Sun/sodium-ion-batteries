However, it suffers from continued capacity fading. This problem could be overcome by hybridizing MoS<sub>2</sub> with nanostructured carbon‐based materials, but it is quite challenging. Herein, we demonstrate a single‐step strategy for the preparation of MoS<sub>2</sub> coupled with ordered mesoporous carbon nitride using a nanotemplating approach which involves the pyrolysis of phosphomolybdic acid hydrate (PMA), dithiooxamide (DTO) and 5‐amino‐1H‐tetrazole (5‐ATTZ) together in the porous channels of 3D mesoporous silica template. The sulfidation to MoS<sub>2</sub>, polymerization to carbon nitride (CN) and their hybridization occur simultaneously within a mesoporous silica template during a calcination process. The CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrid prepared by this unique approach is highly pure and exhibits good crystallinity as well as delivers excellent performance for SIBs with specific capacities of 605 and 431 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 100 and 1000 mAg<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>A binary carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub></b> was successfully synthesized by a single‐step nanotemplating approach. Sulfidation, polymerization and hybridization were carried out simultaneously, calcining three precursors of phosphomolybdic acid hydrate (PMA), dithiooxamide (DTO) and 5‐amino‐1H‐tetrazole (5‐ATTZ) in a single step in the nanochannels of nanoporous silica template at a high temperature. The resultant CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrid showed excellent capacities of 605 and 431 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 and 1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, for SIBs.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000349:asia202000349-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000349-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="asia202000349-sec-0001"><p>The rapid rise in the demand for clean energy due to the global warming caused by CO<sub>2</sub> emission incessantly spurs the research and the development of highly efficient and sustainable energy storage systems. Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) offer sustainable energy and have been widely used in many electronic devices and electric cars. However, limited resources of Li and its high cost put a major hurdle in meeting the huge demand of these devices all around the world. Therefore, the research on SIBs has been receiving a lot of momentum due to the abundant reserve of sodium in the world and its low cost and their similar electrochemical properties with that of LIBs technologies.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0001 #asia202000349-bib-0002"/> Although graphite has been successfully commercialized as an anode material for LIBs with outstanding performance, this is turned out to be unsuitable for the accommodation of Na owing to its 55% larger ionic radius and the higher magnitude of the oxidation/reduction potential.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0003"/> Therefore, researchers have explored various advanced nanostructured materials for favorable sodium intercalation to improve performances of SIBs.</p><p>The MoS<sub>2</sub> is one of the excellent candidates accommodating sodium ions via intercalation (0.4 V &lt; <i>E</i>≤2 V) and conversion reactions (<i>E</i>≤0.4 V).<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0003"/> The MoS<sub>2</sub> has an interlayer spacing of 6.3 Å that is larger than that of graphite (3.35 Å). This fact enables the MoS<sub>2</sub> to easily intercalate the large size of sodium ions. Furthermore, theoretical specific capacity of the MoS<sub>2</sub> registers a high value of 670 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> for SIBs.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0004 #asia202000349-bib-0005"/> Nevertheless, experimental capacity of the MoS<sub>2</sub> is not remarkable because the MoS<sub>2</sub> suffers from its moderate electronic conductivity and capacity fading attributed to a huge volume expansion during cycling.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0005"/> To overcome these drawbacks, carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> materials have been considered as anode materials for SIBs.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0006 #asia202000349-bib-0007 #asia202000349-bib-0008 #asia202000349-bib-0009"/> In the carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> materials, the carbon acts as an electronic conductive supporting material preventing agglomeration of MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0010 #asia202000349-bib-0011"/> The carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> materials have been synthesized via nano‐structuring, hydrothermal reaction or hybridization approaches.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0012 #asia202000349-bib-0013 #asia202000349-bib-0014"/> Recent studies reported that doping of heteroatoms such as B, N, O, P and S in carbon gives rise to electrode performance of the carbon in SIBs because of a favorable change in adsorption energy of sodium ions upon the heteroatom‐doping.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0015 #asia202000349-bib-0016 #asia202000349-bib-0017"/> In general, synthetic routes for the multicomponent materials are more complex and challenging when the number of component elements is increased.</p><p>To the best of our knowledge, there are only a few studies available on the facile synthesis of MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon composites with a high electrode performance for SIBs.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0018 #asia202000349-bib-0019"/> For example, a spraying pyrolysis approach has been proposed to synthesize and combine carbon and MoS<sub>2</sub> at the same time.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0018"/> However, the synthesis of binary carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> that is composites of MoS<sub>2</sub> and a heteroatom‐included carbon requires multistep or complex synthesis procedures as it involves the formation of two crystal structures in a single system. A nanotemplating method is generally used for the synthesis of highly ordered porous carbon materials. It is a calcination process of precursors impregnated in a silica template with 2D or 3D porous structure, which is followed by an etching process for the silica template.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0020 #asia202000349-bib-0021 #asia202000349-bib-0022"/> The resultant materials could gain advantages including large pore volume, high surface area and short transportation pathways through connected pores, which lead to enhancement in the kinetics of charge carriers.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0023 #asia202000349-bib-0024 #asia202000349-bib-0025"/> By integrating the synthesis of nanostructured carbon and MoS<sub>2</sub> in a single system, facile approach for the synthesis of binary carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> could be achieved.</p><p>In this work, for the first time, we report a single‐step synthesis of MoS<sub>2</sub> coupled with CN with an ordered porous structure via the nanotemplating method as illustrated in Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0001"/>A. Three precursors such as phosphomolybdic acid hydrate (PMA), dithiooxamide (DTO) and 5‐amino‐1H‐tetrazole (5‐ATTZ) that are the sources of molybdenum, sulfur and carbon and nitrogen were impregnated into a porous hard template of KIT‐6.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0026 #asia202000349-bib-0027"/> Sulfidation of PMA and DTO to MoS<sub>2</sub>, polymerization of 5‐ATTZ to CN and hybridization of the MoS<sub>2</sub> and CN simultaneously occurred during the calcination process of the precursors at elevated temperatures of 500–800 °C. Crystallinity and chemical composition of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrid were tailored by controlling the calcination temperature from 500 to 800 °C. The resultant CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids are denoted as CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐T, where T represents the calcination temperature.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000349-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000349:asia202000349-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000349-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(A) Schematic illustration of single‐step synthetic procedure for CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids, (B) Powder XRD patterns in high angle for (i) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, (ii) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, (iii) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and (iv) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800, and (C) FE‐SEM and (D,E) HR‐TEM images of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600. In (C) and (D), red hexagons indicate ordered porous CN whereas yellow lines suggest lattice fringes of (002) planes of MoS<sub>2</sub>. The inset of (E) is corresponding histogram of the (002) planes.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0001"/>B presents high angle X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of all the samples. All the diffraction peaks of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 are in agreement with the Bragg reflections of MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS file no. 37‐1492), indicating successful sulfidation of PMA and DTO into MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0011 #asia202000349-bib-0028"/> The full width at half maximum (FWHM) of all the peaks of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 is smaller than that of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, suggesting higher crystallinity of MoS<sub>2</sub> in the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 than that in the CN−MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500. The CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 show intense diffraction peaks of α‐MoO<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS file no. 05‐0508) with broad peaks of MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0029"/> It is surmised that a calcination process at the elevated temperature above 700 °C induces a reaction of the molybdenum and silica template, resulting in the formation of MoO<sub>3</sub>. The Bragg reflections of molybdenum compounds are clearly observed in the high angle XRD patterns of all the samples whereas Bragg reflections corresponding to CN are not shown in the XRD patterns. The absence of diffraction peaks of CN is attributed to weak X‐ray reflection for the light elements of carbon and nitrogen. The low angle XRD patterns of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 show a peak at 2θ =0.97° (Figure S1, see Supporting Information, <i>SI</i>), demonstrating the formation of the ordered porous structure of the materials.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0030"/> The absence of the peak at lower angle for CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 indicates that the ordered porosity is not achieved for the samples prepared at the elevated temperature above 700 °C.</p><p>Although Bragg reflections of CN with MoS<sub>2</sub> are not reflected on the XRD patterns, carbon nitride content was quantified by CHNS elemental analysis as shown in Table S1. The chemical composition of hybrids is determined as C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>3</sub> ⋅ 0.5MoS<sub>2</sub> for the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, while it is expressed as C<sub>3</sub>N ⋅ 0.5MoS<sub>2</sub> ⋅ <i>x</i>MoO<sub>3</sub> for the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800. The CN content in the total material is 45.18, 41.81, 27.69 and 23.98 wt% for the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 samples, respectively. Considering that CN experiences significant and continuous weight loss with a temperature increase,<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0030 #asia202000349-bib-0031"/> it is believed that the decrease in the CN content is ascribed to the evaporation of partial carbon and nitrogen at elevated temperatures.</p><p>The macro‐ and micro‐crystal morphologies of the samples were investigated by field emission‐scanning electron microscopy (FE‐SEM) and high resolution‐transmission electron microscopy (HR‐TEM), respectively. All the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids exhibit corrugated cardboard‐like morphology (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0001"/>C and Figure S2, see <i>SI</i>) on a macro‐scale. The macro‐porous structure of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids could help the easy permeation of electrolyte into the electrode material of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids. The highly ordered porous structure that is confirmed by the XRD analysis is also clearly observed in TEM image of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0001"/>D). The ordered porous structure of materials could serve as a path for electron transport and effectively accommodate diffused sodium ions. Two different morphologies of 5‐layers stacked nanosheets and nanoparticles are found in a magnified TEM image of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0001"/>E). The stacked nanosheets with an interlayer distance of 6.3 Å are MoS<sub>2</sub> whereas the ordered crystals are CN.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0011 #asia202000349-bib-0032"/> Interestingly, for MoS<sub>2</sub>, lateral size and the number of stacked layers are uniform as 3.5 nm and 5, respectively. The MoS<sub>2</sub> layers are confined by ordered mesoporous carbon nitride, which is a unique hierarchical structure obtained via single‐step nanotemplating method. It is believed that the uniform crystal morphology of the MoS<sub>2</sub> is attributed to restricted crystal growth of MoS<sub>2</sub> within defined pores of KIT‐6 template. The stacked layers of MoS<sub>2</sub> are homogeneously distributed and intimately contacted with the ordered mesoporous carbon nitride. The scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM)‐elemental maps with the energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectrum (EDX) indicate uniform distributions of Mo, S, C and N in the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 (Figure S3, see <i>SI</i>). In contrast with the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 show collapsed pore structure of CN as presented in Figure S4 (see <i>SI</i>).</p><p>Textural parameters of the present samples were examined by N<sub>2</sub> isotherm sorption analysis (Figure S5, see <i>SI</i>). As displayed in Table S2, the Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller (BET) surface area of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 is 73.7, 84.2 and 87.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> while their pore size is 5.0, 5.3 and 6.4 nm, respectively. The increase in surface area and pore size upon rising of the synthesis temperature is closely related to the generation of atomic vacancies in a CN framework at a high temperature. The decreases in surface area (82.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) and pore size (3.7 nm) of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 are attributed to the collapse of the ordered structure at 800 °C.</p><p>To identify chemical bonding features of CN and MoS<sub>2</sub> in the hybrids, Fourier‐transformed infrared spectroscopy (FT‐IR) and near‐edge X‐ray absorption fine structure (NEXAFS) analysis were carried out. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0002"/>A, a broad band between 1200 and 1700 cm<sup>−1</sup> is ascribed to stretching modes of heterocyclic C−N bonds, which is the characteristic band of carbon nitride. The CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids present peaks at 2229, 2047 and 1047 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which are assigned to the C≡N, N=C−S and S=O stretching modes, respectively. The C≡N and N=C−S peaks are gradually eliminated as calcined temperature is elevated, which is attributed to changes of C≡N and N=C−S bonds to C=C and C=C−S bonds upon the evaporation of nitrogen at high temperature. The peaks observed at the lower wavenumber lower than 850 cm<sup>−1</sup> are ascribed to the vibration of Mo species such as Mo−S and Mo−O while the O−H bond observed at 2600–3700 cm<sup>−1</sup> is contributed to water adsorbed in pores.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000349-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000349:asia202000349-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000349-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(A) FT‐IR spectra and NEXAFS spectra at (B) C K‐, (C) N K‐ and (D) S K‐edges for (i) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, (ii) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, (iii) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and (iv) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 and (E) chemical structural evolution upon temperature rise for the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids (left: CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, right: CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800).</p></caption></figure><p>For C K‐edge NEXAFS spectra (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0002"/>B), absorption peaks at 288.4, 286.5 and 285.0 eV are attributed to the excitations to π* <sub>C=N−C</sub>, π*<sub>C=C</sub> and π*<sub>out‐of‐plane C=C</sub>, respectively. All the samples show a significant X‐ray absorption at 288.4 eV, demonstrating that all samples exhibit mainly CN characteristics rather than graphite or N‐doped graphite features. The intensity of the peaks at 286.5 and 285.0 eV becomes strong for the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800, suggesting replacement of a number of C=N bonds with C=C bonds in its CN framework.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0021 #asia202000349-bib-0027"/> The effect of the calcination temperature in the CN framework is more clearly observed in N K‐edge NEXAFS spectra (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0002"/>C). Absorption peaks at 398.5, 399.6 and 401.2 eV are responsible for excitations to π*<sub>pyridinic N</sub>, π*<sub>N=C−N</sub> and π*<sub>tertiary N</sub>, respectively.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0027 #asia202000349-bib-0030 #asia202000349-bib-0033 #asia202000349-bib-0034"/> A characteristic peak at 397.1 eV of the N‐doped MoS<sub>2</sub> is not observed in the N K‐edge NEXAFS spectra of all present samples, which excludes the possibility of N‐doping into the MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0035"/> While the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500 shows a dominant feature of pyridinic N, characteristics of N=C−N and tertiary N bonds become distinct for the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800. It is surmised that the pyridinic N is thermodynamically less stable compared to N=C−N and tertiary N, resulting in a prior loss of pyridinic N in the CN framework. When it comes to S K‐edge NEXAFS spectra (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0002"/>D), all the samples commonly display two peaks at 2471.3 and 2482.3 eV.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0021 #asia202000349-bib-0036 #asia202000349-bib-0037"/> The peak at 2471.3 eV corresponds to excitation to S 3p states hybridized with Mo 4p states in pure MoS<sub>2</sub>, while the peak at 2482.3 eV is responsible for excitation to S 3p states of C−S−O bond. The C−S−O bond character is enhanced as the calcination temperature increases, which is in good agreement with the fact of formation of MoO<sub>3</sub> at temperatures of 700 and 800 °C. Absence of a peak corresponding to heterocyclic sulfur at 2475 eV rules out the possibility of S‐doping in the CN for all the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids. Based on the observation, chemical structures of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids are illustrated in Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0002"/>E.</p><p>To demonstrate the hybridization effect between the MoS<sub>2</sub> and carbon nitride on the electrode performances of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids for SIBs, galvanostatic measurements were carried out for bulk MoS<sub>2</sub> and all CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> samples. 2032‐type coin cells with a half cell configuration were fabricated, which was followed by galvanostatic cycling at a constant current density of 100 mAg<sup>−1</sup> in a potential range of 0.01–3.00 V. As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0003"/>A, the 1<sup>st</sup> coulombic efficiencies are 85, 81, 70, 62 and 57% for the MoS<sub>2</sub>, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800, respectively. The capacity reduction between 1<sup>st</sup> and 2<sup>nd</sup> cycles is due to formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer on the surface of the electrode material caused by decomposition of partial electrolyte upon cycling.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0038"/> The bulk MoS<sub>2</sub> experiences continuous capacity fading over 100 cycles. Although the MoS<sub>2</sub> shows 2<sup>nd</sup> discharge capacity of 504 mAhg<sup>−1</sup>, it retains only 64% capacity (323 mAg<sup>−1</sup>) after 100 cycles. In contrast with this, all the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids exhibit capacity retentions of 75–96% after 100 cycles, which suggests the beneficial effect of hybridization of MoS<sub>2</sub> with CN to improve its electrochemical instability. The 100<sup>th</sup> discharge capacity values are 351, 605, 502 and 477 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> for CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000349-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000349:asia202000349-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000349-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(A) Discharge capacities at 100 mAg<sup>−1</sup> and (B) rate capabilities of (i) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐500, (ii) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, (iii) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐700 and (iv) CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐800 and (v) bulk MoS<sub>2</sub> and (C) long‐run cyclability at 1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, (D) 1<sup>st</sup>, 10<sup>th</sup> and 100<sup>th</sup> potential profiles at 100 mAg<sup>−1</sup> and (E) 1<sup>st</sup>, 2<sup>nd</sup> and 3<sup>rd</sup> cyclic voltammograms at 0.2 mVs<sup>−1</sup> for CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600.</p></caption></figure><p>The rate capability of all the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids was investigated as plotted in Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0003"/>B. At various current densities of 50, 100, 200, 300, 400 and 500 mAg<sup>−1</sup>, the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 delivers discharge capacities of 656, 626, 593, 572, 555 and 542 mAhg<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. After cycling at a high current density of 500 mAg<sup>−1</sup>, its discharge capacity at 50 mAg<sup>−1</sup> is totally recovered, registering a discharge capacity of 662 mAhg<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, the discharge capacity of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 at 1 Ag<sup>−1</sup> is 431 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0003"/>C). The electrode performances of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 were compared with those of carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids reported in the literature. As presented in Table S3 (see <i>SI</i>), the rate capability of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 is significantly better among carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> materials reported so far.</p><p>The CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 shows the best electrode performance among all CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> samples. Compared to the other CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids, the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 has advantages for superior electrochemical functionalities such as high purity and good crystallinity of MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0001"/>B) and ordered porous structure (Figure S1, see <i>SI</i>) and a large proportion of electronic conductive CN (41.81%). The high purity and good crystallinity of MoS<sub>2</sub> are responsible for the uptake of a large number of sodium ions in the MoS<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0039"/> The mass of the carbonaceous component cannot be the main factor governing capacity of the hybrids, but the large proportion of electronic conductive CN with the ordered porous structure contributes to fast charge transfer kinetics in SIBs.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0021"/></p><p>The charge and discharge potential profiles (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0003"/>D) and cyclic voltammograms (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0003"/>E) of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 show that its charging and discharging operating potentials are 2.0 and 1.7 V respectively. The operating potentials below 2.0 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup> suggest that the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 hybrid is a suitable candidate as an anode material for the battery component of hybrid sodium‐ion capacitors. Moreover, little difference between 10<sup>th</sup> and 100<sup>th</sup> potential profiles of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 provides additional evidence of the excellent cyclability of the hybrid. An irriversible peak at ∼0.9 V is observed in the 1<sup>st</sup> cylic voltammogram of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, which is ascribed to reduction of used organic electrolyte and subsequent formation of SEI on the surface of electrode material.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0038"/></p><p>Since the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 has the highly ordered porous system for the CN coupled with MoS<sub>2</sub>, it is believed that the superior electrode performance might be originated from surface capacitive charge storage.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0040"/> To confirm the charge storage mechanism in the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600, the surface capacitive contribution was quantified according to Power law (equation 1) and cyclic voltammogram measurements at various scan rates (Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0004"/>A).<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0040"/><displayedItem xml:id="asia202000349-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/asia202000349-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000349:asia202000349-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="asia202000349-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000349:asia202000349-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000349-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(A) Cyclic voltammograms at different scan rates from 0.2 to 1.0 mVs<sup>−1</sup>, (B) corresponding log(<i>i</i>) vs. log(<i>v</i>) profiles at the anodic and cathodic peaks and <i>b</i>‐values, (C) separated capacitive charge area (green shadow) from the total charge at the scan rate of 0.2 mVs<sup>−1</sup> and (D) comparison of the capacitive contribution at different scan rates for CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600.</p></caption></figure><p>where <i>i</i> and <i>v</i> are a measured current at a given potential and a scan rate respectively. As presented in Figure <link href="#asia202000349-fig-0004"/>B, all the <i>b</i>‐values calculated at the five potentials are close to 1, suggesting that sodium ions are mainly stored at the surface of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 rather than its interlayer space. The <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> are diffusion‐controlled and surface capacitive contribution components, respectively. The surface capacitive contribution to the charge storage property of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 is above 80% at all the scan rates from 0.2 to 1.0 mVs<sup>−1</sup>, which indicates that the good electrochemical performance is mainly due to the high surface adsorption rather than the sodium intercalation into the MoS<sub>2</sub>. The 91% surface capacitive contribution at 1.0 mVs<sup>−1</sup> suggests a fast charge storage property of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600.</p><p>The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was conducted to estimate a charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) and internal resistance of the electrode material in the cell (Figure S6, see <i>SI</i>). All the hybrids exhibit a smaller <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> and internal resistance than those of MoS<sub>2</sub>. The small <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> of the hybrids may be contributed to good wettability of the CN with the ordered porous structure whereas high electronic conductivity of the CN is mainly responsible for the diminution of internal resistance of the electrode material. Among all the hybrid samples, the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 exhibits the smallest <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> and internal resistance, which accounts for its superior electrode performances.</p><p>In summary, binary carbonaceous MoS<sub>2</sub> was successfully synthesized for the first time by a single‐step nanotemplating approach. The MoS<sub>2</sub> layers with a uniform lateral size of 3.5 nm and 5‐layer stacking are homogeneously distributed in a void space of highly ordered porous carbon nitride. The CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 hybrid exhibits excellent anode performances for SIBs. Its specific capacity at 100 mAg<sup>−1</sup> is 605 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. Moreover, its rate capability and long‐run cyclability are outstanding with a capacity value of 431 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> at 1 Ag<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles. The superior electrode performance of the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub>‐600 is attributed to large charge storage sites on the surface of the hybrid material with improved electronic conductivity and ordered porous structure. Considering the simple synthetic route to the CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrid, this novel hybrid provides great opportunities for developing anode materials for high‐performing SIBs.</p><section xml:id="asia202000349-sec-0002"><title type="main">Experimental</title><p><i>Preparation of CN/MoS<sub>2</sub> hybrids</i>: KIT‐6 silica was prepared according to the method reported previously.<link href="#asia202000349-bib-0027"/> 0.0362 mol (0.55 g) of phosphomolybdic acid hydrate (H<sub>3</sub>PMo<sub>12</sub>O<sub>40</sub>) was dissolved in 11.00 g of ethanol at 50 °C. Then, the solution was added to a mixture of the KIT‐6 silica (1.00 g), 5‐amino‐1H‐tetrazole (0.78 g) and dithiooxamide (3.27 g). The mixture was kept in the oven at 100 °C, followed by aging at 160 °C for 6 h. After cooling down, the material was calcined at a different temperature such as 500, 600, 700 and 800 °C for 1 h under N<sub>2</sub> flow. The obtained samples were stirred in 5 wt% hydrofluoric acid and ethanol to remove KIT‐6 template. The samples were dried at 100 °C overnight.
