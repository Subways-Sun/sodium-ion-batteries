Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Efficient electrode materials, that combine high power and high energy, are the crucial requisites of sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), which have unwrapped new possibilities in the areas of grid‐scale energy storage. Hard carbons (HCs) are considered as the leading candidate anode materials for SIBs, however, the primary challenge of slow charge‐transfer kinetics at the low potential region (&lt;0.1 V) remains unresolved till date, and the underlying structure–performance correlation is under debate. Herein, ultrafast sodium storage in the whole‐voltage‐region (0.01–2 V), with the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient enhanced by 2 orders of magnitude (≈10<sup>–7</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>) through rationally deploying the physical parameters of HCs using a ZnO‐assisted bulk etching strategy is reported. It is unveiled that the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>) and diffusion barrier (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) are in a positive and negative linear relationship with the carbon p‐band center, respectively, and balance of <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> is critical in enhancing the charge‐storage kinetics. The charge‐storage mechanism in HCs is evidenced through comprehensive in(ex) situ techniques. The as prepared HCs microspheres deliver a record high rate performance of 107 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> @ 50 A g<sup>–1</sup> and unprecedented electrochemical performance at extremely low temperature (426 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> @ −40 °C).</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202109282-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Ultrafast sodium storage in the whole‐voltage‐region is realized by rational manipulation of the microstructure of hard carbons (HCs) using a ZnO‐assisted bulk etching strategy. It is revealed that the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>) and diffusion barrier (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) are in a positive and negative linear relationship with the carbon p‐band center, respectively, which can be balanced by manipulating the physical parameters of HCs.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202109282-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202109282-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), owing to the abundant and cheap sodium resources, have been surfaced as the next generation large‐scale energy storage systems to support the sustainable energy exploitation of renewable sources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0001 #adma202109282-bib-0002 #adma202109282-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, SIBs have to deal with various challenges including inferior rate capability, and condescending lifetime, which generally instigated by the lack of appropriate electrode materials, especially the anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0004 #adma202109282-bib-0005 #adma202109282-bib-0006 #adma202109282-bib-0007"><sup>4–7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hard carbons (HCs) have attracted extensive attention with suitable potential versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na, good structural stability, decent coulombic efficiency, superior reversible capacity etc., and are considered as the most feasible anode materials for further commercialization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0008 #adma202109282-bib-0009 #adma202109282-bib-0010"><sup>8–10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the unsatisfied rate capability of HCs (generally &lt; 5 A g<sup>–1</sup>) represents the primary constraint in their practical applications, as well as the underlying structure–performance correlation is a substance of controversial discussion.</p><p>Typical HCs comprise a large number of short‐range‐ordered graphitic domains and internal micropore domains (or voids between graphitic domains).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The graphitic‐domain with proper interlayer distance and the internal‐nanopore‐domain are both believed to be capable of accommodating sodium ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0012 #adma202109282-bib-0013 #adma202109282-bib-0014 #adma202109282-bib-0015 #adma202109282-bib-0016 #adma202109282-bib-0017"><sup>12–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Classic charge/discharge curves of HCs comprise a high‐potential slope (&gt;0.10 V) and a low‐potential plateau (0.01–0.10 V). Various mechanisms have been proposed to explicate the charge‐storage behavior at different potential regions, yet no conclusive outcome has been accomplished.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0018 #adma202109282-bib-0019 #adma202109282-bib-0020 #adma202109282-bib-0021 #adma202109282-bib-0022 #adma202109282-bib-0023 #adma202109282-bib-0024 #adma202109282-bib-0025"><sup>18–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A few studies correlate the charge‐storage behavior with the interlayer distance (<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>), concluding that short range ordered graphitic layers with <i>d</i> of 0.36–0.40 nm are capable of storing Na<sup>+</sup> ions through “interlayer intercalation,” providing a high plateau capacity,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0026 #adma202109282-bib-0027 #adma202109282-bib-0028 #adma202109282-bib-0029"><sup>26–29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> whereas some other studies contemplate that the filling of Na ions (or clusters) into the internal micropores (or voids between graphitic domains) is responsible for the plateau capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0012 #adma202109282-bib-0013 #adma202109282-bib-0030 #adma202109282-bib-0031 #adma202109282-bib-0032"><sup>12,13,30–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Despite the diverse opinions on the charge‐storage mechanism, it is a well‐established fact that the low potential plateau region represents the rate‐determining step for HCs. Various studies have unveiled that, HCs with obvious plateau below 0.10 V, encounter a dramatic drop of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0021 #adma202109282-bib-0028 #adma202109282-bib-0033 #adma202109282-bib-0034 #adma202109282-bib-0035"><sup>21,28,33–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Generally, the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient at the sloping area (3.00–0.10 V) is between 10<sup>–11</sup> and 10<sup>–9</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>, which is of two orders lower (10<sup>–13</sup> to 10<sup>–10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>) at the plateau region (&lt;0.10 V).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0021 #adma202109282-bib-0033"><sup>21,33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Serious efforts have been implemented to improve the rate performance of HCs by introducing more porosity or defect into their structures.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0028 #adma202109282-bib-0036"><sup>28,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, such strategies typically promote the rate performance at the expense of lowering their initial coulombic efficiency (ICE) and vanishing of the low‐potential plateau. Also, the specific capacity of HCs below 1.00 V is usually lower than 50%.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0026 #adma202109282-bib-0028 #adma202109282-bib-0037"><sup>26,28,37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> So far, construction of a “perfect” structure for HCs, that allows fast Na<sup>+</sup> transfer in the whole voltage region, as well as maintaining the dominated plateau capacity, has been given the utmost priority above all.</p><p>Herein, we report HC microspheres with a well‐regulated microstructure, synthesized via ZnO‐assisted bulk etching method. The plateau (0.10–0.01 V) capacity of the synthesized HC account for ≈70%, while the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient at the plateau region can be readily improved from ≈10<sup>–9.5</sup> to ≈10<sup>–7.4</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>–1</sup>. It is worth mentioning that we have been successful in establishing a correlation between electronic structure and the electrochemical performance of HCs: we identify that the carbon p‐band center upholds a linear relationship with both Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>) and diffusion energy barrier (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>), which can be readily manipulated by adjusting the physical parameters of the HCs. Furthermore, the charge‐storage mechanism in HCs during charge/discharge has been elaborately elucidated by various in situ and ex situ characterizations. The as synthesized material delivers an incredible reversible capacity both at room temperature (RT) and low temperature (501 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> @ RT, 426 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> @ −40 °C at 0.05 A g<sup>–1</sup>) and exhibits a record high rate performance of 107 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at 50 A g<sup>–1</sup>. This work illustrates a fundamental principle for the microstructure manipulation of HC materials, which eventually unbolts new opportunities for building up a high‐performance and sustainable energy storage system.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202109282-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adma202109282-sec-0030"><title type="main">Preparation and Structural Characterization</title><p>Briefly, the synthesis of HC microspheres (HCM) has been illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. 3‐aminophenol containing phenol and amino groups are selected as the monomer precursor, which forms a highly cross‐linked resin microsphere by autocatalytic polymerization with formaldehyde. ZnO nanoparticles (30–400 nm, Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information) are encapsulated in the resin microsphere, which gently catalyzes the local graphitization of the HC during the high temperature (1300 °C) carbonization process,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and meanwhile cause bulk‐etching of the graphitic layers through the reaction ZnO + C = Zn↑+ CO↑. The microstructure of the sample, including the length (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>), thickness (<i>L</i><sub>c</sub>), and interlayer distance (<i>d</i>) of the graphitic layers can thus be precisely calculated. The as produced CO increases the bulk oxygen content of HC and the metallic Zn produced, can be completely evaporated at above 900 °C. The final product with well manipulated microstructure is denoted as HCM‐1300‐ZBE. For comparison, control samples calcined at different temperatures (800–1500 °C) are also obtained with or without adding ZnO, denoted as HCM‐≈800–1500, HCM‐1500‐ZBE respectively. The detailed synthesis procedure is displayed in Supporting Information.</p><figure xml:id="adma202109282-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202109282:adma202109282-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202109282-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration and morphological structure characterizations. a) Schematic diagram illustrating the synthesis route of the samples. b) SEM images and c) mapping of HCM‐1300‐ZBE. d,e) HRTEM images of HCM‐1300‐ZBE (d) and HCM‐1300 (e). The inset shows the corresponding FFT patterns.</caption></figure><p>The morphology and microstructure of the as prepared samples are characterized using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0001">1b–e</link> and Figures <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S1,S2</link>, Supporting Information). The HRTEM image of HCM‐1300‐ZBE depicts the widely distributed graphitic domains with an average domain length (<i>L</i><sub>a</sub>) of ≈2.57 nm, graphitic crystallite thickness (<i>L</i><sub>c</sub>) of ≈1.2 nm, and interlayer distance (<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>) of ≈0.406 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0001">1d</link> and Table <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). As contrast, HCM‐1300 presents more disordered structure with similar <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> (≈2.45 nm), <i>L</i><sub>c</sub> (≈0.86 nm), and smaller interlayer distance (0.382 nm) (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0001">1e</link>). Notably, HCM‐1300‐ZBE shows smaller <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> than HCM‐1300 and HCM‐1500, while HCM‐1500‐ZBE shows larger <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> than HCM‐1500. Therefore, the encapsulated ZnO particles can not only efficiently catalyze the local graphitization (&lt;1300 °C) and inhibit the excessive graphitization (1500 °C) during calcination, and meanwhile enlarge the interlayer distance. Meanwhile, HCM‐1500‐ZBE presents a much shorter <i>L</i><sub>a</sub> in comparison to HCM‐1500 (≈2.80 nm versus ≈5.02 nm), indicating that the ZnO‐assisted synthesis validates an obvious bulk etching effect, which could inhibit the overgrowth of graphitic layers. The structural evolution of samples at different temperatures is examined with X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and Raman spectra (Figures <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S3–S5</link> and Tables <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S2,S3</link>, Supporting Information), and the parameters obtained from various characterization techniques are compared in Table <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information. The <i>R</i>‐factor is obtained by measuring the ratio between the (002) peak intensity (B) and the background intensity (A) according to the test methods reported in the literature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0039 #adma202109282-bib-0040"><sup>39,40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The low <i>R</i>‐factors suggest that under the formation conditions, there was insufficient thermal energy for graphene layers to rotate into a parallel alignment in accordance with Dahn's “falling cards model.”<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0041"><sup>41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For our samples, as the carbonization temperature increases (from 800 °C to 1500 °C), the <i>R</i>‐factor increases from 2.282 to 2.530. This indicates that the order degree of carbon materials increases (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). Compared with HCM‐1300 (2.467), the addition of ZnO makes the <i>R</i>‐factor of HCM‐1300‐ZBE (2.484) slightly increased, which proves that ZnO can slightly promote the graphitization transformation of materials from disorder to order. When the temperature continues to rise to 1500 °C, compared with HCM‐1500 (2.530), HCM‐1500‐ZBE (2.506) has a relatively small <i>R</i>‐factor, which indicates that ZnO can inhibit the excessive graphitization of the material. This trend agrees well with the XRD and Raman results.</p><p>In addition, the aromaticity and degree of aromatic condensation of HCs can be obtained by the change of the O/C ratio of X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) and the characteristic peaks of Near‐edge X‐ray absorption fine structure spectroscopy (NEXAFS).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0042 #adma202109282-bib-0043"><sup>42,43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S6a</link>, Supporting Information, as the carbonization temperature increases, the O/C ratio gradually decreases, indicating an increase in aromaticity. NEXAFS was also conducted to further analyze the aromaticity and condensation information of the sample. It can be seen from Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S6b–d</link>, Supporting Information that as the carbonization temperature increases, the signal intensity of aromatic C continues to increase. Among them, HCM‐1500 has the highest aromaticity, and the effect of ZnO to inhibit over‐graphitization makes HCM‐1500‐ZBE have a low aromatic C ratio. Aromatic index (aromatic C/carboxylic acid C or amide C) also shows a similar trend (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S6d</link>, Supporting Information), which is in accordance with the results characterized with other techniques. It is worth noting that all carbons are mainly composed of condensed (fused) aromatic ring clusters.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As the carbonization temperature increases, the proportion of aromatic carbon increases (i.e., the aromaticity increases); and with higher temperatures, the planar hexagonal array of carbon atoms becomes larger, and the size of micro‐graphite domain (aromatic structure) becomes larger (i.e., increase in aromatic condensation).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0043"><sup>43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The size change of graphitic crystallites was obtained through XRD data fitting, which agrees well with the changing trend of aromatic condensation degree.</p><p>The nitrogen sorption isotherms (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S7a</link>, Supporting Information) indicate the typical microporous structures of the as prepared samples. With the upsurge of carbonization temperature, the average pore size gradually increases while the specific surface area (SSA) notably decreases from 494.6 to 7.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>. The ZnO bulk etching is proved to be beneficial to the formation of micropores and the SSA of HCM‐1300‐ZBE (48.9 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) is twofold that of HCM‐1300 (23.0 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) (Table <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). The pore size of HCM‐1300‐ZBE is dominant at ≈1.10 nm while HCM‐1300 presents a wider pore size distribution (≈0.55–0.80 nm). Similarly, HCM‐1500‐ZBE shows an SSA of 58.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, which is only 7.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for HCM‐1500 with almost no micropores (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S7b–d</link>, Supporting Information). The increased micropore content is considered favorable for the Na<sup>+</sup> filling in the electrochemical test.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0012 #adma202109282-bib-0013 #adma202109282-bib-0014 #adma202109282-bib-0017 #adma202109282-bib-0032"><sup>12–14,17,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> XPS analysis of the samples with different etching depths was conducted to reveal the elemental composition of HCMs from surface to bulk (Figures <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S8–S11</link> and Tables <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S6–S9</link>, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution O 1s spectrum shows high CO (532.2 eV) concentration of the sample surfaces (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S10</link>, Supporting Information). This is in well accordance with the literature, indicating the redox reaction between CO and Na<sup>+</sup> can happen around ≈0.6–0.5 V.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0004">4i</link> illustrated the different stages of the sodium storage for HCM‐1300‐ZBE.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202109282-sec-0070"><title type="main">Fundamental Understanding about the Structure−Performance Correlation</title><p>Although close connections between the physical parameters and the electrochemical performances of the HC samples have been established, till date, no fundamental understanding of this phenomenon has been achieved. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations are carried out to further explain the Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism in HCs. Previous reports in the literature state that the interlayer spacing smaller than 0.35 nm is not sufficient for Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0001 #adma202109282-bib-0010 #adma202109282-bib-0017"><sup>1,10,17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> while nanopores with smaller diameters (&lt;1.0 nm) are not conducive to the filling of sodium clusters.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0007 #adma202109282-bib-0009"><sup>7,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To simulate the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation in the graphitic domains of HCs, a representative bilayer graphene model<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0031 #adma202109282-bib-0036"><sup>31,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> (AB stacking) of 5 × 5 supercell with varied interlayer distance and a 15 Å vacuum space along the <i>y</i>‐axis were considered. The Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior within a short graphitic layer (SGL) is denoted as SGL‐0.36, SGL‐0.38, and SGL‐0.40, respectively, and a bilayer model with a distance of 1 nm between the top and bottom layers has been built to simulate the nanopores among graphitic domains (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link> and Figures <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S23,S24</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202109282-bib-0036 #adma202109282-bib-0075 #adma202109282-bib-0076"><sup>36,75,76</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The positive values of adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>) for SGL‐0.36‐inside (1.26 eV), SGL‐0.38‐inside (0.849 eV), SGL‐0.40‐inside (0.011 eV) indicate that the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation is thermodynamically difficult in the short graphitic layers without any defects, even with large interlayer distance (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5b</link>). For comparison, pristine bilayer models without a vacuum space were also constructed to simulate the pristine graphitic layer with different <i>d</i> spacings, denoted as P‐0.36 (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub> = 2.34 eV), P‐0.38 (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub> = 1.62 eV), P‐0.40 (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub> = 1.00 eV) respectively. Lattice oxygen (SGL‐O) and vacancy defects (SGL‐VD) are then introduced into the models to manipulate the HCs in practical conditions (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S23</link>, Supporting Information). To simulate the effect of the length of SGL layers on the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation, the <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> of Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption at different positions within the above models (edge and inside) are performed (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5c</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S25</link>, Supporting Information). It is found that, <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> values are negative for both SGL‐O‐0.40‐inside (−0.061 eV) and SGL‐0.40‐inside‐VD (−0.93 eV) which affirms the significant promotion of the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation by the lattice oxygen and vacancy defects. Under the same condition, the larger interlayer distance has been found to cause more negative <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> (<i>E</i><sub>a‐0.40</sub> &lt; <i>E</i><sub>a‐0.38</sub>), while the <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> for Na<sup>+</sup> at different positions follows the sequence: <i>E</i><sub>a‐edge</sub> &lt; <i>E</i><sub>a‐inside</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5c</link> and Table <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S16</link>, Supporting Information)<sub>.</sub> Therefore<sub>,</sub> increasing the <i>d</i> spacing or decreasing the length of short graphitic layers can surely benefit the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation. Likewise, when the nanopore wall is composed of pristine graphene, the nanopore model (1 nm) calculations also show a positive <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> of 0.64 eV (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5d</link>). On contrary, <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> is reduced to −0.85 eV in the presence of vacancy defect (1 nm‐inside‐VD) and Na tends to form clusters in the nanopore (Cluster‐1 nm‐inside‐VD) with a much more negative <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> (−1.14 eV).</p><figure xml:id="adma202109282-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202109282:adma202109282-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202109282-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>DFT Calculation. a) Schematic of short graphitic layer and nanopore. b) Optimized configurations of Na adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>) on different models. c) Adsorption energy of different intercalation positions (edge and inside) on different models. d) Optimized configurations of Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy on different nanopore models. e,f) Diffusion barrier energies (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) of different models. g) Partial density of states. h) The relationship between the p‐band center, the <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> (Na adsorption), and <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> (the diffusion barrier energies) for each model.</caption></figure><p>The Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier energies (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) of selected models are calculated to understand the ion diffusion ability within the graphitic domain and nanopores (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S26</link>, Supporting Information). In Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5e</link>, the energy changes at different positions in the diffusion path represented by 0–6 are calculated to measure the diffusion capacity of sodium ions in different models. Among them, the <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> of the pristine graphitic layer (P‐0.36, P‐0.38, P‐0.40) and short graphitic layer (SGL‐0.36, SGL‐0.38, SGL‐0.40) model with different layer spacing, and the oxygen (SGL‐O) and vacancy defects (SGL‐VD), nanopore model (1 nm) are mainly calculated. As shown in the Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5e,f</link>, the larger <i>d</i> spacing will cause smaller <i>E</i><sub>b</sub>, that is, SGL‐0.40‐inside &lt; SGL‐0.38‐inside &lt; SGL‐0.36‐inside, which is 0.25 eV, 0.31 eV, 0.37 eV respectively. It is worth noting that, the adsorption capacity of SGL for Na<sup>+</sup> is greater than the pristine graphitic layer model, but their <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> variations are very small (P‐0.40 (0.18 eV) &lt; P‐0.38 (0.26 eV) &lt; P‐0.36 (0.37 eV), Table <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S17</link>, Supporting Information). This authenticates that the short graphitic layer structures are beneficial for the adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup> and have minimal effect on the diffusion of sodium amidst the carbon layers. Meanwhile, the edge site and vacancy defect of the SGL model could significantly enhance the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption but also cause serious energy barrier, which reaches 1.10 eV for SGL‐0.40‐inside‐VD, 3.43 eV for SGL‐0.40‐edge (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S26c</link>, Supporting Information). The bulk oxygen on the other hand, decreases the <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> values, in the following sequence: SGL‐O‐0.40‐inside (0.21 eV) &lt; SGL‐O‐0.38‐inside (0.27 eV) &lt; SGL‐O‐0.36‐inside (0.31 eV). Therefore, the bulk oxygen introduced by the ZnO bulk‐etching strategy plays an important role in enhancing the charge‐storage kinetics. The nanopore model (1 nm) demonstrates the smallest diffusion barrier energy (0.13 eV), as compared to that of 1 nm‐inside‐VD (0.91 eV), suggesting that the filled Na ions are likely to form clusters within the nanopores.</p><p>Therefore, a significant phenomenon is that <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> respond in a different way to various physical parameters which has been a significant observation. For an in‐depth understanding, of how these physical parameters influence the adsorption and diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup>, partial density of states (PDOS) has been calculated and the p‐band center of carbon in the vicinity of the adsorption position is obtained through DFT calculations (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5g</link>, Figures <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S27–S31</link>, Supporting Information). Interestingly, the position of p‐band center is linearly correlated with both adsorption energy of <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>E</i><sub>b</sub>. The farther (closer) the p‐band center away from the Fermi level, the weaker (stronger) is the adsorption and the smaller (larger) is the diffusion barrier (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-fig-0005">5h</link>, Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S32</link> and Table <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S18</link>, Supporting Information). When the perfect carbon with 0.40 nm layer distance (P‐0.40) is measured, its p‐band center is calculated to be −7.11 eV which is the lowest, with the most positive <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> (1.006 eV) and smallest <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> (0.18 eV). On the contrary, the short graphitic layer model (SGL‐0.40‐inside) will raise the p‐band center (−6.95 eV), enhance the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>, 0.011 eV) but increase the diffusion barrier (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>, 0.25 eV). Similarly, vacancy defects (SGL‐0.40‐inside‐VD, −0.93 eV for <i>E</i><sub>a</sub>, 1.10 eV for <i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) and bulk oxygen (SGL‐O‐0.40‐inside, −0.061 eV for <i>E</i><sub>a</sub>, 0.21 eV for <i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) also push the p‐band centers moving toward the Fermi level, giving rise to stronger adsorption ability for Na<sup>+</sup>, but more difficulty for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion. Henceforth, a moderate p‐band center with balanced <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> and <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> would be much more favorable for the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and diffusion amongst the graphitic domains as well as the nanopores. For the transfer and storage of Na<sup>+</sup> within the graphitic carbon layer, the interlayer distance demonstrates significant effect on the adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>), but less effect on the diffusion energy (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>) (Figure <link href="#adma202109282-supitem-0001">S32</link>, Supporting Information). Consequently, the experimentally observed superior performance has been well rationalized by the positive (negative) linear relationship between p‐band center and <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> (<i>E</i><sub>b</sub>), and can be readily manipulated by adjusting the physical parameters of the HCs, including the length and thickness of graphitic layers, interlayer distance, lattice defects, bulk oxygens, as well as the nanopore distributions.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adma202109282-sec-0080"><title type="main">Conclusions</title><p>We have reported an HC material with exceptionally high rate performance by a simple ZnO‐assisted bulk etching method. Comprehensive structural characterization and theoretical modeling implemented in this study provide clear insights into the correlation between the microstructure of HC and the corresponding capacity performance and mechanism of sodium storage. We evidenced the co‐existence of interlayer Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and nanopore Na filling in the low‐voltage plateau region through various in/ex situ techniques, and the primacy of the layer spacing and nanopores determine the dominance amongst both the mechanisms. Meanwhile, the bulk etching strategy enables elaborate modulation of length and thickness of the short graphic layers, and introduces bulk oxygen, which plays a significant role in enhancing the ion transfer mobility. Serious efforts have been made to establish a connection between the carbon p‐band center and the sodium storage ability, thus providing a fundamental understanding on the structure–performance relationship for Na<sup>+</sup> storage in HCs. The full cell of HCM‐1300‐ZBE delivered a high energy density of 294.6 Wh kg<sup>–1</sup>, and a high power density of 6.73 kW kg<sup>–1</sup> (based on the total mass of both electrodes).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202109282-sec-0090" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>X.Y., Z.L., J.W., and X.F. contributed equally to this work.
