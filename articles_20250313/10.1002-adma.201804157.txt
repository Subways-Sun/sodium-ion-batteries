The major obstacle for the practical application of SIBs is the absence of suitable negative electrode materials with long cycling stability and high rate performance. Here, sulfur‐doped double‐shell sodium titanate (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>) microspheres constructed from 2D ultrathin nanosheets are synthesized via a templating route combined with a low‐temperature sulfurization process. The resulting double‐shell microspheres deliver a high specific capacity (≈222 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C), excellent cycling stability (162 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 15 000 cycles at 20 C), and superior rate capability (122 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 C) as anode for SIBs. The improved electrochemical properties originate from synergistic effects between the unique double‐shell nanostructures built from 2D nanosheets architecture and sulfur doping. This synergistic effect not only stabilize Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>‐based electrode during the cycling, but also improve the sluggish Na insertion/extraction kinetics by narrowing the bandgap of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. The synthesis strategy proposed here can be developed into a technical rationale for generating high‐performance sodium‐storage devices.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201804157-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Sulfur‐doped double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> microspheres</b> constructed from 2D ultrathin nanosheets are synthesized via a templating route combined with a sulfurization process. The microspheres deliver a high specific capacity (≈222 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C), excellent cycling stability (162 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 15 000 cycles at 20 C), and superior rate capability (122 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 C) as anode for sodium‐ion batteries..<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201804157-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201804157-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Urgent concerns involving limited quantity and uneven distribution of lithium sources have driven researchers to pay more attention to exploiting novel rechargeable batteries to replace lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), especially in the large‐scale energy storage field. As the neighbor of lithium in the periodic table, sodium displays similar physical and chemical properties, but with abundant natural resources, much lower cost, and ubiquitous presence around the globe. Therefore, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are expected to be the most promising candidates as next‐generation batteries.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0001 #adma201804157-bib-0002"/> The commercial LIB anode (graphite), however, exhibits extremely poor sodium storage (capacity of only 31 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) in ester‐based electrolytes, and has a low insertion voltage (close to 0 V, which easily causes sodium plating and arouses safety concerns), which largely restricts it in practical use in SIBs.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0002 #adma201804157-bib-0003 #adma201804157-bib-0004"/> Alloying and conversion‐type materials also suffer from huge volume changes during cycling that will lead to electrode pulverization and thus capacity fading.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0005 #adma201804157-bib-0006 #adma201804157-bib-0007"/> Therefore, searching for suitable anodes with high capacity, long cycling stability, and high rate capability is the critical issue for bringing SIBs into practical application.</p><p>As a typical Ti‐based compound, sodium titanate (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>) comprises zigzag layers of titanium and oxygen octahedral, where 3.5 Na ions per formula unit can be easily inserted into the interlayer space, resulting in a capacity of 310 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0008"/> In addition, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> displays the lowest average Na intercalation potential (0.3 V for Na<sup>+</sup> insertion, but above the voltage for the formation of Na dendrites) compared to the reported insertion‐type oxides, which is beneficial for achieving high operating voltage and energy density in full batteries.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0009 #adma201804157-bib-0010"/> In particular, the large interlayered spacing of ≈0.83 nm is favorable for the charge storage properties and the diffusion of electrolyte ions.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0011 #adma201804157-bib-0012 #adma201804157-bib-0013 #adma201804157-bib-0014"/> Notwithstanding, there are still some shortcomings of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> anode<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0015 #adma201804157-bib-0016 #adma201804157-bib-0017 #adma201804157-bib-0018 #adma201804157-bib-0019"/>: i) the low electronic conductivity caused by the large bandgap (3.7 eV) results in ineffective charge transport and thus limits the rate performance of SIBs;<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0008 #adma201804157-bib-0009 #adma201804157-bib-0010 #adma201804157-bib-0011"/> ii) the aggregation and overlapping of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> layers due to the large surface energy leads to fast capacity decay during Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction;<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0015"/> and iii) the stability and lifetime concerns aroused by the volume expansion upon Na uptake are still an obstacle to the use of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> in large‐scale SIB application.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0008"/> To address these issues, a series of effective strategies have been developed, including widening the interlayer spacing of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>,<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0016"/> regulating the morphology on the nanoscale,<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0008 #adma201804157-bib-0010 #adma201804157-bib-0012"/> and using carbonaceous materials as a conductive matrix.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0014 #adma201804157-bib-0017"/></p><p>Here, we report the design and fabrication of sulfur‐doped double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> microspheres (S‐NTO) constructed from 2D ultrathin nanosheets as anode for SIBs. This strategy involves the synthesis of the double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> microspheres by a hard‐templating route and the sulfurization of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets via a scalable and low‐cost low‐temperature annealing process. This unique electrode design to improve the sodium storage performance has several benefits. First, the ultrathin thickness (layer number in the range of 2–6) and wider interlayer spacing (0.87 nm) of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets provide shorter and more rapid ion diffusion pathways, which can facilitate the sodium intercalation/deintercalation. Second, the unique double‐shell structure allows for smooth electrolyte penetration and fast ion/mass transport, and the 3D hierarchical architecture can avoid the stacking/restacking of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets, thus leaving enough space for buffering the volume expansion. Third, sulfurization can narrow the bandgap and enhance the electronic conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. As a result, the S‐NTO microspheres were evaluated as SIB anode, and they exhibited a high specific capacity (≈222 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C), excellent cycling stability (162 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 15 000 cycles at 20 C), and superior rate capability (122 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 C).</p><p>The products were achieved by a templating route followed by a sulfurization process, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. First, SiO<sub>2</sub> nanospheres with a mean diameter of ≈300 nm were synthesized through a typical Stöber method (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Second, coating a thick TiO<sub>2</sub> layer (90 nm) on the surface of SiO<sub>2</sub> through hydrolysis of tetrabutyl titanate (TBOT) (Figure S2, Supporting Information). Finally, double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> microspheres, with the shells constructed from ultrathin 2D nanosheets were obtained by hydrothermal treatment of SiO<sub>2</sub>@TiO<sub>2</sub> in NaOH solution. The average diameter of the double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> microspheres was 580 nm (Figure S3, Supporting Information), which is larger than that of SiO<sub>2</sub>@TiO<sub>2</sub> because of the expanded Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets. In the general hard‐template route, the obtained Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> microspheres should consist of single‐shelled hollow sphere, not a double‐shelled structure, because only one single‐shell processor was coated on the surface of the template. Therefore, the time evolution of this unusual double‐shell structure was studied to understand its formation mechanism. As shown in Figure S4 in the Supporting Information, the inner SiO<sub>2</sub> sphere had corroded away at the begining and formed a very thick single‐shell hollow structure (Figure S4a–c, Supporting Information). As the reaction proceeded, ultrathin nanosheets were detected on the inner surface of the shell, and a void space was observed in the middle of the thick shell (Figure S4d, Supporting Information). When the reaction time was further prolonged, the void space became bigger and bigger (Figure S4e, Supporting Information), and then the double‐shell was generated (Figure S4f, Supporting Information). The reason may be that the thick and porous TiO<sub>2</sub> layer is sufficient enough to provide two interfaces for independent nucleation, so that the sodium titanate nanosheets grow in opposite directions (inner and outer). As the TiO<sub>2</sub> is etched, the Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets grow larger and larger. When the TiO<sub>2</sub> is depleted completely, an in situ interface is generated, resulting in the double‐shell structure. Broken microspheres also confirmed the formation process. As shown in Figure S3e in the Supporting Information, all the nanosheets of the inner shell grow inward. On further prolonging the reaction time, the nanosheets of the inner shell self‐assemble and shrink to form a smaller shell to reduce the surface energy (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0001"/>c–g; Figure S4d–f, Supporting Information). The titania‐etching and titanate‐growth may be smoothed through Ostwald ripening.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0020 #adma201804157-bib-0021 #adma201804157-bib-0022"/> These double‐shell microspheres have exceptional structural virtues that make them brilliant candidates for advanced anode materials: 1) their highly porous nature offers high specific surface area (151.8 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) with plentiful active sites for sodium redox chemistry (Figure S5, Supporting Information); 2) the double‐shell not only provides void space for accommodation of the mechanical stresses aroused by the volume variations during de/sodiation, but also makes reasonable use of space to improve the density of the sample. To further improve the electrochemical properties of the Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, we sulfurized the double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, using thiourea to narrow its bandgap and thus increase its electronic conductivity. For comparison, double‐shell Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> was also calcined under the same conditions, but without thiourea. Here, the hydrothermal product obtained at 140 °C after 12 h is denoted as NTO, the product after calcination is denoted as C‐NTO, and the sulfurized product is denoted as S‐NTO for ease of differentiation.</p><figure xml:id="adma201804157-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the formation process for sulfur‐doped Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.</caption></figure><p>The morphologies and detailed microstructures of the synthesized products were characterized by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and TEM. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a, S‐NTO is composed of uniform microspheres with rough surfaces and an average size of 580 nm, which is completely the same as NTO. Their double‐shell feature is revealed by the broken microsphere (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"/>b), in which one can clearly see the inner shell. TEM images further demonstrate that the microspheres are double‐shell structures constructed from connected ultrathin nanosheets (2−6 layers) (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"/>c–e). The thickness of the outer shell is about 100 nm, and that of the inner shell is ≈90 nm. The broken microspheres demonstrate that all the ultrathin nanosheets of the inner shell have grown inward (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"/>d). The interlayer spacing of S‐NTO is 0.87 nm, corresponding to the (001) planes of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"/>e), which is larger than that of C‐NTO (0.84 nm; Figure S6, Supporting Information) and NTO (0.83 nm; Figure S7, Supporting Information). This is in agreement with the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns which show a trifling shift to lower angle (Figure S8, Supporting Information) after sulfuration. The enlarged interlayer spacing arise from the atomic radius of sulfur larger than that of oxygen, and which is favorable for the sodium insertion and extraction. From the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of S‐NTO nanosheets, the clear lattice spacing could correspond to the (−204) and (401) planes of NTO (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"/>f).<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0023"/> The elemental mapping displays the homogeneous elemental distribution of O, Na, and Ti in the C‐NTO sample (Figure S9, Supporting Information). After the sulfuration, the element S is also evenly distributed in the S‐NTO sample (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0002"/>g), regardless of whether it is the inner shell or the outer shell (Figure S10, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201804157-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of S‐NTO, c) TEM image, d–f) high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning TEM (HAADF‐STEM) images, with the inset in (e) showing the structure, and g) EDS mapping images of O, Na, S, and Ti for the S‐NTO sample.</caption></figure><p>In previous reports, sulfur doping could narrow the bandgap of TiO based materials and thus increase their ionic/electronic conductivity.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0024"/> Therefore, the bandgap energy of the synthesized samples was investigated through ultraviolet–visible (UV–vis) diffuse reflectance spectra (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a,b). Compared to NTO, the absorption edge of C‐NTO shifts to ≈330 nm, and that of the S‐NTO sample shifts to 360 nm. The corresponding trailing absorption of the S‐NTO sample runs from about 320 to 480 nm, as observed in the UV‐vis spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>a). Therefore, the bandgap energy of the NTO, C‐NTO, and S‐NTO is 3.7, 3.49, and 2.12 eV, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>b). The bandgap narrowing is ascribed to the doping of S atoms into the lattice of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, which could reduce the electron transition energy from the valence to the conduction band and thus induce an obvious shift in the absorption edge to lower energy.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0025 #adma201804157-bib-0026"/> These changes are also reflected in the colors of the samples. The color of NTO is white, but it became light grey and yellow after calcination and sulfurization, respectively (insets in Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>a).</p><figure xml:id="adma201804157-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) UV–vis diffuse reflectance spectra of NTO, C‐NTO, and S‐NTO with corresponding photographs in the insets; b) plots of (<i>αhν</i>)<sup>1/2 </sup>versus <i>hν</i>, where <i>v</i> is the frequency, <i>h</i> is Planck's constant, and α is the absorption coefficient. c–f) High‐resolution XPS spectra of S 2p for S‐NTO (c), and Na 1s (d), Ti 2p (e) and O 1s (f) for the NTO, C‐NTO, and S‐NTO samples.</caption></figure><p>The composites and the chemical states of the synthesized samples were further investigated by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The survey spectra of the samples are very similar, except that the element sulfur appears in the S‐NTO (Figures S11–S13, Supporting Information). The S 2p peaks can be deconvoluted into two peaks centered at 162.3 and 167.7 eV (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>c), corresponding to the STi and SO bonds, respectively.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0027"/> The formidable STi peak demonstrates that the major doping state of sulfur is S<sup>2−</sup>.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0028 #adma201804157-bib-0029 #adma201804157-bib-0030 #adma201804157-bib-0031 #adma201804157-bib-0032"/> This is very different from what was reported previously in the literature, where most of the sulfur in doped samples was S<sup>4+</sup>.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0028 #adma201804157-bib-0029"/> This difference may be due to the different doping sources. In their cases, they used sulfur powder as the sulfur source, while in our case, we used H<sub>2</sub>S (generated from thiourea) instead. H<sub>2</sub>S as the sulfurization agent always leads to S<sup>2−</sup> formation in the final products.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0028 #adma201804157-bib-0029"/> Because of the substitution of sulfur with lower electronegativity for oxygen, the binding energy of Ti 2p in S‐NTO shifts to lower energy (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>d).<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0027 #adma201804157-bib-0028 #adma201804157-bib-0029"/> However, the Ti 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ti 2p<sub>1/2</sub> peaks of the C‐NTO sample are also slightly shifted to lower energy compared to NTO. This result may be originated from the fact that Ti<sup>4+</sup> is partially reduced to Ti<sup>3+</sup> under inert atmosphere.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0033"/> Similarly, the Na 1s peak of S‐NTO is shifted to lower energy due to the changed chemical environment surrounding the sodium (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>e). Obvious changes in the O 1s peaks between the NTO, C‐NTO, and S‐NTO samples are also observed (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0003"/>f). In NTO and C‐NTO, the O 1s peaks can be deconvoluted into peaks at 529.2 and 531.4 eV, which attributed to OTiO and TiO bonds, respectively. In the S‐NTO sample, two more peaks located at higher energy (531.8 and 532.9 eV) are detected, which are assigned to OTiS and O‐S bonds.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0029"/> All the results indicate that the S‐NTO is a sulfur‐doped sample. The sulfur content is 2.0 wt.% for S‐NTO, which was calculated based on the XPS results.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0023"/></p><p>The Na‐storage mechanism of the as‐fabricated samples was investigated through the cyclic voltammetry (CV) technique. As shown in Figures S14 and S15 in the Supporting Information, the CV curves of C‐NTO and NTO are almost the same, which demonstrate the typical Na‐storage features of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets, displaying energy storage at high voltages in the range of 0.5–2.5 V (vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>).<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0016"/> For the CV curves of S‐NTO shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a, the broad peak at around 0.5 V in cathodic process of the initial cycle is ascribed to electrolyte decomposition to form the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer, and these peaks disappear in the following cycles due to the irreversible reaction.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0011 #adma201804157-bib-0034 #adma201804157-bib-0035"/> The peak centred at 0.8 V is due to the sodium‐ion insertion into Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> along with the reduction of Ti<sup>4+</sup> to Ti<sup>3+</sup>.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0011 #adma201804157-bib-0035 #adma201804157-bib-0036 #adma201804157-bib-0037"/> This peak shifts to 0.85 V in the subsequent cycles, probably stemmed from reduced overpotential for the reversible sodium storage.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0008"/> S‐NTO shows a very similar CV curve to NTO and C‐NTO in the first cycle (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"/>a). However, a new obvious peak at 0.25 V is observed in the second anodic process. This peak may arise from the increased sodium storage sites that are created by the sulfurization process. Furthermore, the CV curves of S‐NTO are more stable than those of NTO and C‐NTO because the curves of S‐NTO overlap very well after the first cycle, while those of NTO and C‐NTO do not overlap completely. These results indicate that S‐NTO electrode has better electrochemical reversibility than NTO and C‐NTO electrodes. Galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles of the synthesized samples are in agreement with the CV curves. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"/>b, after the first cycle, the profiles of S‐NTO are overlapping from 10 to 100 cycles. The voltage profiles of NTO and C‐NTO, however, do not coincide well at the same condition (Figures S16 and S17, Supporting Information). Significantly, a voltage plateau below 0.3 V is observed in the discharge profiles of S‐NTO. Simultaneously, a very small plateau at 0.3 V in the charge profiles is detected. These two plateaus cannot be discovered in the profiles of the NTO and C‐NTO samples, however (Figures S16 and S17, Supporting Information), indicating that they are originated from the sulfur doping. The S‐NTO electrode delivers a discharge capacity of 457 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and charge capacity of 250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C (1 C = 177 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, referring to 2 sodium ions inserted into NTO in 1 h) in the first cycle, corresponding to a coulombic efficiency of 54.7%, which is higher than that of C‐NTO (50.2%) and NTO (43.0%). Their low first cycle coulombic efficiencies are attributed to the irreversible formation of the SEI layer.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0011 #adma201804157-bib-0035 #adma201804157-bib-0036 #adma201804157-bib-0037"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201804157-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of S‐NTO electrode. a) CV curves for the first 4 cycles of S‐NTO measured at a sweep rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Galvanostatic charge–discharge curves of S‐NTO at the 1 C rate for different selected cycles. c) Cycling performances of S‐NTO, C‐NTO, and NTO at a current density of 2 C. d) Long cycling stability of S‐NTO at the 50 C rate. e) Rate performances of S‐NTO, C‐NTO, and NTO at different rates. f) The rate capability of S‐NTO compared to other reported NTO‐based electrodes for SIBs.</caption></figure><p>The cycling performances of the samples were studied and are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"/>c. All the samples display capacity fading in the first few cycles and then attain stable values because of the SEI layer formation. As predicted, S‐NTO delivers the highest capacity and the most stable cyclability. After 100 cycles at 2 C, S‐NTO retains a specific capacity of 188 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In contrast, the parent NTO only delivers a capacity of 97 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is only half that of S‐NTO. Although the capacity of C‐NTO is increased (127 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) by the Ti<sup>3+</sup> produced in the calcination,<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0010"/> it is still much lower than that of S‐NTO. The improved performance is related to the lower charge transfer resistance and the better interfacial kinetics endowed by the sulfur doping (Figure S18, Supporting Information). The long‐term cycling stability of the S‐NTO samples at high rate 20 C and 50 C were further investigated (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"/>d; Figure S19, Supporting Information). S‐NTO could retain a capacity of 162 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 15 000 cycles at 20 C, and 122 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 13 000 cycles at 50 C. Both coulombic efficiencies are approximately 100% after the first several active cycles, revealing the stable SEI layer of S‐NTO and the structural stability during cycling. Such an outstanding cyclability agrees well with the charge transfer resistance, which decreases as the cycling proceeds due to the dynamic activation process of the electrodes (Figure S20, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0011"/> To the best of our knowledge, these are the longest cycling performances and the highest capacity values reported for Na storage in titanium‐based electrodes, representing the extreme efficiency of our designed sulfur‐doped NTO (Table S1, Supporting Information).</p><p>Rate capabilities of the as‐prepared samples were also elevated and are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"/>e. Strikingly, the S‐NTO still delivers the best rate capability. The specific capacity of S‐NTO is higher than those of NTO and C‐NTO at every stage of current density. This is consistent with the cyclability and CV curves. S‐NTO exhibits specific capacities of 222, 196, 167, 150, 142, 133, 127, and 105 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, and 50 C, respectively. It is impressive that the capacity recovers to 215 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when the current density is restored to 1 C, demonstrating that the S‐NTO structure can tolerate various current densities (Figure S21, Supporting Information). From the galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles at different current densities (Figures S22 and S23, Supporting Information), S‐NTO displays smaller polarization at higher rates than the C‐NTO and NTO samples, further confirming the better sodium ion diffusion and ion conductivity. This superior rate performance is also better than those of the reported Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>‐based materials, especially at high rates (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0004"/>f; Table S1, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0014 #adma201804157-bib-0017 #adma201804157-bib-0038 #adma201804157-bib-0039 #adma201804157-bib-0040 #adma201804157-bib-0041"/></p><p>For better understanding of the outstanding electrochemical properties of the S‐NTO sample, CV curves at various sweep rates were collected to assess the kinetic characteristics of this material toward Na storage (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>). The CV curves share a similar shape in the range of 0.1–100 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, namely, all include wide cathodic and anodic peaks (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0005"/>a; Figure S24, Supporting Information). As the sweep rate is increased, the shape of the curve becomes more and more rectangular, which is characteristic of a surface diffusion process.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0042 #adma201804157-bib-0043 #adma201804157-bib-0044"/> Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0005"/>b displays the <i>b</i> values determined by the slope of the plot of log <i>i</i> versus log <i>v</i> on the basis of the following equation:<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0044 #adma201804157-bib-0045 #adma201804157-bib-0046"/><displayedItem xml:id="adma201804157-disp-0001" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma201804157-math-0001.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo> </mo><mo>=</mo><mo> </mo><mi>a</mi><msup><mi>v</mi><mi>b</mi></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem>(<i>i</i>: current density; <i>v</i>: sweep rate; <i>a</i>, <i>b</i>: constants). In general, the <i>b</i> value of 0.5 demonstrates diffusion‐controlled behavior (battery‐type), and the <i>b</i> value of 1.0 reveals a surface diffusion process (capacitor‐type). In S‐NTO, the calculated <i>b</i> value is 0.98 for the cathodic peak and 0.95 for the anodic peak from 0.1 to 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, both quite close to 1, illustrating the high contribution of capacitive behavior. The large <i>b</i> values demonstrate that most of the sodium storage occurs on the surface or near the surface, which agrees well with the HRTEM and Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) measurements. The ultrathin nanosheets (2–6 layers) and the large BET surface area guarantee that sodium storage only occurs on the surface or near the surface. Above the scan rate of 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the <i>b</i> value decreases to 0.60 and 0.64 for the cathodic and anodic peaks, respectively, which may be attributed to the increasing ohmic resistance and diffusion constraints at high rates.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0044"/> The relationship between the normalized capacity and the sweep rate, which could distinguish the charge storage mechanism, can be divided into two regions (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0005"/>c).<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0045"/> For the sweep rates &gt;2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the normalized capacity decreases with increasing sweep rate, demonstrating the diffusion‐controlled charge storage mechanism. In contrast, the capacity is mostly independent of the sweep rate below 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, indicating a surface diffusion process.</p><figure xml:id="adma201804157-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Kinetic analysis of the electrochemical behavior of S‐NTO electrode. a) CV curves of S‐NTO at various sweep rates from 0.1 to 100 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Plots of peak current versus the corresponding sweep rate. c) Plot of normalized capacity versus corresponding sweep rate<sup>−1/2</sup>, inset CV curve showing the capacitive contribution at 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (shaded area). d) The capacitive contribution ratio at various sweep rates.</caption></figure><p>Further analysis of the relationship between the sweep rate and current responses could quantitatively discriminate between the relative capacitive and diffusion‐controlled contributions at a specific voltage. The current response (<i>i</i>) at a given potential (<i>V</i>) is a blended process of diffusion‐controlled insertion (<i>k</i><sub>2</sub>ν<sup>1/2</sup>) and capacitive effects (<i>k</i><sub>1</sub>ν), where ν is the sweep rate and <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> are parameters.<link href="#adma201804157-bib-0047 #adma201804157-bib-0048"/><displayedItem xml:id="adma201804157-disp-0002" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/adma201804157-math-0002.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mrow><mfenced><mi>V</mi></mfenced></mrow><mo> </mo><mo> </mo><mo>=</mo><mo> </mo><mo> </mo><msub><mi>k</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mi>ν</mi><mo> </mo><mo>+</mo><mo> </mo><msub><mi>k</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><msup><mi>ν</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>/</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><p>Based on this equation, the contribution of surface capacitive effects and diffusion‐controlled intercalation to total charge storage can be identified at a given potential (details in the Supporting Information). It can be seen that the diffusion‐controlled process mainly occurs in the potential range of the redox peak, where the Ti<sup>4+</sup>/Ti<sup>3+</sup> redox reaction may smooth the Na diffusion process (Figure S25, Supporting Information). As presented in Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0005"/>d, the relative ratio of the capacitive contribution improves with increasing sweep rate, reaching 89% at the sweep rate of 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Such pseudocapacitive behavior is highly beneficial to long‐term cyclability and high rate capacity.</p><p>The high rate capacity and long cycling stability of S‐NTO is attributed to the synergistic effects of the unique double‐shell structure and sulfur doping. The ultrathin nanosheets promote easy pathways for sodium‐ion insertion/extraction and much more contact area between the electrode and the electrolyte. Importantly, after the 100th cycle, the outer layer of the structure has negligible deformation, and the inner shell totally keeps its original morphology (Figure S26, Supporting Information), demonstrating that the unique double‐shell structure is very stable during cycling (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0006"><b>6</b></link>a). A rough SEI layer is first formed on the surface of S‐NTO after the first few cycles (Figure S27, Supporting Information), but it finally presents a very uniform, continuous, and thin layer after long cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0006"/>b), confirming the excellent cycling stability without much side reaction between the electrode and electrolyte. In addition, the elemental mapping and energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) analysis reveal that sulfur is still distributed evenly in the S‐NTO material (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0006"/>d,e). Furthermore, high‐resolution XPS spectra of S 2p confirm that the status of sulfur is very stable and like its initial state (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0006"/>f). The sulfur doping enhances the electronic/ionic conductivity and provides more sodium‐ion storage sites in the S‐NTO sample, with benefits for improved capacity and cyclability (Figure <link href="#adma201804157-fig-0006"/>g). These effects both contribute to the intercalation pseudocapacitive behavior of sodium ions in the S‐NTO sample, ensuring superior electrochemical performance.</p><figure xml:id="adma201804157-fig-0006"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804157:adma201804157-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804157-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) TEM images, d) elemental mapping, and e) EDS spectrum of S‐NTO after 100 cycles. f) High‐resolution XPS spectra of S 2p for S‐NTO after 100 cycles. g) Schematic illustration of the sodiation/desodiation process.</caption></figure><p>In summary, S‐NTO microspheres constructed from ultrathin nanosheets were hydrothermally synthesized with a subsequent sulfidation using thiourea as sulfur source. The S‐NTO double‐shell microspheres delivered superior Na‐storage capability endowed by its unique nanostructure and the sulfur‐doping. Kinetic analysis by CV demonstrated that the sodium storage process of the as‐synthesized sample is a surface diffusion process (pseudocapacitive process), and the pseudocapacitive contribution can attain a high level of 89% of the total charge storage at the sweep rate of 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Therefore, the double‐shell microspheres delivered a high specific capacity (≈222 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C), excellent cycling stability (162 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 15 000 cycles at 20 C), and superior rate capability (122 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 C). We are convinced that similar sulfur doping strategies will contribute to developing advanced Na‐storage electrodes in the future.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201804157-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Australian Research Council (ARC) through Discovery Projects (DP160102627) and a Linkage Project (LP160100273).
