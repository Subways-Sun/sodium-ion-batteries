However, it remains a great challenge to understand the reaction mechanism of materials upon monovalent‐ion insertion. Here, triclinic Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub>/carbon composite (MgVP/C) with high thermal stability is synthesized via ball‐milling and carbon‐thermal reduction method and applied as a pseudocapacitive negative electrode in LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs. In operando and ex situ studies demonstrate the guest ion‐dependent reaction mechanisms of MgVP/C upon monovalent‐ion storage due to different sizes. MgVP/C undergoes an indirect conversion reaction to form Mg<sup>0</sup>, V<sup>0</sup>, and Li<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> in LIBs, while in SIBs/PIBs the material only experiences a solid solution with the reduction of V<sup>3+</sup> to V<sup>2+</sup>. Moreover, in LIBs, MgVP/C delivers initial lithiation/delithiation capacities of 961/607 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (30/19 Li<sup>+</sup> ions) for the first cycle, despite its low initial Coulombic efficiency, fast capacity decay for the first 200 cycles, and limited reversible insertion/deinsertion of 2 Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> ions in SIBs/PIBs. This work reveals a new pseudocapacitive material and provides an advanced understanding of polyanion phosphate negative material for monovalent‐ion batteries with guest ion‐dependent energy storage mechanisms.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs5274-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub>/carbon composite shows large electrochemical difference for Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage suggests different electrochemical mechanisms among Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage. alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Currently, monovalent‐ion batteries, in particular, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are playing critical roles in modern society and our daily life and have achieved great success in applications for portable electrical devices and electronic cars<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> due to their high energy densities, lightweight, and long cycle life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, some drawbacks need to be overcome, such as the limited capacities of the commercial negative electrode materials, the high cost of lithium, and potential safety concerns.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0001 #advs5274-bib-0002"><sup>1a,2b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As monovalent‐ion batteries, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs), which share a similar “rocking chair” working principle to LIBs, have received much attention and are considered as promising candidates for large‐scale energy storage, thanks to their abundant resources and low cost.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, aluminum can be used as the current collector in positive and negative electrodes for both SIBs and PIBs, whereas an expensive and heavier Cu foil must be used in negative electrodes of LIBs, due to the Li–Al alloy formation at low potential.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The potassium‐ion in propylene carbonate (PC) displays a lower ion‐solvent interaction due to lower desolvation energy and a smaller solvated ion as compared to Li‐ and Na‐ions, resulting in fast diffusion kinetics and high rate capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Many different types of cathode and anode materials have been studied for monovalent‐ion storage so far, including transition metal oxides, Prussian blue analogs, polyanionic compounds, fluorides, organic materials, sulfur‐based materials, titanium‐based, alloy based and carbonaceous materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0001 #advs5274-bib-0006"><sup>1a,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, in SIBs and PIBs, big challenges still exist in developing electrode materials suitable for commercialization due to the large ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) and K<sup>+</sup> (1.38 Å)<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0002"><sup>2d</sup></link><sup>]</sup> resulting in sluggish diffusion kinetics. Moreover, safety concerns and the reactive property of Na and K metal make them very difficult as direct negative electrodes for commercial cells.</p><p>The famous graphite negative electrode demonstrates guest‐dependent reaction mechanisms upon monovalent‐ion insertion with the formation of LiC<sub>6</sub>, NaC<sub>64</sub>, and KC<sub>8</sub>, respectively. Nowadays, graphite is commonly used as negative electrode material for LIBs because of its flat and low working potential (0.1–0.2 V vs Li<sup>+</sup>/Li) and relatively high specific capacity (372 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite its high specific capacity, the very low operating voltage close to lithium plating may lead to safety concerns.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, graphite is electrochemically less active with the formation of NaC<sub>64</sub> in Na cells and cannot be used as an insertion host of SIBs since Na<sup>+</sup> insertion into graphite is significantly impeded.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This might be due to the unfavorable mismatch between the graphite structure and the size of the Na ion and the energetic instability of the Na‐graphite intercalation compounds (GICs).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0009"><sup>9b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Surprisingly, graphite can form KC<sub>8</sub> by K<sup>+</sup> ion insertion with a high initial charge capacity of 273 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in PIBs. However, KC<sub>8</sub> suffers from large volume expansion during potassium ions intercalation/deintercalation, which might cause safety issues and low coulombic efficiency.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite the good Na storage performance in hard carbon, the likely formation of sodium dendrites at a low working voltage (below 0.1 V) can cause safety issues.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Recently, polyanion‐type phosphate materials have gripped much attention due to their pronounced structural and thermal stability and open framework.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, vanadium‐based polyanion‐type compounds are very promising. For example, both monoclinic and rhombohedral Li<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (LVP) can be utilized as both positive and negative materials in LIBs due to the variety of oxidation states of vanadium and its open structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The monoclinic LVP negative electrode exhibits a stable reversible capacity of 203 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with multiple plateaus in the potential ranges of 3.0–0.0 V versus Li<sup>+</sup>/Li as reported by Rui et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> while the rhombohedral LVP shows a reversible capacity of ≈115 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a plateau at ≈1.75 V in the potential range of 3.0–1.0 V versus Li<sup>+</sup>/Li as reported by Jian et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Fu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> studied calcium‐substituted monoclinic LVP negative electrodes in LIBs, all of which showed improved electrochemical performance and can deliver specific capacities higher than 50 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the extremely high C‐rate of 200 C. LVP/C undergoes two‐phase reaction during cycling, whereas Ca<sub>1.5</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C shows capacitive‐like mechanism. NASICON‐structured Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) can serve as both positive and negative electrode material for SIBs, because of its unique structural feature and multi redox couples of V. Jian et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> first reported NVP as a negative electrode for SIBs by combined computational and experimental studies. The NVP material exhibits two pairs of redox peaks at around 1.6 and 0.3 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na with a reversible charge capacity of ≈150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, exhibiting two‐phase reaction during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The symmetric Na‐ion full cell consisting of Na<sub>2</sub>VTi(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C reported by Wang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> exhibited a superior rate capability and long lifetime over 10 000 cycles, where Na<sub>2</sub>VTi(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C shows three plateaus at 3.4, 2.1, and 1.6 V (V<sup>4+</sup>/V<sup>3+</sup>, Ti<sup>4+</sup>/Ti<sup>3+</sup>, and V<sup>3+</sup>/V<sup>2+</sup> redox couples, respectively) in the potential range of 1.5–4.5 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. NASICON‐type K<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (KVP) delivers a reversible specific capacity of 130 and 91 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with the formation of K<sub>5</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and KV<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> as both negative and positive electrodes in PIBs, respectively, in the range of 1.0–2.5 and 2.0–4.0 V versus K<sup>+</sup>/K.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Interestingly, the abovementioned M<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (M = Li/Na/K) only serve as insertion‐type hosts and exhibit clear redox peaks, typical for battery‐type materials (with diffusion‐controlled kinetics). Nevertheless, it remains a great challenging to understand the reaction mechanism of materials upon monovalent‐ion insertion. Magnesium vanadium phosphate Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub> (MgVP) crystallizes in the space group <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs5274:advs5274-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/advs5274-math-0001.png"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mover accent="true"><mn>1</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">$P\bar{1}$</annotation></semantics></math> and is isostructural with a known quaternary V<sup>3+</sup>‐containing Zn<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which has been prepared recently and whose magnetic property is once evaluated. It consists of bioctahedral V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>10</sub> units of two edge‐sharing octahedra, monophosphate groups, distorted MgO<sub>6</sub> octahedra, and distorted MgO<sub>5</sub> trigonal bipyramids. Its structure suggests that MgVP can be a promising negative electrode material with high thermal stability. However, its electrochemical properties and reaction mechanisms in Li/Na/K‐ion batteries have not been studied so far.</p><p>Here in this work, we report the synthesis of MgVP/C composite via ball‐milling and the carbon‐thermal reduction method, where in situ formed carbon not only could relieve the growing up and aggregation of particles during annealing, but also increase the electronic conductivity of the electrode. The electrochemical performance of MgVP/C in LIBs, SIBs, and PIBs was studied, demonstrating significant differences in terms of electrochemical reaction among Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup>. The guest ion‐dependent electrochemical reaction mechanisms upon Li‐, Na‐, and K‐ion insertion were carefully investigated via in operando synchrotron diffraction and X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) together with ex situ magnetic measurements.</p></section><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0020"><title type="main">Result and Discussion</title><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0030"><title type="main">Structural and Morphological Characterization</title><p>The crystal structure of the as‐prepared sample was characterized by synchrotron diffraction, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs5274-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. All featured reflections observed for the pristine MgVP/C can be well indexed according to triclinic Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub> with a space group of <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs5274:advs5274-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/advs5274-math-0002.png"><semantics><mrow><mi>P</mi><mover accent="true"><mn>1</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">$P\bar{1}$</annotation></semantics></math>, in agreement with a previous report.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Rietveld refinement result demonstrates that its lattice parameters are <i>a</i> = 6.325(1) Å, <i>b</i> = 7.903(1) Å, <i>c</i> = 9.286(1) Å, <i>α</i> = 105.287(3)°, <i>β</i> = 108.567(3)°, and <i>γ</i> = 101.339(3)° (Table <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Moreover, there is no evidence for diffraction reflections belonging to the graphitic carbon, suggesting an amorphous state of carbon. Hence, MgVP/C composite is successfully prepared. To estimate the amount of carbon in MgVP/C, TGA was performed under O<sub>2</sub> flow. Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S1a</link> (Supporting Information) demonstrates that the slight weight loss below 300 °C is attributed to the evaporation of absorbed and chemically bonded water in the sample. With further heating, an abrupt weight loss is observed between 300 and 500 °C and is ascribed to the combustion of residual carbon. Therefore, the amount of carbon in MgVP/C material is determined as 4.9 wt%. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) reveals that MgVP/C consists of aggregated submicron particles as well as smaller aggregated carbon particles in between. The microstructure of MgVP/C was investigated using high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) as displayed in Figure <link href="#advs5274-fig-0001">1b,c</link> and Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S2b,c</link> in the Supporting Information, indicating that MgVP is highly crystalline, as well as amorphous carbon was distributed unevenly between the aggregated particles as a connection between MgVP crystalline grains. The BET surface area of Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub>/C is 78 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much larger than that of LVP/C (54.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) and calcium substituted LVP (25–40 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>)<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and the pore‐size distribution curves show that the pore size lies in both, the micro‐ and mesopore range (Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S1b</link>, Supporting Information). A broad reduction peak at 0.7 V and a peak near 0 V can be seen in the first cycle. The peak at 0.7 V is assigned to the SEI formation and does not appear in the following cycles. While the peak near 0.1 V can be ascribed to the Li<sup>+</sup> ions insertion into amorphous carbon and the SEI formation due to the decomposition of the electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0014 #advs5274-bib-0025"><sup>14,25a,b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the following two scans, this peak is weaker and contributes to a lower capacity than that for the first cycle. After the second scan, no obvious redox peaks can be observed but a box‐type CV shape, similar to what is generally obtained for typical (pseudo)capacitive materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To analyze the reaction kinetics of MgVP/C, CV was further carried out at different scan rates from 0.05 to 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs5274-fig-0004">4b</link>). According to Muller's work,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the peak current obeys a power‐law relationship with the sweep rate: <i>i</i> = <i>av<sup>b</sup></i>. Here <i>a</i> is a constant and <i>v</i> is the sweep rate. The <i>b</i> value can vary between 0.5 and 1.0, indicating a diffusion‐controlled process for <i>b</i> = 0.5 and a surface‐controlled process for <i>b</i> = 1.0 (generally attributed to a capacitive‐type process). The <i>b</i> value can be determined by the slope of log(<i>i</i>) versus log(<i>v</i>), as displayed in Figure <link href="#advs5274-fig-0004">4c</link>. In the case of MgVP/C in LIBs, <i>b</i> values are 0.81 and 0.82 at 1.0 and 1.1 V, respectively (close to 1), suggesting that it is a pseudocapacitive controlled surface process. Pseudocapacitive materials generally charge/discharge much faster than battery materials. The pseudocapacitive nature of electrode materials can guarantee fast ions diffusion kinetics, thus improving the rate performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In contrast, polyanion‐type phosphate materials M<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (M = Li/Na/K) exhibit clear redox peaks and only serve as insertion‐type hosts with diffusion‐controlled kinetics as discussed in the introduction section.</p><figure xml:id="advs5274-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs5274:advs5274-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs5274-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Kinetics properties of MgVP/C in LIBs. In Region I, corresponding to a lithiation capacity of 200 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, all reflections of MgVP shift toward lower angles with a significant decrease in intensities. This indicates the coexistence of a two‐phase transition and solid solution, which probably can be attributed to nonequilibrium conditions after Li insertion. The material goes through an insertion reaction to form the solid solution phase of Li<i><sub>x</sub></i>Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub> and, simultaneously, a conversion reaction to form Mg<sup>0</sup>, V<sup>0</sup>, and Li<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> takes place. During the delithiation process, the reflections do not completely return to their initial positions, indicating the large irreversibility of MgVP/C during cycling. In SIBs, it shows reversible changes of lattice parameters in the second cycle (Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information), implying better reversibility compared with the first cycle. One can assume that the MgVP/C still undergoes poor reversibility in LIBs and high reversibility in SIBs/PIBs from a structural point of view. The slight difference between the fully lithiated electrode and V foil reference could be attributed to the isolated V nanoparticles and incomplete conversion reaction as observed via in operando synchrotron diffraction. Moreover, one isosbestic point at 5474.5 eV is observed, which further confirms the two‐phase reaction from the Li<i><sub>x</sub></i>Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub> to Mg, V, and Li<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>. However, even the complete conversion reaction (maximum 18 Li<sup>+</sup> ions) cannot justify such a large amount of lithium, suggesting that the formation of SEI, the amorphous carbon, and the conductive additive in the electrode would consume part of the lithium in the first lithiation process. However, no hysteresis effects could be observed within the experimental resolution (≈10 Oe) from the magnetic field scan at 2 K (Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information). From Curie–Weiss fit of MgVP/C from 150 to 390 K (Figure <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information), Curie constant <i>C</i>, the paramagnetic effective moment <i>µ</i><sub>eff</sub>, and the Weiss constant <i>θ</i> are estimated as listed in Table <link href="#advs5274-supitem-0001">S2</link> (Supporting Information), which are in good agreement with previous work<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> except that no bifurcation of the ZFC and FC curve was reported therein down to 2.5 K. In MgVP, vanadium is formally present as V<sup>3+</sup> with an [Ar] 3d<sup>2</sup> electronic configuration leading to a <sup>3</sup>F ground and first excited <sup>3</sup>P term. In ideal octahedral coordination the <sup>3</sup>F ground term splits into a triplet <sup>3</sup>T<sub>1</sub> ground state (t<sup>2</sup>e<sup>0</sup>), a first excited triplet <sup>3</sup><i>T</i><sub>2</sub> state (t<sup>1</sup>e<sup>1</sup>) and a nondegenerate <sup>3</sup>A<sub>2</sub> state (t<sup>0</sup>e<sup>2</sup>) whereas the <sup>3</sup>P term only transforms into a <sup>3</sup>T<sub>1</sub> state. MgVP/C shows a broad reduction peak at 0.7 V and a peak near 0 V for the first CV scan in LIBs. The electrode displays no obvious oxidation and reduction peaks but a box‐type CV shape and resembles a capacitive‐like behavior so that MgVP/C is classified as new pseudocapacitive material. MgVP/C delivers a high initial lithiation/delithiation capacity of 961/607 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and a reversible capacity of over 547 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the following five cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, while the material shows much lower initial sodiation/desodiation and potassiation/depotassiation capacities of 236/63 and 301/64 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. This suggests that MgVP/C is a promising negative electrode material in LIBs for practical application due to its high initial reversible capacity. However, further effort should be made to improve the poor electrochemical cycling stability and low initial Coulombic efficiency using electrolyte engineering, nanoengineering and surface coating. The combination of in operando and ex situ studies demonstrates that MgVP/C undergoes an indirect conversion reaction to form Mg<sup>0</sup>, V<sup>0</sup>, and Li<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> in LIBs, while the material only experiences a solid solution in both SIBs and PIBs with the reduction of V<sup>3+</sup> to V<sup>2+</sup>. This result reveals that, due to the size differences of alkali ions, a different electrochemical mechanism occurs among Li<sup>+</sup> and Na<sup>+</sup>/K<sup>+</sup> storage, leading to the large difference in capacity. Moreover, magnetic measurements demonstrate that in the pristine MgVP, the V ions are present as V<sup>3+</sup> and that strong magnetic anisotropies induced by highly distorted VO<sub>6</sub> octahedra are responsible for the observed paramagnetic properties. After lithiation, most of the V<sup>3+</sup> ions (≈97%) are reduced to metallic vanadium, ≈0.5% contribute to a ferromagnetic signal and ≈2.2% still contribute to a paramagnetic signal. With this work, we discovered a new material that can be used for high energy/high power devices (as this features capacitive‐like behavior) and most importantly, this work also provides an advanced understanding of polyanion‐type phosphate negative electrode material (whose storage mechanism depends on the guest ion) for monovalent‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0070"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0080" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub>/C</title><p>Mg<sub>3</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>6</sub>/C was synthesized by ball‐milling (Planetary Micro Mill, PULVERISETTE 7, premium line, FRITSCH) and carbon‐thermal reduction method. The electrolytes (150 µL) were 1 <sc>m</sc> LiPF<sub>6</sub> in EC/DMC (1:1 wt%, LP30, BASF), 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in a mixture of ethylene carbonate (EC):propylene carbonate (PC) (1:1 by volume) and 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC), and 1 <sc>m</sc> potassium bis(fluorosulfonyl)imide (KFSI) in a mixture of EC:diethyl carbonate (DEC) (1:1 by volume) for LIBs, SIBs and PIBs, respectively. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) and galvanostatic cycling with potential limitation (GCPL) were performed in the potential window of 3 and 0.01 V. The CV measurement was performed at different scan rates from 0.05 to 2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. All electrochemical measurements (CV and GCPL) were done on a VMP3 multichannel potentiostat (Biologic Science Instrument) with an EC‐lab software for instrument operation and data evaluation.</p></section><section xml:id="advs5274-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">In Operando Synchrotron Diffraction and In Operando X‐Ray Absorption Spectroscopy (XAS)</title><p>In operando synchrotron diffraction was performed at Material Science and Powder Diffraction beamline (MSPD) at ALBA, Spain<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0040"><sup>40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and at PETRA‐III beamline P02.1 at DESY in Hamburg, Germany.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs5274-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The electrochemical cells consist of 2025‐type coin cells with glass windows of 5 mm diameter for beam entrance.
