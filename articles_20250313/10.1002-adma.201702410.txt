However, similar to other 2D materials, MXene nanosheets are inclined to stack together, limiting the device performance. In order to fully utilize MXenes' electrochemical energy storage capability, here, processing of 2D MXene flakes into hollow spheres and 3D architectures via a template method is reported. The MXene hollow spheres are stable and can be easily dispersed in solvents such as water and ethanol, demonstrating their potential applications in environmental and biomedical fields as well. The 3D macroporous MXene films are free‐standing, flexible, and highly conductive due to good contacts between spheres and metallic conductivity of MXenes. When used as anodes for sodium‐ion storage, these 3D MXene films exhibit much improved performances compared to multilayer MXenes and MXene/carbon nanotube hybrid architectures in terms of capacity, rate capability, and cycling stability. This work demonstrates the importance of MXene electrode architecture on the electrochemical performance and can guide future work on designing high‐performance MXene‐based materials for energy storage, catalysis, environmental, and biomedical applications.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en" xml:id="adma201702410-abs-0001"><p><b>Hollow Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> spheres and 3D macroporous MXene films</b> are fabricated using a sacrificial template approach. The 3D MXene films are free‐standing, flexible, and highly conductive. They can serve directly as electrodes for Na‐ion storage and exhibit high capacities accompanied with excellent stabilities and rate performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702410:adma201702410-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702410-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201702410-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201702410-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>A family of 2D transition metal carbides and nitrides (MXenes) has been attracting increasing attention since their discovery in 2011.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0001"/> MXenes are synthesized by selective extraction of the “A” element (such as Al and Ga) from their parent MAX phases. They have a general formula <i>M<sub>n+</sub></i><sub>1</sub><i>X<sub>n</sub>T<sub>x</sub></i>, where <i>M</i> stands for an early transition metal (such as Ti, V, Nb, and Mo), <i>X</i> stands for C and/or N, <i>n</i> = 1, 2, or 3, and <i>T<sub>x</sub></i> represents various surface terminations (OH, O, and/or F groups).[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0001" style="qv">1a</link>] The unique structure and surface chemistry render MXenes a combination of properties such as metallic conductivity, a hydrophilic surface, and good mechanical stability.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0001 #adma201702410-bib-0002" style="qv">1c,2</link>] These superior properties distinguish MXenes from other 2D materials and make them promising candidates for energy storage,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003"/> nanocomposites,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0002 #adma201702410-bib-0004"/> transparent conductor,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0005"/> separation membranes,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0006"/> catalysis,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0007"/> electromagnetic interference shielding,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0008"/> sensors,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0009"/> and biomedical<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0010"/> applications.</p><p>So far, applications of MXenes in electrical energy storage have attracted the most interest.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0001" style="qv">1a,c</link>] MXene‐based electrodes with varied chemistry and structures showed competitive performance in electrochemical capacitors,[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003 #adma201702410-bib-0011" style="qv">3d,11</link>] Li/Na‐ion batteries,[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003 #adma201702410-bib-0012" style="qv">3c,12</link>] and Li–S batteries.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003" style="qv">3e,f</link>] However, similar to other 2D materials, the performance of MXenes is still hindered by their tendency to stack or aggregate, which limits the ionic transport through the electrodes. In order to fully utilize their electrochemical performance, strategies including creating porous structures and introducing interlayer spacers to MXene sheets were proposed. For example, Li‐ion storage capacity was increased by a factor of 4 after creating mesopores in MXene nanosheets, along with an impressive rate performance.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0013"/> Using the ab initio density functional theory calculations, Yu's group demonstrated that the enlarged interlayer distance of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene enables the stable multilayer Na‐ion adsorption and therefore significantly enhances its theoretical capacity.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0014"/> Experimentally, the increase in interlayer spacing of MXenes achieved by the introduction of interlayer spacers, such as nanocarbons,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0015"/> polymers,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0002 #adma201702410-bib-0016"/> metal oxide nanoparticles,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0017"/> and large ions,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0018"/> has led to significantly improved energy storage capability as well.</p><p>Recent reports and reviews have suggested that the integration of 2D materials into 3D macroscopic structures, such as porous films, scaffolds, and networks, could provide a good solution to the issue of poor ionic and electronic transport in electrode materials, thereby resulting in high‐performance devices.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0019"/> In particular, 3D macroporous graphene frameworks have been produced by integrating graphene materials with polymers and self‐assembly, offering significantly enhanced performance in applications in energy, environment, sensing, and biological fields.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0020"/> The same is true for other 2D materials like molybdenum disulfide (MoS<sub>2</sub>).<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0021"/> Among different strategies for the construction of 3D macroporous architectures, the template approach is the most promising and attractive due to its ability to produce well‐defined and shaped architectures.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0022"/></p><p>In this communication, we report on processing 2D transition metal carbides (MXenes) into 3D macroporous frameworks via sacrificial poly(methyl methacrylate) (PMMA) spherical templates. Electrochemical performance of the 3D macroporous MXene frameworks was assessed by their Na‐ion storage capacity, which has been receiving increasing interest as an alternative to the current Li‐ion batteries due to its suitable energy density, low cost, and the abundance of sodium salts in nature.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0023"/></p><p>A schematic of the preparation procedure is depicted in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. To capitalize on the strong interaction between MXene flakes and PMMA spheres, individual hollow MXene flakes were fabricated first. Upon mixing the two solutions, MXene flakes spontaneously wrap the surface of PMMA spheres, driven by the interaction between their surface hydroxyl groups (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"/>a–c). MXene/PMMA spheres can be separated from the solution by centrifugation (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"/>c). By thermal evaporation at 450 °C, PMMA is removed, leading to the formation of self‐supporting, hollow MXene spheres (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"/>d). In order to make a free‐standing film, PMMA/MXene spheres were vacuum‐filtered (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"/>e), followed by thermal evaporation at 450 °C to remove the PMMA. A flexible and conductive 3D macroporous MXene architecture is obtained after that (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"/>f), which is expected to be a promising candidate as a high‐performance electrode in energy storage devices.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702410-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702410:adma201702410-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702410-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic showing the construction of hollow MXene spheres and 3D macroporous MXene frameworks. a–c) Upon mixing MXene with PMMA solution, MXene flakes spontaneously wrap around the surface of PMMA spheres, driven by the interaction between their surface hydroxyl groups and collected by centrifugation. d) Thermal annealing of the MXene/PMMA hybrids leads to the formation of hollow MXene spheres. e) The MXene/PMMA hybrid spheres can be filtered into a free‐standing film, f) which will be transformed into a 3D macroporous MXene film after removing the PMMA by thermal evaporation at 450 °C.</caption></figure><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a,b shows the morphologies of PMMA spheres and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene flakes. The PMMA spheres are of 2–3 µm in diameter with a rough surface. The Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes have a lateral size of several micrometers and a thickness of ≈1 nm. The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0002"/>c shows the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrid spheres with a smoother surface than the PMMA precursors due to the wrapping of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes. Self‐supporting, hollow Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> spheres were obtained after the thermal evaporation of PMMA (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0002"/>d). As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0002"/>e,f, these hollow spheres have good electron transparency, indicating thin walls consisting of few layers of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>. Note that such self‐supporting hollow spheres can be easily obtained from graphene but were reported as difficult to make from 2D MoS<sub>2</sub> flakes due to their inherent rigidity. These hollow MXene spheres can be easily dispersed in solvents, such as water and ethanol, by sonication, which was demonstrated by the images of well‐dispersed hollow spheres obtained on a mica substrate after drying one drop of the corresponding suspension (Figure S1, Supporting Information), indicating their potential use in the environmental, biological, and biomedical applications.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702410-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702410:adma201702410-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702410-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image showing the morphology of the as‐produced PMMA spheres. b) TEM image of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes. SEM image of c) Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrids and d) hollow Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> spheres after the removal of PMMA, showing spherical shape is kept after PMMA's thermal evaporation at 450 °C. TEM images showing e) a hollow Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> sphere and f) its wall MXene layers.</caption></figure><p>As described above in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0001"/>e, the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrid spheres can be assembled into a free‐standing film by a vacuum‐assisted filtration process (Figure S2, Supporting Information). After thermal annealing at 450 °C in Ar, flexible macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> films were obtained (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a). Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>b shows the thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) curves of the PMMA and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrids. It is obvious that PMMA was completely evaporated at temperatures higher than 400 °C. For the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrid film, ≈80% of the weight was lost, indicating that the mass ratio of PMMA: Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> was around 4:1. The small weight jump at ≈400 °C in the TGA curve of the hybrid can be ascribed to the oxidation of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> by its oxygen‐containing functional groups or the decomposition products of PMMA. The cross‐sectional SEM image shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>c reveals that the macroporous film is composed of a large number of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres, forming a 3D framework. The film has a thickness of ≈13 µm, about an order of magnitude larger than the film of a similar weight (≈5 mg) built of compactly stacked Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes (Figure S3, Supporting Information). This results in a decrease in the film packing density from 3.8 g cm<sup>−3</sup> for the compact film to ≈0.4 g cm<sup>−3</sup> for the macroporous film. The electrical conductivity of the macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film was measured to be ≈200 S cm<sup>−1</sup>, which is smaller than the conductivity of the compact Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> films,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0002"/> but much higher than that of a 3D graphene film with a similar structure (≈12 S cm<sup>−1</sup>).[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0022" style="qv">22b</link>]</p><figure xml:id="adma201702410-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702410:adma201702410-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702410-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Digital image showing the flexibility of a 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film. b) TGA curves of pure PMMA spheres and a Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrid film under Ar. c) Cross‐sectional and d) top‐view SEM images of the 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film. Water contact angles of the e) 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film and f) a compact Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film. g) TEM image showing the interfacial structure between two neighboring Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres in the 3D film. Cross‐sectional SEM image of the 3D macroporous h) V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> and i) Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> films. j) Comparison of XRD patterns of a compact Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film and the 3D macroporous MXene films.</caption></figure><p>The stacking of hollow Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> spheres promotes the roughness of the macroporous film surface, leading to a good hydrophobicity with a large water contact angle of 135° due to the lotus effect (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>e).<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0024"/> This distinguishes the surface property of the macroporous films as compared to the compact ones, which usually exhibit a water contact angle of ≈35° (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>f). The interfacial structure between neighboring Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres is shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>g. A good contact between spheres is evident therein, which is probably contributed by the van der Waals interactions and hydrogen bonds between neighboring 2D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes. This is crucial for the construction of robust and conductive 3D frameworks. Note that hollow MXene spheres are tunable in size and their wall thickness. For example, the wall thickness of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres and flexibility of the 3D macroporous films can be easily tuned by the mass ratio of the PMMA to Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>. A smaller PMMA to Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> ratio leads to thicker walls and improved film strength and flexibility, as shown in Figure S4 (Supporting Information). If thinner MXene spheres are desired, the film flexibility can be maintained up to the PMMA:Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> mass ratio of 10:1. Additionally, the size of hollow MXene spheres can be tuned by the size of PMMA spheres used. For example, a film composed of ≈200 nm hollow MXene spheres is shown in Figure S5 (Supporting Information).</p><p>To demonstrate the generality of the template approach to produce 3D MXene architectures, macroporous films of V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> were prepared. Both of these films exhibit similar stacking structure of hollow MXene spheres and good flexibility (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>h,i). Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0003"/>j shows the comparison of X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns for a compact Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film and the as‐fabricated 3D macroporous films of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>. Intrinsic (002) peaks for MXenes are observed for all of these films. The compact and 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> films show a similar <i>c</i>‐lattice parameter (<i>c</i>‐LP) of ≈27.3 Å, with their (002) peaks located at ≈6.47°. However, a larger full width at half maximum of the microporous films and the disappearance of higher order of (00<i>l</i>) peaks for MXenes, such as (004) and (006) peaks, can be attributed to the bending of the flakes due to the introduction of the macroporous architecture and their random orientation in the film.<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0013"/> The absence of peaks for transition metal oxides in the patterns of macroporous films demonstrates a good retention of the MXene flakes after annealing with no oxidation. This is confirmed by the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of the annealed Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes and the corresponding selected area electron diffraction pattern (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The 3D Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film shows a <i>c</i>‐LP of 22.8 Å. The (002) peak for 3D V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film splits into two peaks, which show <i>c</i>‐LPs of 40.2 and 32.4 Å, respectively. The exact reason for larger interlayer spacing and two (002) peaks in the 3D V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film is not clear at the moment. It can possibly be explained by the presence of adsorbed water or carbonized residues of organic molecules in the film, due to a different delamination method that was used for V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> (see Supporting Information).[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0006 #adma201702410-bib-0025" style="qv">6b,25</link>]</p><p>The combination of good conductivity and macroporous structure of the 3D macroporous MXene films makes them promising candidates for Na‐ion storage and other electrochemical applications. Also, it is important to note that the MXenes exhibit a pseudocapacitive Na‐ion storage mechanism, which renders them a higher Na‐ion capacity than double‐layer electrodes and a higher rate capability relative to bulk Faradic electrodes.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003 #adma201702410-bib-0026" style="qv">3c,26</link>] Herein, the free‐standing and binder‐free films were directly used as anodes without any current collectors and their electrochemical performance was tested in a two‐electrode cell with Na foil as the counter and reference electrode. The cyclic voltammetry (CV) profiles of the 3D MXene films are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a and Figure S7 (Supporting Information). A pair of cathodic/anodic peaks located at around 0.8/1.4 V versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup>, corresponding to the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction from the electrodes, are observed (Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"/>a). Their broadness indicates a nondiffusion limited and pseudocapacitive mechanism of Na‐ion storage at the surface of MXene flakes, which is in good agreement with previous studies on MXenes.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003 #adma201702410-bib-0012" style="qv">3c,12c</link>] For all three 3D MXene film electrodes, irreversible cathodic peaks are observed in the first cycle of CVs, which can be attributed to the formation of a solid electrolyte interface as well as the irreversible reaction of Na ions with the surface functional groups (F, OH, and O) and/or water molecules confined between MXene flakes (Figure S7, Supporting Information).[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003 #adma201702410-bib-0012 #adma201702410-bib-0027" style="qv">3c,12c,27</link>] Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"/>b compares the rate performance of the three different 3D MXene films. At 0.25 C, the Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film achieved a reversible capacity of ≈370 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, compared with ≈330 and 340 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> and V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film electrodes, respectively. All these films exhibited excellent capacity retention with increasing current rates. At a high current rate of 25 C, the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> retained a capacity of 120, 125, and 170 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. It was noticed that the V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> exhibited the best rate performance among the three 3D films, which is ascribed to its largest interlayer spacing. The charge–discharge curves at different current rates for the 3D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film electrode are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"/>c. At 0.25 C, the presence of charge–discharge plateaus at 0.8/1.4 V corresponds well with the broad redox peaks in CV curves shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"/>a. These features disappear at higher current rates, which confirms the pseudocapacitive Na‐ion storage mechanism in MXenes.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003" style="qv">3c</link>] Similar phenomena are also observed for 3D V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film electrodes (Figure S7, Supporting Information), making them promising for Na‐ion capacitor applications.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702410-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702410:adma201702410-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702410-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV curves at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and b) rate profiles of the 3D macroporous MXene film electrodes for Na‐ion storage. c) Charge–discharge curves of 3D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film electrode at different current rates. d) Comparison of the capacity achieved by the 3D macroporous MXene film electrodes with other reported materials. e) Long cycling performance of the 3D macroporous MXene film electrodes at 2.5 C.</caption></figure><p>Comparison of the Na‐ion storage capability of the 3D macroporous MXene films with other reported materials is shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"/>d. These 3D MXene films showed better gravimetric performance than many other reported materials, including banana peels‐derived carbon materials,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0028"/> porous carbon nanofibers,<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0029"/> MoS<sub>2</sub>/graphene papers,[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0023" style="qv">23c</link>] etc. At low current densities, the 3D MXene films showed capacities comparable with the banana peels‐derived carbon materials. However, they outperformed all other materials in the rate performance due to their excellent electrical conductivity and macroporous structure. In particular, much better Na‐ion gravimetric storage capability is achieved by the 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> films in comparison to multilayer Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> particles[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003" style="qv">3c</link>] and porous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> /carbon nanotube (CNT) hybrid films,[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0012" style="qv">12c</link>] revealing the advantage of the 3D macroporous architecture. While the macroporous structure will lead to a somewhat lower volumetric capacity, elimination of binder, conductive additives, and metal current collector will, at least partially, compensate for it.</p><p>Figure <link href="#adma201702410-fig-0004"/>e shows the 1000‐cycle stability of the 3D macroporous MXene film electrodes at 2.5 C. The initial discharge capacities for Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> were ≈390, 470, and 400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The reversible capacities retained by these film electrodes were ≈210, 260, and 210 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, with corresponding first‐cycle Coulombic efficiencies as 53.8%, 55.3%, and 51.2%, respectively, which were higher than the previously reported Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/CNT film electrode (≈40%).[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0012" style="qv">12c</link>] As discussed above, the initial irreversible capacities were mainly attributed to the irreversible reaction between MXene flakes and the electrolyte.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0003 #adma201702410-bib-0012 #adma201702410-bib-0027" style="qv">3c,12c,27</link>] Some improvement in Coulombic efficiency here can be attributed to the partial removal of MXene surface functional groups by thermal annealing during the manufacturing process. Further improvement can be achieved by optimizing the surface chemistry of MXenes. The 3D Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> and V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film electrodes exhibited a gradual increase in capacity during the first 300 cycles, while the capacity for Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> electrode kept increasing until after 700 cycles. This can be ascribed to the improved electrolyte accessibility to active sites caused by increased interlayer spacing during cycling.[<link href="#adma201702410-bib-0012 #adma201702410-bib-0013" style="qv">12c,13</link>] The V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film showed the best stability, probably due to its largest initial interlayer spacing. All 3D MXene films were stable during the entire 1000 cycles, with a Coulombic efficiency close to 100% and no obvious changes in their pseudocapacitive Na‐ion storage mechanism (Figure S8, Supporting Information). After 1000 cycles, the capacity values retained by Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> were ≈295, 310, and 290 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.</p><p>In summary, 2D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene flakes were self‐assembled onto polymer surface and hollow spheres and 3D macroporous architectures were made via a sacrificial template approach. The Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres were stable and could be easily dispersed in solvents such as water and ethanol, suggesting their potential applications in environmental and biomedical fields. The as‐fabricated 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> films were free‐standing, flexible, and highly conductive due to the good contacts between spheres and metallic conductivity of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>. 3D macroporous MXene films with other compositions, such as V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, were also successfully fabricated. These 3D MXene films were directly used as anodes for Na‐ion storage; no current collectors or binders were needed. At a low charging rate of 0.25 C, reversible capacities of ≈330, 340, and 370 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> were obtained for 3D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> film electrodes, respectively, along with outstanding rate performance and long cycling stability. After 1000 cycles, reversible capacities of ≈295, 310, and 290 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.5 C were retained by 3D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> films, respectively. These values were significantly better than the values obtained on multilayer MXenes and MXene/CNT hybrid open structures. This work demonstrates the importance of MXene electrode architecture on the electrochemical performance and can guide future work on designing high‐performance MXene‐based materials for energy storage, catalysis, environmental, and biomedical applications.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201702410-sec-0020"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Fabrication of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> Hollow Spheres</i>: To obtain the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres, A Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> colloidal solution (Supporting Information) (2 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) and the PMMA sphere dispersion (10 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) were directly mixed together and kept under stirring for 10 min. The mass ratio of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> to PMMA spheres was controlled at 1: 10. The mixture was then centrifuged at 3500 rpm for 10 min and the solid residue was washed with distilled water and centrifuged at 3500 rpm for another 10 min. After that, the solid precipitate was collected and dried at 60 °C under vacuum for 10 h to obtain Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrid spheres. These hybrid spheres were then annealed at 450 °C under flowing argon for 1 h to remove the PMMA, leaving Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> hollow spheres.</p><p><i>Manufacturing of 3D Macroporous MXene Films</i>: To obtain the 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film, the mass ratio of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> to PMMA spheres was controlled at 1: 4. The mixture was then sonicated for 10 min to ensure a uniform dispersion of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes and PMMA spheres in the solution. After that, the mixture was filtered through a polypropylene membrane (3501 Coated PP, Celgard LLC, Charlotte, NC) to yield a film. The latter was dried in air at room temperature for 10 min and peeled off from the polypropylene membrane, yielding a flexible freestanding Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>/PMMA hybrid film. The composite film was annealed at 450 °C for 1 h under flowing argon to remove PMMA, leaving a macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> film. The film thickness was controlled by varying the amount of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> flakes used. The 3D macroporous Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> films with different Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> to PMMA mass ratios, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> films were prepared by a similar process.</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurements</i>: To test the sodium‐ion storage properties, the as‐prepared 3D macroporous MXene films were cut into self‐supported electrodes. CR2032‐type coin cells were assembled in an argon‐filled glove box using the macroporous MXene film electrode as the working electrode and Na foil as the reference and counter electrode, which were electronically separated by a glass microfiber (Whatman) saturated with an electrolyte. The loading of MXene film electrodes was 0.5–1.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The electrolyte solution was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> dissolved in a mixture of ethylene carbonate and propylene carbonate with a volume ratio of 1:1, in which 5 vol% fluoroethylene carbonate was added as electrolyte additive. The charge–discharge measurements were performed at different current densities (1C = 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) in the voltage range from 0.01 to 3.0 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na using an Arbin system (Arbin BT‐2143‐11U, College Station, TX, USA). CV measurements were conducted by using a VMP3 potentiostat (Biologic, France) and measured between 0.01 and 3.0 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>Other information on the synthesis and delamination of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, V<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and Mo<sub>2</sub>CT<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXenes, synthesis of PMMA spheres, and characterization techniques can be found in the Supporting Information.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201702410-sec-0030" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>M.Q.Z. and X.X. contributed equally to this work.
