type="main">Abstract</title><p>In this paper, the synthesis of ultrasmall Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles (≈3.8 nm) delicately embedded in porous N‐doped carbon nanofibers (denoted as Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C) by electrospinning is reported. The as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C fiber film tightly adherent on aluminum foil features great flexibility and is directly used as binder‐free cathode for sodium‐ion batteries, exhibiting admirable electrochemical performance with high reversible capacity (117.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C), outstanding rate capability (46.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C), and unprecedentedly high cyclic stability (85% capacity retention after 2000 cycles). The reaction kinetics and mechanism are explored by a combination study of cyclic voltammetry, ex situ structure/valence analyses, and first‐principles computations, revealing the highly reversible phase transformation of Na<sub>2</sub>Fe<sup>II</sup>PO<sub>4</sub>F ↔ NaFe<sup>III</sup>PO<sub>4</sub>F, the facilitated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion dynamics with low energy barriers, and the desirable pseudocapacitive behavior for fast charge storage. Pouch‐type Na‐ion full batteries are also assembled employing the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers cathode and the carbon nanofibers anode, demonstrating a promising energy density of 135.8 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and a high capacity retention of 84.5% over 200 cycles. The distinctive network architecture of ultrafine active materials encapsulated into interlinked carbon nanofibers offers an ideal platform for enhancing the electrochemical reactivity, electronic/ionic transmittability, and structural stability of Na‐storage electrodes.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs1124-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Ultrasmall Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanograins (≈3.8 nm) uniformly embedded in porous N‐doped carbon nanofibers</b> are synthesized by electrospinning, and the obtained Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C fiber membrane tightly adhered to aluminum foil is directly adopted as binder‐free cathode for sodium‐ion batteries. demonstrated, enabling admirable electrochemical performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1124:advs1124-gra-0001" role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have aroused tremendous attention and emerged as the promising candidate for large‐scale electric energy storage owing to their advantages of abundant Na resources, low cost, feasibility of using aluminum as both cathode and anode current collectors, and similar working principle to that of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs).<link href="#advs1124-bib-0001 #advs1124-bib-0002 #advs1124-bib-0003 #advs1124-bib-0004"/> Nevertheless, inert reaction kinetics and low power/energy density resulted from the relatively large radius and heavy mass of Na<sup>+</sup> ion are the major obstacles that hinder the widespread applications of SIBs.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0005 #advs1124-bib-0006 #advs1124-bib-0007"/> Therefore, it is imperative to develop reliable Na‐storage electrodes with superior rate capability and long‐term cycle life,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0008 #advs1124-bib-0009 #advs1124-bib-0010"/> especially cathode materials which are more difficult for sodium‐ion transport.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0011 #advs1124-bib-0012 #advs1124-bib-0013"/> In research to date, there are four main types of cathode materials suitable for SIBs, namely, transition metal oxides,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0014 #advs1124-bib-0015 #advs1124-bib-0016"/> polyanionic compounds,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0017 #advs1124-bib-0018 #advs1124-bib-0019 #advs1124-bib-0020"/> Prussian blue analogues,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0021 #advs1124-bib-0022 #advs1124-bib-0023"/> and organic sodium salts.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0024 #advs1124-bib-0025"/> Among them, the layered iron‐based fluorophosphate of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F is particularly attractive by virtue of its facile 2D Na<sup>+</sup> pathways formed by interconnected PO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra and FeO<sub>4</sub>F<sub>2</sub> octahedra, great structural stability with small volume variation (only 3.7%) during sodiation/desodiation, huge availability of iron ore reserves, satisfactory theoretical capacity (124 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), and decent operating voltage (≈3.0 V vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) induced by polyanion effect.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0026 #advs1124-bib-0027 #advs1124-bib-0028"/> Unfortunately, the primary two challenges facing Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F lie in the low electronic conductivity correlated with poor rate property and the undesirable actual capacity caused by inferior active materials utilization (especially for bulk materials).<link href="#advs1124-bib-0029"/> Hence, it is urgently necessary to boost the reaction kinetics and activity of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F cathode toward improved electrochemical performance.</p><p>At present, considerable efforts have been devoted to fabricating the carbon‐coated nanostructured Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F aiming at solving the abovementioned issues. In 2007, Nazar's group first proposed that the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles (≈200 nm) with a carbon coating layer of 2–4 nm were electrochemically active in SIBs.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0030"/> Thereafter, a Na‐storage capacity of around 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> was obtained for nano‐Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F by Tarascon and co‐workers.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0031"/> Wang's group then synthesized the carbon‐coated porous hollow Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F spheres (diameter of 500 nm, wall thickness of 80 nm) and extended the cycle life to 750 cycles with a capacity retention of 80%.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0032"/> Dunn and co‐workers further reduced the size of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparicles (enwrapped by graphene) to 15–25 nm, leading to rapid Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics and thus a respectable rate capability of 60 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at 10 C.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0027"/> Through very encouraging findings, more decreased particle size (&lt;5 nm) and more uniform carbon coating with regard to this material are highly anticipated to simultaneously realize the high‐rate performance and long lifespan. On the other hand, although Liu's and Yang's teams pioneeringly probed the structural evolution of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F upon Na‐ion insertion/extraction using in operando X‐ray diffraction (XRD), and discovered a single‐electron phase transition of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F ↔ NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F with the intermediate phase of Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0028 #advs1124-bib-0033"/> the thorough understanding of Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation sites and migration paths in the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F crystal, as well as the relevant reaction dynamics, has not yet been well established.</p><p>Herein, ultrasmall Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles (≈3.8 nm) homogeneously embedded in porous nitrogen‐doped carbon nanofibers (labeled as Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C) are successfully prepared through electrospinning and subsequent annealing processes. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on such a distinctive nanostructure tailored for Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. The as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C fiber membrane is firmly adhered to aluminum foil and directly used as a binder‐free cathode for SIBs, enabling both high rate performance (117.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C in comparison of 46.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C) and unprecedentedly high cycling stability (85% capacity retention over 2000 cycles). By means of electrochemical analyses, ex situ structure/valence detections, and first‐principles computations, the highly reversible phase transformation, and the facilitated reaction kinetics concerning fast ionic diffusion, proper pseudocapacitance contribution, and low activation energy of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C cathode are demonstrated. In addition, soft package Na‐ion full batteries are rationally constructed by pairing the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers cathode with the pure carbon nanofibers anode, showing great application prospects with high energy density and long cycle life.</p></section><section xml:id="advs1124-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs1124-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a schematically illustrates the preparation process for the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers. Briefly, an aqueous solution of polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP), Fe(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub>, NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, and NaF was first electrospun to be smooth and continuous nanofibers (Figure S1, Supporting Information) adherent on aluminum foil. Citric acid was used to protect the Fe<sup>2+</sup> from being oxidized. Thereafter, the as‐spun fibers underwent a thermal treatment in N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere during which PVP was carbonized and the metal salts decomposed and then chemically combined to form the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. It is worth noticing that porosity due to the released gases and nitrogen‐doping owing to the N‐rich character of PVP were introduced to the generated carbon nanofibers during the calcination process.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0034"/> The obtained Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers weaved into a flexible membrane tightly attached onto Al foil, which can be directly adopted as binder‐free cathode for SIBs without the mechanical milling or slurry casting procedure in traditional battery processing. Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0001"/>b shows the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of the as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C, revealing its uniform nanofiber morphology with an average diameter of 130 nm. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image in Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0001"/>c further indicates that all of the ultrasmall Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanograins (mean size of 3.8 nm) are homogeneously embedded in the carbon nanofibers (high‐magnification TEM image is provided in Figure S2, Supporting Information), the clear lattice fringes with a <i>d</i>‐spacing of 0.34 nm in the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image can be well assigned to the (122) plane of orthorhombic Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. As far as we know, the achieved Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles are the smallest ones ever reported. Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0001"/>d displays the SEM energy‐dispersive spectrometer (EDS) mapping images of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers, demonstrating uniform distributions of Na, Fe, P, O, F, C, and N elements along the fibrous outline.</p><figure xml:id="advs1124-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1124:advs1124-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs1124-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic fabrication process of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers. b) SEM, c) TEM (insets are the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F particle size distribution diagram and HRTEM image), and d) SEM EDS element mapping images of the as‐synthesized Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C.</caption></figure><p>Indeed, the calcination temperature and time play crucial roles in building the ideal Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C morphology and microstructure. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs1124-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a, the sample annealed at 600 °C for 6 h features XRD peaks in great coincidence with the standard orthorhombic Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F structure (Pbcn space group, JCPDS No. 72‐1829).<link href="#advs1124-bib-0027 #advs1124-bib-0030 #advs1124-bib-0031 #advs1124-bib-0032 #advs1124-bib-0035"/> The Le Bail refinement of the XRD pattern (Figure S3, Supporting Information) indicates that the cell parameters are <i>a</i> = 5.2354 Å, <i>b</i> = 13.8365 Å, <i>c</i> = 11.7734 Å, and <i>V</i> = 852.858 Å<sup>3</sup>, respectively, also in good accordance with the previous reports regarding Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0029 #advs1124-bib-0030 #advs1124-bib-0031"/> However, the target phase cannot be well formed under a low temperature (500 °C) or a short heating time (3 h), a high temperature (700 °C) or a long sintering time (9 h) results in larger Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles that are not totally confined in the carbon nanofibers (Figures S4 and S5, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0002"/>b presents the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherm of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C, the typical type‐IV behavior with an obvious hysteresis loop ranging from 0.4 to 1.0 (<i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub>) suggests the existence of mesopores among the nanofibers,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0034 #advs1124-bib-0036"/> and the pore diameters are mainly distributed around 4.2 nm based on the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) model. Moreover, the specific surface area is measured to be 112.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> according to the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method. To gain insight into the chemical content and valence of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed. From the survey spectrum (Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0002"/>c), no impurity signals are detected, and the atomic ratio of Na/Fe/P/O/F is approximately the stoichiometric composition of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, this is indicative of high purity of the product. The doped nitrogen content is estimated to be 1.6 wt%. The high‐resolution Fe 2p spectrum with the Fe 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Fe 2p<sub>1/2</sub> doublets located at 711.2 and 724.6 eV, respectively (Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0002"/>d), is characteristic of Fe<sup>2+</sup>,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0028 #advs1124-bib-0034"/> agreeing well with the bivalence state of iron in Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0002"/>e displays the N 1s spectrum, three deconvoluted peaks centered at 398.3, 399.7, and 400.8 eV correspond to the pyridinic N, pyrrolic N, and graphitic N, respectively,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0037 #advs1124-bib-0038"/> their content percentages are in sequence, 31.45, 35.23, and 33.32%, based on the area percentages of the corresponding fitted peaks. The formation of the CN (pyridinic) and CN (pyrrolic) bonds is further confirmed by the C 1s spectrum in Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0002"/>f. Noticeably, nitrogen doping can synchronously contribute electrons and create defects to the carbon lattice,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0037 #advs1124-bib-0039"/> thus effectively facilitating the electronic/ionic transportation.</p><figure xml:id="advs1124-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1124:advs1124-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs1124-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of the samples under different annealing conditions. b) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherm of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers (inset is the pore size distribution curve). c) Survey XPS spectrum, and d–f) high‐resolution Fe 2p, N 1s, and C 1s XPS spectra of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C.</caption></figure><p>For the evaluation of the electrochemical properties, the as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C fiber membrane adherent on Al current collector was cut into suitable size and directly assembled into Na‐ion half cells as binder‐free working electrode. As can be concluded, the homogeneous encapsulation of ultrasmall Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles into carbon nanofibers could simultaneously boost the electrons transfer and sodium ions diffusion. The tailored ultrasmall Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F nanoparticles exposing abundant active sites favor the easy access to Na<sup>+</sup> ions and the sufficient utilization for Na‐storage, the construction of a robust 3D network by nanofibers benefits the electronic/ionic mobility and the architecture steadiness, and the confinement of active materials into porous carbon matrix effectively alleviates the side reactions with electrolyte,<link href="#advs1124-bib-0036 #advs1124-bib-0037 #advs1124-bib-0039"/> thus rendering the outstanding electrochemical performance in terms of high reversible capacity, exceptional rate capability, and distinguished cyclic stability.</p><p>The electrode process kinetics of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C is then investigated to explain its fascinating Na‐storage performance. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs1124-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a presents the CV profiles of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C recorded at various sweep rates from 0.1 to 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, as the scan rate increases, the redox peak currents increase prominently, while the potential intervals between the oxidation (O1 and O2) and reduction (R1 and R2) peaks increase slightly, this implies the minor polarization of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C cathode. The Na‐ion diffusivity can be determined according to the Randles–Sevcik equation of <i>i</i><sub>p</sub> = 2.69 × 10<sup>5</sup><i>n</i><sup>3/2</sup><i>AD</i><sub>Na</sub><sup>1/2</sup><i>C</i><sub>Na</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>, where <i>i</i><sub>p</sub> (A) is the peak current, <i>n</i> is the electron transfer number per molecule (<i>n</i> = 1 in this system), <i>A</i> (cm<sup>2</sup>) is the contacting area between electrode and electrolyte, <i>C</i><sub>Na</sub> is the Na ions concentration in the electrode (≈3.8 × 10<sup>−3</sup> mol cm<sup>−3</sup>), and <i>v</i> (V s<sup>−1</sup>) is the scan rate.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0034 #advs1124-bib-0043"/> Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0004"/>b plots the fitting curves between the peak currents and the square root of scan rates, as calculated, the <i>D</i><sub>Na</sub> values for the O1, O2, R1, and R2 peaks are 7.18 × 10<sup>−11</sup>, 4.69 × 10<sup>−11</sup>, 4.36 × 10<sup>−11</sup>, and 8.21 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The smaller <i>D</i><sub>Na</sub> of the O2/R2 redox couple suggests that the reaction at high potential needs more activation energy. Notably, the achieved Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C compares favorably with those of other reported Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F‐based cathodes in Na‐ion batteries (mostly at magnitude of ≈10<sup>−12</sup>–10<sup>−15</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#advs1124-bib-0027 #advs1124-bib-0028 #advs1124-bib-0041"/> This is responsible for the high rate performance. Additionally, considering that the <i>D</i><sub>Na</sub> obtained based on CV data is a little larger than that determined from the EIS result, the pseudocapacitive behavior in the electrode should be further identified. The capacitance contribution of battery chemistry can be estimated according to the relation of <i>i</i><sub>p</sub> = <i>av<sup>b</sup></i> or log<i>i</i><sub>p</sub> = <i>b</i> × log<i>v</i> + log<i>a</i>, of which <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are variable parameters. A <i>b</i> value of 0.5 indicates a diffusion‐dominated process, whereas a <i>b</i> value of 1.0 signifies a surface‐controlled capacitive process.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0044 #advs1124-bib-0045"/> As calculated from the slopes of log<i>i</i><sub>p</sub> versus log<i>v</i> linear relationships (Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0004"/>c), the <i>b</i> values for the O1, O2, R1, and R2 peaks are 0.62, 0.59, 0.63, and 0.56, respectively. This means that a combination of the diffusion‐limited and capacitive behaviors synergistically contributes to the charge storage process, enabling the fast reaction kinetics. Furthermore, the capacitance response in the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C electrode process can be quantified by dividing the current (<i>i</i>) at a fixed potential (<i>V</i>) into capacitive effect (<i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i>) and diffusion‐controlled intercalation (<i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>) using the formula of <i>i</i>(V) = <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> + <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>.<link href="#advs1124-bib-0045 #advs1124-bib-0046"/> Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0004"/>d shows the representative CV profile scanned at 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> with around 34.6% of the total current deriving from the pseudocapacitance contribution (cyan area). As expected, the capacitive effect gradually strengthens when increasing the sweep rate (Figure <link href="#advs1124-fig-0004"/>e), and the desirable capacitive behavior is originated from the favorable heterostructure incorporating nanoscale particles, carbon coating, and porosity.</p><figure xml:id="advs1124-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs1124:advs1124-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs1124-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) CV profiles at different scan rates ranging from 0.1 to 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, b) fitting curves between the peak currents (<i>i</i><sub>p</sub>) and the square root of scan rates (<i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>), and c) log<i>i</i><sub>p</sub> versus log<i>v</i> linear relationships of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C cathode. d) CV profile at 0.5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> illustrating the pseudocapacitance contribution (cyan region) to the total current, along with e) percentages of the capacitive response and the diffusion‐controlled behaviour in the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C electrode process at various scan rates.</caption></figure><p>To better understand the Na‐storage mechanism of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C cathode, ex situ XPS and XRD were carried out. In addition, the intensities of the diffraction peaks generally weaken when charging and strengthen when discharging, reflecting the reversible phase transitions. To deeply probe the Na<sup>+</sup> migration mechanism in the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F crystal, first‐principles computations based on the density functional theory (DFT) were performed. The Na‐storage behavior of the carbon anode in the potential range of 0.01–2.0 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na is provided in Figure S15 (Supporting Information), showing a stable reversible capacity of ≈205 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for 200 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C fiber membrane tightly adhered to aluminum foil featured great flexibility and served directly as a binder‐free cathode for Na‐ion batteries, showing the advantages of enhanced electrochemical activity, accelerated electrons/ions transportation, and reinforced structural stability. Consequently, intriguing Na‐storage performance of high reversible capacity (117.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C), superior rate capability (46.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C), and unprecedentedly long cycling life (85% capacity retention over 2000 cycles) was exhibited. In addition, the reaction kinetics and mechanism of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C cathode were systematically investigated by techniques of CV study, ex situ XPS, XRD, and first‐principles computations, demonstrating the highly reversible phase transition of Na<sub>2</sub>Fe<sup>II</sup>PO<sub>4</sub>F ↔ NaFe<sup>III</sup>PO<sub>4</sub>F with facilitated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion dynamics and low ion‐migration energy barriers, the pseudocapacitive behavior is also identified to benefit the high‐rate performance. More significantly, pouch‐type sodium‐ion full batteries were constructed by coupling the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers cathode with the carbon nanofibers anode, delivering a promising energy density of 135.8 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> along with a high durability of 84.5% capacity retention after 200 cycles. To our knowledge, such distinguished overall performance has scarcely been achieved for the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F‐based Na‐storage cathode materials and is also prominent among all the reported SIB cathodes to date. The controllable/green fabricating approach, the fascinating electrochemical performance, and the deep understanding of electrode process illuminate bright prospects for the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers as practicable SIB cathode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="advs1124-sec-0040"><label role="interim">4</label><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Materials Synthesis</i>: The Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers were fabricated via a simple electrospinning method followed by a two‐step heat treatment. In a typical preparation, 2.1 g PVP (average <i>M</i><sub>w</sub> = 1 300 000, Aladdin) was first dissolved in 15 mL deionized water under magnetic stirring at room temperature. Then, stoichiometric quantities (4 mmol) of citric acid (99.5%, Aladdin), Fe(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub> (97%, J&amp;K), NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> (99.5%, Aladdin), and NaF (99.99%, Macklin) were added to the above solution in order, and vigorously stirred at room temperature for another 2 h to form an uniform precursor spinning solution.</p><p>The obtained solution was transferred into a 10 mL plastic syringe furnished with a 21‐gauge needle. An aluminum foil as the collector was used to gather the fibers. The electrostatic voltage applied between the collector and the needle was 17 kV, and the distance was set to be 15 cm. The flowing rate of fluid was 10 µL min<sup>−1</sup> controlled by a syringe pump. After continuous electrospinning at 40 °C for 36 h, fiber membrane with a thickness of about 100–120 µm can be achieved.</p><p>The as‐spun fiber film tightly adhered to aluminum foil was first stabilized at 300 °C for 1 h and then thermally treated at 600 °C for 6 h under N<sub>2</sub> environment to get the target NaFe<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers. The temperature ramping rate was 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. For comparison, counterpart samples with lower or higher Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F content (designated as L‐Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C or H‐Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C), and N‐doped carbon nanofibers without Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F by adjusting the dosage of citric acid/Fe(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub>/NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>/NaF to 3, 5, or 0 mmol, respectively (the other conditions were kept constant) were also prepared. In addition, the annealing temperature (500 or 700 °C) and time (3 or 9 h) to optimize the synthesis conditions were modulated.</p><p><i>Materials Characterizations</i>: The crystal structure and phase purity of the as‐prepared samples were analyzed by XRD (Rigaku D/Max‐2500) using Cu Kα radiation (λ = 1.5418 Å) in the 2θ range of 10°–70°. The morphology and microstructure were investigated by SEM (JEOL JSM7500F) and TEM (TECNAI G2 20). Thermogravimetric analysis‐differential scanning calorimetry (TG‐DSC) analyzer (STA 449 F3) was used to determine the carbon content of composites (heating from room temperature to 650 °C in air). Raman spectra were obtained on a confocal Raman spectrometer (ThermoFisher Scientific) using argon‐ion laser excitation at 532 nm. XPS was performed on a PHI 1600 ESCA spectrometer (Perkin‐Elmer). N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption measurement was carried out with an ASAP 2460 instrument (MicroActive) at 77 K.</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurements</i>: The electrochemical properties were evaluated with CR2032 coin cells assembled in a glove box (Mikrouna Universal 2440/750) filled with ultrapure argon. The as‐synthesized Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C film attached on Al foil was cut into a suitable shape and directly applied as working electrode without using conductive additives or binder. The electrode was dried overnight at 80 °C in vacuum and the mass loading density was ≈2.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. Na metal foil was used as the counter/reference electrode and glass microfiber was adopted as the separator. The electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate (PC) solution with addition of 5 vol% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC). Galvanostatic charge/discharge tests were conducted on a LAND battery tester (CT2001A) in the potential scope of 2.0–4.0 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) at different rates. CV profiles at diverse scanning speeds (0.1–2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) between 2.0 and 4.0 V were recorded on a CHI660C electrochemical workstation (Chenhua, Shanghai), and EIS were collected at open‐circuit voltage (OCV) in the frequency range of 100 kHz to 100 mHz with the AC disturbance signal of 5.0 mV (the electrodes for EIS measurements were mass normalized). In order to inspect the cycled active materials by XRD, SEM, TEM, and XPS, the electrodes were taken out from cells in a glove box and then repeatedly rinsed with dimethyl carbonate (DMC) solution to remove the residual electrolyte.</p><p>For sodium‐ion full batteries, the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F@C nanofibers cathode was paired with the pure carbon nanofibers anode. The electrochemical performance was examined within the voltage window of 1.0–3.3 V. To balance the capacity, the active mass ratio of anode to cathode was set to be 1:1.8. Furthermore, the specific capacity and current density of full batteries were normalized to the mass of cathode active materials.</p><p><i>Computational Details</i>: Ab initio simulations based on DFT were performed by Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) using projector augmented wave (PAW) method with Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) function. The energy cutoff was set to 500 eV and the (4 × 1 × 2) k‐point meshes were used for Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. The convergence tolerance for the force and energy during relaxation were 0.01 eV Å<sup>−1</sup> and 10<sup>−5</sup> eV, respectively.
