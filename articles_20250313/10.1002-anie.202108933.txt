However, inherent rigid nature of the TMO<sub>6</sub> (TM: transition metals) subunits in the layered materials makes it hardly tolerate the inner strains induced by lattice glide, especially at high voltage. Herein, P2‐Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.13</sub>[Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.07</sub>□<sub>0.13</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (□: TM vacancy) is designed that contains vacancies in TM sites, and Mg ions in both TM and sodium sites. Vacancies make the rigid TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron become more asymmetric and flexible. Low valence Co<sup>2+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> redox couple stabilizes the electronic structure, especially at the charged state. Mg<sup>2+</sup> in sodium sites can tune the interlayer spacing against O‐O electrostatic repulsion. Time‐resolved in situ X‐ray diffraction confirms that irreversible structure evolution is effectively suppressed during deep desodiation benefiting from the specific configuration. X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) and density functional theory (DFT) calculations demonstrate that, deriving from the intrinsic vacancies, multiple local configurations of “□‐O‐□”, “Na‐O‐□”, “Mg‐O‐□” are superior in facilitating the oxygen redox for charge compensation than previously reported “Na‐O‐Mg”. The resulted material delivers promising cycle stability and rate capability, with a long voltage plateau at 4.2 V contributed by oxygen, and can be well maintained even at high rates. The strategy will inspire new ideas in designing highly stable cathode materials with reversible anionic redox for sodium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A P2‐Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.13</sub>[Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.07</sub>□<sub>0.13</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (□: transition metal (TM) vacancy) cathode material is designed that contains vacancies and Co<sup>2+</sup> in TM sites, and Mg<sup>2+</sup> in both TM and Na sites. The synergic effect of low‐valence Co<sup>2+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> redox and Mg<sup>2+</sup> in sodium sites stabilizes the structure of this cathode material significantly during cycling.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108933:anie202108933-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108933-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202108933-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable batteries with layer‐structured cathodes have gained great success in electric vehicles and electronic devices.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Among the high energy density battery technologies, oxygen redox based electrochemistry has begun to achieve crucial attractions, which sufficiently utilized the oxidation/reduction process of O<sup>2−</sup>/O<sup>−</sup>.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0002"><sup>[2]</sup></link> The generated extra charge compensation from triggering oxygen redox significantly increases the specific capacity and energy density of the layered cathode materials beyond cationic redox of transition metals.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0003"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Oxygen redox in layered cathodes is closely related to the energy of the TM (<i>n</i>)<i>d</i> bands and the O 2p bands. When electron holes are generated in the narrow nonbonding O 2<i>p</i> orbits (highly localized states) rather than in the TM‐O anti‐bonding states, the oxygen redox is activated.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0004"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, in most of the A<sub>1+<i>x</i></sub>TM<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (A: alkali metals) oxides, where TM ions are partly substituted by A ions, cation migration and O<sub>2</sub> release will happen during charge and discharge processes. Thus, most of these oxides suffer from structure instability and poor kinetics originated from oxygen redox, leading to irreversible capacity loss and voltage drop in the subsequent cycles, which severely hinder further development of this kind of cathode materials.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0009"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Thus, much efforts have been tried to obtain intercalated oxides with stable and reversible oxygen redox.</p><p>Researches have shown that the shortened bonding to form O‐O peroxo/dimer (≈1.5/2.5 Å vs. ≈2.8 Å in a typical TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron)<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0010"><sup>[6]</sup></link> and metal‐oxo between TM and oxygen atoms (≈1.8 Å vs. 2.0 Å in a typical TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron)<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0011"><sup>[7]</sup></link> can effectively stabilize oxidized oxide species against oxygen release. Hu and co‐workers<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0012"><sup>[8]</sup></link> successively reported a P3‐type Na<sub>0.6</sub>Li<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and a P2‐type Na<sub>0.72</sub>Li<sub>0.24</sub>Mn<sub>0.76</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode, which exhibited highly reversible lattice oxygen redox with suppressed phase transition. Meanwhile, on account of the energy distinction between Li 1<i>s</i> and O 2<i>p</i> orbits and the intensive ionic feature of Na−O bond, the existence of nearly unhybridized oxygen 2<i>p</i> orbits along “Na‐O‐Li” configuration is allocated above those interacting with TM ions at the oxygen valence band, thus triggering reversible oxygen redox.</p><p>Song et al.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0015"><sup>[9]</sup></link> discovered that partially replacing TM cations exhibiting stronger ionic bonding with O in P3‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mg<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> could effectively trigger reversible oxygen redox. Maitra et al.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0016"><sup>[10]</sup></link> reported that oxygen redox could also be involved in P2‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Mg<sub>0.28</sub>Mn<sub>0.72</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> without oxygen loss due to Mg ions that always remained in the TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron and interacted with O 2<i>p</i> orbits to form “Na‐O‐Mg” configuration. Lately, Wang et al.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0018"><sup>[11a]</sup></link> reported that in P2‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Ni<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Mg 3<i>s</i> orbits that interacted with O 2<i>p</i> orbits possess higher energy, thus generating ionic Mg−O bonds in both “Na‐O‐Mg” and “Mg‐O‐Mg” configurations. This ionic feature places O 2<i>p</i> interacting with Mg 3<i>s</i> states above the valence band of O that relevant to those interacting with TM 3<i>d</i> orbits, also leading to reversible oxygen redox. Yang's group<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0019"><sup>[11b]</sup></link> developed resonant inelastic X‐ray scattering (RIXS) technique to quantify the oxygen redox in Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mg<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. These findings imply that the existence of alkaline metal ions in TM layers would not be the essential condition to promote oxygen redox. Recently, Na<sub>4/7</sub>Mn<sub>6/7</sub>□<sub>1/7</sub>O<sub>2</sub><link href="#anie202108933-bib-0020"><sup>[12]</sup></link> and Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Mn<sub>7/9</sub>Mg<sub>1/9</sub>□<sub>1/9</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#anie202108933-bib-0023"><sup>[13]</sup></link> compounds containing native TM vacancies have been reported, in which orphaned oxygen 2<i>p</i> orbits around TM vacancies can contribute to the charge compensation and still keep Mn−O bond stable. These works suggest that introducing vacancies in TM sites is a promising approach instead of alkline/alkaline‐earth metal ions substitution, for triggering reversible oxygen redox reaction as generating nonbonding oxygen 2<i>p</i> orbits along “Na‐O‐□”, “□‐O‐□” and “Mg‐O‐□” configurations. However, vacancies in TM layer bring about structure instability, thus resulting in severe capacity fading.</p><p>Herein, by introducing Mg<sup>2+</sup> into a P2‐type Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.4</sub>O<sub>2</sub> framework, we prepared Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mg<sub>0.13</sub>[Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.07</sub>□<sub>0.13</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> cathode with native TM vacancies successfully, and realized that Mg ions located in both sodium sites and TM sites. With the help of advanced synchrotron‐based techniques, neutron powder diffraction (NPD) and DFT calculations, the participation of active orbits, structure evolution, charge compensation mechanism during electrochemical cycling were investigated systematically. Vacancies in the TM layer make TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron more flexible against structural distortion induced by Na<sup>+</sup> extraction. Partial Mg<sup>2+</sup> occupied in a trigonal prismatic sodium interlayer can buffer the O‐O repulsion force and stabilize structure along <i>c</i> axis upon electrochemical cycling even in high‐voltage region. During desodiation, low‐spin (LS) Co<sup>3+</sup> (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>6</sup>) with stable electronic structure rather than low‐spin (LS) Co<sup>4+</sup> (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>5</sup>) could effectively stabilize the CoO<sub>6</sub> octahedron. Vacancies and Mg<sup>2+</sup> in TM layer can create “Na‐O‐□”, “Mg‐O‐□”, “□‐O‐□” and “Na‐O‐Mg” configurations to achieve extra capacity from oxygen redox.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202108933-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p><b>Crystal Structure</b>. P2‐layered Na<sub>0.8</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.4−<i>x</i></sub>Mg<sub>x</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0, 0.1, 0.2) with various Mg contents (denoted as MCM‐0, MCM‐1, and MCM‐2) were synthesized by solid‐state reaction. The structure comparison of the three samples is examined by synchrotron X‐ray diffraction (XRD) technique, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 a</link>. A typical P2‐type hexagonal structure was confirmed for the samples without any impure phase, which can be fully indexed by <i>JCPDS</i> No. 54‐0894 with the space group of <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i> (No. 194). It should be noted that with increased content of Mg from 0 to 0.2, the (002) diffraction peak shifts continuously to higher angle and the (100) peak moves reversely (inset of Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 a</link>), revealing the shrinkage of <i>c</i>‐axis spacing, and the expansion of <i>a‐b</i> plane, which is inversed in comparison with the previous reports in P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33−<i>x</i></sub>Mg<sub>x</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0≤<i>x</i>≤0.33), where Mg ions were only implanted in TM sites.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0024"><sup>[14]</sup></link> The crystal lattice evolution in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 b</link> depicts the unit cell continuously shrinks along <i>c</i>‐axis and expands in <i>a‐b</i> plane as the amount of Mg<sup>2+</sup> increasing from <i>x</i>=0 to 0.2. Δ<i>c</i> and Δ<i>a</i>(<i>b</i>) values are also calculated and shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 b</link>. According to Pauling's rule, Mg possesses stronger electronegativity (1.31 for Mg and 0.93 for Na) and smaller ionic radius (0.72 Å for Mg<sup>2+</sup> and 1.02 Å for Na<sup>+</sup>) than sodium, displaying more intensive electrostatic attraction with oxygen from the adjacent TMO<sub>6</sub> layer. Mg ions in sodium layer result in the contraction of <i>c</i> parameter, which is dominating and surpass the expansion effect caused by Mg<sup>2+</sup> located in TM layer (<i>r</i><sub>Mg</sub><sup>2+</sup> &gt; <i>r</i><sub>TM</sub><sup><i>n</i>+</sup>). In order to precisely distinguishing the atom sites, NPD was conducted by general purpose powder diffractometer (GPPD) with the center of diffraction angle <i>θ</i><sub>c</sub>=90°. Rietveld refinement results of the NPD and XRD patterns for MCM‐0, MCM‐1, and MCM‐2 are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 c</link> and S1, respectively. Corresponding structure parameters deduced from refinements are shown in Table S1 to S4. These results imply that Mg ions occupy both 2<i>d</i> site of Na<sub><i>e</i></sub> and 2<i>a</i> site of TM layer. The occupation ratio of Mg<sub>2d</sub>/Mg<sub>2a</sub> is about 2:1. Given the initial chemical composition we designed, it can be confirmed that TM layer (2<i>a</i> site) is not fully replenished due to the existence of vacancies. The morphology of MCM‐2 (Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 d</link> and e) shows evenly distributed particles with the size range of 1–5 μm. The regular edge and smooth surface of the particles represent high crystallinity of MCM‐2. Element mappings in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 f</link> indicate Na, Mn, Co, Mg are homogeneously distributed in the particle. Energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) and corresponding atomic contents are shown in Figure S2 and Table S5, respectively. It can be seen that atomic stoichiometry is close to 0.6: 0.2: 0.2 for Mn: Co: Mg, matching well with the designed composition. Synchrotron‐based X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) was carried out to illustrate the valence state and local environment of Mn and Co in the pristine MCM‐2. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 g</link> and h, the average valance of Mn and Co are +3.8 and +2.6, respectively, which is consistent with charge balance of the expected stoichiometric ratio. The oscillations in R‐space of the Fourier‐transformed extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (FT‐EXAFS) spectra of Mn and Co and corresponding least‐square fit are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0001">1 i</link> and j. The structural parameters derived from the fitting results are shown in Table S6. It should be noted that the R values of the peaks are ca. 0.3–0.5 Å shorter than the actual bond lengths because the FT‐EXAFS spectra were not phase‐corrected.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0003 #anie202108933-bib-0017"><sup>[3, 11]</sup></link> It can be seen that the coordination numbers of Mn‐TM and Co‐TM interactions in the second coordination shells are 5.2 and 5.4, respectively, lower than 6.0 for the standard hexagonal coordination in the TM layer, which further confirms the existence of vacancies in the TM layer.</p><figure xml:id="anie202108933-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108933:anie202108933-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108933-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Synchrotron XRD patterns of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2). The wavelength of synchrotron X‐ray is 0.6884 Å. b) Crystallographic evolution of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2) with Mg substitution. c) Rietveld refinement of the neutron powder diffraction (NPD) data for MCM‐2. d–f) SEM images of MCM‐2 and corresponding EDX mapping of elements Na, Mn, Co, Mg, respectively. g, h) Mn and Co K‐edge XANES spectrum of MCM‐2. i, j) Least‐square fits of the calculated FT‐EXAFS phase and amplitude functions to the experimental EXAFS spectra of Mn and Co in the pristine MCM‐2.</p></caption></figure><p><b>Electrochemical Performance</b>. The initial galvanostatic charge and discharge (GCD) profiles of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2) at 0.1 C (1 C=170 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) between 1.6 and 4.4 V are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 a</link>. MCM‐0 exhibits an initial charge/discharge capacities of 95.6/118.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. For comparison, the specific capacities of MCM‐1 and MCM‐2 in the first cycle are 96.3/144.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 165.9/176 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Mg substitution resulted in the reduction of the capacity below 4.25 V, owing to the dilution of the Co ions concentration with low valence that can be oxidized to higher valence during charging.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0025"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Interestingly, as Mg content increased to 0.2, a flat voltage plateau with about 110 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in capacity appeared, which is generally considered as the contribution of oxygen redox, thus allowing more Na<sup>+</sup> extracted from MCM‐2. GCD curves in the following cycles are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 b</link>. After 80 cycles, 73.6 % of the discharge capacity can be achieved relative to the initial value. Figure S3 exhibits cycle stability of the MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2) at different current rates. After 120 cycles, the capacity retention rates of MCM‐0, MCM‐1 and MCM‐2 are 34.5 %, 36.2 % and 58.5 % at 0.1 C and 64.2 %, 72.3 % and 82.5 % at 1 C, respectively, which are obviously improved as Mg substituted to 0.2. Typical CV curves of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2) in the first five cycles at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> are shown in Figure S4. Two redox couples at about 2.0/1.7 V and 4.0/3.9 V are observed, representing the redox of Mn and Co ions. When Mg content increases to 0.2, a sharp oxidation peak attributed to oxygen oxidation at 4.3 V emerges, which matches well with the voltage plateau in the initial charge. CV profiles at different scanning rates (<i>v</i>: 0.1–1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) for MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2) are shown in Figure S5a, S5b and Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 c</link>. It can be seen that the cathodic and anodic peaks expand equably with the increase of scanning rate, while the general shape of the curves basically maintained. Diffusion coefficients of 3.7×10<sup>−10</sup>, 5.8×10<sup>−10</sup> and 6.2×10<sup>−10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> are obtained for the cathodic peaks of MCM‐0, MCM‐1 and MCM‐2, respectively, shown in Figure S5c. It indicates Mg substitution can effectively improve the kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> transportation. Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 d</link> exhibits the rate capabilities of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2). When the current densities increase to 8 C, 68.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of the discharge capacity can be reversibly achieved in MCM‐2. After setting back to 0.1 C, the capacity can recover to 163.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, while MCM‐0 and MCM‐1 suffer irreversible capacity decay. Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 e</link> is the GCD curves of the initial two cycles under the current rates of 0.1 C, 0.5 C and 1 C. Even at 0.5 C and 1 C, 130.1 and 114.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained in the initial charge with flat plateau. The plummets during discharge below 3.0 V at high rate are indentified as the redox of Mn at low voltage range generally presents hysteretic kinetics,<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0017 #anie202108933-bib-0026"><sup>[11, 16]</sup></link> thus large polarization occurs at high current. Cycle performances of MCM‐2 at 4 C and 10 C are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 f</link>. The capacity retention rate can be maintained to 89.9 % and 81.7 % after 150 cycles, respectively, exhibiting superior cycle stability of MCM‐2. In order to examine the kinetics and polarization of MCM‐2 during cycling, galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was conducted (Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 g</link>). In the charge process, the overpotential<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0027"><sup>[17]</sup></link> is relatively low in the middle of the voltage range, where Co ions began to contribute to the charge compensation. It indicates that the electrochemical reaction associated with Co has better kinetics. On the contrary, the values of polarization are higher near the start and the end of GITT curves, where charge compensations are mainly ascribed to Mn and O, indicating sluggish reaction kinetics.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0028"><sup>[18]</sup></link> The diffusion coefficients derived from GITT curves were also plotted in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 g</link>. The different kinetics further demonstrates the diversity of charge compensation mechanisms at the corresponding Co, Mn and O sites. To explore the potential application of MCM‐2 cathode, full cells were tested with MCM‐2 as the cathode and hard carbon as the anode. In Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 h</link>, the first discharge capacity of MCM‐2/hard carbon full cell is 131.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (calculated by the mass of active material in cathode) with an average potential of 2.62 V at 1.3–4.4 V. The cycle performance of the full cell shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0002">2 i</link> presents a capacity retention rate of 73.2 % after 50 cycles at 0.2 C. The results indicate that MCM‐2 electrode is a feasible and promising cathode.</p><figure xml:id="anie202108933-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108933:anie202108933-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108933-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The initial and b) the following GCD curves of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2) between 1.6 and 4.4 V at 0.1 C. c) CV curves of MCM‐2 with various scan rates from 0.1 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 1.5 and 4.5 V. d) Rate capability of MCM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2). e) GCD curves of MCM‐2 at various current densities. f) Cycle performance of MCM‐2. g) GITT curves of MCM‐2. h) GCD curves of MCM‐2/hard carbon full cell at 0.2 C. i) Cycle performance of MCM‐2/hard carbon full cell at 0.2 C.</p></caption></figure><p><b>Structure Evolution</b>. Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0003">3 a</link> shows the synchrotron‐based in situ XRD patterns of MCM‐2 in the first cycle. The contour plots of the peak evolution are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0003">3 b</link>. During charge, a new (004) peak at 14.1° appears beside the original (004) peak at the very beginning, suggesting that a two‐phase reaction occurs. Along with charging from the open‐circuit voltage to 4.2 V, the new peak keeps shifting to lower angle, indicating the interlayer distance (correlated to lattice parameter <i>c</i>) is increasing along with a solid‐solution behavior. On the contrary, the (100) peak at 15.8° shifts to higher two‐theta angle, indicating the lattice parameters <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are decreasing during charge. From 4.2 to 4.4 V, the peak shift is not obvious compared with the low‐potential region and no new phase can be observed after charging up to 4.4 V. The diffraction peaks at the fully charged state can be assigned to a P′′2 phase similar to the initial P2 phase. P′′2 phase has larger lattice parameter <i>c</i> and smaller <i>a</i> (<i>b</i>) than P2 phase. These results suggest that Mg ions partially doped in the Na layer can effectively suppress the irreversible phase transition from P2 to O2 or OP4.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0029"><sup>[19]</sup></link> In addition, the existence of TM vacancies makes the TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron differ from conventional rigid one. The octahedron becomes more flexible against structural distortion upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction, thus can stabilize the structure even at the highly desodiated state. In the discharge process, the (002) and (004) peaks of the P′′2 phase shift back toward higher angle, indicating a reversible structure recovery. It can be found that peaks of original P2 phase are regenerated when the voltage decreases to 2.5 V, then accompanying with P′′2 phase till 1.6 V. No distorted orthorhombic P′2 phase (<i>Cmcm</i> symmetry) appears, which is different from the former research.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0017"><sup>[11]</sup></link> The high‐resolution synchrotron‐based in situ XRD patterns provide a full picture of the structural evolution of MCM‐2 in the first cycle.</p><figure xml:id="anie202108933-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108933:anie202108933-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108933-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Time‐resolved in situ XRD during the initial cycle between 1.6 and 4.4 V at 0.5 C. The wavelength of X‐ray is 0.6884 Å. b) Contour plots of the different peaks evolution. c) Contour plot of the (002) peak evolution during the initial cycle and the second charge at 10 C. The wavelength of X‐ray is 0.8857 Å.</p></caption></figure><p>Synchrotron‐based time‐resolved XRD was also employed to investigate the structural evolution of MCM‐2 at high current rate. Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0003">3 c</link> shows the contour plots of (002) peak evolution of MCM‐2 in the first cycle and the second charge processes at 10 C captured by synchrotron X‐ray with a wavelength of <i>λ</i>=0.8857 Å. Original XRD data can be seen in Figure S7. It shows that the peak evolution becomes more continuous, and three coexistent intermediate phases occur during GCD at 10 C rate, which indicate that high current could induce intermediate phase formation in the MCM‐2 cathode materials, similar to the results of LFP and NMC111 cathode materials in LIBs.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0032"><sup>[20]</sup></link> The splitting intermediate phases generated are due to the non‐uniform Na distribution in the bulk materials that driven by high current densities.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0017"><sup>[11]</sup></link></p><p><b>Charge Compensation</b>. To explore valence and local structure change of transition metals in MCM‐2 during cycling, XAS was measured at different states. Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 a</link> shows voltage points chosen for XAS measurements. The X‐ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) of Mn and Co elements at different states are displayed in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 b</link>–e. During the first charge process, Mn K‐edge shifts to higher energy towards MnO<sub>2</sub> reference, suggesting that the valence of Mn changes from +3.8 to +4. Meanwhile, Co K‐edge also moves to higher energy during charge, indicating Co is oxidized from +2.6 to +3 referring to CoO and LiCoO<sub>2</sub> references. It is very interesting that the valence state of Co after charge is 3+, rather than 4+ in most of Co‐based layered cathode materials, such as LiCoO<sub>2</sub> or Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>CoO<sub>2</sub>. Co<sup>3+</sup> formed at the charged state is quite beneficial for structure stabilization, since all <i>t</i><sub>2g</sub> orbits are fully occupied in CoO<sub>6</sub> ligand with empty <i>e</i><sub>g</sub> orbits for Co<sup>3+</sup> ions. Based on the valence change of Mn and Co upon charging, the specific capacity contributed by the oxidation of Mn and Co were estimated to be 32.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Mn<sup>3.8+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup>) and 21.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Co<sup>2.6+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup>), respectively. However, the total specific capacity in the initial charge up to 4.4 V is 165.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much higher than the total contribution from Mn and Co (53.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, close to the actual capacity 56.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when charging to 4.2 V). In previous studies, it has been revealed that oxygen could contribute to the charge compensation in Mn‐based layered cathode materials, where oxygen ions were partially oxidized when charging to high voltage region. Therefore, it is very possible that the extra charge capacity for MCM‐2 comes from oxygen redox.</p><figure xml:id="anie202108933-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108933:anie202108933-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108933-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) GCD curves and corresponding points of MCM‐2 electrode for taking ex situ XAS measurement. b, d) Mn and c, e) Co K‐edge XANES of MCM‐2 at various states in the first GCD states. f) TFY mode of O K‐edge SXAS at different charge states. g) Variation of the integrated area under the pre‐edge in low‐energy region (from 528 to 533 eV) of the O K‐edge SXAS during the first cycle. h) Schematic of charge compensation contributed by different elements.</p></caption></figure><p>In order to probe the origin of the extra capacity beyond TM redox and investigate the electronic structure changes of O in MCM‐2 during cycling, O K‐edge soft XAS at different charge states were measured in both total fluorescence yield (TFY) mode and total electron yield (TEY) mode,<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0035"><sup>[21]</sup></link> as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 f</link> and S8. The spectra can be divided into two regions. Pre‐edge peaks at 529.8 and 532.2 eV are attributed to the transition of O 1<i>s</i> electron to the unoccupied hybridized state of TM 3<i>d</i> and O 2<i>p</i> orbits just above the Fermi level, which splits into two peaks with different energies of <i>t</i><sub>2g</sub> and <i>e</i><sub>g</sub> symmetries. The broad peaks above 535.0 eV correspond to transitions to O 2<i>p</i>‐TM 4<i>sp</i> hybridized states. In Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 f</link>, it can be seen that the pre‐edge peak changes obviously in area during charge, corresponding to the changes in the density of empty states just above the Fermi level (528–533 eV). The integrated area changes of the pre‐edge peak relative to the pristine sample were calculated and shown as histogram in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 g</link>. The density of empty states continues to increase as the material is charged to 4.4 V (Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 g</link>). Below 4.2 V, the peak area increase could be mainly due to the oxidation of TM ions, similar as the previous reports for Mn‐rich materials.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0006 #anie202108933-bib-0038"><sup>[4b, 22]</sup></link> However, The peak area increase above 4.2 V cannot be ascribed to the contribution of TM ions, because no edge shift of Mn and Co K‐edge XAS is observed in this voltage region. Therefore, the increase of peak area above 4.2 V must correspond to the removal of electrons from oxygen and the creation of electron‐hole states on O ions.</p><p>During the discharge process, Mn and Co K‐edge shift back to lower energy throughout the sodiation stage. It can be quantitatively estimated that Mn and Co ions contribute to the charge compensation with Mn<sup>4+</sup>/Mn<sup>3.6+</sup> and Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2.5+</sup> redox, corresponding to 64.3 and 26.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in discharge capacity, respectively. However, the discharge capacity is 176 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, much higher than the sum of Mn and Co contributions, further confirming the charge compensation by oxygen reduction seen in the reversible changes in the oxygen SXAS, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 f</link> and g, implying the oxygen‐hole states are repopulated during discharge. The oxygen contribution was further evidenced by O 1<i>s</i> X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (Figure S9) and FT‐EXAFS spectrum of Mn and Co at different GCD states (Figure S10).<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0039"><sup>[23]</sup></link></p><p>According to the analysis above, charge compensation contributed by different elements during electrochemical cycling is summarized in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0004">4 h</link>. In the first charge, Mn<sup>3.8+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> and Co<sup>2.6+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> redox participate in the voltage region below 4.2 V, O<sup>2−</sup>/O<sup>−</sup> redox takes part in 4.2–4.4 V. In the first discharge, Mn<sup>4+</sup>/Mn<sup>3.6+</sup>, Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>2.5+</sup> and O<sup>2−</sup>/O<sup>−</sup> contribute to the charge compensation synergistically. Other than the traditional Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>4+</sup> redox in many cathode materials in LIBs, we here realized valence change of Co below +3. Although the exact active voltage range has not been unambiguously established, the presence of divalent cobalt at lower potentials is already reported.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0040"><sup>[24]</sup></link> When charging to 4.4 V, Co<sup>2+</sup> ions are oxidized to Co<sup>3+</sup> ions, which do not have a magnetic moment and serves as a buffer atom in the transition‐metal layer.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0043"><sup>[25]</sup></link> The as‐oxidized LS Co<sup>3+</sup> (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>6</sup>) and Mn<sup>4+</sup> (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>3</sup>) with stable electronic structures in MCM‐2 phase can ultimately moderate the layered lattice at the charged state, beneficial for cycle stability.</p><p><b>Theoretical Calculation</b>. To understand the redox mechanism, DFT calculations were performed to obtain the partial density of states (pDOS) and electron localization functions (ELF) of Na<sub>0.8−<i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0, 0.19, 0.37, 0.62) during the desodiation process. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0005">5 a</link>, Typical <i>d</i> band profile of transition metal with split <i>t</i><sub>2g</sub> and <i>e</i><sub>g</sub> orbits can be clearly observed. Within the valence band, the electron densities of Mn and Co 3<i>d</i> orbits neighboring <i>E</i><sub>F</sub> decreased to approximately zero after 0.19 mol Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalated, which is more than those in O 2<i>p</i> states, indicating the non‐negligible usage of Mn and Co ions redox reactions between <i>x</i>=0 and 0.19. Then, it was found that the intensity of electron densities on O 2<i>p</i> states dominate DOS in the vicinity of <i>E</i><sub>f</sub> and change obviously from <i>x</i>=0.19 to 0.62, indicating that O is capable of donating electron and participating in charge compensation process in the latter desodiation process. Meanwhile, electron densities of Mn and Co barely changed. It is in good agreement with the valence changes of Mn, Co and O from XAS data. The redox chemistry can be further cognized by using the schematic of the TM‐O interactions and the relationship of energy versus DOS shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0005">5 b</link> and c, respectively. The O 2<i>p</i> orbits in MCM‐2 interact with Mn, Mg and □ simultaneously. Mg 3<i>s</i> states are relatively high in energy compared with O 2<i>p</i>, which results in weak, ionic Mg−O bonds and similar to Li‐O,<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0005"><sup>[4a]</sup></link> places the O 2<i>p</i> states relatively high in energy.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0016"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Previous reports have calculated that nonbonding O 2<i>p</i> orbits which point to TM vacancies have the highest energy compared with the neighboring occupied orbits.<link href="#anie202108933-bib-0020 #anie202108933-bib-0023"><sup>[12, 13]</sup></link> In contrast, strongly covalent TM‐O interactions would push the O 2<i>p</i> states down in energy. Thus, □‐O and more ionic interaction Mg‐O in MCM‐2 placed the O 2<i>p</i> states at relatively elevated energies, which make the oxygen redox reaction easier.</p><figure xml:id="anie202108933-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108933:anie202108933-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108933-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Spin resolved pDOS of Na<sub>0.8−<i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0, 0.19, 0.37, 0.62). b) Schematic illustration of the electron energy level diagrams showing the covalent TM <i>d</i>‐O 2<i>p</i> and Mg/□ containing TM <i>d</i>‐O 2<i>p</i> bonding interactions. c) Schematic illustration of the energy vs. DOS in MCM‐2. d) Top view and e) side view of the electron localization functions (ELF) in Na<sub>0.8−<i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0, 0.19, 0.37, 0.62).</p></caption></figure><p>As the presence of both vacancies and Mg<sup>2+</sup> in the TM layer can trigger oxygen oxidation, it is of great significance to illuminate the impacts of the vacancies and Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions on the oxygen oxidation. ELF analysis was calculated to reveal orbital contributions in the vicinity of <i>E</i><sub>f</sub>. Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0005">5 d</link> and e are the top view and side view of the ELF in Na<sub>0.8−<i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0, 0.19, 0.37, 0.62). A cross section parallels to (001) was set to cut off one layer of O‐TM‐O slabs as a contour plot present from the top perspective. It can be concluded that O atoms close to the vacancies display more intensive electron density than those close to the Mg atoms, and exhibit a deflection to the vacancy site (black arrow). From <i>x</i>=0.19 to 0.62, electron density of O atoms near the vacancies shows prominent changes (red marked), demonstrating the dominating participation of charge compensation during desodiation. The oxygen in “Na‐O‐□”, “Mg‐O‐□” and “□‐O‐□” configurations lose charge because the vacancies can induce lone‐pair electrons and increase the energy for O 2<i>p</i> orbits. Since the weak and ionic Mg−O bond also increases the energy of the O 2<i>p</i> electrons, O around Mg ions in “Na‐O‐Mg” configuration can provide charges during charging as well, which can be distinguished from the side view in Figure <link href="#anie202108933-fig-0005">5 e</link> (yellow splattering around Mg). Therefore, O atoms close to the vacancies can donate more charges than the O atoms near the Mg atoms during Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation. This is responsible for the high activity of these O 2<i>p</i> orbits.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202108933-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In this work, P2‐layered MCM‐2 cathode with native TM vacancies and Mg ions in both Na and TM sites was rationally designed to activate oxygen redox and stabilize the structure successfully. Native TM vacancies lead to the formation of nonbonding 2<i>p</i> orbits around oxygen that pointing toward TM vacancies, triggering the oxygen redox at the very beginning of the charge process. In addition, vacancies in the TM layer make the TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron more flexible against structural distortion induced by Na<sup>+</sup> extraction. On the other hand, Mg ions in Na sites play the role of “pillars” to stabilize the layered structure during cycling by buffering O‐O repulsion to prevent structure collapse in <i>c</i> direction upon charging to high voltage. The charge compensation mechanism of MCM‐2 was disclosed, where reversible low‐valence‐range Co<sup>2+</sup>/Co<sup>3+</sup> redox couple was realized, rather than traditional Co<sup>3+</sup>/Co<sup>4+</sup> redox in most Co‐based layered cathode materials (LiCoO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>CoO<sub>2</sub> etc.). Co<sup>3+</sup> formed at the charged state is quite beneficial for structure stabilization in high voltage region, since all <i>t</i><sub>2g</sub> orbits are fully occupied in CoO<sub>6</sub> ligand with empty <i>e</i><sub>g</sub> orbits for Co<sup>3+</sup> ions. It further stabilizes the layered structure to ensure cycle stability of this material. The concept of this design strategy provides new opportunities for promoting anion redox to achieve higher capacity without the expense of structural and cycle stability for layered cathode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202108933-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The work at Fudan University was financially supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 52071085, 51922031, 52071083), Science &amp; Technology Commission of Shanghai Municipality (No. 19ZR1404200, 20XD1420600), National Key R&amp;D Program of China (2020YFA0406200), China Postdoctoral Science Foundation (No. 2021M690645) and Zhuhai Fudan Innovation Institute.
