However, the layered cathode tends to be thermodynamically and electrochemically unstable during (de)sodiation. Herein, we propose the pinning effect and controllable pinning point in sodium storage layered cathodes to enhance the structural stability and achieve optimal electrochemical performance. 0 %, 2.5 % and 7.3 % transition‐metal occupancies in Na‐site as pinning points are obtained in Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5−<i>x</i></sub>Fe<sub><i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub>. 2.5 % Na‐site pinned by Fe<sup>3+</sup> is beneficial to restrain the potential slab sliding and enhance the structural stability, resulting in an ultra‐low volume variation of 0.6 % and maintaining the smooth two‐dimensional channel for Na‐ion transfer. numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are widely regarded as a promising candidate of large‐scale energy storage devices.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Furthermore, the cheap aluminum foil can be used as the current collector for both cathode and anode of a battery, so both unipolar and bipolar electrodes can be designed for SIBs.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> As one of the most critical components of SIBs, the cathode dominates nearly one‐third of the cost of a battery, and determines the output voltage, capacity and safety of the battery.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0009"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Among various cathode materials, layered oxides have received the most extensive attention owing to their high capacity, abundant elements, scalable preparation process, and flexible composition.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0013"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, the layered cathodes are still constrained by unfavorable structural rearrangement arising from transition‐metal (TM) slab sliding which is due to the strong TM layer interaction at the highly de‐sodiated state, thus the unsatisfactory electrochemical stability. Generally, the unfavorable TM slab sliding will cause the large internal stress and subsequent collapse of layered structure, which are ultimately reflected in the deterioration of electrochemical performance.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0019"><sup>[5]</sup></link></p><p>In fact, these basic structural failures are also common in alloy science, and they are generally reflected in strength and ductility of alloy. The pinning effect has been one of the most effective strategies to enhance the strength or/and ductility of alloy materials.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0022"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Doping an appropriate proportion of solute atoms, including non‐metallic atoms and metal atoms, can be functionally used as pinning points to inhibit the unfavorable dislocation movement in the alloy material under external load. For example, Lu et al.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0025"><sup>[6c]</sup></link> and Taneike et al.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0023"><sup>[6a]</sup></link> respectively doped non‐metallic oxygen and carbon in alloys to pin the boundary, and Nie et al.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0024"><sup>[6b]</sup></link> doped metal gadolinium and zinc. Furthermore, Guo et al. also introduced pinning effect into lithium storage cathode to improve the electrochemical performances.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0027"><sup>[7]</sup></link> However, the pinning effect has not been further explored because of the weak TM slabs interaction of such materials during the lithiation and de‐lithiation process. Similarly, the pinning effect may also be effective in enhancing the structural stability and electrochemical performance of oxide‐based layered cathodes by forbidding the adverse slab slipping in the repeated sodiation and de‐sodiation (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0001">1 a</link>). As expected, recent research results indicate that TM accidentally occupied in the sodium layer does improve the structural stability of the layered cathode.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0028"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Our recent work revealed that part of the TM irreversibly occupied from the TM layer to the Na layer during the first de‐sodiation process, and the unfavorable phase transformation also disappeared during the subsequent sodiation and de‐sodiation process.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0030"><sup>[8b]</sup></link> But, it should be noted that too much TM in sodium layer would severely block the diffusion of sodium‐ions, while insufficient pinned TM would not form the rock‐steady structure. Therefore, it remains a huge challenge to intelligently control the pinned TM in sodium layer to enhance the thermodynamic stability without affecting the normal Na‐ion diffusion.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100917-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100917-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Illustration of layered cathode without (left) and with (right) pinned TM in Na layer, where the blue and red balls represent TM and oxygen, respectively. The pinned multivalent TM (dark blue ball) in sodium layer exhibit a stronger interaction with lattice oxygen than sodium, thus the pinned TM in sodium layer would help improve the structural and thermodynamic stability of the layered cathode. b) XRD pattern and corresponding Rietveld refinement of NMCF0.1. c) Comparison of cell parameters <i>a/b</i> (black histograms) and <i>c</i> (red histograms) of NMC, NMCF0.1, and NMCF0.2.</p></caption></figure><p>In this work, we report that the pinned Fe<sup>3+</sup> in sodium layer is tactfully controllably realized by a doping strategy to contribute to the design of robust layered cathode. Atomic resolution scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) and powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) well reveal the Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in sodium layer and the abnormal lattice parameter variation in Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5−<i>x</i></sub>Fe<sub><i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0, 0.1 and 0.2), respectively. Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.4</sub>Fe<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (NMCF0.1) with an optimal pinning point of 2.5 % shows superior electrochemical performance than Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (NMC) without Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.3</sub>Fe<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (NMCF0.2) with a dense pinning points of 7.3 %. In‐situ XRD, Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT), and differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) reveal that the pinned Fe<sup>3+</sup> in Na layer can greatly stabilize the layered structure, maintain the smooth Na transfer path as well as enhance the thermodynamic stability.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202100917-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>All the three samples of NMC, NMCF0.1, and NMCF0.2 are synthesized by a typical solid‐state phase reaction method. Inductively coupled plasma‐atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP‐AES) results (Supporting Information, Table S1) show that the actual element composition in the prepared samples is consistent with the target composition. The micro‐morphology of the three samples were characterized by scanning electron microscope (SEM), and all the three samples show typical micro‐scale bulk characteristics with a particle diameter of about 1–5 μm (Supporting Information, Figure S1). The crystal structures and cell parameters are characterized and refined by XRD and Rietveld refinement, respectively. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0001">1 b</link> and the Supporting Information, Figure S2, all the three samples show a P2 type layered structure with a space group of <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i>. A direct comparison of XRD patterns of the three samples are provided in Figure S3. Compared with the diffraction peaks of NMC sample, all diffraction peaks of Fe‐doped NMCF0.1 are shifted to lower 2‐theta degrees, which is mainly due to the crystal lattice expansion of NMCF0.1 caused by substituting Fe<sup>3+</sup> (0.645 Å, high spin) with a larger ionic radius for Co<sup>3+</sup> (0.545 Å, low spin) in NMC.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0033"><sup>[9]</sup></link> With the increase of doping amounts, the diffraction peaks of NMCF0.2 at 35–80° continue to shift to lower 2‐theta degrees, but the peak positions of (002) and (004) show an opposite shifting behavior. Based on the structural refinement, the detail cell parameters and atomic occupancy information of the three samples are listed in the Supporting Information, Table S2, S3, S4, and S5, where the low agreement factors indicate reliable refinement results. The detail cell parameters of the three samples are shown in the Supporting Information, Table S2, where the low agreement factors indicate reliable refinement results. As listed in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0001">1 c</link>, the cell parameters of <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> increase with the introduction of Fe<sup>3+</sup>. Interestingly, the cell parameter of <i>c</i> first increases in NMCF0.1 and then decreases in NMCF0.2 with the increase of iron introduction, which suggests that TMs may be pinned in the sodium layer because the TMs in the sodium layer can effectively reduce the repulsive force between adjacent planes of oxygen in the sodium layer. Since Fe has more remarkable thermodynamic stability than Co in the alkali metal layer,<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0036"><sup>[10]</sup></link> the pinned TMs in the sodium layer of NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2 consist of major Fe<sup>3+</sup> (0.645 Å, high spin) and negligible Co<sup>3+</sup> (0.545 Å, low spin). The refinement results indicate that 2.5 % and 7.3 % Na‐sites are occupied by Fe<sup>3+</sup> in NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2, respectively.</p><p>To further confirm the TM pinning in sodium layer, scanning transmission electron microscopy‐high angle annular dark field (STEM‐HAADF) technique was conducted to directly observe the pinned TMs at an atomic level. Since the image contrast of STEM‐HAADF is related to <i>Z</i><sup>1.7</sup> where the <i>Z</i> stands for atomic number, heavy TM can be well observed while light sodium and oxygen cannot.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0042"><sup>[11]</sup></link> As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 a</link>, no TM signal is observed in the sodium layer of NMC sample. For NMCF0.1 sample, slight TM signal in the sodium layer is observed, shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 b</link>. A clearer simulated STEM image with slight TM in sodium layer is shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S4, where the image was obtained by QSTEM. With the increase of iron doping amounts, more TM signal can be observed in sodium layer in NMCF0.2 sample (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 c</link>). Line profiles are further analyzed to confirm the pinned TM in sodium layer, shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 d</link>–f. NMC shows standard signal peaks in TM position and no TM signal is observed in sodium layer (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 d</link>). TM signal is observed in sodium layer of NMCF0.1 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 e</link>) and NMCF0.2 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 f</link>). These results directly confirm that NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2 exhibit TM pinning in sodium layer, and the TM pinning can be adjusted by controlling the iron amount. The atomic STEM X‐ray energy dispersive spectroscopy (STEM‐EDS) of NMCF0.1 is shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 g</link>, where the TM layer is occupied by Mn, Co and Fe, and the sodium layer is occupied by Na and a small amount of Fe, that is, the TMs occupied in the sodium layer are Fe. Additionally, the Mössbauer spectrum (Supporting Information, Figure S5) of NMCF0.1 further confirms the existing of Fe<sup>3+</sup> in sodium layer. Since the pinned Fe<sup>3+</sup> in sodium layer can tune the lattice stress of the crystal, geometric phase analysis (GPA) based on STEM‐HAADF was carried out to image the internal stress of the three samples. During the GPA calculating, <i>[002]*</i> and <i>[101]*</i> were, respectively chosen as <i>x</i> and <i>y</i> directions. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0002">2 h</link>–j, both NMC and NMCF0.1 show slight internal stress or lattice distort along the <i>c</i> axis, while NMCF0.2 exhibits drastic internal stress or lattice distort along the <i>c</i> axis. The internal stress or lattice distort in NMCF0.2 can be reasonably attributed to the strong interaction of lattice oxygen with the excessive Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinned in sodium layer, which may adversely affect the stability of layer structure and the diffusion of sodium ions.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100917-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100917-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Atomic STEM‐HAADF images of a) NMC, b) NMCF0.1, and c) NMCF0.2 along [100] zone axis. Line‐profiles of the dotted‐line rectangles for d) NMC (corresponding to Figure a), e) NMCF0.1 (corresponding to Figure b) and f) NMCF0.2 (corresponding to (c)), where the obvious peaks indicate the TMs in TM layers and the arrow indicated areas are the Fe pinned in the sodium layers. g) Atomic STEM‐HAADF image and corresponding atomic EDS mapping of NMCF0.1 along [100] zone axis. The pinned Fe in sodium layer is shown in the dotted‐line oval. GPA patterns of h) NMC, i) NMCF0.1, and j) NMCF0.2, corresponding to (a), (b), and (c), respectively.</p></caption></figure><p>Coin cells were assembled with sodium foil as a counter electrode to evaluate sodium‐ion diffusion in NMC, NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2. The TM pinned in sodium layer is generally considered detrimental to sodium‐ion diffusion during charge/discharge processes.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0037 #anie202100917-bib-0039 #anie202100917-bib-0041 #anie202100917-bib-0045"><sup>[10a,c,e, 12]</sup></link> Therefore, GITT was performed to examine the kinetic characteristics. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 a</link>, the sodium‐ion diffusion coefficients (<i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0001"/>) of NMC are 2.2E<sup>−14</sup>–2.0E<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, which is similar to the <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0002"/> of other layered cathode materials reported previously,<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0046"><sup>[13]</sup></link> suggesting the reliability of the <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0003.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0003"/> calculated by GITT data in this work. Take NMCF0.1 as an example to illustrate the calculation of <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0004.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0004"/> via GITT. Based on the Fick's law, <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0005.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0005"/> can be calculated by the following equation:<displayedItem xml:id="anie202100917-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><mediaResource href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0006.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0006"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="anie202100917-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100917-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>GITT curves and corresponding sodium‐ion diffusion coefficients <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0007.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0007"/> of a) NMC, b) NMCF0.1 and c) NMCF0.2. d) Rate and e) cycle performances of NMC, NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2. f) Long cycle stability of NMCF0.1 electrode at the current density of 10 C.</p></caption></figure><p>In this equation, <i>τ</i> is the pulse time. <i>n<sub>m</sub></i> and <i>V<sub>m</sub></i>, respectively stand for the molar amount and molar volume of the active materials in the electrode, and <i>S</i> represents the contact area between the active materials and the liquid electrolyte.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0049"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Δ<i>E<sub>s</sub></i> and Δ<i>E<sub>t</sub></i>, respectively stand for the potential change of galvanostatic charge/discharge and equilibrium state in the closest two processes, where they can be read from GITT curves (potential vs. time) in the Supporting Information, Figure S6a. The Δ<i>E<sub>s</sub></i> and Δ<i>E<sub>t</sub></i> in the charge and discharge process can be read from the Supporting Information, Figure S6b and c, respectively. The GITT curves (potential vs. specific capacity) of NMCF0.1 are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 b</link>, accompanied with <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0008.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0008"/> (0.8E<sup>−13</sup>–0.9E<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>) which is higher than that of NMC, suggesting that appropriate Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning exhibit negligible influence on sodium‐ion diffusion due to the two‐dimensional sodium‐ion diffusion paths. In fact, the enhanced sodium‐ion diffusion can be attributed to the increase in the cell parameter <i>c</i> of NMCF0.1 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0001">1 c</link>), which makes the sodium‐ion diffusion channel spacious. With more Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in the sodium layer, <i>D</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100917-math-0009.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-math-0009"/> of NMCF0.2 decrease to 1.3E<sup>−15</sup>–1.1E<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 c</link>), which is lower than that of NMC, suggesting that excessive Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in the sodium layer would hinder the diffusion of sodium ions.</p><p>The rate capabilities of the three electrodes were evaluated to reveal the influences of sodium‐ion diffusion coefficient or Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning on electrochemical performance under different current densities. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 d</link>, NMC shows similar rate capability as previous reports,<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0052"><sup>[15]</sup></link> while NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2 respectively show superior and inferior rate capabilities than NMC at the current density of 0.2–10 C (1 C=100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). The results are in accord with the sodium‐ion diffusion coefficient of the three electrodes. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) results (Supporting Information, Figure S7) show that Mn, Co and Fe all participate in charge compensation during charge and discharge, which explains why NMC, NMCF0.1, and NMCF0.2 exhibited similar specific capacity. The typical charge/discharge curves of NMCF0.1 are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S8 where the electrode shows a slight voltage hysteresis. Based on the curves, the energy efficiency is calculated to be as high as about 94 %. A high‐energy efficiency means that more power in a battery is effectively utilized, that is, less power is consumed in the form of heat. From this perspective, batteries with high energy efficiency may be substantially safer.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0055"><sup>[16]</sup></link> This suggests that NMCF0.1 shows great application prospects in high‐safety SIBs.</p><p>Generally, in addition to affecting the de‐intercalation of sodium ions in the electrode materials, the pinned TMs in the alkali metal layer are also considered to show significant impact on the structure/cycle stability of materials.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0038 #anie202100917-bib-0056"><sup>[10b, 17]</sup></link> Electrochemical cycle stability of the three electrodes were evaluated by galvanostatic charge and discharge at 2 C within the voltage window of 1.5–4.0 V. For the layered cathode materials with open two‐dimensional sodium‐ion transport channels, the structure of the material would be destroyed due to the slipping or peeling of the TM layer during the repeated extraction of sodium‐ions, which leads to the subsequent deterioration of the cycle performance.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0032 #anie202100917-bib-0061"><sup>[8d, 18]</sup></link> As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 e</link> and the Supporting Information, Figure S9, the capacity of NMC decays more than 30 % after 100 cycles. This rapid capacity decay process has been reported in many other layered cathode materials with different Mn/Co ratios or TM elements.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0064"><sup>[19]</sup></link> For NMCF0.1 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 e</link>; Supporting Information, Figure S9) with two‐dimensional sodium‐ion diffusion channel stabilized by pinned Fe<sup>3+</sup>, the capacity decay is reduced to about 10 % after 100 cycles. The increase of capacity retention rate from 68.7 % of NMC (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3</link>, black points) to 89.7 % of NMCF0.1 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3</link>, red points) can be attributed to the pinned multivalent Fe<sup>3+</sup> in the sodium layer which have stronger interaction with lattice oxygen than sodium. Thus, they can function as pins to maintain the layer structure or the two‐dimensional sodium‐ion diffusion path during repeated sodiation and de‐sodiation.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0030 #anie202100917-bib-0058"><sup>[8b, 17b]</sup></link> However, the capacity retention of NMCF0.2 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 e</link>; Supporting Information, Figure S9) with excessive Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in the sodium layer is more than 15 % lower than that of NMCF0.1, which can be rationally attributed to that excessive Fe<sup>3+</sup> in the sodium layer leads to drastic lattice distortion or anisotropic stress in the layered structure.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0058 #anie202100917-bib-0068"><sup>[17b, 20]</sup></link> Furthermore, the long cycle performance of NMCF0.1 was tested. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0003">3 f</link> and the Supporting Information, Figure S10, a high capacity retention of about 71 % is obtained over 1000 cycles. The layer structure of the material is well maintained after 1000 cycles (Supporting Information, Figure S11), suggesting the excellent structure stability of NMCF0.1.</p><p>The structural stability of layered NMCF0.1 with optimal pinning concentration was investigated through in situ XRD. The peak shifting and the corresponding color intensity mapping of (002), (004), (100), and (102) peaks are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0004">4 a</link> and the Supporting Information, Figure S12, where the electrode was charged and discharged at a current density of 0.2 C within the voltage window of 1.5–4.0 V. During the charge process, the (002) and (004) peaks related to the lattice parameter <i>c</i> continuously shift to lower 2‐theta degrees without appearance of new peaks and the (100) and (102) peaks related to the lattice parameter <i>a</i>/<i>b</i> continuously shift to higher 2‐theta degrees without appearance of new peaks, indicating the expansion of <i>c</i>‐axis and shrinkage <i>a</i>‐axis. The expansion of parameter <i>c</i> is due to the increased repulsive force between the two adjacent oxygen layers in the sodium layer arising from the removal of sodium‐ions from the crystal lattice. Meanwhile, the TM oxidation during the removal of sodium‐ions makes TM‐O distance shorter, thus the shrinkage of lattice parameter <i>a</i>/<i>b</i>. During the discharge process, the peaks shifting and lattice parameters show opposite behavior, indicating the reversible structure evolution. Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0004">4 b</link> lists the specific numerical changes in the lattice parameters <i>a</i>/<i>b</i> and <i>c</i> during charge and discharge, where the volume (<i>V</i>) variation is as low as 0.6 %. Furthermore, the volume variation is still as low as about 0.6 % after 100 cycles (Supporting Information, Figure S13). The results indicate that the NMCF0.1 electrode undergoes highly reversible solid‐solution structural evolution during charge and discharge, which well ensures the excellent structural stability and electrochemical stability.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100917-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100917:anie202100917-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100917-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The typical in situ XRD patterns of NMCF0.1 electrode at 0.2 C in the voltage window of 1.5–4.0 V. b) The variation of cell parameters <i>a</i>/<i>b</i> (pink points), <i>c</i> (green points), and <i>V</i> (blue points). c) <i>V</i> variation of NMCF0.1, LiCoO<sub>2</sub>, LiFePO<sub>4</sub>, several typical sodium‐storage cathode materials and two typical sodium‐storage layered anode materials (P2 type Na<sub>0.66</sub>Li<sub>0.22</sub>Ti<sub>0.78</sub>O<sub>2</sub> &amp; However, the volume variation of NMCF0.1 in this work is even smaller than that of so‐called zero‐strain Na<sub>0.66</sub>Li<sub>0.22</sub>Ti<sub>0.78</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>3</sub>Ti<sub>0.5</sub>V<sub>0.5</sub>(PO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>N, so NMCF0.1 can also be defined as a new zero‐strain material. The small volume variation of NMCF0.1 can be attributed to that the unfavorable slab sliding is suppressed because of the pinning effect.</p><p>DSC was also conducted to evaluate the thermodynamic stability of the three electrodes after charging to 4.0 V. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0004">4 d</link>, the three electrodes show exothermic reaction peaks between 200–300 °C in the DSC curves, similar to other layered cathodes.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0079"><sup>[31]</sup></link> The first exothermic peak represents the reaction between the electrolyte and the material, and the second exothermic peak can be attributed to the material structure transformation.<link href="#anie202100917-bib-0079"><sup>[31]</sup></link> Charged NMCF0.1 exhibits higher thermal decomposition peak position than charged NMC and NMCF0.2 (Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0004">4 e</link>). Heat generation, based on the peak areas, of charged NMCF0.1 (213.2 J g<sup>−1</sup>) is lower than that of charged NMC (607.9 J g<sup>−1</sup>) and NMCF0.2 (252.8 J g<sup>−1</sup>), shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100917-fig-0004">4 f</link>. These results show that the intelligently controlled Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in sodium layer can enhance the thermodynamics stability via improving the thermal decomposition temperature and lowering heat generation.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202100917-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>Accurate modulation of Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in sodium layer is achieved in layered cathode to obtain a new zero‐strain cathode material via the pinning effect, where an optimal component of NMCF0.1 shows the best electrochemical performance. STEM and XRD characterizations show that the Fe<sup>3+</sup> pinning in sodium layer occurs in NMCF0.1 and NMCF0.2. GITT, in situ XRD and DSC reveal that the pinning effect can allow rapid sodium‐ion diffusion, stabilize the layer structure, and enhance the thermodynamic stability during sodiation and de‐sodiation. This work provides a strategy to enhance the structural stability of layered oxide by pinning effect toward a zero‐strain cathode material for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202100917-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (22075132, 51802149, U1801251, and 11874199), the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities and the International Cooperation and Exchange Program by NSFC (11911530174).</p></section><section xml:id="anie202100917-sec-0005" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie202100917-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="anie202100917-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="anie202100917-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie202100917-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>N.</givenNames> <familyName>Yabuuchi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Kubota</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Dahbi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Komaba</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
