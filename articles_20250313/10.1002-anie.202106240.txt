However, it suffers from severe capacity decay during battery cycling due to the destabilizing Jahn–Teller distortions it undergoes as Mn<sup>2+</sup> is oxidized to Mn<sup>3+</sup>. Herein, the structure is stabilized by a thin epitaxial surface layer of nickel‐based Prussian Blue (Na<sub>2−δ</sub>Ni[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]). The one‐pot synthesis relies on a chelating agent with an unequal affinity for Mn<sup>2+</sup> and Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions, which prevents Ni<sup>2+</sup> from reacting until the Mn<sup>2+</sup> is consumed. This is a new and simpler synthesis of core–shell materials, which usually needs several steps. The material has an electrochemical capacity of 93 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, of which it retains 96 % after 500 charge–discharge cycles (vs. 37 % for MnPB). Its rate capability is also remarkable: at 4 A g<sup>−1</sup> (ca. 55 C) it can reversibly store 70 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which is also reflected in its diffusion coefficient of ca. 10<sup>−8</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>. The epitaxial outer layer appears to exert an anisotropic strain on the inner layer, preventing the Jahn–Teller distortions it normally undergoes during de‐sodiation.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A Mn‐based Prussian Blue analogue prone to destabilizing Jahn–Teller distortions upon oxidation is coated with an epitaxial layer of Ni‐based Prussian Blue. This outer layer exerts an anisotropic strain on the lattice of the inner layer, largely preventing structural degradation during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction and re‐insertion. As a sodium‐ion battery cathode, the material combines the high capacity and high stability of its two components.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202106240-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Because of their cheaper and more abundant raw materials, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have come into the focus of researchers worldwide as a potential alternative to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs),<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> especially in the domain of stationary energy storage.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0002"><sup>[2]</sup></link> For the moment, however, their real‐world viability remains held back by the paucity of scalable, high‐energy and stable electrode materials. The biggest classes of SIB cathode materials are metal oxides,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0003"><sup>[3]</sup></link> metal phosphates,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0007"><sup>[4]</sup></link> NASICON‐type materials,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0010"><sup>[5]</sup></link> and Prussian Blue analogues (PBAs). The lattermost group is considered particularly encouraging because of their simple and inexpensive synthesis and their high conductivities, which are rooted in their open framework structure that allows rapid diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0011"><sup>[6]</sup></link> PBAs, also known as metal metallocyanides, resemble metal‐organic frameworks in which two metals—usually Fe and another 3d transition metal—are bridged by cyanide ligands to form a cubic or near‐cubic lattice; Na<sup>+</sup> ions or H<sub>2</sub>O molecules occupy the interstitial cavities. Among the PBAs studied to date, sodium manganese ferrocyanide (MnPB) exhibits one of the highest capacities due its two redox‐active metals Mn and Fe.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0012"><sup>[7]</sup></link> However, this initially high capacity decays rapidly during battery cycling; it is generally agreed that this is rooted in the Jahn–Teller distortions the MnN<sub>6</sub> octahedra undergo when Mn<sup>2+</sup> is oxidized to Mn<sup>3+</sup>.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0011 #anie202106240-bib-0012 #anie202106240-bib-0013"><sup>[6–8]</sup></link> The strain exerted by this distortion destabilizes the lattice, causing it to gradually but continuously break apart. Charge disproportionation of Mn<sup>3+</sup> followed by dissolution of Mn ions, a phenomenon known to contribute to the cycling instability of many Mn‐containing compounds used in LIBs,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0016"><sup>[9]</sup></link> may also play a role,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0017"><sup>[10]</sup></link> although this has not yet been confirmed for Mn‐containing PBAs specifically. Strategies to improve the cycle life of MnPB have included coating with protective polymer layers,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0014 #anie202106240-bib-0020"><sup>[8a, 11]</sup></link> doping with electrochemically inactive elements such as Ni<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0021"><sup>[12]</sup></link> or Sn,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0025"><sup>[13]</sup></link> and dehydrating the structure.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0026"><sup>[14]</sup></link> However, all of these methods have disadvantages in terms of cost or scalability.</p><p>In this work, we report on a method of stabilizing MnPB via an epitaxial outer layer of nickel ferrocyanide (NiPB). The electrochemical properties of NiPB are the mirror image of MnPB: a low but extremely stable capacity.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0027"><sup>[15]</sup></link> The similar lattices of these two materials<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0030"><sup>[16]</sup></link> means that they can be coupled to one another if one is grown on the surface of the other. Epitaxial growth, in which a material's crystal layers have the same orientation as those of the substrate they are grown on, is known to contribute to the stability of certain Jahn Teller‐distorted phases that would not be stable on their own, such as spinel Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> grown on MgO.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0031"><sup>[17]</sup></link> There have been a few reports of epitaxially grown materials used in LIB cathodes.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0032"><sup>[18]</sup></link> A number of epitaxial Prussian Blue heterostructures have also been reported elsewhere, mainly focusing on their magnetic properties.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0035"><sup>[19]</sup></link> To our knowledge, this is the first report of a Prussian Blue phase, and the first Jahn–Teller distorted SIB cathode material, whose oxidized form is epitaxially stabilized. A recent work by Huang et al.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0041"><sup>[20]</sup></link> on a hollow MnPB@void@NiPB core–shell relied on the creation of voids within the particles and between the phase layers and did not address the Jahn–Teller dynamics. The one‐step procedure reported herein, relying on the unequal chelation of Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup>, also improves on the synthesis procedure of previously reported core–shell materials, which has to date always required multiple steps.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202106240-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The one‐pot procedure reported herein to synthesize epitaxial core–shell PBAs exploits the unequal binding strength of sodium citrate, a chelating agent used to control PB nucleation, for Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup>. The free energy of formation and stability constants both favor the [Ni<sup>2+</sup>‐citrate] complex over [Mn<sup>2+</sup>‐citrate].<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0022 #anie202106240-bib-0042"><sup>[12a, 21]</sup></link> As demonstrated in this work, this preference for Ni<sup>2+</sup> appears to be so pronounced that the Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions are released from their citrate complexes only once the Mn<sup>2+</sup> ions are fully consumed, which allows the NiPB to grow as an epitaxial layer on the existing MnPB surfaces. This one‐step procedure is attractive for its simplicity, as the synthesis of epitaxial Prussian Blue heterostructures has to date always required two‐step processes.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0036 #anie202106240-bib-0037 #anie202106240-bib-0038 #anie202106240-bib-0039"><sup>[19a–d]</sup></link> The synthesis method is similar to one reported by Chen et al.,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0022"><sup>[12a]</sup></link> although that work reported homogeneous, single‐phase materials with very low Ni‐contents.</p><p>To study its effect on the morphology and composition, the citrate concentration in the synthesis of MnPB/NiPB composites (hereafter referred to as MnNiPB) was varied at a constant Mn:Ni ratio of 1:2. Citrate contents of 0, 2, 4 and 6 equivalents relative to the total amount of Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> were used; the respective products are referred to as MnNiPB‐0xcit, MnNiPB‐2xcit, etc. (Table S1). According to energy‐dispersive <i>x</i>‐ray spectroscopy (EDS), all materials except MnNiPB‐0xcit, which is poorly crystalline and whose particles are not readily discernible (Figure S1), exhibit core–shell structures. Because it exhibited the highest stable electrochemical capacity of the four materials tested (Figure S2b), MnNiPB‐4xcit was selected for further study. The basic morphology and compositional heterogeneity of this material are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0001">1 a</link>–d; the corresponding data for the other materials are available in the supporting information (Figure S1,S2). Its particles are relatively uniform, with an average particle size of 341±49 nm (based on 107 nanocubes). EDS phase analysis (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0001">1 c</link>) reveals that the particles contain two distinct phases: a Mn‐rich core surrounded by a Ni‐rich outer layer. Intuitively, the phase boundary becomes sharper at higher citrate contents. However, a competing effect is that the more citrate is present during the reaction, the more the nucleation of NiPB is inhibited. This is compounded by the fact that the use of sodium citrate also results in larger particles. Consequently, smaller amounts of available NiPB must cover a higher MnPB surface area, meaning the NiPB outer layer grows increasingly thin the more citrate is used: 2, 4 and 6 equivalents citrate result in NiPB shells 22 %, 9 % and 6 % of the thickness of the MnPB core, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="anie202106240-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) SEM image and b) bright field TEM image of MnNiPB‐4xcit, c) EDS phase analysis and d) EDS element mapping of a MnNiPB‐4xcit nanocube, e) XRD pattern of MnNiPB‐4xcit, NiPB and MnPB; insets: close‐ups of the first two major peak regions.</p></caption></figure><p>For optimized electrochemical performance, the NiPB outer layer should be as thin as possible to maximize capacity, but still thick enough to be able to imbue its stability on the MnPB inner layer. MnNiPB‐4xcit best strikes this balance (Figure S2b). At higher citrate contents than this, the product resembles pure MnPB in its cycling behavior: a high initial capacity that declines rapidly. The coulombic efficiency tracks this pattern (Figure S2c). The use of chelating agents also decreases the proportion of crystal water in the material (see TGA data, Figure S3); although beyond 4 equivalents this approach yields no additional reductions in water content. Based on XPS data (Figure S4), the material synthesized without any sodium citrate is somewhat more susceptible to oxidation; the Fe 2p bands contain shoulders from the presence of Fe<sup>3+</sup>. Overall, MnNiPB‐4xcit, due to its optimal Mn:Ni ratio, crystallinity and low water content, offers the best compromise of capacity, stability and ease of synthesis.</p><p>To compare MnNiPB‐4xcit to those of its isolated components, samples of MnPB and NiPB were synthesized using conditions identical to those used for MnNiPB‐4xcit. Both materials were refined to monoclinic structures using FullProf<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0045"><sup>[22]</sup></link> based on the structure reported by Song et al.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0026"><sup>[14]</sup></link> (Figures S5,S6 and Tables S2,S3). This is consistent with previous findings; monoclinic structures of MnPB<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0014 #anie202106240-bib-0046"><sup>[8a, 23]</sup></link> and NiPB<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0049"><sup>[24]</sup></link> have been widely reported elsewhere. The obtained structures are highly similar but nonetheless distinct: the MnPB unit cell is 0.2–0.3 Å bigger along all three edges. This similarity in the lattices is what makes the growth of NiPB on the MnPB surface possible: Dumont et al.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0036"><sup>[19a]</sup></link> report a maximum lattice mismatch of ca. 0.5 Å to achieve epitaxial growth; above this threshold a mixture of single‐phase particles are produced. In the XRD pattern of MnNiPB‐4xcit, the peaks of both phases are present, with small but significant shifts in position (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0001">1 e</link>). The NiPB peaks are slightly weaker since the NiPB layer is thinner than the MnPB core. The MnPB and NiPB phases within the composite material were refined individually (Figure S7) using the refinements of the respective pure materials. The resulting lattice parameters differ slightly from the pure phases (Table <link href="#anie202106240-tbl-0001">1</link>): the MnPB lattice contracts while the NiPB lattice expands slightly. This is likely due to the stress exerted by the coupling of the lattices to one another, since they differ slightly in size. By the MnPB lattice contracting and the NiPB lattice expanding, the lattices become more similar to one another. The contractions and expansions are also conspicuously anisotropic: the parameter <i>c</i> of the MnPB phase decreases more than <i>a</i>, while <i>b</i> is essentially unchanged; in the NiPB phase, the magnitude of the shifts are reversed. This anisotropy may play a role in stabilizing the MnPB during battery cycling, which will be discussed in more detail further below.</p><tabular xml:id="anie202106240-tbl-0001" pRights="unknown" valign="top"><p><b>3.77</b></p></entry></row></tbody></tgroup></table></tabular><p>In electrochemical capacity, MnNiPB‐4xcit represents roughly the midpoint between MnPB and NiPB, as expected given that they are present in an almost 1:1 ratio. In the charge‐discharge curves (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 a</link>), the voltage plateaus of both the Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox pairs and Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> redox pairs are visible, the latter being much shorter since the material contains twice as much Fe as Mn. MnNiPB‐4xcit also combines the high capacity of MnPB with the stability of NiPB. At 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, it displays an initial capacity of 93 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 96 % of which is retained after 500 cycles (compared to 37 % for MnPB) (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 b</link>). Based on ex‐situ XRD data of the MnNiPB electrodes during cycling (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 c</link>), both phases undergo phase transformations similar to those of pure NiPB (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 e</link>) and MnPB (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 f</link>). The changes in cell parameters of the core–shell phases are similar to those of their component pure phases. Monoclinic MnPB has been reported to undergo a phase transition first into a cubic, then a tetragonal structure during charging,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0026 #anie202106240-bib-0047"><sup>[14, 23a]</sup></link> while monoclinic NiPB becomes cubic in a one‐step phase transition.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0049"><sup>[24]</sup></link> The present data are consistent with those previously reported, showing the melding of two peaks in the 23–25° into a single peak (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 d</link>–f). The high stability of NiPB shows that such phase transitions are not, on their own, problematic; it is the Jahn–Teller distortions that destabilize the MnPB lattice and which the core–shell structure of MnNiPB appears to prevent. The high cycling stability of MnNiPB‐4xcit is also reflected in the preservation of its lattice: the XRD pattern remains essentially unchanged after 100 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0003">3 a</link>), indicating the high reversibility of the phase transformation shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 c</link>. MnPB, in contrast, suffers a permanent structural breakdown over the same period (Figure S8).</p><figure xml:id="anie202106240-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) First‐cycle charge–discharge curves and b) cycling performance of MnNiPB‐4xcit, NiPB and MnPB, c) ex situ XRD pattern of MnNiPB‐4xcit, d)–f) close‐ups of peak regions in the ex situ XRD patterns of MnNiPB‐4xcit, NiPB and MnPB, respectively.</p></caption></figure><figure xml:id="anie202106240-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) XRD pattern of MnNiPB‐4xcit after 100 cycles, b) first to fifth CV cycles of MnNiPB‐4xcit.</p></caption></figure><p>It is well‐established that Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions are electrochemically inactive in Prussian Blue analogues, so only the Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> contribute to the electrochemical capacity. In the cyclic voltammetry (CV) data (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0003">3 b</link>), the distinct peaks of the Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> redox pairs are visible both during the charging and discharging steps. In contrast, interestingly, pure MnPB shows three redox peaks for both steps (Figure S9). The appearance of a third peak has been reported before and explained by the poor conductivity of MnPB after a certain portion of sodium has been extracted from the material,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0014"><sup>[8a]</sup></link> which alters the energy levels of the corresponding redox pairs. The absence of such a third redox peak in MnNiPB‐4xcit suggests that it does not suffer this drop‐off in conductivity. In addition, in a testament to its electrochemical stability, the CV graph of MnNiPB‐4xcit becomes stable after the 3<sup>rd</sup> cycle (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0003">3 b</link>), whereas that of MnPB displays significant current attrition throughout.</p><p>The capacity retention of MnNiPB‐4xcit at elevated charge‐discharge rates is presented in Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0004">4 a</link>. At 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> the material still delivers ca. 70 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, permitting a one‐minute charge or discharge time. The capacity is stable over 10 cycles at all current densities except the highest, 8000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>; a kinetically limited decay mechanism is likely responsible. The material is clearly an improvement over its constituent components, NiPB and MnPB, although the rate capability of NiPB is still higher in relative terms. The rate capability of MnPB also appears artificially lower due to its rapid degeneration during cycling: if its capacity at various current densities is measured using pristine electrodes, rather than consecutively with the same electrode, the rate capabilities are roughly comparable (Figure S10). The main contribution of the Ni‐rich outer layer to the rate capability is simply to improve the cycling stability of the Mn‐rich inner layer.</p><figure xml:id="anie202106240-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) rate capability of MnNiPB‐4xcit, NiPB and MnPB from 10 to 8000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, b) GITT curves, c)–d) diffusion coefficients as calculated by GITT during discharging and charging, e) CV curves at a range of scan rates and f) Randles‐Sevcik plot of the maximum currents recorded at each scan rate versus the square root of the scan rate. The error bands in (a), (c) and (d) refer to the standard deviations (<i>n</i>=5).</p></caption></figure><p>The shelf life of MnNiPB‐4xcit was studied by characterizing its physico‐chemical properties after being stored under ambient conditions for 15 months. The material's stability against moisture was also tested by suspending it in water for 24 hours, followed by centrifuging and drying. Both tests resulted in no damage to the material's properties. The XPS and XRD patterns show no evidence of oxidation or structural degradation (Figure S11,S12); more importantly, it retains its full electrochemical capacity and cycling stability. In light of the air or moisture‐sensitivity of many battery electrode materials,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0052"><sup>[25]</sup></link> a shelf life of over one year makes this material an attractive one for practical applications.</p><p>The diffusion coefficients of MnNiPB‐4xcit, MnPB and NiPB were determined via the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT). Pulses of 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> were applied for 10 minutes, followed by a 30‐minute relaxation period, until the voltage reached 4.2 V. The cell was then discharged to 2.5 V using the same procedure (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0004">4 b</link>). By evaluating the ratio of the increase or decrease in voltage during each pulse step to the actual change in steady‐state voltage this effects (after excluding the iR drop), the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient in the electrode material can be determined.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0055"><sup>[26]</sup></link> Graphs of the results for the discharge and charge steps over the applicable voltage range are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0004">4 c,d</link>. The diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> is slightly higher in MnNiPB‐4xcit than NiPB, despite the latter's higher relative rate capability; this disparity may be related to the lower particle size of NiPB. In MnPB, the diffusion coefficient is at least an order of magnitude lower at all points; in particular, a cusp appears in the graph at 3.45 V where it becomes very low. Such features are characteristic of materials in which two material phases coexist during a phase transition.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0055 #anie202106240-bib-0056"><sup>[26, 27]</sup></link> Diffusion coefficients from GITT are less reliable in this two‐phase region, since the movement of the phase boundary can limit Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion, which the model used to develop GITT does not take into account.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0057"><sup>[28]</sup></link> Nevertheless, the results imply that the phase transitions experienced by MnNiPB‐4xcit during charging and discharging (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0002">2 c</link>) do not cause significant phase‐boundary effects.</p><p>The diffusion coefficient can also be determined via the Randles‐Sevcik method<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0058"><sup>[29]</sup></link> by evaluating the relationship of CV scan rate and current. Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0004">4 e</link> displays the CV curves of MnNiPB‐4xcit at a range of scan rates between 0.2–1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The diffusion coefficient is typically calculated by plotting the current maxima against the square root of the scan rate (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0004">4 f</link>). The linear nature of the graph provides evidence for the reversibility of the Na<sup>+</sup> extraction; from the slope, the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient may be calculated for the voltage region the peak occurs in.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0059"><sup>[30]</sup></link> Values of 2.54×10<sup>−8</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> and 4.69×10<sup>−8</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> are obtained for the cathodic and anodic peaks, respectively—more than an order of magnitude higher than those obtained via GITT. Disparities between values acquired using GITT and CV are not uncommon<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0060"><sup>[31]</sup></link> and reflect the different conditions under which they are obtained: GITT is performed at low currents and near equilibrium conditions, while the Randles‐Sevcik method subjects the material to continuous and much higher voltage differentials. It is not clear in such cases which value is more accurate, although the values obtained by CV can be somewhat inflated due to the large concentration gradients that occur during measurements.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0064"><sup>[31d]</sup></link></p><p>The stabilizing effect of the outer NiPB layer on the MnPB phase is striking, especially considering it is only 9 % the thickness of the latter, and raises the question of its mechanism of action. Although the origin of the cycling instability of MnPB has not been studied specifically, for Mn‐containing materials generally in SIBs and LIBs it is usually ascribed to one or two related factors: (i) structural destabilization brought on by Jahn–Teller distortions upon formation of Mn<sup>3+</sup> and (ii) charge disproportionation of Mn<sup>3+</sup> followed by the dissolution of Mn ions into the electrolyte.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0016 #anie202106240-bib-0065"><sup>[9, 32]</sup></link> (The reaction of HF, a decomposition product of NaPF<sub>6</sub>,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0069"><sup>[33]</sup></link> with surface Mn<sup>3+</sup> is also sometimes cited,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0066"><sup>[32a]</sup></link> although this is unlikely in the present work, which used NaClO<sub>4</sub>‐based electrolytes.) These events are likely related—i.e., disproportionation may occur <i>because</i> of the Jahn Teller‐induced structural destabilization.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0066"><sup>[32a]</sup></link> The contributions of the Jahn–Teller distortions have generally received the most attention in the literature on MnPB.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0011 #anie202106240-bib-0012 #anie202106240-bib-0013"><sup>[6–8]</sup></link> Whatever the predominant reasons for the instability of MnPB, the epitaxial NiPB layer clearly alleviates them in some way. Moreover, the fact that the relative thickness of the NiPB layer is correlated to the stability of the material (for instance, MnNiPB‐6xcit has a NiPB layer ca. 50 nm thick, or 6 % the thickness of the MnPB phase, and experiences rapid capacity fading, as shown in Figure S2b) suggests that it affords more than simple surficial protection. In the following, a mechanism for this stabilization is proposed.</p><p>Jahn–Teller distortions occur in transition metal environments with degenerate ground states, as is the case with octahedrally coordinated Mn<sup>3+</sup>. This degeneracy is lifted by anisotropic changes in the bond lengths—usually a contraction of the equatorial Mn−N bonds and an elongation of the axial ones. This lowers the energy levels of the orbitals in the xy‐plane of the MnN<sub>6</sub> octahedron, resulting in a net total energy gain for the system. In MnPB specifically, the distortion manifests itself as a contraction of the four equatorial Mn−N bonds only,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0048"><sup>[23b]</sup></link> although the overall orbital dynamics remain the same<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0070"><sup>[34]</sup></link> (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0007">7 a</link>). Because [MnN<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> is Jahn–Teller distorted while [MnN<sub>6</sub>]<sup>2+</sup> is not, these octahedra undergo destabilizing asymmetric contractions and expansions with each charge and discharge cycle, causing the structure to gradually break apart.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0068"><sup>[32c]</sup></link></p><p>The NiPB shell of MnNiPB‐4xcit may prevent or stabilize the Jahn–Teller distortions of the MnPB core in some way. The most plausible mechanism would be an anisotropic strain, which would slightly alter the orbital dynamics that cause the distortions. As the Rietveld refinements already indicated, the XRD spectrum does indeed contain evidence of macrostrain: both the MnPB and NiPB phases of MnNiPB‐4xcit exhibit small but significant peak shifts relative to their “pure” materials. The diffraction angles of the MnPB phase increase slightly while those of the NiPB phase decrease, indicating a slight expansion and contraction, respectively of the respective lattices (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0005">5</link> and Table <link href="#anie202106240-tbl-0002">2</link>). The peak shifts are also noticeably asymmetric in magnitude, a sign that the strain is, in fact, anisotropic. Since no significant broadening of the peaks of the core–shell material is visible relative to the pure materials, the strains are homogeneous within their respective phases.</p><figure However, since the NiPB phase is also strained in the opposite direction, it more likely results from the coupling of the two lattices to one another. The NiPB phase forms directly on the MnPB surface and the two materials have very similar lattices—conditions which have been demonstrated to lead to epitaxial growth, in which the lattices of the two PBA structures are coupled to one another.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0037 #anie202106240-bib-0040"><sup>[19b,e]</sup></link> Scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) of the interface, which would permit visualization of the strain fields in the two phases, proved infeasible due to the material's sensitivity to the required beam currents. Nevertheless, the lattice could still be resolved using low dose, low temperature techniques developed for cryo‐electron microscopy.<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0071"><sup>[35]</sup></link> Magnification of the border region clearly shows a continuous and coherent lattice across the interface of the Mn‐rich inner layer and the Ni‐rich outer layer (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0006">6 a,b</link>). Separate local FFTs of the particle core and the outer edge regions (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0006">6 d</link>–e) also show identically oriented lattices.</p><figure xml:id="anie202106240-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Cryo‐HRTEM image of a MnNiPB‐4xcit crystal; the blue dotted line marks the border between the MnPB and NiPB phases based on the EDS data shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0001">1</link>, b) close‐up image of the border region, c) FFT of the entire particle, d) FFT of the core (MnPB phase) only and e) FFT of the outer layer (NiPB phase) only.</p></caption></figure><p>The precise nature of the strain anisotropy, that is, along which axes of the MnN<sub>6</sub> octahedra the strain runs, is not clear. However, it is plausibly responsible for the considerably improved reversibility of sodium extraction from the MnPB phase. An anisotropic contraction of the MnPB phase of the MnNiPB‐4xcit core–shell material in the pristine state—that is, before charging—would alter the energy levels of the Mn d‐orbitals. This could minimize or even prevent the Jahn–Teller distortion that normally occurs upon oxidation (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0007">7 a</link>). For instance, a strain exerted primarily on the equatorial Mn−N bonds would lower the energy levels of the orbitals on the <i>z</i>‐axis (d<sub><i>xz</i></sub>, d<sub><i>yz</i></sub> and <inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202106240-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-math-0001"/>) and raise those on the xy‐plane (d<sub><i>xy</i></sub> and <inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202106240-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-math-0002"/>) (Figure <link href="#anie202106240-fig-0007">7 b</link>). That would avoid the formation of a degenerate ground state upon oxidation of the Mn<sup>2+</sup> and prevent the associated Jahn–Teller deformation. This explanation would also hold if the anisotropic stress ran along the <i>z</i>‐axis rather than the <i>x</i>−<i>y</i> plane, since that, too, would result in the lifting of the orbital degeneracies, although the relative energy levels of the orbitals would be different.</p><figure xml:id="anie202106240-fig-0007"><label>7</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106240:anie202106240-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106240-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>illustration of the proposed mechanism of MnPB stabilization by anisotropic strain along with crystal‐field splitting of unmodified octahedral Mn<sup>2+</sup> (a) and Mn<sup>2+</sup> subjected to equatorial compression from the NiPB shell (b).</p></caption></figure><p>It should be noted that although the consensus in the literature ascribes a significant role to Jahn–Teller distortions and charge disproportionation in the destabilization of MnPB during battery cycling, this is largely derived from Mn‐containing cathode materials for lithium‐ion batteries and not necessarily directly transferable to the Mn‐sites in Prussian blue. In lithium manganese oxide, the active redox pair is Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup>, and it is the reduced form (Mn<sup>3+</sup>) that is unstable and disproportionates into Mn<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>4+</sup>, the former of which dissolves into the electrolyte. It is not clear that this applies to MnPB, which relies on the Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> redox pair and whose reduced form (Mn<sup>2+</sup>) is known to be structurally stable (and indeed usually synthesized in this form). Disproportionation may well still play a significant role in the capacity decay of MnPB, but the extent of this has not been the subject of specific study. It remains plausible that other mechanisms are at play in the stabilization of MnNiPB‐4xcit during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction and reinsertion. For instance, Mn<sup>3+</sup> and [Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3+</sup> ions do not form a precipitate in aqueous solution, suggesting that the fully discharged MnPB (Mn<sup>III</sup>[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]) may simply dissolve into the battery electrolyte even without the contribution of structural distortions.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202106240-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In this work, a series of Prussian Blue analogues containing nickel and manganese were synthesized using sodium citrate to control the particle size, Ni:Mn ratio and crystallinity. The citrate anion's uneven binding strength to Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> causes these materials to form as two discrete layers: manganese‐rich cores and nickel‐rich shells. While the differential binding strength of sodium citrate to Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Mn<sup>2+</sup> is known in the context of Prussian Blue syntheses,<link href="#anie202106240-bib-0022"><sup>[12a]</sup></link> it was not previously known that this effect can be exploited to create heterogeneities at the particle level. The material with the highest cycling stability, synthesized with 4 equivalents of sodium citrate, contains roughly equal amounts of nickel and manganese. This material was characterized in detail and found to offer an excellent compromise between MnPB and NiPB—combining the high capacity of the former with the stability of the latter. It exhibited a capacity of 93 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and a capacity retention of 96 % after 500 cycles, which is comparable to that of pure NiPB. Even at the very high current density of 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, this material still delivered a stable capacity of 70 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, giving it among the highest rate capabilities of sodium‐ion battery cathodes reported to date.</p><p>The diffusion coefficient was characterized via both the Randles‐Sevcik method and GITT and found to be around an order of magnitude higher than that of MnPB. Based on ex‐situ XRD, both the Mn‐rich and the Ni‐rich phases undergo phase transitions during charging and discharging much like their constituent pure components; however, in contrast to pure MnPB, these phase transitions are fully reversible and remain so even after extended cycling. This suggests that the nickel‐rich outer layer plays an active role in stabilizing the normally electrochemically unstable manganese‐rich core. The stabilization may lie in the way the particles grow: since the NiPB forms on the surface of an already‐formed MnPB template, its crystals grow epitaxially—that is, match the orientation of the MnPB. This allows it to exert a stabilizing counterbalancing strain on the Jahn–Teller‐distorted oxidized form of MnPB and prevent its disintegration. Such phenomena have been reported in other materials, but not to date for Prussian Blue‐based cathode materials.</p><p>The synthesis reported in this work provides a new and much‐simplified approach for preparing epitaxial core–shell particles and opens up the possibility of experimenting with layered Prussian Blue materials consisting of other metal pairs for which sodium citrate has unequal affinities, or testing other chelating agents. It may also be interesting to domains outside of battery applications, such as the exploration of the material's magnetic properties.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202106240-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge use of facilities within the UOW Electron Microscopy Centre.
