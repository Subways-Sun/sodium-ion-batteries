Herein, we report high crystalline and Na‐rich Prussian white Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> nanocubes synthesized by an optimized and facile co‐precipitation method. The influence of crystallinity and sodium content on the electrochemical properties was systematically investigated. The optimized Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> nanocubes exhibited an initial capacity of 151 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which is close to its theoretical capacity (170 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). Meanwhile, the Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> cathode demonstrated an outstanding long‐term cycle performance, retaining 78 % of its initial capacity after 500 cycles. Furthermore, the Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> Prussian white nanocubes also achieved a superior rate capability (115 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 92 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). The enhanced performances could be attributed to the robust crystal structure and rapid transport of Na ions through large channels in the open‐framework. Most noteworthy, the as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> nanocubes are not only low‐cost in raw materials but also contain a rich sodium content (1.87 Na ions per lattice unit cell), which will be favorable for full cell fabrication and large‐scale electric storage applications.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Inside the White (Nano)Cube</b>: High crystalline and Na‐rich Prussian white nanocubes were prepared using an optimized and facile co‐precipitation method. The influence of crystallinity and sodium content on the electrochemical performances of the materials was investigated. The as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> nanocubes presented outstanding electrochemical performances.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are widely considered as energy storage devices for electric vehicles (EVs) and energy storage systems (ESS).<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0001"/> However, the shortage, uneven distribution and high cost of lithium resources pose an obstacle to their applications in large‐scale energy storage. As an alternative to lithium‐ion batteries, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been extensively investigated owing to abundance of sodium.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0002"/> As an important component of sodium‐ion batteries, cathode materials play an important role on determining the overall performance of sodium‐ion batteries. Over the past few years, many cathode materials have been studied for sodium‐ion batteries, including oxide compounds,<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0003"/> phosphates<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0004"/> and fluorides.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0005"/> However, as for oxide compounds, close‐packed, oxide‐ion arrays connected by first‐row transition metal elements do not have enough interstitial space in the structure for rapid Na ions diffusion.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0006"/> On the other hand, phosphates and fluorides exhibit better rate performance, but their bulky three‐dimensional network structures limit their specific capacity.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0007"/> Prussian blue and its analogues (PBAs) are attractive candidates because they are host structures with tunable, open channels that allow the facile insertion and extraction of Na ions without lattice distortion.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0008"/> With the increase of sodium content, PBAs can be divided into Berlin green (BG), Prussian blue (PB) and Prussian white (PW).<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0009"/> Prussian white as a Na‐rich cathode material, is desirable to be coupled with non‐sodium metal anode to fabricate full Na‐ion batteries.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0010"/> Prussian white has a formula of A<sub>2</sub>[M<sup>II</sup>M<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>], (A=Na<sup>+</sup> or K<sup>+</sup>, M=Mn, Fe, Co, Ni), which means two electrons can be delivered per formula mass, giving rise to a high theoretical specific capacity of 170 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>However, this material generally suffers from low discharge capacity, poor cycling stability and low Coulombic efficiency due to the structural imperfection and the structure collapse caused by crystalline water in the compound.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0011"/> Accordingly, many efforts have been devoted to overcoming these issues. For instance, Guo's group<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0012"/> reported a Na‐rich cathode material prepared by simply controlling the reducing agent and reaction atmosphere during synthesis. This Na‐rich sodium iron hexacyanoferrate demonstrates a high specific capacity of 150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. Besides this, Cui's group<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0013"/> succeeded in crystallizing almost perfectly stoichiometric Na<sub>1.96</sub>Mn[Mn(CN)<sub>6</sub>] by using a large excess of sodium in solution during the synthesis, and achieved a high capacity of 209 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, proving the effectiveness of minimizing the fraction of vacant sites. Recently, Goodenough and co‐workers<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0014"/> successfully prepared a novel air‐stable and dehydrated sodium iron hexacyanoferrate (R‐Na<sub>1.92</sub>Fe[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]) by a hydrothermal method, demonstrating a superior performance as a cathode in Na‐ion batteries. Wu et al.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0009"/> adopted a chemical inhibition method to synthesize Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> with suppressed lattice defects and achieved a reversible 2‐Na storage reaction with a specific capacity of 150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. However, the rate performances of these materials are still not satisfied. Besides, carbon coating has also been widely applied to improve the cycling stability and low Coulombic efficiency.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0008b #asia201701715-bib-0015"/> In this work, we prepared high crystalline and perfectly shaped Prussian white Cobalt Hexacyanoferrate (CoHCF) nanocubes by an optimized co‐precipitation synthesis method. When applied as cathode materials in sodium‐ion batteries, the CoHCF nanocubes show an impressive sodium storage capacity (151 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) and outstanding cycling stability (long cycle‐life performance retained 80 % of the initial capacity at 500 cycles). Furthermore, superior rate capacities (111 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 91 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) are also achieved. This work provides a promising strategy for the development of high performance Prussian white cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Scheme <link href="#asia201701715-fig-5001"/> shows the schematic of the growth process of high crystalline Na<sub>2</sub>CoFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (H‐CoHCF) nanocubes. Firstly, we utilized a trisodium citrate chelating agent to coordinate metal ions. Then, the trisodium citrate coordinated metal ions slowly reacted with hexacyanometallic complex ions, allowing slow nucleation and controllable crystal growth. Next, a large amount of sodium chloride was added to increase the sodium content and suppress deficiencies in the hexacyanometallate. Consequently, Na‐rich H‐CoHCF naocubes were obtained.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-5001"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic of the growth process of H‐CoHCF nanocubes.</p></caption></figure><p>Numerous studies have shown that zeolitic water molecules in Prussian white frameworks will severely deteriorate the framework of the electrode materials.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0009 #asia201701715-bib-0016"/> The reason can be attributed to that zeolite water may compete with Na ions to occupy the interstitial spaces, resulting in blocking off the Na ions diffusion and decreasing the capacity of the Prussian white compounds. The mass ratios of H<sub>2</sub>O in the three samples were determined by Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) from room temperature to 500 °C. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0001"/> a, TG analyses of these samples show two distinct weight‐loss processes. The first process corresponds to the elimination of water adsorbed at the surface and zeolitic water absorbed in Na deficiency sites. The subsequent sharp weight loss is associated with the decomposition of the CoHCF framework. The water content of H‐CoHCF, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF samples is calculated to be 11.2 wt.%, 12.5 wt.%, 17.9 wt.%, respectively. Compared to the other two materials, H‐CoHCF sample shows the lowest crystal water content and greatly improved thermal stability (it does not decompose until 290 °C), indicating a more stable and well‐defined crystal structure. This result is well agreed with Infrared transmittance spectra <b>(</b>Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0001"/> b). The 3447 and 1636 cm<sup>−1</sup> peaks can be attributed to the stretching and in‐plane deformation of the O‐H, respectively. The very strong peak at 2082 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be indexed to the stretching vibration of CN ligands (Fe<sup>II</sup>‐CN‐Co<sup>II</sup>). The 598 cm<sup>−1</sup> peak corresponds to the in‐plane deformation of Fe−C bond.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) The thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) of the three samples at heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> in N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. (b) The Infrared transmittance spectra of the three samples in the wavenumber range from 4000 to 500 cm<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>Indeed, based on the Inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP‐MS) characterization, the metal atom molar ratios (Na:Fe:Co) are calculated to be 1.87:0.98:1.00, 1.47:0.90:1.00 and 1.79:0.87:1.00 (normalized to the Co content) for H‐CoHCF, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF samples, respectively. Thus, the three samples with the accurate chemical composition as Na<sub>1.87</sub>Co[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>0.98</sub>⋅2.2 H<sub>2</sub>O for H‐CoHCF, Na<sub>1.47</sub>Co[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>0.90</sub>⋅ 2.3 H<sub>2</sub>O for <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and Na<sub>1.79</sub>Co[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>0.87</sub>⋅3.8 H<sub>2</sub>O for R‐CoHCF, respectively (Table S1, Supporting Information). This result is mainly caused by the different content of NaCl and the nucleation rate during the preparation process. The H‐CoHCF with higher final Na content require a higher Na<sup>+</sup> content in the water solution during sample preparation. Adding excess Na source not only improves the Na<sup>+</sup> content of the sample but also contributes to stabilizing the crystal structure.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0006"/> On the other hand, the water content in H‐CoHCF and <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF samples are very low with the decreasing nucleation rate via the addition of trisodium citrate.</p><p>The crystal structures of the three samples were characterized by X‐ray diffraction (XRD). As shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0002"/> a, for H‐CoHCF and <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF materials, the wide‐angle XRD profiles show sharp peaks, indicating that the synthesized compounds are well‐crystallized without any impurities. In contrast, the diffraction peaks of the R‐CoHCF material have relatively low intensity, indicating poor crystallization. Comparing with the standard data of Fe<sub>4</sub>[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS No. 73–0687), the XRD peaks of all samples are shifted slightly toward lower diffraction angles, indicating an increase of the lattice parameters. Besides this, it should be noted that the (220), (420), (440) and (620) diffraction peaks for all samples (shaded parts) split into two diffraction peaks with almost equal intensities, which demonstrated all the samples had a rhombohedral instead of cubic structure (Table S2, Supporting Information).<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0017"/> A similar structural change has been reported for Na<sub>1.72</sub>Mn[Fe(CN)]<sub>6</sub> and Na<sub>1.92</sub>Fe[Fe(CN)]<sub>6</sub>.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0006 #asia201701715-bib-0014"/> Furthermore, the enlarged view of Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0002"/> a around 23°–30° is shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0002"/> b. Apparently, the higher the sodium content, the higher the intensity of the split peaks. The reason can be attributed to the different sodium‐ion concentration in the aqueous solution during precipitation, which has a strong influence on the composition and structure of the final product. With higher Na<sup>+</sup> concentration, a cooperative Na<sup>+</sup> displacement along a single [111] direction induces a transition from cubic to rhombohedral symmetry. The rhombohedral‐cubic structural phase transition process of CoHCF can be clearly visualized in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). The schematically sketch illustrates the structural change in the CoHCF lattice from a completely de‐sodiated cubic Co<sup>III</sup>Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub> phase to a Na<sup>+</sup> deficient cubic NaCo<sup>III</sup>Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub> phase and then to a fully sodiated rhombohedral Na<sub>2</sub>Co<sup>II</sup>Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub> phase. This phase transition is reversible according to the Na<sup>+</sup> concentration.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD patterns of H‐CoHCF, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF. (b) The enlarged view of Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0003"/> a around 23°–30°.</p></caption></figure><p>The morphology and crystal structures were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). As shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0003"/> a–c, both H‐CoHCF and <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF samples present a cubic morphology with sharp edges and flat surfaces with a uniform size ranges from 200 to 300 nm. This confirmed that citrate has a strong chelating ability, which can slow down crystal growth by coordinating with cobalt ions, allowing ordered growth of high crystalline and poly‐disperse nanocubes. Conversely, the R‐CoHCF sample has an irregular shape. In TEM images <b>(</b>Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0003"/> d–f), uniformly dispersed nanocubes can be observed in H‐CoHCF. Although <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF also presents nanocubes morphology, these nanocubes overlapped to some extent. Beyond that, zeolitic water may compete with Na<sup>+</sup> ions to accommodate the interstitial spaces due to a sodium deficient environment for <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF. Owing to rapid nucleation process, R‐CoHCF forms agglomerates with a particle size ranges from 30 to 50 nm.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a–c) SEM images of H‐CoHCF, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF (d–f) TEM images H‐CoHCF, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF.</p></caption></figure><p>For H‐CoHCF samples, we conducted high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) observation and selected area electron diffraction (SAED) to further analyze the detailed crystal structure. As displayed in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0004"/> a, the HRTEM image shows clear lattice fringes, in which the lattice spacing of 2.5 Å can be identified for the (400) plane. The SAED pattern (Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0004"/> b) reveals that the nanocubes have a single‐crystal structure, and the diffraction spots can be indexed to the (200), (220), (400) and (420) crystal planes along the [001] zone axis. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0004"/> c, the corresponding elemental mapping images are also presented. The EDS mapping confirms the uniform dispersion of Na, Co, Fe elements in the H‐CoHCF sample.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) HRTEM image of H‐CoHCF nanocubes (b) SAED image of H‐CoHCF nanocubes (c) EDS mapping for H‐CoHCF nanocubes.</p></caption></figure><p>The sodium storage performance of samples was evaluated by assembling CR 2032 coin‐type cells using Na metal as the counter electrode. The cyclic voltammogram (CV) curves of H‐CoHCF electrode are shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0005"/> a, two pairs of sharp and symmetric oxidation/redox peaks at 3.43/3.12 V and 3.83/3.74 V can be observed, which can be attributed to the nitrogen‐coordinated Co<sup>II</sup>/Co<sup>III</sup> couple and carbon‐coordinated Fe<sup>II</sup>/Fe<sup>III</sup> couple, respectively.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0017b #asia201701715-bib-0018"/> An oxidation peak observed at 3.43 V, corresponding to the transition from a fully sodiated rhombohedral Prussian white phase to a Na<sup>+</sup> deficient cubic Prussian blue phase. The second oxidation peak at 3.83 V is attributed to the Na<sup>+</sup> deficient cubic Prussian blue phase becoming a completely de‐sodiated cubic Berlin green phase, and vice versa.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) The cyclic voltammogram (CV) curves of H‐CoHCF electrode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> with a potential window between 2.0 and 4.5 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>); (b) Charge and discharge profiles of H‐CoHCF electrode at a current density of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>; (c) Cycle performances of three samples at a current of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>; (d) The specific capacities (1st and 100th) of three samples and (e) Long‐term cycle performance of H‐CoHCF electrode.</p></caption></figure><p>Moreover, in accordance with cyclic voltammetry (CV), charge and discharge curves (Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0005"/> b) further provide insights into the electrochemical process for H‐CoHCF electrodes. The voltage profiles on charge and discharge of H‐CoHCF in a Na half‐cell with a current of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> were tested. All the charge and discharge curves display two distinct and flat plateaus, indicating the entire electrochemical process is based on a 2‐Na reaction mechanism. The electrochemical reaction mechanism can be expressed as:<displayedItem xml:id="asia201701715-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/asia201701715-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-math-0001"/></displayedItem><displayedItem xml:id="asia201701715-disp-0002" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/asia201701715-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-math-0002"/></displayedItem></p><p>To further understand the influence of zeolitic water content and sodium content on electrochemical performances of three electrodes, the charge‐discharge curves are also presented (Figure S2, Supporting Information). Apparently, the high initial Na concentration of H‐CoHCF electrode is reflected in a high capacity of 151 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> contributing to an energy density as high as 510 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, which will be favorable for large‐scale electric storage applications. Although R‐CoHCF electrode is also achieved a high capacity of 150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, the third reductive plateau appears, which indicating the side reaction brought about by decomposition of zeolitic water during charging and discharging process. Thus, the crystal structure of the material might be damaged. Owing to the poor crystallinity, the initial capacity of <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF electrode is the lowest.</p><p>Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0005"/> c shows the cycling performance of three samples at a current density of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Obviously, H‐CoHCF and <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF electrodes exhibit similar and better cycling stability. However, R‐CoHCF electrode shows a relatively low cycling stability. The initial discharging capacities of H‐CoHCF, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF, and R‐CoHCF are 151.1, 128.8, and 148.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. It is quite clear that the H‐CoHCF electrode has the highest discharge capacity. After cycled for 100 cycles, the discharging capacities remains 128.7, 100.5 and 71.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0005"/> d). Although R‐CoHCF electrode appears to have an initial capacity as high as H‐CoHCF electrode, cycling performance is very poor, with a specific capacity of retaining only 48.3 % after 100 cycles. Therefore, we conducted XRD to further determine the structure evolution after cycling tests (Figure S3, Supporting Information). For H‐CoHCF electrode, the intensity and the location of splitting peaks are not changed, indicating that the rhombohedral structure is well maintained. However, for R‐CoHCF electrode, the intensities of the all peaks significantly decreased, especially for the peak at 17°. This demonstrates that the crystallinity of the electrode materials has degraded, leading to poor cycling performance.</p><p>Besides, the initial Coulombic efficiencies of the three samples are 99.5 %, 97.6 % and 87.8 % for H‐CoHCF <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF, respectively (Figure S4, Supporting Information). These results can be ascribed to the different sodium content and vacancy content in the samples. A high sodium content could effectively improve the actual capacity and obtain stable rhombohedral structure. Meanwhile, high crystallization could suppress vacancies in the samples to enhance the cycling stability and promote the capacity retention. Deficiencies might be expected to decrease the number of active sites for Na ion storage, but instead they capture Na ion, resulting in an irreversible reaction. Benefiting from the high Na ions content and low vacancies content, H‐CoHCF exhibits the highest discharge capacity (151.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), and superior cycling performance (85.2 % capacity retention over 100 cycles) among the three samples.</p><p>The cycling stability of the H‐CoHCF electrode at a moderate current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> is shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0005"/> e. The reversible capacity decreases slightly from 144.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> to 110.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over 500 cycles, corresponding to a capacity retention of ≈78 % with a high Coulombic efficiency near to 100 %. Moreover, the capacity fading of the electrode mainly occurs within the first 100 cycles, in the following cycles, capacity barely declines. Therefore, it can be inferred that structural evolution and adverse side reactions occur during initial cycles. In addition, the electrode was also tested at a large current density of 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The capacity still reaches 130 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and retains 83 % of the initial capacity for 100 cycles.</p><p>Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0006"/> a shows the discharge profiles of the H‐CoHCF electrode at various current densities, from 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> to 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The H‐CoHCF electrode delivers specific capacities of about 149.1, 145.1, 141.3, 130.7, 115.4 and 92.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 10, 50, 100, 200, 400 and 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. With the current density increasing, the capacity loss mainly occurs at the high‐voltage plateau, which signals a slower polarization of reduction of Co<sup>III</sup>N<sub>6</sub> octahedra than that of the Fe<sup>III</sup>C<sub>6</sub> octahedra. This high rate capabilities are superior to the previous reports for cubic Na<sub>2‐<i>x</i></sub>CoHCF (<i>x</i>&gt;1) Prussian white electrodes.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0019"/> For comparison, we also present the rate performances of three samples. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0006"/> b,c, <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF and R‐CoHCF electrodes show a dramatic decrease of the capacity when current density is increased. Although R‐CoHCF electrode exhibits a rather high capacity at low current densities, the discharging capacity only retains 40 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 800 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Interestingly, the discharge capacity of H‐CoHCF at 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup> is higher than that of <sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and R‐CoHCF at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. This behavior reflects the stable crystal structure of H‐CoHCF electrode. The robust structure and open framework are favorable for Na ion insertion and extraction.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0006"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) The discharge profiles of the H‐CoHCF electrode at various current densities (b) Specific charging and discharging capacities of three CoHCF samples at various current densities (c) The rate performances of three electrodes.</p></caption></figure><p>The electronic conductivity and charge transfer dynamics of the three electrodes have been further studied by Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS). As shown in Figure <link href="#asia201701715-fig-0007"/>, all Nyquist plots show similar features: a depressed semicircle in the high frequency region and sloping straight lines in the low frequency region. The semicircles in the high frequency region are associated with the electrode and electrolyte interface impedance of the charge transfer (<i>R</i>ct) process in the system; While the slopping lines in the low frequency region are correlated to the Warburg impedance (<i>Z</i>w) associated with sodium ions diffusion into the bulk of the electrode material. The H‐CoHCF electrode exhibits the smallest <i>R</i>ct (∼350 Ω) among the three electrodes (<sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF ∼450 Ω and R‐CoHCF ∼1300 Ω). Besides, the EIS of the H‐CoHCF electrode after cycled for 50 and 100 cycles were tested. The result reveals that there is no significant change of EIS for the H‐CoHCF electrode (Figure S6, Supporting Information). Furthermore, the sodium deficiency has been effectively suppressed by adding trisodium citrate during the synthesis process.</p><figure xml:id="asia201701715-fig-0007"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201701715:asia201701715-fig-0007"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201701715-fig-0007-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>EIS spectra for three different electrodes. When applied as cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries, the H‐CoHCF nanocubes exhibit a high reversible capacity of 151 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, an excellent cycling performance and superior rate capability in sodium‐ion batteries. These enhanced electrochemical performances could be ascribed to: (a) the high crystallization and suppressed deficiencies of H‐CoHCF obtained by adding chelating agent trisodium citrate, which are beneficial to the improvement of cyclic stability, and (b) the high sodium content during precipitation that has a strong influence on the composition and structure of the final product, resulting in enhanced reversible capacity and increased specific capacity. Hence, this modified synthetic method could open a new avenue for developing high‐capacity cathode materials for rechargeable sodium‐ion batteries. Furthermore, understanding the critical effects of crystallization and alkali metal content also provides an instructive insight for the development of other hexacyanometallates for alkali‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of high crystalline CoHCF (H‐CoHCF) nanocubes</title><p>All chemicals were of analytical grade and used without further purification. H‐CoHCF was prepared by a facile co‐precipitation method with some modification.<link href="#asia201701715-bib-0012 #asia201701715-bib-0013 #asia201701715-bib-0020"/> In a typical synthesis process, an aqueous solution (100 mL) of Na<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (2 mmol) in the presence of excess amount of NaCl (14 g) in a dark container. Meanwhile, CoCl<sub>2</sub>⋅6 H<sub>2</sub>O (3 mmol) and trisodium citrate (10 mmol) were dissolved into 100 mL deionized water and stirred for 1 h to form a clear solution. Then, the citrate<sup>3−</sup>‐Co<sup>2+</sup> chelate solution was added dropwise at a rate of 1 mL min<sup>−1</sup>. The reaction solution was maintained at room temperature and stirred for 12 h, then aged for 24 h. Finally, the obtained yellow‐green precipitate was centrifuged, washed three times with distilled H<sub>2</sub>O and alcohol then dried under vacuum at 100 °C for 30 h. For comparison, in order to explore the effect of crystallinity and sodium content on electrochemical properties of materials, rapidly crystallized CoHCF (R‐CoHCF) was synthesized using the same procedure with H‐CoHCF but in the absence of trisodium citrate. Low sodium content CoHCF (<sc>L</sc>‐CoHCF) nanocubes were also prepared using the same procedure with H‐CoHCF except a decreased amount of (7 g) sodium chloride.</p></section><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials characterization</title><p>The crystal structure of the samples was characterized by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) using a Bruker D8 Advance diffractometer with Cu<sub>Kα</sub> radiation. TGA measurement was conducted on a TG‐DSC instrument (NETZSCH STA 409 PC) in a temperature range from 25 °C to 500 °C at heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> in N<sub>2</sub>. The Fourier‐transform infrared (FT‐IR) spectra was tested by Bruker infrared spectrometer (VERTEX 80/80 v, Bruker, Germany) in a wavelength range from 4000 to 500 cm<sup>−1</sup>. The chemical composition of materials was determined by ICP‐MS (PE ICP 8300) calibration of Na, Co and Fe. The morphology and crystal structure of materials were characterized by scanning electron microscope (SEM, SU8010), transmission electron microscope (TEM, Hitachi H‐7500) and high‐resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM, JEM 2100).</p></section><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical testing</title><p>The electrochemical tests were carried out by assembling CR2032 coin cells using CoHCF as working electrode and a Na disk as reference and counter electrode. The work electrode was made by mixing 70 wt.% CoHCF, 20 wt.% acetylene black, and 10 wt.% polytetrafluoroethylene (PVDF) binder onto an Al foil then drying at 80 °C in vacuum for 12 hours. Glass fiber filter (Whatman GF/D) was used as separator. The mass loading of the active materials was about 0.8–1.0 mg. The electrolyte was a solution of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in PC with 5 vol. % FEC additive. All the cells were assembled in a glovebox with water/oxygen content lower than 1 ppm. Galvanostatic charge‐discharge tests were performed on a Land battery tester, with the cut‐off voltages between 2.0 V and 4.2 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). The cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements were carried out at a scanning rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> using a CHI660E electrochemical workstation. The Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) was carried out with 0.1 mV amplitude in the frequency range between 0.01 Hz and 100 KHz using a CHI660E electrochemical workstation.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia201701715-sec-0008" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>We acknowledge financial supports from the National Nature Science Foundation of China (No.21573110) and the Project Funded by the Priority Academic Program Development of Jiangsu Higher Education Institutions (PAPD).
