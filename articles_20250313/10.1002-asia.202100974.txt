Bismuth (Bi)‐based anode materials are considered as candidates for fast‐charging anodes of sodium‐ion batteries due to their excellent rate performance. Herein, we designed a two‐dimensional Bi/MXene anode material based on a hydrogen thermal reduction strategy. Benefitting from microstructure advantages, Bi/MXene anodes exhibited an excellent rate capability and superior cycle performance in Na//Bi/MXene half‐batteries and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C//Bi/MXene full‐batteries. Moreover, full‐batteries can complete a charge/discharge cycle in 7 min and maintain an excellent cycle life (over 7000 cycles). The electrochemical test results showed that Bi/MXene is a promising anode material with fast charge/discharge capability for sodium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>A two‐dimensional Bi/MXene nanocomposite</b> based on the thermal reduction of hydrogen was rationally designed and synthesized. It exhibited superior capability of fast‐charging and long cycle life as anode for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100974:asia202100974-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100974-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202100974-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The rapid development of human society depends on the massive consumption of fossil energy.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> After the industrial revolution, various machines powered by coal or oil, were constantly developed, a large amount of energy consumption has caused unprecedented energy crisis.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> In addition, the burning of a large amount of fossil energy has caused serious environmental problems such as haze, acid rain, and greenhouse effect.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0005"><sup>[3]</sup></link> People gradually realize the importance of environmental protection. It is a good way to develop renewable energy to deal with the current energy crisis and environmental problems.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0006"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, the common renewable energy sources such as wind energy, solar energy, geothermal energy, and tidal energy are uneven distributed in time or space, which need effective energy storage equipment to store and distribute the energy to the grid.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0010"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are a good choice for large‐scale energy storage.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0013"><sup>[6]</sup></link> And in recent years, it has also been widely used.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0010 #asia202100974-bib-0016"><sup>[5,7]</sup></link> However, the lithium (Li) on the earth is scarce and the distribution is extremely uneven in the world. It is far from meeting the future development for LIBs.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0019"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Therefore, the development of LIBs is limited. It is urgently developing a new battery system to replace lithium‐ion batteries. It′s worth noting that sodium (Na) and lithium have similar physical and chemical properties.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0022"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Moreover, sodium is abundant in nature and easy to obtain. Therefore, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted a lot of attention in large‐scale energy storage.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0023"><sup>[10]</sup></link> In addition, since the aluminum does not undergo an alloying reaction with sodium, the current collector of SIBs can use no copper foil, which will greatly reduce costs of battery production. Based on the above factors, the development of sodium‐ion batteries is feasible. It is an effective method to develop sodium‐ion battery materials by referring to the synthesis process of electrode materials of lithium‐ion battery.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0022"><sup>[9]</sup></link></p><p>SIBs are facing huge challenges in developing electrode materials with high current density charging and discharging, especially in terms of anode materials.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0024"><sup>[11]</sup></link> As common anode of lithium‐ion batteries, it is difficult for graphite to storage sodium ions.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0025"><sup>[12]</sup></link> Therefore, graphite is not a good choice for anode materials in sodium‐ion battery. Alloy materials [tin, antimony, germanium, bismuth (Bi), silicon and phosphorus] are ideal anode materials for SIBs due to their large specific capacity and low working potential.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0029"><sup>[13]</sup></link> It is worth noting that among these alloy anodes, Bi is an ideal choice for future SIBs due to its non‐toxicity, stable chemical properties, and high electrochemical activity.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0034"><sup>[14]</sup></link> In addition, Bi has a large layered crystal structure, which is conducive to the accommodation of sodium ions. So, it is an ideal fast charging anode.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0037"><sup>[15]</sup></link> However, as alloy‐type materials, Bi also has experienced huge volume expansion (240%) during the cycle.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0038"><sup>[16]</sup></link> This serious volume expansion will cause the electrode material to pulverize and fall off, ultimately reducing the battery performance. So, the volume expansion during the cycle is an obstacle to improving the cycle performance of Bi‐based anode.</p><p>Constructing a three‐dimensional conductive network can alleviate the volume change of the Bi anode during charging and discharging, and on the other hand, it can also enhance the electron transmission between active materials.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0039"><sup>[17]</sup></link> Conductive two‐dimensional materials are commonly used for constructing three‐dimensional conductive networks.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0043"><sup>[18]</sup></link> The graphene‐like material Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>X</sub>‐MXene has been widely used in the energy storage field due to its excellent electrical conductivity.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0044"><sup>[19]</sup></link> It is widely used in anode materials for metal ion batteries to solve the problem of poor conductivity.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0047"><sup>[20]</sup></link> In addition, since the ‐F, ‐O and ‐OH groups are produced during the synthesis process of MXene, which benefits the absorption of metal ions, which can improve the cycle stability of the battery.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0044 #asia202100974-bib-0051"><sup>[19,21]</sup></link> Therefore, it is a more effective method to combine Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub> nanosheet with Bi to improve the electrochemical performance of Bi‐based anode in sodium‐ion batteries.</p><p>Herein, we innovatively designed a two‐dimensional Bi/MXene composite based on the thermal reduction of hydrogen. The Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub>‐MXene nanosheets were successfully peeled off and blended with the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles then reduced by hydrogen. Benefitted from the active groups on the MXene nanosheets, the reduction Bi nanoparticles crystallized and uniformly absorbed on the surface of MXene during the synthesis process. In the composite, the two‐dimensional interface of MXene not only enables rapid electron and ion transfer, but also prevents the accumulation of Bi nanostructures during the cycling process. Furthermore, Bi nanoparticles can prevent the restacking of MXene nanosheets. Therefore, the multiple synergistic effect keeps the active region of the anode not lost. In addition, the synergistic effect of the pseudo‐capacitance of MXene and the larger interlayer spacing of Bi crystals improves the high‐current charging and discharging capabilities of Bi/MXene anode. Tested in the half‐cell system, the Bi/MXene anode could stably cycle 2500 cycles and maintained a specific capacity of 312 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C (NVP/C) cathode was synthesized. The cathode was matched with Bi/MXene anode, Bi/MXene//NVP/C full battery showed excellent electrochemical performance, especially under the high current density. The full battery can complete a charge and discharge cycle within 7 mins. Furthermore, the full battery can cycle 7000 cycles and still maintain a specific capacity of 53 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (calculated based on the mass of the positive electrode material), which is 78.83% of the initial discharge capacity. The capacity decay rate per cycle is only 0.003%. All electrochemical test results show that Bi/MXene is a promising anode material for sodium‐ion batteries with fast charging and discharging capabilities.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100974-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0001">1</link> shows the synthesis steps of the composite. Firstly, bismuth nitrate and thioacetamide formed Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles by hydrothermal reaction. At the same time, the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub>‐MXene nanosheets are stripped from MAX (Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub>) by the etching of hydrofluoric acid, and it is evenly dispersed in the aqueous solution. Next, the obtained nanoparticles were added to the MXene suspension, then the suspension was freeze‐dried. The mixture of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> and MXene nanosheets was reduced to Bi/MXene. Due to the surface functional groups on the MXene, the Bi nanoparticles are uniformly anchored on the surface of the nanosheets.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100974-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100974:asia202100974-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100974-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The synthesis route of Bi/MXene.</p></caption></figure><p>The microscopic morphology of MXene was observed by scanning electron microscope (SEM). As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0002">2</link>a, a typical two‐dimensional flake morphology showed that MXene nanosheets was successfully synthesized. The lamellar accumulation folds of MXene flakes can be seen through transmission electron microscope (TEM) (see Figure S1, Supporting Information). The morphological structure of large layer ensures the continuity of electronic conduction of the electrode during the cycle, thereby enhancing the cycle stability. Figure S2 (Supporting Information) shows the SEM images of the Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MXene precursor. Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> with a particle size about 50 nm is irregularly distributed on the surface of MXene. After hydrogen thermal reduction, Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> precursor was converted to Bi. Besides, the reduction temperature is higher than the melting point of Bi, the obtained Bi will become liquid. Due to the absorption of active groups (‐OH, ‐F) on MXene, Bi nanoparticles uniformly anchored on the surface of MXene during the cooling and crystallization process. Bi nanoparticles uniformly distributed on the MXene nanosheets ultimately. The morphology of Bi/MXene can be verified from SEM and TEM images (Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0002">2</link>b and c). The high‐resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) image was shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0002">2</link>(d), the layered stack structure of MXene and the lattice fringes of the (012) crystal plane of Bi were successfully discovered. In addition, the elements distribution of Bi/MXene were identified by High‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) in Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0002">2</link>(e), the Bi nanoparticles are uniformly distributed on the surface of the MXene. The existence of Ti, C, O, and F in MXene can be found and their uniform distribution indicated the successfully synthesis of composite. In particular, the presence of O and F elements further proves the presence of ‐OH and ‐F active groups on the surface of MXene.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100974-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100974:asia202100974-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100974-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) SEM image of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub>‐MXene; (b) SEM image of Bi/MXene; (c) TEM image of Bi/MXene; (d) HRTEM image of Bi/MXene; (e) HAADF‐STEM image of Bi/MXene and the corresponding distribution of Bi, Ti, C, O, and F elements.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure S3 (Supporting Information) showed the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) of MXene. The characteristic diffraction peak of MXene (002) at about 6.5 degrees was observed, and other characteristic peaks were also apparent. The diffraction spectra of Bi/MXene composite were shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0003">3</link>(a). Because Bi nanoparticles contributed the main capacity in the sodium‐ion storage process, the more obvious diffraction peaks can be classified as characteristic diffraction peaks of Bi crystals, which also proves that Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was completely reduced to Bi. The crystal plane of MXene (002) is observed from the XRD diffraction spectrum.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0052"><sup>[22]</sup></link> The chemical bonding conditions of the Bi/MXene composite were analyzed by XPS. Figure S4 (Supporting Information) shows the XPS spectra of Bi/MXene composites. In the spectrum, all elements in the composite can be indexed as Ti, C, Bi, O and F elements. No S element was detected, which further verified that Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> had been completely reduced to metallic Bi. Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0003">3</link>(b) and (c) showed the spectrum of Ti 2p and O 1s. The binding energy was consistent with the binding energy of Ti 2p and O 1s in MXene (Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0003">3</link>e and f) which proves that the reduction process of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> did not change the chemical bonding of MXene.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0053"><sup>[23]</sup></link> It is worth noting that there is a new Bi−O bond generated, which should attribute to the Bi combines with active group of ‐OH on the surface of MXene.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0056"><sup>[24]</sup></link> In addition, in the spectrum of the Bi 4f, two peaks corresponding to the 4f<sub>5/2</sub> and 4f<sub>7/2</sub> orbitals of metallic Bi, two peaks of bismuth oxide appear in the spectrum, which proves the chemical bonding between the Bi nanoparticles and the MXene in Bi/MXene composite.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0037 #asia202100974-bib-0057"><sup>[15,25]</sup></link> Due to the strong chemical bonding, the Bi nanoparticles can be uniformly dispersed and anchored in the Bi/MXene composite.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100974-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100974:asia202100974-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100974-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD spectra of Bi/MXene; (b) Ti 2p, (c) O 1s and (d) Bi 4 f spectra of Bi/MXene; (e) Ti 2p and (f) O 1s spectra of MXene.</p></caption></figure><p>The binding effect of Bi and MXene suggests that the composite may exhibit excellent electrochemical properties. Then, the sodium storage capacities of MXene and Bi/MXene were evaluated in half battery. Firstly, the sodium storage capacity of pure MXene was evaluated (Figure S5 Supporting Information), MXene electrode exhibited excellent cyclic stability (60 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and satisfactory rate performances. This may be attributable to the pseudocapacitive reaction on the surface of MXene.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0044"><sup>[19]</sup></link> However, MXene as anode of sodium‐ion battery, the low capacity limits its application. So, compounding with Bi to obtain larger specific capacity is an effective strategy. In order to detect the oxidation/reduction reaction of Bi/MXene in the Bi/MXene//Na half cell, a cyclic voltammetry (CV) test was carried out. Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0004">4</link>(a) shows the CV result of the first three cycles. In the scan, two pairs of obvious redox reaction peaks (0.48/0.61 V and 0.68/0.76 V) were detected, which corresponds to the reversible reaction of sodium ions with Bi (Na<sup>+</sup>+Bi↔NaBi and 3Na<sup>+</sup>+Bi↔Na<sub>3</sub>Bi).<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0058"><sup>[26]</sup></link> In the first cycle, the width and intensity of the peaks do not match well with the subsequent cycles, which should contribute to the decomposition of electrolyte and some irreversible reactions (such as the forming the solid electrolyte interface (SEI). As the cycle goes on, CV curves gradually overlapping indicated that the Bi/MXene anode has good electrochemical reversibility. Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0004">4</link>(b) showed the capacity‐potential curves of Bi/MXene anode cycled in 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The curves showed two charge‐discharge plateaus which were consistent with the CV results. In the first cycle, the discharge specific capacity is 460 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and the charge specific capacity is 350 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, the coulombic efficiency reaches 76%. After three cycles, the discharge capacity becomes stable. Specifically, the Bi@MXene can exhibit a reversible charge/discharge plateau after 500 cycles. Such results demonstrate that the Bi in the composite has high reactivity and utilization. In the long‐term cycle test the Bi/MXene anode can stable cycled 2500 cycles at large current density of 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> and maintain a capacity of 301 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0004">4</link>c). In the cycle, the anode quickly reaches a stable state, which should be attributed to the Bi nanoparticles absorbed uniformly on the nanosheet can fully contact with the electrolyte. In addition, the MXene sheets also prevent the active material accumulation during cycle. Furthermore, the rate capability was also be evaluated (Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0004">4</link>d). The current density was gradually increased from 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> to 8.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and the specific capacities of 329 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>), 326 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>), 325 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>), 324 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>(0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>), 323 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>), 320 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>(2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>), 312 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and 307 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (8.0 A h g<sup>−1</sup>) were obtained. Surprisingly, a specific capacity of 324 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> also obtained when the current density decreased to 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The rate capability result demonstrate the Bi/MXene anode has superior reversibility in high current density.</p><figure xml:id="asia202100974-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202100974:asia202100974-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202100974-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves of Bi/MXene anode; (b) Charge/discharge curves of Bi/MXene anode at different cycles; (c) Long‐term cycling performance of Bi/MXene anode; (d) Rate capabilities.</p></caption></figure><p>The electrochemical test results showed the Bi/MXene anode exhibited excellent sodium‐ion storage performance at high current density. In order to figure out the reason for this result. CV was performed from 0.2 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> to figure out the pseudocapacitive contribution in the electrochemical performance. As shown in Figure <link href="#asia202100974-fig-0005">5</link>(a), with the scan rate increasing, the redox peak position is slightly shifted which is attributed to the polarization of the electrode. Specifically, the 2D structure prevents the aggregation of Bi active materials from aggregation during cycling, and the large sheet of MXene ensures the contact between the electrode and the electrolyte. Additionally, the MXene nanosheets not only serve as conductive pathway for electrons transport but also as buffer layer for alleviating volume expansion of Bi during the cycle.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100974-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In conclusion, we synthesized a novel 2D Bi/MXene composite as anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries. In this composite, Bi nanoparticles were uniformly anchored on the MXene nanosheets, which can fully contact with electrolyte. The larger MXene nanosheets enhanced the electronic conduction and alleviated the volume expansion of Bi during sodium‐ion insertion/extraction. Benefitting from the advantages of structural construction, the anode exhibited excellent sodium‐ion storage performance, including long cycle stability and remarkable rate capability. Furthermore, Bi/MXene//NVP/C full battery also exhibited satisfactory cycling stability at high current density (53 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 7000 cycles at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>). Electrochemical test results show that the Bi/MXene is a promising anode material for fast‐charging sodium‐ion battery.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100974-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p>Synthesis of Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>: 1 mmol Bi (NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub> ⋅ 5H<sub>2</sub>O was dissolved in 8 mL ethylene glycol, and then 1 mmol thioacetamide was dissolved in 8 mL deionized water. After the two compounds were fully dissolved, thioacetamide solution was added dropwise to the bismuth nitrate solution, and then the suspension was transferred to a polytetrafluoroethylene reactor and put into a blast drying oven, and kept at 140 °C for 8 h. After cooling, the product was washed repeatedly with deionized water and ethanol to obtain Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles.</p><p>Synthesis of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub>‐MXene: The Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<sub>x</sub>‐MXene was obtained by etching the MAX(Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub>) phase. 5 mL of deionized water was added to the polytetrafluoroethylene beaker, then 1 g of LiF was added to the deionized water. Then 15 mL of concentrated hydrochloric acid was added. When the LiF was completely dissolved, 1 g Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> was added. The reaction was kept at 35 °C for 24 h. The product was centrifuged and washed with oxygen‐free water until the pH&gt;6.5. Next, the suspension was sonicated for 30 minutes and centrifuged for 1 h (3500 r/min). The obtained black product that was the MXene dispersion.</p><p>Synthesis of Bi/MXene: Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was added to the MXene dispersion with a certain proportion (MXene: Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>=3 : 10). The mass of the MXene can be calculated by freeze‐drying the MXene dispersion. After ultrasonic dispersion, the suspension is freeze‐dried. Then the solid product was placed in a tubular furnace and reduced by H<sub>2</sub>/Ar (5% H<sub>2</sub>). The temperature was kept at 550 °C for 3 h and the heating rate is 5 °C /min. After cooling to room temperature, the Bi/MXene material was obtained.</p><p>Synthesis of NVP/C: The NVP/C was synthesized according to our previous report.<link href="#asia202100974-bib-0060"><sup>[28]</sup></link> In the typical synthesis process, glucose, ammonium metavanadate and sodium dihydrogen phosphate were added into the ball mill tank at the ratio of 0.8 : 1 : 2, and an appropriate amount of ethanol was added. Then the mixture was ball milled for 24 h. After drying, the precursor was transferred into a tube furnace with nitrogen and calcined at 850 for 5 hours.</p><p>Electrochemical tests. The electrochemistry test of anodes and NVP/C cathode in half cells were performed in CR‐2032 type‐cell. For the Bi/MXene and MXene anode test, the electrode was composed of active material, super‐P, and CMC binder (mass ratio of 7 : 2 : 1). And the current collector was copper foil. The mass loading is about 1.0 g/cm<sup>2</sup>. For the cathode test, the active material, super‐P, and PVDF binder was coated on aluminium foil with mass ratio 7 : 2 : 1. The mass loading of the cathode is about 3.2 g/cm<sup>2</sup>. Bi/MXene//NVP@C full battery performance was evaluated in CR‐2016 type‐cell. After assembling the full battery, Bi/MXene anode should be activated for 3 cycles in half cell. The temperature was maintained at 25 °C during the test.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100974-sec-0005" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>Chengyin Wang would like to acknowledge the supported by the Priority Academic Program Development Project of Jiangsu Higher Education Institutions.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202100974-sec-0006" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="asia202100974-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="asia202100974-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="asia202100974-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="asia202100974-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Yang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.
