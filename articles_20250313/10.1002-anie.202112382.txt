However, as an ultimate solution, the use of non‐flammable electrolyte is susceptible to the side effects, and its poor compatibility with electrode, causing failure of batteries. Here, we report a non‐flammable electrolyte design to achieve high‐performance sodium ion battery, which resolves the dilemma via regulating the solvation structure of electrolyte by hydrogen bonds and optimizing the electrode–electrolyte interphase. The reported non‐flammable electrolyte allows stable charge‐discharge cycling of both sodium vanadium phosphate@hard carbon and Prussian blue@hard carbon full pouch cell for more than 120 cycles with a capacity retention of &gt;85 % and high cycling Coulombic efficiency (99.7 %).</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Safety is always a topic of concern after reports of devices catching fire due to battery failure. Using non‐flammable electrolyte as an ultimate solution is difficult to be applied to commercial batteries because of various problems such as poor compatibility, high viscosity and so on. Here, we report a non‐flammable electrolyte with excellent comprehensive performance, and applied it to sodium‐ion pouch cells with different promising materials.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112382:anie202112382-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112382-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="anie202112382-ntgp-0004"><note xml:id="anie202112382-note-1001"><label>+</label><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202112382-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>To live in a sustainable society, developing energy storage systems with all the features combining high energy density, cost‐effectiveness, environmental friendliness, and safety is still one of the greatest challenges for mankind. Currently commercially available lithium ion batteries (LIBs) cannot fulfil all these requirements due to the serious safety hazards and cost issues that arise if used for large‐scale grid energy storage.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Sodium ion batteries (SIBs) appear to be the most appealing strategy, owing to the practically infinite resources of sodium minerals that are evenly distributed worldwide with much lower cost than lithium.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Similar to their Li counterparts, however, sodium has high chemical activity, and the use of conventional carbonate‐based electrolyte are the dominant factors that pose significant safety hazards under conditions of abuse, such as mechanical shock, thermal shock, overcharge, short‐circuiting, and others.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0004 #anie202112382-bib-0009"><sup>[1c, 3]</sup></link> Furthermore, the practical applications of these promising electrode materials are greatly hindered by the poor electrode‐electrolyte interphase layers formed on such electrode surfaces in conventional carbonate‐based electrolytes such as sodium hexafluorophosphate (NaPF<sub>6</sub>) in ethylene carbonate/diethyl carbonate (EC/DEC).<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0012"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Therefore, advanced electrolytes with higher safety that can work on both the anode and the cathode sides in one full cell are critically important for enabling application of SIBs. Changing the composition of the electrolyte by using fire‐retardant or non‐flammable solvents seem to be an effective solution to reduce the risk of battery accidents.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0013"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Significant efforts have been made to develop these novel electrolytes, mainly through using solvents containing phosphorus or fluorine.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0012 #anie202112382-bib-0016"><sup>[4, 6]</sup></link> Nevertheless, due to their inferior passivation capability, these solvent molecules show poor compatibility with most electrode materials, especially carbonaceous materials, which are regarded as the most promising anode materials for industrial applications of SIBs.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0017 #anie202112382-bib-0019"><sup>[6a, 7]</sup></link> Therefore, the importance of inhibiting the oxidative decomposition of free solvent molecules on the surface of the anode to enhance the compatibility of carbonaceous materials with non‐flammable solvents has been highlighted recently.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0020"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Wang et al. demonstrated remarkable cycling performance of hard carbon (HC) with a highly concentrated non‐flammable electrolyte consisting of 3.3 M sodium bis(fluorosulfonyl)imide (NaFSI) in trimethyl phosphate (TMP), which was attributed to the enhanced interaction between the cations and the solvent molecules, as well as the robust salt‐derived solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0004 #anie202112382-bib-0021"><sup>[1c, 9]</sup></link> Zheng et al. developed a highly fluorinated electrolyte consisting of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in fluoroethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate/1,1,2,2‐tetrafluoroethyl‐2′,2′,2′‐trifluoroethyl ether/perfluoro‐2‐methyl‐3‐pentanone (FEC/PC/HFE/PFMP), which significantly enhanced the cycling stability of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>F cathode by forming an F‐rich cathode electrolyte interphase (CEI) on the surfaces of the particles.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0022"><sup>[10]</sup></link> In fact, the practical application of non‐flammable electrolytes in SIBs needs comprehensive consideration of their conductivity, viscosity, thermal stability, electrochemical stability, and intricate compatibility with the electrodes.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0023"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Recently, Zhang′s group proposed the concept of localized high‐concentration electrolytes to reduce the viscosity of high‐concentration electrolyte by introducing an “inert” fluorinated ether cosolvent, where the original solvation units are believed to be “maintained”.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0026"><sup>[12]</sup></link> Despite some outstanding performance, there is still no successful report on non‐flammable SIBs that are compatible with both cathode and anode performance in one full cell and have verified safety.</p><p>Herein, a non‐flammable electrolyte based on 1.2 M sodium bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide‐TMP/bis(2,2,2‐trifluoroethyl) ether/vinylene carbonate (1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC, with a mole ratio of NaTFSI:TMP:BTFE:VC=0.53:1:1.5:0.15) electrolyte, where an intrinsic non‐flammable property with stable charge‐discharge cycling can be synchronously achieved. In this design, the highly fluorinated ether of BTFE is applied as a diluent to disperse the highly concentrated salt‐solvent clusters, while VC serves as a “recovery solvent” and film forming additive. The polar additive VC is critical for reducing the free TMP molecules to recover the solvated structure destroyed by BTFE and facilitates the formation of stable electrode‐electrolyte interphase. Therefore, the optimization of the solvation structure via regulating the effects of intermolecular H‐bond and tuning the composition of both cathode and anode electrolyte interphase has been developed with high safety and excellent cycling stability in both Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>∥commercial hard carbon (NVP∥HC) and Prussian blue∥commercial hard carbon (PB∥HC) pouch cells. Our non‐flammable electrolyte has demonstrated stable cycling performance with enhanced safety performance, which provides a guidance for the commercialization of SIBs with high safety and superior electrochemical performance.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202112382-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in EC/DEC (1:1 vol%) was typically used in SIBs. This electrolyte was highly flammable, however, and has poor compatibility with electrodes due to the continual decomposition of the electrolyte and its less‐stable passivation film protection of electrode surfaces (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 a</link> and Figure S1). Although a high salt concentration in phosphates has proved to be an effective strategy to improve the safety and electrochemical stability of the lithium/sodium battery,<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0004 #anie202112382-bib-0030"><sup>[1c, 13]</sup></link> commercialization of the high‐concentration electrolytes is still challenging due to their high viscosity and low ionic conductivity. In this context, we diluted the high‐concentration electrolyte consisting of NaTFSI in TMP (3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP, corresponding to a NaTFSI/TMP molar ratio of 0.53:1) with an electrochemically “inert” and poorly solvating fluorinated ether (BTFE) to form a localized high‐concentration electrolyte (1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE, corresponding to a NaTFSI/TMP/BTFE molar ratio of 0.53:1:1.5), which effectively reduces the viscosity and improves the ionic conductivity of the electrolyte. Unfortunately, with the introduction of BTFE diluent, the electrochemical window and cycling performance of Na∥Na symmetric cells in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte became narrower and poorer than that in the original electrolyte (3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP) (Figure S1 and S2). In addition, excess BTFE causes NaTFSI recrystallization of the substrate by breaking the solvation balance in the original solution (Figure S3). Even with the use of other diluents and phosphate solvents, such a phenomenon has not been alleviated (Figure S4). This phenomenon may be caused by intermolecular H‐bonds which are formed between TMP and BTFE molecules, and the details will be discussed later.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0031"><sup>[14]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202112382-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112382:anie202112382-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112382-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Physical properties and electrochemical stability of the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. a),b) Flammability tests for the conventional 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC electrolyte, and the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. c) Frontier molecular orbital levels of TMP, BTFE, TFSI<sup>−</sup>, and VC. The red and blue regions represent the positive and negative parts of the LUMO and HOMO wave functions, respectively. d) Galvanostatic plating/stripping of Na/Na symmetrical cells in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte at 1.0 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, 2.0 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. e) The CVs of NVP, PB and HC electrodes in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, and the electrochemical stability window of 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. f),g) Long‐term cycling stability test of NVP∥Na, PB∥Na, and HC∥Na cells in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte (1 C equals 113 mA g<sup>−1</sup> or ca. 3.5 mA cm <sup>−2</sup> of NVP, 75 mA g<sup>−1</sup> or ca. 3.3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> of PB, and 300 mA g<sup>−1</sup> or ca. 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for HC,). h) Comparison of the properties and performances of the conventional 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC, high‐concentration 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP, and diluted 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte.</p></caption></figure><p>To restore the damaged Na<sup>+</sup> solvent structure and improve the electrochemical stability of the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte, we introduced vinylene carbonate (VC), a polar additive, to alleviate the intermolecular bonding between TMP and BTFE molecules and enhance the stability of the electrode‐ electrolyte interphase. By tailoring the solvent ratio, VC also enables the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte to be intrinsically non‐flammable at a molar ratio of 0.53:1:1.5:0.15 (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 b</link>). According to molecular orbital analysis on TMP, BTFE, TFSI<sup>−</sup>, and VC by density functional theory (DFT) calculations. VC exhibits a low lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) energy of −0.28 eV, suggesting its prior decomposition on the anode surface (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 c</link>). Moreover, TFSI<sup>−</sup> possess a very high highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy level, which indicates a high priority to decompose on the cathode surface. The electrochemical stability window of the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte is significantly expanded from 4.65 to 4.90 V compared to that in VC‐free electrolyte (Figure S2). As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 d</link> and Figure S1, Na metal in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte exhibits more stable plating and stripping with a small polarization potential, and even at a higher cycling capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>, the Na/Na symmetrical cell can cycle stably for over 1000 h.</p><p>To realize a workable electrolyte, cathode and anode compatibility are necessary. Polyanionic compounds, Prussian blue analogs, and commercial HC, which are considered as promising electrodes for SIBs, were used to verify the feasibility of the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. The electrolyte possesses an electrochemical window of about 4.90 V, which is larger than that of the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE without VC (4.65 V). Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) of these three electrodes in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 e</link>. The NVP and PB electrodes show a pair of peaks around 3.4 and 2.9 V, corresponding to the reversible transformation of V<sup>3+</sup>/V<sup>4+</sup> and Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup>, respectively.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0032"><sup>[15]</sup></link> The CV of HC shows a broad cathodic peak from 1.0 to 0.2 V and a pair of redox peaks at approximately 0.1 V, corresponding to a process of Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction into/from the carbon layers.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0035"><sup>[16]</sup></link> This demonstrates the excellent electrochemical compatibility of these materials in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte.</p><p>Long‐term cycling stability tests for NVP∥Na and PB∥Na cells were performed at 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup> with 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 f</link>), and both these two cells exhibited superior long‐term cycling performance with a stable average Coulombic efficiency &gt;99.9 %. As the cycling proceeded, very slight capacity attenuation was observed, and the capacity decay was only 0.5 % (after 1000 cycles) in the NVP∥Na and PB∥Na cells, respectively. As a comparison, other electrolytes show a continuous capacity fading along with an obvious increase in voltage polarization in voltage profiles at room temperature, which is explained by the aggressive electrolyte consumption to grow the CEI or SEI during cycling (Figure S5). The NVP∥Na and PB∥Na cells in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte presents the best cycling, retaining ca. 98.5 % of the original reversible capacity after 200 cycles, with an ultrahigh average Coulombic efficiency (CE) &gt;99.9 %. The charging/discharging curves show a negligible increase of the electrochemical polarization from the 1<sup>st</sup> to the 200<sup>th</sup> cycle (Figure S6), implying low cell resistance and great structural stability for the surfaces of NVP and PB cathode, which can achieve facile Na<sup>+</sup> transfer through the electrolyte‐electrode interphase (Figure S7). Compared to 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC and 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte, the significantly improved rate capability when employing 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC can be attributed to the fast interphase reaction kinetics caused by the reduced viscosity and thinner CEI (Figure S8). Even in a low temperature range, the NVP∥Na and PB∥Na cells retained 71 % and 95 % of their room temperature capacity when cycled at −20 °C, respectively, and they presented stable performance over 50 cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S9).</p><p>In the cycling test on a HC∥Na cell, the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte showed stable charge‐discharge operation for over 100 cycles at a low rate of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, which corresponds to a running time of over 4 months (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 g</link>). The capacity retention was 99.6 % with negligible voltage polarization after 100 cycles (Figure S10 and S11), which is much superior to that with the 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC (capacity retention of 66.2 %) and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE (capacity retention of 26.6 %). Meanwhile, the average Coulombic efficiency reached 99.3 % at the low rate of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating suppressed side reactions between the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte and the HC during cycling. The HC∥Na cell using 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte exhibited a severe capacity decay occurred within 20 cycles, which may be explained by the high viscosity and poor wettability with this electrolyte, eventually leading to a lower capacity retention of 81.6 % after 100 cycles.</p><p>Measurements of thermogravimetric analysis, ionic conductivity, and viscosity were also used to evaluate the properties and performances of these electrolytes (Figure S12–S14). A quantitative comparison of the practical considerations concerning the conventional 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC, high‐concentration 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP, and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte can be found in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0001">1 h</link>. Based on the above results, the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte integrates most of the excellent properties of conventional 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC electrolyte and high‐concentration 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte to obtain a comprehensive improvement, and has shown better electrochemical performance while giving consideration to safety.</p><p>Building upon the superior performance achieved with both cathode and anode, pouch full cells were fabricated to investigate the practicability of 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0002">2</link>). In the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cells, the cathode and anode with a double‐face coating had high loadings of 14 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> and 6 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, respectively. The dimensions (ca. 75 mm × 25 mm × 5 mm) and practical weight (ca. 22.7 g) of the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC cells are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0002">2 e</link>. The electrochemical performances of the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cells were evaluated with 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte between 2–3.7 V and 1.7–3.2 V, respectively. At a low charge‐discharge current (0.1 C), the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cells delivered an initial discharge capacity of 385 and 270 mAh with an initial Coulombic efficiency of 74.1 and 71.9 %, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0002">2 a,c</link>). The low initial Coulombic efficiency of the pouch cell is explained by the aggressive electrolyte consumption to grow the SEI and the small electrolyte consumption for the CEI during the first cycle. The cycling tests showed that the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cells had a high cycling Coulombic efficiency of 99.7 % and capacity retention of 85.1 and 85.7 % after 120 cycles, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0002">2 b,d</link>). For a visual exhibition, these pouch cells could easily drive a fan to rotate at high speed and kept a 12 watts bulb lighted (Figure S15 and Video S1).</p><figure xml:id="anie202112382-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112382:anie202112382-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112382-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical behavior of the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cell with 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC as electrolyte. a),c) The initial charge‐discharge curves of the NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cells. b),d) Corresponding cycling performances at a rate of 0.1 C. e) The dimensions of a pouch cell. f) Flammability tests on the pouch cell. g) Post‐mortem dissection of the burned cell.</p></caption></figure><p>The safety of these non‐flammable SIBs was also investigated using a flame test on pouch cells shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0002">2 f</link> and Video S2, S3. In this test, we used scissors to cut a notch on the edge of the pouch cell, which was driving a light bulb. Although the inside of the battery was exposed to the air, the bulb can still work normally. Even when we kept burning the gap with a lighter, the pouch cell containing 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte did not emit any smoke or fire, and the burned cell was still capable of lighting a light bulb. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0002">2 g</link>, after the flame test, except for the scorched part, which was directly in contact with the flame, the other parts of the cell remained intact. This indicated that the1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte with significant flame‐retardancy improved the intrinsic safety of the battery</p><p>Drastic compositional and morphological differences were observed between the four electrolytes on both the cathode and the anode surfaces because of the solvent composition. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis on the CEI and SEI were carried out to understand the reasons for the excellent cycling stability of the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte over the other three electrolytes. The elements and their related atomic ratios detected in the CEI and SEI layers are summarized in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 a</link>–c, and the detailed narrow scan XPS spectra are shown in Figure S16–18. The carbonaceous species such as C‐O, C=O, and poly(CO<sub>3</sub>), which are derived from EC/DEC decomposition covered the surface of the cathodes with a thick organic layer (Figure S16, S17, and S19).<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0004"><sup>[1c]</sup></link> This seriously hinders the transmission of Na<sup>+</sup> ions at the interphase, resulting in a loss of battery capacity.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0036"><sup>[17]</sup></link> In the 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte, the components of the CEI on the cathode surface were derived from the decomposition of TFSI<sup>−</sup> anions and trace TMP. The presence of a large amount of highly resistive metal fluorides on the cathode surface, however, is reported to be detrimental to carrier transport kinetics, resulting in increased voltage polarization (Figure S6).<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0039"><sup>[18]</sup></link> In sharp contrast, a trace NaF peak was detected on the NVP cathode surface, and a small proportion of Na content was detected as a component of the CEI. Combining this information with the S 2p spectra (Figure S16), much sodium sulfur oxynitride (NaSON), SO<sub>3</sub><sup>2−</sup>, and Na<sub>2</sub>S were detected on the surface, indicating the continuous decomposition of TFSI<sup>−</sup> due to the failed CEI formation.</p><figure xml:id="anie202112382-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112382:anie202112382-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112382-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Ex situ surface analysis on the cycled NVP, PB, and HC electrodes. a)–c) Characterization of the CEI and SEI components on NVP, PB, and HC after 100 cycles for the cathode and 20 cycles for the anode by XPS. d) High‐resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) images of the three electrodes cycled in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. e)–g) Atomic concentrations from the the C 1s, O 1s, N 1s, F 1s, S 2p, and Na 1s spectra on the NVP, PB, and HC electrodes in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte after 100 cycles for the cathode and 20 cycles for the anode for various durations of Ar<sup>+</sup> sputtering. h) Illustration of the formation of the CEI and SEI on the cathode and anode in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte.</p></caption></figure><p>Eventually, the TMP solvent would be decomposed, which would lead to a rapid decay of the capacity. Apparent polyphosphate growth could also be observed on the NVP particle surface after 200 cycles by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Figure S20). This indicates that 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte has poor compatibility with NVP. With the introduction of BTFE, similar atomic ratios in the CEI formation on NVP and PB, more apparent signal of C‐F species, a faint NaF peak were observed (Figure S16 and 17). Such differences suggest the fluorinated ether diluent, BTFE, participated in the CEI formation on both the NVP and PB surfaces.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0040"><sup>[19]</sup></link> On comparing 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolytes, the content of P element in the CEI increased slightly with the addition of BTFE in both NVP and PB cathodes (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 a,b</link>), implying that BTFE in the electrolyte can make a special contribution to the formation of the CEI.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0041"><sup>[20]</sup></link> The formation mechanism for the CEI in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte was slightly different. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 a</link> and <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 b</link>, the atomic ratio of S to N element was significantly increased which only come from the TFSI<sup>−</sup>. Accordingly, the F 1s spectrum shows more apparent enrichment in the NaF peak than in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte, which illustrates that TFSI<sup>−</sup> easier to decompose in the electrolyte with VC additive (Figure S16, and 17). In addition, there is a small peak at ca. 534.1 eV due to the polycarbonate species produced by the decomposition of VC.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0042"><sup>[21]</sup></link> The atomic ratio of P element in both PB and NVP is almost null (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 a,b</link>). These results demonstrate that the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte not only provides a moderate amount of NaF as a component of the inorganic layer of CEI, but also reduces the availability of free TMP solvent to react with the cathode surface. The CEI chemical composition in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte was further examined via XPS with Ar<sup>+</sup> sputtering depth profiling (Figure S21 and S22). After sputtering for more than 25 s, there is little change in the C 1s and O 1s spectra on either of the NVP and PB surfaces. In the F 1s and S 2p spectra, the C‐F signal gradually weakens, and the NaF signal and Na<sub>2</sub>S signal become stronger. These results further demonstrate that an organic‐inorganic hybrid CEI layer was formed on the NVP and PB particles, which is derived from a large amount of TFSI<sup>−</sup> decomposition and a small amount of VC. The inorganic layer produced by the decomposition of TFSI<sup>−</sup> is in contact with the cathode, and the organic layer produced by the decomposition of BTFE and VC forms on top to cover it and constitutes the outermost layer.</p><p>In addition to the stability of an electrolyte on the cathode surface, another critical factor for the cell cycling stability is the electrolyte stability on the anode. There are significant differences on the HC anode surface when using the four electrolytes. As shown in Figure S18, the results of conventional electrolyte and 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte is similar to previous reports.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0004 #anie202112382-bib-0045"><sup>[1c, 22]</sup></link> With the introduction of fluorinated ether, however, the signal of S 2p and the NaF peak are significantly weaker in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte. The atomic ratio of P element is increased significantly (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 c</link>). In contrast, the SEI with 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte presented the lowest F 1s, P 2p, and S 2p signals, indicating that the addition of VC can suppress the decomposition of TMP solvent and NaTFSI salt. Using the XPS depth profiling, a thin layer of organic shell also can be observed in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. As shown in Figure S24, the F 1s spectrum reveals an almost vanished signal of C‐F at a depth of 5 nm, accompanied by dramatically reduced intensity of C‐O, C=O, O=C‐O, and Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> species in the C 1s and O 1s spectra. This means that the organic composition in SEI decreases with the increase of sputtering depth. Instead, inorganic species start to prevail in the inner SEI, with the appearance of a stronger peak of NaF. The evolution of the selected elements with depth profiling reveals, however, that the SEI originating from 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC is characterized by a distinctly low fluorine content. The results show that the formation of the SEI on the HC surface may be caused by the decomposition of a large amount of VC and small amounts of TFSI<sup>−</sup> and BTFE. These results can also be verified by SEM (Figure S25). After 20 cycles, the SEI layer formed on HC surface using 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte is more homogeneous and compact than that of the other three electrolytes.</p><p>More evidence of effective electrode protection in the 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte can be obtained from the transmission electron microscope (TEM) characterization (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 d</link>). For a sharp contrast, after the same number of cycles in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte, CEI and SEI layers with different thicknesses can be clearly observed on both cathode and anode. The CEI and SEI layers on the cycled NVP, PB, and HC electrodes are thinner and more homogeneous in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC than in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC electrolyte. Combining these results with the previous XPS results, it can be deduced that the CEI and SEI formed with conventional 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐EC/DEC electrolyte is enriched with organic components, which cannot passivate the electrode, which thus leads to severe electrolyte (both salt and solvent) decomposition and electrode cracking. Benefitting from the stability of the decomposition products of TFSI<sup>−</sup> and VC on the electrode surface, we are able to effectively realize long‐term cycling stability in NVP∥HC and PB∥HC full cells by forming stable organic‐inorganic interphase on both the cathode and the anode (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0003">3 h</link>).</p><p>The excellent electrochemical performance of these cells is not only due to the formation of stable CEI and SEI films in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte, but also owing to the regulation of the electrolyte structure by H‐bonds. H‐bonding is a weak intermolecular force, but it has a significant effect on the solvent structure.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0031 #anie202112382-bib-0048"><sup>[14, 23]</sup></link> As shown in Figure S3, 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte forms a stable homogeneous phase with BTFE diluent, when the molar ratio of TMP/BTFE is 1:1 and 1:1.5. When the molar ratio is exceeded to 1:1.5, however, some white precipitates are clearly visible at the bottom, and these white precipitates are identified as NaTFSI (Figure S26). This finding implies that the stable solvation structure of Na<sup>+</sup> with TMP is broken when excess diluent is added. This phenomenon is not accidental; the same phenomenon occurs when using other diluents and phosphate solvents (Figure S4).</p><p>To understand the effects of the BTFE diluent on the microscopic structures of NaFSI/TMP mixtures, various spectra were collected, including <sup>2</sup>H‐nuclear magnetic resonance (<sup>2</sup>H‐NMR), <sup>17</sup>O‐nuclear magnetic resonance (<sup>17</sup>O‐NMR), and Raman spectra. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0004">4 a</link>, with the addition of BTFE, the peaks of 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte becomes much sharper, and the <sup>17</sup>O signal of oxygen atoms in TMP showed a slight shift from 21.19 to 21.74 and 22.15 ppm, respectively. This indicates a further increase in the electron cloud and a stronger solvation structure of Na<sup>+</sup>‐TMP local environments.<link href="#anie202112382-bib-0029 #anie202112382-bib-0051"><sup>[12c, 24]</sup></link> In fact, the cause of the chemical shift of the oxygen atoms in P=O bonds is not only due to the effect on the solvation structure of Na<sup>+</sup>‐TMP, but also derives from the intermolecular interaction between BTFE and TMP. The <sup>17</sup>O signal of P=O bonds in TMP molecules shows a upshift from 23.95 to 22.57 ppm with the introduction of BTFE diluent into pure TMP, manifesting a further increase in the electron cloud due to the intermolecular interaction between TMP and BTFE molecules. According to the <sup>2</sup>H‐NMR characterization (Figure S27), we can also find that the <sup>2</sup>H signal of C‐H in BTFE molecules show a downshift in the BTFE‐TMP system. Combining the results of <sup>17</sup>O‐NMR and <sup>2</sup>H‐NMR, we can infer that the O electron cloud of the P=O bond in TMP increases, while the H electron cloud of the C−H bond in BTFE decreases, indicating that an intermolecular H‐bond is formed between TMP and BTFE molecules (P=O⋅⋅⋅H−C). The H‐bond between BTFE and TMP was also confirmed by Raman spectra (Figure S28). Compared with pure TMP, the P=O stretching (ca. 1270.3 cm<sup>−1</sup>) gradually moves to higher wavenumbers (blue shift) in the BTFE‐TMP system, which is attributed to the interaction between H of the C−H bond in BTFE molecules and O of the P=O double bond in TMP. Consequently, as the content of BTFE increases in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte, BTFE molecules gradually attract the TMP molecules out of the Na<sup>+</sup> solvation due to the H‐bond formation. This has a significant impact on Na<sup>+</sup> solvation, which disrupts the coordination balance of TMP and Na<sup>+</sup> in the inner solvation shell. Na<sup>+</sup> will be ultimately recombined with TFSI<sup>−</sup> and result in recrystallization during charging and discharging. The free BTFE‐TMP complex is also easily decomposed and forms highly resistive polyphosphate on the electrode surface, which may lead to fading of the capacity of the cell and rapid failure (Figure S29–31).</p><figure xml:id="anie202112382-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112382:anie202112382-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112382-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Experimental and theoretical studies of electrolyte solvation structures. a) Natural‐abundance <sup>17</sup>O‐NMR spectra of different solvents and electrolytes. b)–d) Simulated electrolyte structures of 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP electrolyte, 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte, and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte. e) Radial distribution functions of Na<sup>+</sup> in 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP, 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE, and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolytes calculated from cMD simulation trajectories. f) ESP comparison of BTFE, TMP, and VC. g) Illustration of changes in the electrolyte structure with the addition of BTFE and VC in sequence. Aggs: Aggregates, CIPs: Contact ion pairs.</p></caption></figure><p>Adding a small portion of polar additives into 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte effectively restored the damaged Na<sup>+</sup> solvent structure and suppressed the electrochemical TMP decomposition. Similar to the TMP‐BTFE system, H‐bonds can also be formed between VC and BTFE. In the BTFE‐VC solution, both the <sup>17</sup>O signal of oxygen atoms in VC and the <sup>2</sup>H signal of hydrogen atoms in BTFE show obvious chemical shifts (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0004">4 a</link> and Figure S27). With the introduction of VC additive into the TMP‐BTFE solution and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE electrolyte, the <sup>17</sup>O signal of P=O bonds in TMP‐BTFE‐VC solution and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte exhibited a shift from 22.57 and 21.74 ppm to 23.09 and 22.15 ppm, respectively. In the <sup>2</sup>H‐NMR spectrum, the chemical shift showed a similar change to that of <sup>17</sup>O‐NMR. These results indicate that the addition of polar additives significantly alleviates the H‐bond effect of BTFE on TMP, and the salt precipitate caused by the breaking of the solvent structure is redissolved.</p><p>To further understand the effects of the diluent BTFE and the additive VC on the microscopic structures of NaTFSI‐TMP mixtures, the three electrolytes, i.e., 3.1 M NaTFSI‐TMP, 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE, and 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC were investigated by classical molecular dynamics (cMD) simulations. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0004">4 c</link>–e, the NaTFSI salt was closely coordinated with TMP, while the coordination between Na<sup>+</sup> and BTFE was negligible. Analysis of the radical distribution function data revealed that Na<sup>+</sup> ions are highly coordinated with TMP and barely coordinated with BTFE. A sharp peak of the Na‐O<sub>TMP</sub> pair was identified at 2.45 Å for all three systems studied, and the intensity of this peak was stronger due to the presence of BTFE. This indicates that the BTFE addition strengthens the interactions of Na<sup>+</sup> with TMP and TFSI<sup>−</sup>. The peak Na‐O<sub>TMP</sub> pair at 2.45 Å is also shorter than Na‐O<sub>VC</sub> pair (at 2.55 Å), which is due to stronger attraction between TMP and Na in 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte (Figure S32). These intermolecular interactions of TMP‐BTFE and BTFE‐VC can be rationalized by the electrostatic potential (ESP) calculations (Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0004">4 f</link>), which are strongly correlate with non‐covalent interactions. The isopotential surfaces of TMP and VC show similar trends, where the negative parts are only concentrated on O atoms of the double bonds. In the isopotential surface of BTFE, the positive charge is concentrated only on the H atoms, which attract negatively charged O atoms and form H‐bonds with them.</p><p>The schematic illustration in Figure <link href="#anie202112382-fig-0004">4 g</link> shows how the evolution of Na<sup>+</sup> solvation changes with BTFE and VC addition. Without BTFE diluent, the solution mainly exists in two solvated structures, aggregates (AGGs) and contact ion pairs (CIPs). From the analysis of the TMP molecular state by cMD (Figure S33), when the content of BTFE diluent is gradually increased, BTFE molecules are gradually inserted into solvation shell of AGGs. Because of the H‐bond formation, BTFE molecules attract TMP molecules out of the Na<sup>+</sup> solvation, and the Na<sup>+</sup> recombines with TFSI<sup>−</sup> to form crystals. With the introduction of VC additive, the precipitated NaTFSI crystals are redissolved and restore the solvation balance in the original solution. The Na<sup>+</sup> solvation structure can therefore be regulated by carefully controlling VC addition.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202112382-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We have demonstrated a strategy to optimize the solvation structure of electrolyte and the composition of interphases (CEI and SEI) by adding small amounts of additives to regulate the effect of intermolecular H‐bonds. As a result, a non‐flammable electrolyte, 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC (0.53:1:1.5:0.15 molar ratio), was developed for the sodium ion battery. The non‐flammable electrolyte exhibits good compatibility with various electrodes, which show high cycling Coulombic efficiency and good cycling stability. This electrolyte can simultaneously stabilize the interphases at both the cathode (NVP and PB) and the anode (HC), enabling stable cycling performance. To demonstrate the practical application of the electrolyte, the as‐constructed NVP∥HC and PB∥HC pouch cells containing 1.2 M NaTFSI‐TMP/BTFE/VC electrolyte displayed excellent cycling stability with enhanced safety performance. The electrolyte design, with its extreme availability and compatibility, can be extended to various combinations of non‐flammable or flame‐retardant solvents and alkaline salts, providing a welcome boost to the development of safe and high‐performance rechargeable batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202112382-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 51971124 and 52171217).
