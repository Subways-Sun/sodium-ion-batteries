Based on the mechanisms of the sodium ions storage in carbon materials, herein we present a multiple active sites decorated amorphous carbon (MAC) with rich structural defects and heteroatom doping as an anode material for SIBs. The full utilization of fast bonding–debonding processes between the active sites and sodium ions could bring a capacitive strategy to achieve superior sodium storage properties. Consequently, after materials characterization and electrochemical evaluation, the as‐prepared electrode could deliver high rate and long‐life performance. This active‐site‐related design could be extended to other types of electrode materials, thereby contributing to future practical SIB applications.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Carbon</b> decorated with multiple active sites, from heteroatom doping and an amorphous state is presented. xml:id="anie201908159-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium ion batteries (SIBs) are considered quite promising alternatives to lithium ion batteries (LIBs) for grid‐scale energy storage applications, as sodium is an abundant and low‐cost resource.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0001 #anie201908159-bib-0002"/> The study of electrode materials, especially anode materials, has attracted much attention, since the traditional graphite anode in LIBs has very limited sodium storage properties.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0003 #anie201908159-bib-0004"/> Therefore, it is necessary to explore SIB anode materials which have high specific capacity, good rate performance, long cycling stability, and abundant resources.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0005 #anie201908159-bib-0006 #anie201908159-bib-0007"/></p><p>Carbon allotropes are always favorable as anode candidates owing to their electronic conductivity and abundant resources.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0008 #anie201908159-bib-0009"/> Hard carbon is becoming promising and has been intensively studied for SIBs due to its electrochemical activity for sodium storage in 0.01–1.5 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0010 #anie201908159-bib-0011 #anie201908159-bib-0012"/> It is composed of numerous distortional graphite nanocrystals (DGCs) and pores which are created by the random stacking of these DGCs and has a specific capacity of 250–300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for sodium‐ion accommodation.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0013 #anie201908159-bib-0014"/> For a typical sodiation process, researchers usually divide it into two regions, a slope in a high‐potential region of 1.5–0.1 V and a plateau in a low‐potential region of 0.1–0.01 V.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0014 #anie201908159-bib-0015 #anie201908159-bib-0016 #anie201908159-bib-0017 #anie201908159-bib-0018 #anie201908159-bib-0019"/> Although the specific mechanism is being explored, some consensus has been reached. The capacity of the slope region is mostly due to the adsorption of structural defects, edges, and intercalation of doped or distorted graphite nanocrystals. In the plateau region, the capacity is primarily due to the sodium metal plating at the pores among the DGCs and some intercalation reactions. More than half of the total capacity of hard carbon comes from this plateau region. However, it should be noted that the deposition of agglomerate metallic sodium in this plateau region may cause the growth of sodium dendrites, which would lead to safety issues. Furthermore, the sodium intercalation reaction in DGCs are commonly related to slow kinetics, resulting in poor rate capability. Therefore, researchers have recently begun to consider the possibility of utilizing the slope region and the related fast capacitive sodium storage process.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0020 #anie201908159-bib-0021"/></p><p>According to the structure and discharge behavior of carbon materials, it could be inferred that the slope capacity is mainly contributed by the reversible Faradic capacitive adsorption of sodium ions. The sodium storage processes do not involve much ion transport in the solid state, which could significantly accelerate the sodium storage ability. During charge–discharge processes, there are only rapid chemical contacts and detachments between sodium ions and the electrode materials. Moreover, theoretically, these active sites dominated process would not change the structure of the host materials during charge–discharge processes, which could boost the cycling stability of the electrode materials.</p><p>To achieve these features, abundant active sites and unique morphology are required for electrode material design. First, the amorphous state of carbon could be a suitable choice.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0022 #anie201908159-bib-0023 #anie201908159-bib-0024"/> It could offer many carbon structural defects due to the electron distribution or charge being unevenly distributed or polarized. Doping is another effective strategy to create reversible active sites. It is generally considered that doping could not only enhance the electronic conductivity of the electrode materials, but also enlarge the interlayer distance of carbon, enhancing the sodium ion storage capability.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0017 #anie201908159-bib-0025 #anie201908159-bib-0026 #anie201908159-bib-0027 #anie201908159-bib-0028 #anie201908159-bib-0029"/> Furthermore, carbon with micro/meso pores could offer more contacts between the sodium ions and the host materials, shortening the ion transport path lengths and accelerating the electrochemical reactions. This is also useful; indeed much research interest has focused on structure and morphology constructions.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0022 #anie201908159-bib-0026 #anie201908159-bib-0030"/></p><p>Inspired by the discussions above, here we propose the utilization of multiple active sites to achieve fast chemical bonding–debonding processes between the carbon and sodium ions, reducing and abandoning the proportion of traditional sodium storage routes in carbon materials, such as insertion or plating. In particular, we present a multiple active‐sites decorated carbon (MAC) design with heteroatom doping (N, P) and meso‐porous spheres structure in amorphous state. Egg yolk and tetraethyl orthosilicate (TEOS) are used as precursors, and cost‐effective low‐temperature (600 °C, MAC‐600) calcination and etching processes are involved. More details can be found in the experimental section. As illustrated in Scheme <link href="#anie201908159-fig-5001"/>, abundant active sites could be offered by the heteroatoms doping at the carbon surface. Furthermore, rich structural defects from amorphous carbon could also contribute to these active sites (such as dangling bonds, edges, distortions, or other structure defects). Moreover, the pore structure of the carbon spheres creates sufficient contact between ions and the surface of the carbon host. Combining with both electric double layer capacitive and pseudocapacitive effects, it would allow fast and reversible chemical adsorption of sodium ions on the MAC surface. After electrochemical examinations, this as‐prepared carbon could deliver a high reversible capacity of 200 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even after 10 000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-5001"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Illustration of the structure of the designed carbon and the proposed sodium storage process mechanism.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie201908159-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The morphology and the pore structure of the MAC‐600 were first investigated by scanning electronic microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM), which are presented in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0001"/>. The SEM images in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0001"/> a and b show accumulated spheres with a diameter around 700 nm and the porous surface of the MAC‐600. The tap density of the carbon material is 0.3 g cm<sup>−3</sup>. The SEM images and the TEM images in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0001"/> c–e demonstrate that there are many mesopores distributed from 5 to 20 nm in size. These pores are generated both on the surface and inside the MAC‐600 spheres. The observations from the selected‐area electron‐diffraction and the high‐resolution TEM (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0001"/> f) demonstrate that there are no obvious graphite layer structures, suggesting the amorphous state of the designed MAC‐600 sample. Presumably these are caused by the low‐temperature calcination process. The corresponding elemental mapping of the MAC‐600 sample in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0001"/> g illustrates the homogenous distributions of heteroatoms N, P and surface oxidation. To further understand the properties of the carbon materials, a sample with a higher calcination temperature of 1000 °C (MAC‐1000) was prepared for comparison. The SEM and TEM images for MAC‐1000 can be found in the Supporting Information, Figure S1. It shows that the MAC‐1000 have a similar morphology, while some distorted carbon crystal lattice are observed (Supporting Information, Figure S1 c,d), confirming the formation of graphic carbon at 1000 °C.</p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a),b) SEM images of MAC‐600; c)–e) TEM images of MAC‐600; f) High‐resolution TEM image (inset: SAED) of MAC‐600; and g) corresponding mapping of MAC‐600.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0002"/> a shows the XRD patterns of the MAC samples, with broad peaks around 25.5° and 43° corresponding to the (002) and (100) crystal plane of disordered graphite domain, respectively, observed for both samples, which are typical amorphous carbon. It should be noted that MAC‐1000 shows better crystallinity for the small domains compared with MAC‐600. The Raman spectra of the two MAC samples (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0002"/> b) depict two Raman bands at 1350 cm<sup>−1</sup> and 1580 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which can be ascribed to D‐bands (disordered or defective graphite band) and G‐bands (crystalline graphite band), respectively. The ratio of <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>, representing disordering degree, decreases from 0.83 to 0.64 when calcination temperature increases, indicating that MAC‐600 processes higher disordered degree (for example, edges, defects, and heteroatom doping), which can promote sodium ion absorptions and their capacitive storage. The result is in line with the TEM observation. The pore structure for MAC‐600 is further confirmed by nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0002"/> c). The adsorption curve shows a typical type IV shape indicating the existence of mesopores. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas and the total pore volumes are measured as 353.07 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and 0.74 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, for MAC‐600. Moreover, the sample exhibits mesopore (pore size distributed around 9 nm) structure. In comparison, the BET profile for MAC‐1000 are presented in the Supporting Information, Figure S2 a, higher surface area (671 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) and larger pore size distribution (60–120 nm) are displayed, which may be attributed to the restructuring and crystallization of carbon.</p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) XRD profile, b) Raman spectra, and c) nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherm of the MAC samples.</p></caption></figure><p>To investigate the surface elemental compositions and bonding state of the MAC‐600 sample, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is performed (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0003"/>). The survey spectra indicate the existence of C, O, N, and P elements, agreeing with the elemental mapping result. The high‐resolution C 1s spectrum for MAC‐600 (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0003"/> b) could be deconvoluted into several peaks, representing C−C, C−P, C−N, C−O, and O−C=O at 284.8, 285.5, 286.5, 288.4, 289.9 eV, respectively, confirming the heteroatom‐doping state of the amorphous carbon. Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0003"/> c shows the N 1s spectrum, which can be deconvoluted into two peaks, corresponding to different types of N doping. The P 2p spectrum shown in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0003"/> d confirms the existence of P−C (133.3 eV) and P−O (134.3 eV) bonds. The doping of N and P to carbon can create more defects and generate more active sites, which can significantly boost the charge transfer and improve the fast sodium storage capacity. Similar XPS results of MAC‐1000 can be found in the Supporting Information, Figure S2.</p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>XPS spectrum of MAC‐600: survey spectrum: a) High‐resolution XPS spectra of b) C 1s, c) N 1s, and d) P 2p.</p></caption></figure><p>The reversible electrochemical sodium storage processes of the as‐prepared MAC‐600 are then evaluated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) techniques. Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0004"/> a shows the CV curves for the first five cycles of MAC‐600 in the voltage range of 0.01–3 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) at a scanning rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. A broad peak at 1.0 V in the first discharge cycle, which does not appear in the latter cycles, is attributed to the irreversible side reactions and the formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film on the electrode. The reversible cathodic peak at 0.1 V could be attributed to the insertion and plating for sodium on small carbon domains. The rest section of the CV curves (0.3–3 V) presents square shapes without obvious peaks, indicating the capacitive adsorption behavior of sodium ions.</p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) CV curves of MAC‐600 from 0.01 to 3.0 V at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> for the first 5 cycles; b) CV curves at different scan rates; c) Corresponding ln(<i>i</i>) vs. ln(<i>v</i>) fitting line; d) Calculated total current and capacitive current from the CVs at a scan rate of 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>; and e) Column diagram of the capacitive ratio at various scan rates (0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8, 1, 1.5, and 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>); The CCP is short for capacitive controlled process and the DCP is short for diffusion controlled process.</p></caption></figure><p>Furthermore, according to previous research, the capacitive adsorption behavior could be confirmed by the following equation:<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0031 #anie201908159-bib-0032"/><displayedItem xml:id="anie201908159-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie201908159-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><p>By calculating the <i>b</i> values from the CV curves at different scan rate (from 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> to 2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0004"/> b, the values of variable parameter <i>b</i> could reveal the kinetics for sodium ion storage. It could be inferred that the process is diffusion dominated when the values are close to 0.5. If the process is capacitive controlled, the value of <i>b</i> would be closer to 1. According to the fitting results in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0004"/> c, the <i>b</i> values are 0.96, 0.91, 0.94, 0.95, and 0.96 at 0.5, 0.8, 1.2, 1.7, and 2.3 V, respectively. This suggests that all the capacity contributions at the higher potential region are from active site adsorptions. The proportion of capacitive contributions from active sites could be evaluated by the following equation developed by Dunn:<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0033"/><displayedItem xml:id="anie201908159-disp-0002" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie201908159-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-math-0002"/></displayedItem></p><p>The <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> <i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> <i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> terms present the diffusion and capacitive behavior contributions from the total capacity, respectively. After the calculations of <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> (see Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0004"/> d,e), the voltage–current profile suggests that at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the percentage of capacitive contributions is about 81.8 %, and increases from 61.3 % to 91.1 % when the scan rate rises from 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> to 2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The results illustrate that most of the sample capacity for sodium ion storage is from the fast capacitive processes.</p><p>Galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) are conducted to further explore the electrochemical properties of the sample. To understand the sodium ion storage process, the valence‐state changes of N and P after sodiation were investigated by XPS (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0005"/> a and b). After discharge to 0.3 V and then to 0.1 V, the binding energy of N and P both shifted to lower values, illustrating the successful chemical bonding between sodium ions and the doping atoms. It also could be inferred that the sodium ions are chemically stored at the heteroatom doping active sites, agreeing with the electrochemical investigation results in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0004"/>. The charge–discharge profiles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> from the 2nd to 5th cycle of MAC‐600 are presented in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0005"/> c. After building the SEI film in the first cycle (Supporting Information, Figure S3), typical smooth slope‐dominated charge–discharge curves could be clearly observed without plateaus from electrochemical reactions. This is in line with the CV curves and further confirms that the sodium ion storage behavior is caused by capacitive chemical adsorption and desorption. Furthermore, the fully utilization of capacitive slope region may also contribute to the battery safety by alleviating the Na plating and polarization.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0020"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a),b) XPS profile comparison for the N and P element after discharge; c) Charge–discharge profile for MAC‐600 from the 2nd to 5th cycle; d) Capacities for MAC‐600 and MAC‐1000 at different current densities; e) GITT and the diffusivity curves for MAC‐600; and f) Long cycling performance of the MAC‐600 electrode at the current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>The rate performance of the MAC‐600 and MAC‐1000 samples are compared in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0005"/> d. In the first couple of cycles, a high reversible specific capacity of about 300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> could be observed for both samples. With the increase of current densities from 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> to 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, both samples could deliver high specific capacities, which is attributed to the porous structure and the heteroatom doping. Compared with MAC‐1000, the MAC‐600 sample could offer a more stable capacity at high current density. Specifically, at about 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the MAC‐600 sample could present 180 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and even at a high rate of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the specific capacity could still be kept at 165 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Furthermore, the Coulombic efficiency for the cycling is about 100 %, showing good reversibility of the reactions. The charge–discharge curves at different current densities (Supporting Information, Figure S4 a) demonstrate that the curves show characteristic capacitive adsorption properties. Furthermore, the capacity retention at different current densities (over 50 % at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) is superior among other carbon materials reported in recent years (Supporting Information, Figure S4 b).<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0012 #anie201908159-bib-0020 #anie201908159-bib-0022 #anie201908159-bib-0025 #anie201908159-bib-0029 #anie201908159-bib-0034 #anie201908159-bib-0035"/></p><p>To further investigate the kinetics for sodium‐ion storage, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) technique was employed. The diffusivity of sodium ions were evaluated (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0005"/> e) and it could be observed that diffusivity for the sodiation process decreases with voltage drop. However, it jumped to a high value when the desodiation process begins from 0.1 V. This suggests that when abundant sodium ions are chemically adsorbed and gathered on the active sites, in order to be stored in the carbon, other sodium ions need to overcome a repulsive charge force from the former surface sodium ions and vice versa.<link href="#anie201908159-bib-0036"/> This phenomenon could be additional circumstantial evidence for the sodium storage at active sites. It also should be noted here that the diffusivity of sodium ions increases and then decreases at around 1 V during the sodiation process. This might be because of that during the sodiation process, the doping atoms are chemically bonding with the sodium ions at this potential. These chemical bonding processes could enhance the diffusivity of sodium ions.</p><p>The cycling stability of the MAC‐600 and MAC‐1000 samples are then studied at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Supporting Information, Figure S5). The initial Columbic efficiency (ICE) of MAC‐1000 are much lower than that of MAC‐600, which should be caused by the larger surface area. Both the two samples could deliver good cycling stability, while the specific capacities of MAC‐600 are higher, as the amorphous state of carbon could provide more active sites for sodium ions storage. It could be found here the capacities increased slowly during cycling processes, which might be mainly due to the contact between the nanoporous electrode material and the electrolyte are not completed. Some of the active material would be electrochemically activated after several hundreds of cycles. Then the high rate cycling stability is studied at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0005"/> f), after a couple of activation cycles. The sample could deliver a long‐life property, and no capacity decay could be observed even after 10 000 cycles. The superior result is mainly owing to the numerous intrinsic/extrinsic defects, which are used as different types of actives sites for fast sodium ions capacitive bonding‐debonding processes. Also the <i>meso</i>‐porous structure is quite useful here. This remarkable cycling stability could also be attributed to the fully utilization of surface adsorption process at the slope region, alleviating the Na plating and dendrites growing.</p><p>To evaluate the actual performance of the electrode, carbon‐coated Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP@C) was employed as the cathode material for assembling full cells with a pre‐sodiated MAC‐600 anode (more details can be found in the Supporting Information). As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0006"/> a, the sloped charge–discharge plateaus could be clearly observed. The charge and discharge capacities are 180 and 174 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively and the ICE is 97.2 %. The cycling stability at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> are evaluated and displayed in the Supporting Information, Figure S6 and Figure <link href="#anie201908159-fig-0006"/> b, respectively. During cycling, the Coulombic efficiency are almost 100 % and after 100 cycles 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity retention is still 80 %.</p><figure xml:id="anie201908159-fig-0006"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201908159:anie201908159-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201908159-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The charge–discharge profile of the first cycle for the full battery at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (inset: photograph of the full battery powering a light source); b) the cycling stability for the full battery at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie201908159-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In conclusion, we proposed the utilization of multiple active sites to achieve fast chemical sodium‐ion storage. We have presented an amorphous carbon with rich structural defects and heteroatom doping as a multiple‐active‐site decorated carbon for fast and stable sodium storage. The designed MAC‐600 sample could deliver an outstanding high rate performance and after 10 000 cycles, the capacity remained over 200 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The remarkable results are mainly due to the special properties of the sample: with a porous structure, the amorphous state and the heteroatom doping could offer numerous active sites for fast sodium capacitive adsorptions. This utilization of active sites energy storage strategy could be extended to other high rate electrodes designs for SIBs in the future.</p></section><section xml:id="anie201908159-sec-0004"
