However, large volume changes during alloying/dealloying processes lead to poor cycling performance. Herein, bismuth nanoparticle@carbon (Bi@C) composite is prepared via a facile annealing method using a commercial coordination compound precursor of bismuth citrate. The composite has a uniform structure with Bi nanoparticles embedded within a carbon framework. The nanosized structure ensures a fast kinetics and efficient alleviation of stress/strain caused by the volume change, and the resilient and conductive carbon matrix provides an interconnected electron transportation pathway. The Bi@C composite delivers outstanding sodium‐storage performance with an ultralong cycle life of 30 000 cycles at a high current density of 8 A g<sup>−1</sup> and an excellent rate capability of 71% capacity retention at an ultrahigh current rate of 60 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even at a high mass loading of 11.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, a stable reversible capacity of 280 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained after 200 cycles. More importantly, full SIBs by pairing with a Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode demonstrates superior performance. Combining the facile synthesis and the commercial precursor, the exceptional performance makes the Bi@C composite very promising for practical large‐scale applications.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201904771-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A Bi nanoparticle@carbon composite is synthesized by a facile heating method from a commercial product of bismuth citrate. Superior sodium storage performance with ultralong cycle life of 30 000 cycles at 8 A g<sup>−1</sup> and ultrahigh rate capability of 71% capacity retention at 60 A g<sup>−1</sup> are obtained. Full cells by coupling with a well‐developed cathode also deliver impressive capacity and cyclability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201904771:adma201904771-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201904771-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201904771-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201904771-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>The deployment of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) has been extended from portable electronic devices to emerging electrical vehicles and smart grids, driving a rapid demand growth for LIBs.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0001 #adma201904771-bib-0002 #adma201904771-bib-0003"/> Considering the scarcity of lithium resources, it is of great importance to develop alternative energy‐storage technologies that utilize earth‐abundant elements. Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered as a promising alternative to LIBs owing to the abundant sodium resources over the whole world.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0004 #adma201904771-bib-0005"/> Tremendous research efforts over the past few years have made considerable progress in SIB electrode materials.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0006 #adma201904771-bib-0007 #adma201904771-bib-0008"/> Particularly, a wide variety of cathode materials for SIBs have been explored and encouraging performance has been reported.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0009 #adma201904771-bib-0010 #adma201904771-bib-0011 #adma201904771-bib-0012"/> In contrast, there still lack of suitable anode materials to couple with these well‐developed cathode materials. Carbonaceous materials (e.g., graphite and hard carbon) have been the most investigated anode materials in SIBs, but exhibit relatively low capacities and short cycle life.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0013 #adma201904771-bib-0014 #adma201904771-bib-0015"/> Transition‐metal sulfides and oxides based on conversion‐reaction mechanism could deliver high capacities but poor cycle life.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0016 #adma201904771-bib-0017 #adma201904771-bib-0018"/> Alloy‐based electrode materials (forming Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>M<i><sub>y</sub></i>, M = Sn, P, Sb) are capable of delivering high capacities and show great potential for high‐energy SIBs.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0019 #adma201904771-bib-0020 #adma201904771-bib-0021 #adma201904771-bib-0022 #adma201904771-bib-0023 #adma201904771-bib-0024"/> However, the alloying process is accompanied by huge volume changes, which cause severe mechanical fracture or pulverization and thereby a poor cycle life.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0007"/></p><p>As an alloy‐based anode material, bismuth (Bi) has been intensively investigated in SIBs. Based on the reversible reaction of Bi ↔ NaBi ↔ Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, it could deliver a high theoretical capacity of 385 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and a low reaction voltage of ≈0.6 V.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0025"/> Nevertheless, due to the high volume change up to ≈244% caused by alloying processes from Bi to Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, it is a great challenge to achieve adequate performance for practical applications.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0026"/> Enormous attention has been devoted to enhance the electrochemical properties of Bi anodes by designing nanostructures, using composites with conductive agents, and forming alloys or intermetallic.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0025 #adma201904771-bib-0027 #adma201904771-bib-0028 #adma201904771-bib-0029"/> Su et al. reported Bi@graphene nanocomposites and demonstrated a reversible sodium storage capacity of 561 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> but a poor cycle life of only 50 cycles.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0025"/> A Bi nanosphere/porous carbon composite was reported by Li's group, and a low capacity retention of 106 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was obtained after 1000 cycles.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0027"/> Therefore, these Bi‐based anode materials are still suffering from large capacity degradation during long cycling ranges.</p><p>Recently, it is found that electrolytes can considerably influence the structure evolution and electrochemical performance of Bi anodes in SIBs.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0030 #adma201904771-bib-0031 #adma201904771-bib-0032"/> Ether‐based electrolytes behave a strong interaction with Bi, producing a thin but stable solid electrolyte interface (SEI) layer on the surface of Bi anodes to resist the structure deformation during charge/discharge processes.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0032 #adma201904771-bib-0033"/> Using a commercial bulk Bi anode material, a stable cycling performance of 2000 cycles was obtained.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0032"/> Thereafter, Wang and co‐workers developed a Bi@graphite composite anode and demonstrated a prolonged cycle life up to 10 000 cycles with 90% capacity retention and good rate performance of ≈110 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current rate of 48 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0033"/> Unfortunately, the reversible capacity is too low, far lower than that of graphite in LIBs (≈330 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). In addition, the complicated synthetic process makes it less competitive for practical applications.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0034"/></p><p>Herein, we report a bisumth nanoparticle@carbon (Bi@C) composite prepared through a facile annealing method from a commercial coordination compound of bismuth citrate (BC, C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>BiO<sub>7</sub>) precursor. The composite has a uniform structure with Bi nanoparticles (NPs) embedded within a carbon framework. The formation process of the Bi@C composite was systematically investigated by in situ high‐temperature X‐ray diffraction (H‐XRD), high‐temperature Fourier transform infrared (H‐FTIR), and theoretical computations. The nanosized structure and resilient and conductive carbon matrix ensure a fast kinetics, efficient alleviation of stress/strain caused by the volume change, and interconnected electron transportation pathway. Outstanding sodium storage performance was demonstrated with superior long cyclability of 30 000 cycles at a high current density of 8 A g<sup>−1</sup> and excellent rate capability of 71% capacity retention at an ultrahigh current rate of 60 A g<sup>−1</sup>. More importantly, full SIBs fabricated by coupling the Bi@C composite anode with NASICON‐type Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode also demonstrated impressive capacity of 260 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and cyclability of 93% capacity retention after 500 cycles.</p><p>Schematic preparation process of the Bi@C composite is illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a. The Bi@C composite was obtained through directly heating BC at 900 °C for 2 h in argon atmosphere. The morphology and structure of the Bi@C composite were investigated using scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The BC precursor shows a sheet structure with smooth surface (Figure S1, Supporting Information). After calcination, the composite kept the sheet morphology but with a reduced size to nanometers. The composite nanosheets uniformly embed ultrasmall Bi NPs at particle sizes of 15–25 nm within a carbon framework (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>b–d). The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image shows that the Bi NPs are finely wrapped by a carbon layer at a thickness of &lt;5 nm (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>e). The Bi NPs were revealed to have a single crystal structure as verified by the clear fringes with an interlayer distance of 0.322 nm, corresponding to the (012) plane of rhombohedral phase of metal Bi (JCPDS 01‐085‐1331) (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>e). The selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern displays a set of diffraction rings, assigned to the diffractions of (012), (104), (110), (202), (024), (116), (122), and (214) planes of rhombohedral phase Bi (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>f). The crystal structure of the Bi@C composite was further confirmed by XRD pattern (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>g). The energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping images (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>h) reveal a homogenous distribution of Bi, C, and O in the Bi@C composite, indicating the Bi NPs are uniformly embedded within the carbon composite matrix.</p><figure xml:id="adma201904771-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201904771:adma201904771-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201904771-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the synthesis of Bi@C composite. b,c) SEM and d,e) TEM images, f) selected area diffraction and g) XRD pattern of the Bi@C composite. h) Elemental mappings of C, Bi, and O for the marked region. The inset in (d) is size statistics diagram.</caption></figure><p>In the high‐resolution X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectrum, the peaks for BiO bonds (164.1 and 158.8 eV) in Bi 4f spectrum, CO bond (288.3 eV) in C 1s spectrum, and BiOC bonds (530.5 eV) in O 1s spectrum suggest the existence of BiOC bonds in the Bi@C composite (Figure S2b–d, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0035"/> Due to unavoidable oxidation during sample preparation, Bi<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> is detected by the XPS measurement. And only rhombohedral phase Bi for the composite was clearly illustrated by XRD and TEM analysis (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>e–g). These results reveal that the oxidation only occurred on the surface area of the Bi@C composite while the bulk is still Bi metal. The strong peak for BiO bonds at 311 cm<sup>−1</sup> was also observed in the Raman spectrum (Figure S3a, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0036"/> These interaction between Bi, O, and C could stabilize Bi nanoparticles and ensure structure stability of the composite. The content of Bi in the composite is calculated to be 73.3% by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) measurement (Figure S3b, Supporting Information). Meanwhile, the Bi@C composite displays a typical mesoporous structure with specific surface area of 67 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and pore size of 10 nm (Figure S3c,d, Supporting Information). For electrode materials, the control of the particle sizes at nanometer level and carbon‐coated structure could not only effectively enhance mass transport of Na<sup>+</sup> and increase the electrical conductivity, but also provide void space to bear the large volume changes of Bi NPs during charge/discharge processes.</p><p>In order to probe the thermal conversion process of BC, density functional theory (DFT) calculations were conducted with parameters on the optimized structure of C<sub>6</sub>H<sub>5</sub>BiO<sub>7</sub> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). The bond lengths are in the order of BiO &gt; CC &gt; CO, while the bond strengths are in the order of BiO &lt; CC &lt; CO (Table S1, Supporting Information). The BiO bonds are less strong than the CC and CO bonds, and thus decompose prior to CC and CO bonds. Therefore, the decomposition of the metal center is prior to the carbonization of the ligands during the heat treatment. Accordingly, TGA curve of BC shows two obvious weight loss steps (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0002"/>b). The first weight loss of 35.9% between 270–350 °C is attributed to the ligand transformation into carbon, and the second weight loss of 5.5% between 350–420 °C could be assigned to the decomposition of the intermediate products.</p><figure xml:id="adma201904771-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201904771:adma201904771-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201904771-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Optimized structure and b) TGA‐DTA curve of bismuth citrate. c) In situ H‐XRD patterns and d) in situ H‐FTIR spectra of bismuth citrate at the different annealing temperatures. e) The possible decomposition mechanism of bismuth citrate.</caption></figure><p>To further explore the formation mechanism of the Bi@C composite during annealing, in situ H‐XRD and H‐FTIR measurements were conducted with the same heating program (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0002"/>c,d and Figure S4, Supporting Information). In the H‐XRD observation, two peaks for Bi<sub>2</sub>(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and Bi<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>5</sub> (or (BiO)<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) started to appear at 400 °C, indicating that the organic ligands of BC transform into amorphous carbon and the intermediate products begin to decomposite, and became stronger as the temperature increases to 480 °C. When cooled to 40 °C, Bi and BiCO phases appeared, showing that Bi<sub>2</sub>(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and Bi<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>5</sub> are reduced by the resulting carbon (the standard XRD patterns of these compounds are shown in Figure S5, Supporting Information). In the H‐FTIR spectra (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0002"/>d), characteristic peaks of OCO at ≈2349 cm<sup>−1</sup> were observed at 400 °C, and vanished after cooling to 40 °C (Table S2, Supporting Information). The COH groups were transformed into CO groups at ≈250 °C as shown by the corresponding peak intensity variation (Figure S6, Supporting Information). Simultaneously, new ketone groups were produced after 250 °C as indicated by the stronger peak at ≈1723 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The transformation process is illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0002"/>e. At a high temperature of 900 °C, organic ligands of the bismuth citrate precursor were transformed into amorphous carbon, and the Bi center was transformed into liquid Bi metal due to its low melting‐point (271 °C).<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0023"/> During the cooling process, the formed carbon framework can confine the congregated growth of Bi, thus resulting in single nanocrystallite Bi (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0001"/>e). The similar phenomenon was also observed on reported Sb/C and Bi/C materials.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0023 #adma201904771-bib-0037"/></p><p>The electrochemical performance of the Bi@C composite was investigated in coin cells. The cyclic voltammograms (CV) curves of the Bi@C composite show four cathodic peaks at 0.65, 0.47, 0.36, and 0.25 V in the first scan (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a). The former two peaks are attributed to Na–Bi alloy reactions to form NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi phases, while the latter two disappeared in the subsequent scans. In the anodic scans, two anodic peaks at 0.58 and 0.77 V are displayed, which are associated with the desodiation reactions of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi → NaBi → Bi.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0032 #adma201904771-bib-0033"/> The redox peaks become sharper and stronger with cycling progress, suggesting a high reversibility of the Bi@C composite. Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>b displays selected discharge/charge profiles of the Bi@C composite electrodes at a current density of 0.8 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Two discharge plateaus at 0.66 and 0.48 V and two charge voltage plateaus at 0.57 and 0.78 V are apparently presented, related to the reversible reactions of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi ↔ NaBi and NaBi ↔ Bi, respectively, which are consistent with the CV curves. The Coulombic efficiency is 50.3% in the first cycle. The initial capacity loss is mainly attributed to the decomposition of the electrolyte to form SEI films.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0038 #adma201904771-bib-0039"/> From the second cycle, no obvious change in voltage profiles was observed, demonstrating a highly reversible and stable cycling performance. A high reversible capacity of 301.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was achieved after 5 cycles, corresponding to a specific capacity of 383.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with respect to Bi (given the contribution of coating carbon and Super P is ≈150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0033"/> This is close to its theoretical value of 385 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, suggesting a high reactivity and utilization of Bi in the Bi@C composite.</p><figure xml:id="adma201904771-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201904771:adma201904771-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201904771-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of the Bi@C composite: a) CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.01 and 1.5 V, b) galvanostatic charge/discharge curves at 0.8 A g<sup>−1</sup>, c) rate performance, d) rate capability comparison with reported Bi‐based anodes materials for SIBs, e) CV curves at different scan rates (0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>), f) quasi‐equilibrium voltage curve from GITT at 0.08 A g<sup>−1</sup>, g) cycling performance and Coulombic efficiency at 0.8 A g<sup>−1</sup>, h) long‐term cycling performance and Coulombic efficiency at 8 A g<sup>−1</sup> (The electrodes were activated at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 3 cycles prior to cycling tests).</caption></figure><p>Exceptional rate capability was also demonstrated by the Bi@C composite (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>c and Figure S7, Supporting Information). As the current density increases from 0.4 to 40 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a high specific capacity of 247.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> is retained. Even at an extremely high current rate of 60 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a capacity of 232 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (71% retention of the capacity at 0.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>) can still be delivered, corresponding to only ≈14 s to a full charge or discharge. The rate of 60 A g<sup>−1</sup> is so far the fastest one applied to SIBs, even better than those of graphite anodes in LIBs. The capacity is higher than all those Bi‐based anodes reported previously, even more than twice higher than 113 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 48 A g<sup>−1</sup> for Bi@graphite composite in previous work (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>d and Table S3, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0025 #adma201904771-bib-0027 #adma201904771-bib-0032 #adma201904771-bib-0033 #adma201904771-bib-0035 #adma201904771-bib-0040 #adma201904771-bib-0041 #adma201904771-bib-0042"/></p><p>The diffusion process of the Bi@C composite electrodes was investigated by CV measurements at different voltage scan rates from to 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>e). The relationship between peak current (<i>i</i>) and voltage scan rate (<i>v</i>) can be described as <i>i</i> = <i>av<sup>b</sup></i>, where <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are positive variables. It is reported that for a diffusion‐controlled process <i>b</i> approaches 0.5, while for a surface capacitance‐dominated process <i>b</i> is close to 1.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0043 #adma201904771-bib-0044"/> In our work, <i>b</i> values are closed to 0.5 (Figures S8 and S9, Supporting Information), indicating a diffusion process. However, based on the integration of CV curves (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>e), 98.8% of the total charge storage capacity of the Bi@C composite electrode is capacitive at the sweep rate of 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S10, Supporting Information). Such high proportions of pseudocapacitive contribution results from fast kinetics, which may account for the good rate capability of the Bi@C electrode.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0045"/> These results apparently show that the redox process of the Bi@C composite anode is governed by diffusion process at low current rate and by capacitive process at high rate. This is in good agreement with the previous report.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0046"/> The kinetics of the sodium storage in Bi@C composite was further evaluated using galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) test (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>f). The cell was discharged/charged at a current pulse of 0.08 A g<sup>−1</sup> for a duration period of 15 min, followed by an open‐circuit stand for 240 min to allow fully relaxation to a quasi‐equilibrium potential. The overpotentials for the desodiation and sodiation processes are very small, below 0.1 V, suggesting a fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in Bi@C composite. These results strongly suggest that a fast and reversible desodiation/sodiation dynamics for the Bi@C composite.</p><p>In addition to the superior rate behavior, outstanding cycling performance was also delivered by the Bi@C composite with extremely high stability and ultralong cycle life. No capacity loss occurred after 3000 cycles at 0.8 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a high Coulombic efficiency of ≈99.9% (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>g). Even at a very high mass loading of 11.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, the Bi@C composite electrode could still deliver a high reversible capacity of 280 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles, corresponding a reversible areal specific capacity of 3.2 mA h cm<sup>−2</sup>, which is comparable to those commercial LIB anodes (Figure S11, Supporting Information). More amazingly, an ultralong and stable cycling performance was achieved with a high capacity retention of 265 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (95%) over 30 000 cycles (over 8 months) at a high current of 8 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0003"/>h and Figure S12, Supporting Information). No capacity decay was observed from 10 000 cycles. Coulombic efficiency is around unit and the stable cycling is still maintained without any decaying trend. This is so far the best performance for Bi‐based anode materials in SIBs (Table S3, Supporting Information). Such long cycle life, high capacity retention and high Coulombic efficiency make the Bi@C composite a very promising anode material for SIBs.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0034"/> For comparison, the pure Bi metal sample was prepared via the previously reported method. The pure Bi metal sample has particle sizes of 30–100 nm and exhibits the same rhombohedral phase (JCPDS 01‐085‐1331) as the Bi@C composite (Figure S13a,b, Supporting Information). The electrochemical performance of the pure Bi metal was evaluated in the same conditions used for the Bi@C composite anode. As shown by the CV and charge/discharge curves in Figure S13c,d in the Supporting Information, two cathodic peaks/discharge plateaus at 0.66 and 0.48 V and two anodic peaks/charge voltage plateaus at 0.57 and 0.78 V were observed, which are corresponding to the reversible reactions of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi ↔ NaBi and NaBi ↔ Bi, respectively. Although good cycling stability was obtained for the pure Bi metal electrode (Figure S13e, Supporting Information), worse rate capability was presented compared with the Bi@C composite electrode (Figure S13f, Supporting Information). The performance enhancement of the Bi@C composite is attributed to the carbon‐coating nanostructure, which not only ensures a fast kinetics, but also effectively alleviates the stress/strain caused by the volume change.</p><p>The performance of the Bi@C composite was further evaluated in a full cell that was fabricated by pairing the Bi@C composite anode with a Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode. The full cells are denoted as Bi@C//NVP (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a). NVP is a typical NASICON‐type compound, and has been widely investigated as a SIB cathode materials with great cycling stability, fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and superior thermal stability.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0047"/> The performance of NVP cathodes was tested in a half cell using Na metal anodes and electrolyte of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>/DME, and the NVP half cells are denoted as NVP//Na. A high voltage plateau at ≈3.4 V, stable cycling performance for 100 cycles and high rate capability up to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> were obtained for the NVP cathode, showing that it is a reliable cathode material for SIBs (Figure S14, Supporting Information). The Bi@C//NVP full cells display two flat voltage plateaus at 2.6 and 2.8 V (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0004"/>b), respectively, corresponding to the voltage differences between NVP cathodes (≈3.4 V) and Bi@C composite anodes (0.6 and 0.8 V). The electrochemical reversibility of Bi@C//NVP full cells is also validated by the two distinct redox couples of 2.6/2.8 and 2.8/3.0 V in CV curves (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0004"/>c). The overlapped CV and charge/discharge voltage curves in selected cycles indicate good cycling stability of the Bi@C//NVP full cells (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0004"/>d). A high reversible capacity of ≈260 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (with respective to the weight of Bi@C composite) was delivered in stable cycles. The energy density of this system based on Bi@C composite and NVP is calculated to be ≈144 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>. After 500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the full cells can still deliver a high capacity of ≈242 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (≈93% capacity retention), demonstrating excellent cycling stability (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0004"/>e). Good rate capability is also measured with a high capacity retention of 130.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0004"/>f and Figure S15, Supporting Information). These outstanding properties of full cells unambiguously show that our Bi@C composite can serve as a viable sodium storage anode material.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0034"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201904771-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201904771:adma201904771-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201904771-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of Bi@C//NVP full cells: a) schematic working mechanism, b) typical charge/discharge curves of the NVP cathode, the Bi@C anode and Bi@C//NVP full cells, c) CV curves at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, d) galvanostatic charge/discharge curves, e) cycling performance at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and f) rate performance.</caption></figure><p>There is no doubt that the outstanding performance of the Bi@C composite is attributed to the unique structure of the Bi@C composite. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link> displayed the morphology and structure evolution of the Bi@C composite electrodes after different cycles. After the first cycle, the Bi@C composite were coated with a layer of SEI (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>a,b, Supporting Information), and then gradually evolved into small particles after 50 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>c,d) and finally developed into a stable network structure after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>e,f). The evolution of the SEI films was also investigated using atomic force microscope (AFM). The pristine electrodes and those after 1, 10, 50, 100, and 200 cycles were characterized by AFM (Figure S16, Supporting Information). The phase images reveal that the pristine electrode shows obvious phase separation in large area, which becomes small and uniform after 200 cycles, suggesting a thicker SEI layer was formed on the surface of the Bi@C composite anodes. In addition, the more uniform and smaller phase separation after 200 cycles is consistent with the morphology evolution revealed by SEM images (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>a–g). The network structure should be able to reduce the strain and minimize surface energy to tolerate the volume changes during the charge/discharge processes, and thus warranting a stable cycling performance.<link href="#adma201904771-bib-0032 #adma201904771-bib-0048"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201904771-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201904771:adma201904771-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201904771-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–f) SEM image of the pristine electrodes and those after 1, 10, 50, 100, and 200 cycles, respectively. g) TEM image, h,i) HRTEM images, the corresponding inverse FFT images and FFT patterns of marked areas (red frames in (g)) and j) elemental mappings of Bi, C, and Na of the Bi@C composite electrode after 3000 cycles. k) XRD patterns of pristine and cycled electrodes after 20 000 cycles.</caption></figure><p>The structure of the Bi@C composite was investigated by HRTEM after 3000 cycles. The HRTEM images illustrate that the carbon coating layer on the Bi NPs is still tightly attached to the Bi nanocrystallites with no peeling off, showing that the stress/strain produced by the volume changes during charge/discharge processes can be effectively alleviated (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>g). More interestingly, the single nanocrystallite structure is well reserved with no deformation (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>h,i). The lattice space is measured to be 0.32 nm and the corresponding fast Fourier transform (FFT) patterns present the characteristic diffraction points of the characteristic lattice fringes of Bi (012) plane (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>h,i). The element mapping images also reveal that Bi is still situated in the carbon networks, consistent with the results before cycling. This is responsible for facile Na<sup>+</sup> transport and structural stability of the composite electrode during sodiation/desodiation (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>j). In the XRD pattern of the Bi@C composite electrode after 20 000 cycles, the peaks of rhombohedral phase Bi are obviously presented, confirming the existence of crystalline Bi (Figure <link href="#adma201904771-fig-0005"/>k). The structure stability was also revealed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements, where no obvious resistance changes occurred over 1000 cycles (Figure S17, Supporting Information).</p><p>In summary, bismuth nanoparticle@carbon composite comprising carbon‐coated Bi NPs distributed uniformly within carbon networks was synthesized by a facile heating method from a commercial product of bismuth citrate. The in situ H‐XRD and H‐FTIR analysis and theoretical computations revealed that the metal center conversion is prior to the carbonization of the ligand center during the heat treatment, which assures a carbon‐coating nanostructure. As an anode material for SIBs, the Bi@C composite demonstrated superior performance, a long cycle life of 30 000 cycles at a high current density of 8 A g<sup>−1</sup> and ultrahigh rate capability of 71% capacity retention at an ultrahigh current rate of 60 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even at a very high mass loading of 11.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, the Bi@C composite electrode can still deliver a high reversible capacity of 280 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles. Furthermore, full SIBs by coupling the Bi@C composite anode with a well‐developed Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode also delivered impressive performance, a high capacity of 260 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and cyclability of 93% capacity retention after 500 cycles. Combining the facile synthesis and the commercial precursor, the exceptional performance makes the Bi@C composite very promising for practical large‐scale applications.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201904771-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 51672188 and 21703036) and Natural Science Foundation of Tianjin City (Grant No.: 16JCYBJC40900).</p></section><section xml:id="adma201904771-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma201904771-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma201904771-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma201904771-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>G.</givenNames> <familyName>Assat</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.
