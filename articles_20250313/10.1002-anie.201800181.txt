Conventional electrolytes do not contribute to charge storage in battery systems, but redox‐active electrolytes augment this property via charge transfer reactions at the electrode–electrolyte interface. The capacity of the cathode combined with that provided by the electrolyte redox reaction thus increases overall charge storage. An aqueous sodium hexacyanoferrate (Na<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>) solution is employed as the redox‐active electrolyte (Na‐FC) and sodium nickel Prussian blue (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiBP) as the Na<sup>+</sup> ion insertion/de‐insertion cathode. The capacity of DESIB with Na‐FC electrolyte is twice that of a battery using a conventional (Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>) electrolyte. The use of redox‐active electrolytes in batteries of any kind is an efficient and scalable approach to develop advanced high‐energy‐density storage systems.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>A redox‐active electrolyte</b> is used in a dual‐electrolyte Na‐ion battery to enhance its capacity performance. When an aqueous Na<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> solution is used as the redox‐active electrolyte, the overall cathode capacity increases from 47.8 to 107.11 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201800181:anie201800181-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201800181-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201800181-sec-0001"><p>There has been considerable interest in the development of Na‐based batteries, because of the greater abundance and availability of Na compared with Li. Na‐S batteries,<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0001"/> Na<sup>+</sup> ion batteries,<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0002"/> aqueous Na<sup>+</sup> ion batteries (ASIBs),<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0003"/> Na‐air batteries,<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0004 #anie201800181-bib-0005"/> and seawater batteries<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0006 #anie201800181-bib-0007"/> have been explored. ASIBs have received the greatest focus for large‐scale electrical energy storage (EES), because of their benefits of low cost, environmental friendliness, and safety. However, the true applicability of ASIBs remains out of reach owing to their low working voltage (ca. 1.3 V) owing to the limited water stability window (1.23 V) imposed by hydrogen (H<sub>2</sub>) and oxygen (O<sub>2</sub>) evolution. This results in severely compromised energy densities. Therefore, ASIBs deliver an energy density of only 60–100 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>. To achieve high‐voltage ASIBs, the H<sub>2</sub> evolution reaction (HER) should be avoided at the anode. If the potential of the negative electrode can be decreased below that of the HER, metallic Na can be used and a battery voltage greater than 3.0 V can be expected.</p><p>Based on the above considerations, a dual‐electrolyte Na<sup>+</sup> ion battery (DESIB) has been devised<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0008"/> with a solid ceramic membrane separating a Na metal anode (in an organic electrolyte) and a cathode (in an aqueous electrolyte) to elevate the cell voltage to &gt;3.0 V. However, the energy density of the battery depends not only on the cell voltage, but also on the capacity of the electrode materials. Several cathode materials have been reported for Na<sup>+</sup> ion storage in aqueous media. However, traditional intercalation cathode materials such as Na<sub>3</sub>MnTi(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (58.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>),<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0009"/> NaMnO<sub>2</sub>/Na0⋅44 MnO<sub>2</sub> (55/47 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>),<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0003 #anie201800181-bib-0010"/> Na<sub>2</sub>NiFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (65 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>),<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0011"/> Na<sub>2</sub>CuFe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (59 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>),<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0012"/> and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (73 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>)<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0013"/> are incapable of providing a large capacity. Therefore, a feasible strategy for increasing the energy performance of DESIBs is to use a cathode with a greater reversible capacity.</p><p>Rather than finding a high capacity cathode material, we present an alternative concept for enhancing the charge storage of a DESIB by using an aqueous redox‐active electrolyte in the cathode compartment. Because conventional electrolytes in a DESIB act as a mediator for transporting Na<sup>+</sup> ions in the anodic (Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) and cathodic (Na<sup>+</sup> de‐insertion/insertion) reactions, they are electrochemically inactive. However, a redox‐active electrolyte can not only transport Na<sup>+</sup> ions for the anodic and cathodic reactions, but also provide electrochemically active ionic species for an additional charge transfer reaction (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0001"/> a). Therefore, the combined capacity of the cathode and that of the aqueous redox‐active electrolyte will increase the overall capacity. This concept is demonstrated using sodium hexacyanoferrate (Na<sub>4</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (Na‐FC)) dissolved in aqueous solution as the redox‐active electrolyte and sodium nickel Prussian blue (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiBP) as the Na<sup>+</sup> ion insertion/de‐insertion cathode. The Na‐FC redox electrolyte is known to be non‐corrosive when compared to iodine‐ and bromine‐based redox electrolytes and can offer electrochemically active [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> couple to the cathode reaction as well as sufficient Na<sup>+</sup> ions for the anode reaction.<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0014"/> Besides, Na<sub>x</sub>‐NiBP is considered a promising Na<sup>+</sup> ion insertion/de‐insertion cathode among the cathode materials owing to the outstanding high‐rate capability and high cycle stability in aqueous electrolytes.<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0015 #anie201800181-bib-0016"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201800181-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201800181:anie201800181-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201800181-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>DESIB proof‐of‐concept with an aqueous redox‐active electrolyte. a) Illustration of electrochemical processes in a DESIB with an aqueous redox‐active electrolyte, b) configuration and photographic image of the DESIB cell (coin cell assembly is described in the experimental and is shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S2), c) charge–discharge profiles of Na‐FC/DESIB and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB, and d) illustration of the cathodic reaction at the cathode (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB@carbon fiber (CF)) of DESIB with Na‐FC electrolyte.</p></caption></figure><p>A typical X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern (Supporting Information, Figure S1a) shows that Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiBP prepared by a simple precipitation method exhibits high crystallinity and matches well with the face‐centered cubic phase of Prussian blue [JCPDF #99–1161]. The Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB sample contains particle sizes from about 40–130 nm (Supporting Information, Figure S1b). The electrochemical performance of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB was examined as a coating on carbon felt (Supporting Information, Figure S1c). The Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB particles are uniformly coated on the carbon fiber of the felt as shown by SEM (Supporting Information, Figure S1d).</p><p>The configuration and photographic image of a DESIB cell with an aqueous Na‐FC or Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> electrolyte is shown in Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0001"/> b. The coin cell comprises a Na metal anode with 1 <sc>m</sc> NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>/tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) electrolyte, a Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB coated carbon felt cathode with aqueous Na‐FC or Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> electrolyte, and a NASICON (Na<sub>3</sub>Zr<sub>2</sub>Si<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>12</sub>) ceramic membrane (see the Experimental Section in the Supporting Information for details). The NASICON membrane allows only Na<sup>+</sup> ion transport and isolates moisture‐sensitive Na from the aqueous electrolyte. Hereafter, the DESIB with aqueous Na‐FC electrolyte is designated as Na‐FC/DESIB, and the aqueous Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> electrolyte is designated as Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB.</p><p>The electrochemical performances of Na‐FC/DESIB and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB were first examined using a galvanostatic charge–discharge technique. The cut‐off voltages were set within the range of 2.8 to 3.8 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>) in consideration of the limitations of water oxidation and oxygen reduction reactions at the cathode. Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0001"/> c compares the charge–discharge profiles of Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB and Na‐FC/DESIB at a current density of 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> during the first cycle. The average discharge voltage and voltage difference are about 3.27 V and 0.09 V for Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB and 3.24 V and 0.07 V for Na‐FC/DESIB. The initial discharge capacities were 47.8 and 107.11 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB and Na‐FC/DESIB, respectively (the calculated capacity is based on the mass of cathode active material unless otherwise specified). There are no large differences in average discharge voltage and voltage difference. However, Na‐FC/DESIB exhibited nearly twice the capacity of Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB. This result suggests that the use of Na‐FC in a DESIB enhances charge storage performance through their redox reactions between [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> couples combined with Na<sup>+</sup> ion de‐insertion/insertion reactions at Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB. In detail, Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> electrolyte acts solely as an ionic medium to transport Na<sup>+</sup> ions for de‐insertion/insertion at Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB. Thus, charge storage is limited by the cathode when Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> electrolyte is used in the DESIB. However, the Na‐FC electrolyte acts as an ionic medium for transporting Na<sup>+</sup> ions in de‐insertion/insertion at Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB and provide electrochemically active species (that is, [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> redox couples) for an additional charge transfer reaction at the cathode (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0001"/> d). Thus, significant charge storage enhancement is achieved by use of the Na‐FC electrolyte together with Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB.</p><p>Both Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB and Na‐FC/DESIB exhibit a stable capacity with a coulombic efficiency of about 99 % over 100 cycles at 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0002"/> a. The large reversible discharge capacity and excellent cyclic performance of Na‐FC/DESIB suggest that the [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> redox reactions and fast Na<sup>+</sup> ion extraction/insertion reactions at Na<sub>x</sub>‐NiPB do not interfere with one another during charge–discharge processes. Extended cyclic performance is an important requirement in real‐time applications. Thus, Na‐FC/DESIB was subjected to repeated cycling at 0.3 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> as shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S3. The results confirm the excellent stability of the Na‐FC electrolyte and Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB for more than 1000 cycles. The rate performance of Na‐FC/DESIB also was evaluated by cycling at various current densities (0.05–1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>; Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0002"/> b). Capacities of 107.11, 104.49, 101.01, 96.41, 90.65, and 26.61 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> were achieved at 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, and 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, respectively. Moreover, the capacities obtained at 0.1, 0.4, and 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> were 97.55, 90.01, and 24.84 %, respectively, of the capacity at 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, and more than 99 % of the capacity is recovered when the current rate is reduced from 1 to 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="anie201800181-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201800181:anie201800181-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201800181-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical cycling and rate performances of DESIBs. a) Cycling capacity retention and efficiency of Na‐FC/DESIB and Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB and b) rate performance capability of Na‐FC/DESIB.</p></caption></figure><p>In Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> a, traces (i), (ii), and (iii) are the CV curves, respectively, of Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB, Na‐FC/DESIB, and Na‐FC/DESIB without Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB. All of the traces (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> a) show well‐defined redox peaks within the 2.8–3.8 V potential window. Peaks (i), (ii), and (iii) correspond to Na<sup>+</sup> ion de‐insertion/insertion reactions at Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB, [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> redox reactions combined with Na<sup>+</sup> ion de‐insertion/insertion reactions at Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB, and [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> redox reactions,<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0014"/> respectively. It should be noted that to maintain the charge neutrality within Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB, Na<sup>+</sup> ion de‐insertion/insertion takes place through redox reaction of Fe<sup>II</sup>/Fe<sup>III</sup> couple.<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0011 #anie201800181-bib-0017"/> However, the Na‐FC electrolyte plus Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB trace (see curve (ii)) exhibits a large area under the current/voltage curve, which is consistent with the enhanced charge storage of the DESIB. Also, the oxidation onset potential of curve (ii) on the positive scan is similar to that of curve (iii), and the reduction onset potential of curve (ii) is similar to that of curve (i) on negative scan. From these results we conclude the following regarding the Na‐FC/DESIB: 1) oxidation begins with the oxidation of [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup> during positive scan (red arrow in Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> a); and 2) reduction begins with the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion reaction during negative scan (green arrow in Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> a).</p><figure xml:id="anie201800181-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201800181:anie201800181-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201800181-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical reaction and mechanism at the cathode of the DESIB using Na‐FC electrolyte. a) CV curves (at 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) of Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>/DESIB (i), Na‐FC/DESIB (ii), and Na‐FC/DESIB without Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB (iii), b) charge–discharge profiles of Na‐FC/DESIB at 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> including the step‐by‐step charge and discharge routes, and c) kinetic model of the cathode in charge and discharge states.</p></caption></figure><p>Based on the evidence provided by CV, the electrochemical behavior and mechanism at the cathode of the DESIB using Na‐FC electrolyte is described by the charge–discharge profile and representations shown in Figures <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> b and c. Upon charging, [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup> ions first move toward the cathode current collector and are rapidly oxidized to [Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> (that is, [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>→[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup>+e<sup>−</sup>) at the electrode–electrolyte interface. Upon increasing the charging voltage, Na<sub>x</sub>‐NiPB becomes positively charged, and Na<sup>+</sup> ions are expelled from the crystal lattice (that is, Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB→<i>y</i> Na<sup>+</sup>+e<sup>−</sup>+Na<sub><i>x</i>−<i>y</i></sub>‐NiPB), while Fe<sup>II</sup> is oxidized to Fe<sup>III</sup> in Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB. The free Na<sup>+</sup> ions rapidly transfer through the electrolyte owing to their better mobility in the liquid phase (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> b and c(i)). Upon discharge, Na<sub><i>x</i>−<i>y</i></sub>‐NiPB first becomes negatively charged, allowing Na<sup>+</sup> ions to be reinserted in the crystal structure (that is, Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB+<i>y</i> Na<sup>+</sup>+e<sup>−</sup>←Na<sub><i>x</i>−<i>y</i></sub>‐NiPB), and Fe<sup>III</sup> is reduced to Fe<sup>II</sup> in Na<sub>x</sub>‐NiPB. At a more negative potential, [Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> is reduced to [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup> (that is, [Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup>+e<sup>−</sup>→[Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>) at the electrode‐electrolyte interface (Figures <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0003"/> and c(ii)).</p><p>To address practical applications of the Na‐FC/DESIB, electrochemical performance was further studied by replacing the Na metal anode with a semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> (sodium biphenyl complex) anode (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> a). The electrochemical reaction of semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> is NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>↔[C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>]<sup>.</sup>+Na<sup>+</sup>+e<sup>−</sup>. Semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> was prepared by dissolving Na metal in a solution of C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> in TEGDME (see the Experimental Section in the Supporting Information for details). The advantage of semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> anode is that it can act as electrolyte too unlike to traditional Na<sup>+</sup> ion insertion/de‐insertion Na anodes (for example, hard carbon and Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>), and safer than Na metal and can sustain direct contact with the aqueous medium. Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> b shows that no fire is observed when water is added to semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>, contrary to the case with Na metal. Reaction of semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> with water produces NaOH according to the reaction 2 NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>+2 H<sub>2</sub>O→2 C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>+2 NaOH+H<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> b).<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0018"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201800181-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201800181:anie201800181-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201800181-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Diagram and electrochemical performance when using a semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> anode in a Na‐FC/DESIB. a) Image of an Na‐FC/DESIB with a semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> anode, b) reaction between Na metal and water and between semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> and water (the blue arrow indicates the production of C<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> and NaOH after adding water to semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>, and the red arrow indicates the remaining semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>), c) charge–discharge profiles of an Na‐FC/DESIB with semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>, d) cycling capacity retention and efficiency of an Na‐FC/DESIB with semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub>, e) current density vs. capacity behavior, and f) polarization curve.</p></caption></figure><p>Na‐metal‐free Na‐FC/DESIB produces a discharge capacity of 104 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with an average discharge voltage of 3.11 V at 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> c; the calculated capacity is based on the mass of cathode active material unless otherwise specified). This capacity is very close to the value for the Na‐metal DESIB (107.11 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>; Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> c). However, the voltage is slightly less than that of the Na‐metal Na‐FC/DESIB (3.24 V), which could be due to the lower redox potential of NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> versus Na. The results in Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> d indicate that even at a large current density of 0.4 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, Na metal‐free Na‐FC/DESIB yields a capacity retention of about 92.17 % over 500 cycles. Moreover, capacities of 102.28, 99.18, 96.22, 93.37, 88.11, 81.25, and 58.29 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> were obtained at 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 1, 1.25, and 1.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, respectively. The corresponding charge‐discharge curves are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S4.</p><p>The semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> anode enhances the rate performance of Na‐FC/DESIB. Whereas the 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity is 24.78 % of the 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity with Na metal in the Na‐FC/DESIB, the 1.5 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity is 55.82 % of the 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity with semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> e; Supporting Information, Table S1). Moreover, the power of the Na‐FC/DESIB increases slightly from about 12.5 to about 13.8 mW (or about 6.25 to about 6.9 mW cm<sup>−2</sup>) when using a semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> anode as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201800181-fig-0004"/> f. This power is greater than that of dual electrolyte Li batteries reported elsewhere (4 mW cm<sup>−2</sup> (Li/S battery)<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0019"/> and about 5.9 mW cm<sup>−2</sup> (Li/[Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> battery<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0020"/>). The improved rate capability and power performance might arise from a decrease in resistance when using a semi‐liquid NaC<sub>12</sub>H<sub>10</sub> anode. The interfacial resistance also decreases slightly as shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S5, which may be related to the resistance of the NASICON ceramic membrane interface with the semi‐liquid.</p><p>In summary, we find that the overall charge storage performance of a DESIB is enhanced by use of aqueous Na‐FC instead of conventional aqueous Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> as an electrolyte. The Na‐FC electrolyte not only acts as an ionic medium, but contributes the electrochemically active [Fe<sup>II</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>/[Fe<sup>III</sup>(CN)<sub>6</sub>]<sup>3−</sup> couple to the cathode reaction. Use of the Na‐FC electrolyte in DESIB increases the overall cathode (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>‐NiPB) capacity from 47.8 to 107.11 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> without sacrificing cell voltage (3.24 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). This capacity is greater than that of previously reported<link href="#anie201800181-bib-0003 #anie201800181-bib-0009 #anie201800181-bib-0010 #anie201800181-bib-0011 #anie201800181-bib-0012 #anie201800181-bib-0013"/> cathode materials employing aqueous electrolytes. Further improvement in cathode capacity may be possible by employing highly concentrated Na‐FC electrolytes (Supporting Information, Figure S6 and Table S2) or novel multi‐redox active electrolytes. Use of a redox‐active electrolyte is not restricted to DESIBs, and we hope this proof‐of‐concept study will lead to the development of advanced high‐energy‐density batteries.</p><section xml:id="anie201800181-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This research work was supported by the Korea Research Fellowship Program through the National Research Foundation of Korea (NRF) funded by the Ministry of Science and ICT (2016H1D3A1909680).
