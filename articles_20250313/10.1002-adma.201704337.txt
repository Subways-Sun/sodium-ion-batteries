xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) offer a promise of a scalable, low‐cost, and environmentally benign means of renewable energy storage. However, the low capacity and poor rate capability of anode materials present an unavoidable challenge. In this work, it is demonstrated that surface phosphorylated TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube arrays grown on Ti substrate can be efficient anode materials for SIBs. Fabrication of the phosphorylated nanoarray film is based on the electrochemical anodization of Ti metal in NH<sub>4</sub>F solution and subsequent phosphorylation using sodium hypophosphite. The phosphorylated TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube arrays afford a reversible capacity of 334 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 67 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, a superior rate capability of 147 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 3350 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, and a stable cycle performance up to 1000 cycles. In situ X‐ray diffraction and transmission electron microscopy reveal the near‐zero strain response and robust mechanical behavior of the TiO<sub>2</sub> host upon (de)sodiation, suggesting its excellent structural stability in the Na<sup>+</sup> storage application.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201704337-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube arrays on a Ti substrate</b> functionalized by surface phosphorylation are directly adapted as efficient anodes for sodium‐ion batteries. Benefiting from synergy of unique nanotube structure, 3D array architecture, and high surface reactivity, the TiO<sub>2</sub> arrays afford a high reversible capacity of 334 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and a superior rate capability of 147 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 3350 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201704337:adma201704337-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201704337-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201704337-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201704337-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are a key energy storage alternative to current lithium‐ion system, owing to the abundant resources and wide availability of sodium.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0001"/> In pursuit of anode materials for SIBs, titanium‐based compounds have captured the most interest as Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion host materials due to their reasonable operation voltage, stable structure, low cost, and nontoxicity. During the past several years, many titanium‐based oxides have been extensively explored and shown appealing electrochemical features.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0002"/> Among them, network structured titanium dioxides (TiO<sub>2</sub>) have rich polymorphs and sufficient sites to accommodate additional Na<sup>+</sup> ions, and thus are a potential host for Na storage. TiO<sub>2</sub> offers a large theoretical capacity of 335 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a reasonable insertion potential (≈0.6 V, vs Na unless otherwise stated), which essentially eliminates the problem of dangerous Na plating.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0003"/> As there are no <i>d</i> electrons in TiO<sub>2</sub> polymorphs, they are an electrical insulator and exhibit a low electrochemical activity and sluggish Na reaction kinetics.</p><p>In recent years, great efforts have been devoted to development of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanostructures, which can maximize the active surface area for Na reaction. Nonetheless, nanomaterials tend to agglomerate upon electrochemical cycling, thus losing their active surface.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0004"/> This issue can be addressed by carbon modification<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0005"/> or by design of 3D array architecture.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0006"/> The latter is particularly promising as it allows a high material loading and large accessible surface yet maintains short ion‐transport distance.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0007"/> However, previous work demonstrated that the electrochemical activity of TiO<sub>2</sub> arrays might be compromised by the low surface reactivity.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0006"/> To mitigate this issue, doping of a nonmetal element such as F, S, or N into TiO<sub>2</sub> has shown improved performance.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0008"/> The improvement is generally attributed to structural modulation rather than surface functionalization, although surface functionalization has been proven a key approach in the realization of high electrochemical activity.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0009"/></p><p>Surface functionalization through phosphorus has received tremendous attention as it can stimulate high chemical reactivity for fast and efficient charge transport and transfer.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010"/> In the present work, we combined the 3D nanotube architecture with phosphate functionalization to efficiently modulate the Na<sup>+</sup> ion storage behavior in TiO<sub>2</sub>. The phosphorylated TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube array (denoted as P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>) has several intrinsic merits for Na storage. First, the self‐supported configuration eliminates the need for a binder and conducting additive and can be directly adapted as an electrode. Second, the 3D architecture retains a large surface area for electrolyte access and adequate channels for Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion. Third, phosphorylation boosts the reactivity and leads to a high ratio of active surface. As a result, the P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarrays exhibit superior Na‐storage behavior, affording a high reversible capacity of 334 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, a rate capability of 147 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 3350 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, and a stable cycle performance up to 1000 cycles. Furthermore, unlike sulfidation, which can reduce the bandgap of TiO<sub>2</sub> through mixing of the delocalized S 3p states with the valence band, phosphorylation does not induce a narrowing bandgap in TiO<sub>2</sub>.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010" style="qv">10a,b</link>] It acts prominently on the surface rather than on the bulk. This provides an ideal platform to establish a correlation between surface functionalization and reactivity, which is crucial for realization of efficient Na storage. To this end, we further carried out in situ microscopic characterizations to shed light on the role of surface functionalization.</p><p>The P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> arrays were electrochemical grown in a 0.15 <sc>m</sc> NH<sub>4</sub>F solution, and then annealed with NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub>∙H<sub>2</sub>O vapor at 500 °C for 1 h in an Ar flow gas, as illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a. Details of the synthesis can be found in Methods, Supporting Information. X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns show that the anodized TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes (denoted as TiO<sub>2</sub> for comparison with P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>) are disordered in structure and convert to an anatase phase (PDF #21‐1272) after annealing at 500 °C with or without NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub>∙H<sub>2</sub>O (Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0001"/>b). The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0001"/>c,d reveal that both the anodized and phosphorylated TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes have the same diameter of ≈80 nm and a tube thickness of 10 nm. The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0001"/>e reveals a highly ordered architecture of nanotubes. The high‐resolution TEM image in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0001"/>f and selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0001"/>g confirm the anatase phase of TiO<sub>2</sub>. Interestingly, there is a disordered layer near the surface of the nanotubes. This might be the phosphorylation layer, whose formation is related to the mismatch between the phosphate and the oxygen group.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0008"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201704337-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201704337:adma201704337-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201704337-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Synthesis and characterization of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube array grown on Ti substrate. a) Schematic of synthesizing P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarray. TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarray was first electrochemically grown on Ti substrate (I), and then phosphorylated to P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> (II). b) XRD pattern of TiO<sub>2</sub> and P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarrays. SEM images of c) TiO<sub>2</sub> and d) P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarrays. e,f) TEM image of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes. Lattice fringe spacing of 0.352 nm corresponds to the (101) facets of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub>. Disordered area near surface is indicated by arrows. g) SAED of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarray.</caption></figure><p>Both P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub> were probed by Raman spectroscopy (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). The spectra reveal distinct scattering peaks at 199, 396, 506, and 621 cm<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to E<sub>g</sub>, B<sub>1g</sub>, A<sub>1g</sub>, and E<sub>g</sub> phonon modes of crystalline anatase TiO<sub>2</sub>, respectively.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0008" style="qv">8a</link>] Two additional D and G bands can be assigned to trace pyrolytic carbon originating from residual ethylene glycol. No Raman signals due to titanium phosphate or pyrophosphate are observed for P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>, as their crystallization occurs at higher temperatures than 500 °C.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0011"/> To unfold the surface phosphorylation, the chemical composition of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> was examined by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Figure S1, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0002"/>b–d presents the core‐level spectra of P 2p, O 1s, and Ti 2p, respectively. The P 2p broad peak is composed of P 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and P 2p<sub>1/2</sub> doublets at 134.5 and 135.4 eV, which are assigned to phosphate species. No phosphorus or phosphide species are detected, as their signal occurs at ≈130 cm<sup>−1</sup>.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010" style="qv">10c</link>] The O 1s spectroscopy consists of three peaks at 530.9, 532.1, and 533.6 eV, which can be assigned to O–Ti, P–O–Ti, and O–P, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0002"/>c). The Ti 2p spectrum is composed of two sets of doublets (Ti 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ti 2p<sub>1/2</sub>), which may be assigned to Ti–O and Ti–PO<sub>4</sub> species, respectively.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010" style="qv">10b</link>] The introduction of PO<sub>4</sub> species does not alter the bandgap or the conductivity of TiO<sub>2</sub>,[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010" style="qv">10a,b</link>] indicating that phosphorylation is a surface modification technique (Figure S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201704337-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201704337:adma201704337-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201704337-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Raman spectra of TiO<sub>2</sub> and P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarrays. Core‐level XPS spectra of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube array, b) P 2p, c) O 1s, and d) Ti 2p. e) HAADF‐STEM image and EDS mappings of Ti–K, O–K, and P–K. f) Schematic illustrating the evolution from TiO<sub>2</sub> to P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0002"/>e presents a high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning TEM (HAADF‐STEM) image and energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mappings of a single P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube. The images verify the presence of Ti, O, and P elements and their homogeneous distribution on the nanotube, suggesting a neat surface phosphorylation process. The phosphate species likely accumulate near the nanotube surface, as their diffusion into anatase bulk is unfavorable.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010" style="qv">10b</link>] On the basis of the information, we tentatively propose a phosphorylation mechanism (Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0002"/>f). When the sample was heated to 200 °C or above, NaH<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>2</sub>∙H<sub>2</sub>O will decompose to generate PH<sub>3</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O vapor, which then react with the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube to generate phosphates on the surface.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0010" style="qv">10c</link>] In this process, the H<sub>2</sub>O serves as an oxidizing agent to convert phosphide to phosphate.</p><p>To examine the Na storage performance of as‐fabricated 3D arrays, the TiO<sub>2</sub> film with one side being polished is directly adapted as the working electrode coupled with a Na foil as the counter electrode to assemble a coin cell. The simple battery configuration, however, guarantees a nanoscale diffusion path despite the length of the nanotubes reaching several micrometers. Galvanostatic measurements were performed on the coin cell at 0.2 C (1 C = 335 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) to assess the electrochemical properties of the TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode arrays. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a compares the galvanostatic curves of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub> over the initial three cycles. P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> affords an initial discharge (sodiation) capacity of 569 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which is beyond the theoretical 335 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and could be primarily due to the formation of a solid electrode/electrolyte interphase. The charge (desodiation) capacity is 334 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, and the corresponding Coulombic efficiency (CE) is 59%. On the other hand, TiO<sub>2</sub> only affords initial sodiation and desodiation capacities of 402 and 201 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, with a CE of 50%. The larger capacity of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> versus TiO<sub>2</sub> might result from the more rapid electrolyte wetting and spreading (Figure S3, Supporting Information). The better wettability allows more efficient access of the electrolyte ions to the electrode, leading to an improved surface reactivity and electrochemical activity.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0009" style="qv">9c</link>] Hence, the phosphorylation improves not only the charge storage but also the efficiency of the TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode arrays. This Na activity of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> is overwhelmingly superior to that of recently reported TiO<sub>2</sub> electrodes (Table S1, Supporting Information), implying great efficacy by phosphorylation.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0004 #adma201704337-bib-0005 #adma201704337-bib-0006"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201704337-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201704337:adma201704337-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201704337-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoarray for Na storage. a) Comparison of initial galvanostatic cycling of TiO<sub>2</sub> and P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>. b) CV curves of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> at sweep rates from 0.1 to 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. c) Logarithmic relationship between the peak currents and sweep rates. Comparison of TiO<sub>2</sub> and P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> cycling at d) various rates and e) a fixed rate of 10 C. Reversible (desodiation) capacity is adopted.</caption></figure><p>To understand the Na storage process, cyclic voltammetry (CV) at various rates was conducted on P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0003"/>b) and TiO<sub>2</sub> (Figure S4, Supporting Information) electrode arrays. Both P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub> show a well‐defined redox pair at 0.60/0.80 V and 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, representing Na<sup>+</sup> ion storage in the tunnels of anatase phase. When the sweep rate increases from 0.1 to 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the electrodes retain the basic CV shape despite the shift of redox peaks. A logarithmic plot of the peak current versus the sweep rate for P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> gives a perfect linear relationship, from which slopes of 0.83 and 0.84 can be drawn for cathodic and anodic processes, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0003"/>c). These values imply a combination behavior of battery and capacitor, or pseudocapacitor, for P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>.[<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0005" style="qv">5b</link>] As P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> exhibits higher redox reaction currents than TiO<sub>2</sub>, the phosphorylated sample has a higher reactivity.</p><p>The role of phosphorylation in the electrochemical process was investigated by comparing the rate capability of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub> electrodes. The electrodes were first tested at increased rates of 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 C for five cycles each, and then at gradual decreasing rates to 0.2 C. The charge capacities at each stage are summarized in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0003"/>d and Figure S5 (Supporting Information). Clearly, P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> exhibits robust performance at all current rates. For instance, at a rate of 1 C it affords a capacity of 256 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which is comparable to that of graphite utilized in lithium batteries. At a much higher rate of 10 C, it still delivers a capacity of 147 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which is 44% of the theoretical value. More importantly, when the current rate returns 0.2 C, a high capacity of 335 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> is restored, suggesting that the electrode has not been affected by the high rates at all. In contrast, TiO<sub>2</sub> has a much lower capacity, i.e., 164 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C and 85 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 C. More promising is that the surface functionalized P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode displays superior long‐term operating stability. Electrochemical Na cycling at a high rate of 10 C was performed on P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> over 1000 continuous cycles. As Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0003"/>e shows P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> retains a capacity of 143 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over 500 cycles and 141 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (≈94% of that in the 2nd cycle) over 1000 cycles, which is far above that of the TiO<sub>2</sub> counterpart. As P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub> have almost identical particle shape, surface area, bandgap, and reaction resistance (Figure S6, Supporting Information), the higher capacity level of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> can be attributed to the electrochemically more reactive surface.</p><p>To further understand the electrochemical processes, we monitored the structural and morphological evolution of P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrodes by in situ XRD and in situ TEM.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0012"/> <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link> shows the (dis)charge‐related XRD patterns of a working P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode in a sealed coin cell with Na metal as the counter electrode. As one can see, the dominant peaks of (101) and (200) TiO<sub>2</sub> do not change much during the full cycle, although a slight peak broadening can be ascribed to the sodiation‐induced partial pulverization and subtle strain generation, which are negligible. However, no obvious peak shift or new peak generation indicates the good structural stability of TiO<sub>2</sub> upon repetitive (de)sodiation. A similar phenomenon is also observed for the TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode (Figure S7, Supporting Information). <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link> shows time sequential TEM images of the sodiation process in a P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube collected at different sodiation stages with the bias of −2 V against the Na counter electrode. The nanotube has an original diameter of about 95.5 nm. The diameter increases slightly to 96.8 nm at 10 s of sodiation and to 98.7 nm after 30 s of sodiation, as ion exchange between nanocrystalline phases in intercalated TiO<sub>2</sub> anatase is rather rapid.<link href="#adma201704337-bib-0013"/> Further sodiation to 120 s only leads to a subtle increase in the nanotube diameter, suggesting that Na storage in P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube is close to its theoretical capacity. The macroscopic diameter expansion is thus calculated to be about 3.2%. In contrast, TiO<sub>2</sub> manifests a sluggish process toward Na<sup>+</sup> insertion. No obvious change is observed even after 360 s of sodiation (Figure S8, Supporting Information). Upon the subsequent desodiation in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0005"/>e,f, the P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube again exhibits favorable Na reaction kinetics by reducing the diameter almost to its starting value (96.0 nm). This implies that most of the Na<sup>+</sup> ions that are previously inserted into P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> are reversibly extracted, demonstrating the good reversibility of the P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode upon repetitive cycling. The surface of the fresh nanotube is quite smooth (Figure S9, Supporting Information), but it becomes rough after sodiation (Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0005"/>g), indicating the possible formation of amorphous and porous Na<sub>2</sub>O on the surface of the nanotube. The SAED patterns of the nanotube before and after sodiation are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201704337-fig-0005"/>h,i, respectively. A microscopic (101) lattice expansion of about 3% (from 0.343 to 0.354 nm) upon sodiation is captured, and it agrees well with the macroscopic diameter expansion. Such a good mechanical property is important practically since at the bulk level, it can prevent the electrode from delamination and peeling off of the active materials, which can thus maintain the good cycling performance in the long run. SEM observation confirms that the P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrodes retain the ordered nanotubular architecture over long‐term cycles (Figure S10, Supporting Information).</p><p>Encouraged by the excellent electrochemical performance, we assembled full cells using P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> as the anode and carbon‐coated Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C) as the cathode in a rough mass ratio of 1:2.5. The electrochemical activity of this array anode is further boosted by surface phosphorylation. Benefiting from the direct electrical connection, rapid electrolyte ion diffusion, and high surface reactivity, the P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> electrode arrays have achieved a superior electrochemical activity toward Na storage. P‐TiO<sub>2</sub> can afford a high reversible capacity of 334 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, a rate capability of 147 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 3350 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, and a stable cycle performance up to 1000 cycles. In situ TEM observation confirms that phosphorylation plays a center role in boosting the surface reactivity of TiO<sub>2</sub>. We have also developed a P‐TiO<sub>2</sub>//Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>/C full cell that is capable of delivering a specific energy of 150 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>.
