type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Exploring high‐rate electrode materials with excellent kinetic properties is imperative for advanced sodium‐storage systems. Herein, novel cubic‐like XFe (X = Co, Ni, Mn) Prussian blue analogs (PBAs), as cathodes materials, are obtained through as‐tuned ionic bonding, delivering improved crystallinity and homogeneous particles size. As expected, Ni‐Fe PBAs show a capacity of 81 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, mainly resulting from their physical–chemical stability, fast kinetics, and “zero‐strain” insertion characteristics. Considering that the combination of elements incorporated with carbon may increase the rate of ion transfer and improve the lifetime of cycling stability, they are expected to derive binary metal‐selenide/nitrogen‐doped carbon as anodes. Among them, binary Ni<sub>0.67</sub>Fe<sub>0.33</sub>Se<sub>2</sub> coming from Ni‐Fe PBAs shows obvious core–shell structure in a dual‐carbon matrix, leading to enhanced electron interactions, electrochemical activity, and “metal‐like” conductivity, which could retain an ultralong‐term stability of 375 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 10 000 loops even at 10.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The corresponding full‐cell Ni‐Fe PBAs versus Ni<sub>0.67</sub>Fe<sub>0.33</sub>Se<sub>2</sub> deliver a remarkable Na‐storage capacity of 302.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The rational strategy is anticipated to offer more possibilities for designing advanced electrode materials used in high‐performance sodium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201806092-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Cubic‐like X</b><b>Fe (X = Co, Ni, Mn) Prussian blue analogs (PBAs)</b> are designed through as‐tuned ionic bonding of XFe. Ni‐Fe PBAs as cathode show “zero‐strain” insertion traits with physical–chemical stability, and their derived binary metal‐selenide as anode displays strong metal‐like conductivity. Their matched sodium full‐cell systems deliver reasonably high and fast specific capacity.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201806092:adma201806092-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201806092-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201806092-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201806092-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>As an important candidate for energy‐storage systems, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have triggered considerable interest due to their promising potential, but they still suffer from sluggish kinetic behaviors.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0001"/> Cathodes with a large open framework, such as Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and Prussian blue (PB), possess faster ion‐diffusion paths.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0002"/> In the zeolitic construction of PB, multicore metal features render its internal metal‐ions replaced by the alkali‐ions with different valence state.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0003"/> Frankly, it should be acknowledged that the instability of its structure is problematic at high‐rate cycling and the low contribution of capacity is caused by the C‐coordinated Fe ions.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0004"/> That is to say, the sodiation/desodiation process of PB is hardly completed fully, resulting from the relatively low‐spin redox pair of Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup>. In order to further improve the electrochemical capability of PB‐type cathodes, the evolved manners, containing size‐controlled, crystalline increased, and elements substituted, are usually employed.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0005"/> Note that size reduction could broaden the energy distribution of surface and induce more ions adsorbing that brought them more to participate in the redox reaction.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0006"/> As shown by previous reports,<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0007"/> high crystallinity always indicated that less coordinated water would occupy the [Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub>] vacancies, giving rise to the stability of FeCNFe bridge, followed by the considerable capacity at large current density. Importantly, through the replacement of Fe atoms by metal ions (Ni, Co, and Mn), the evolution of physical–chemical features could be effectively tuned, like the morphology, crystalline, and internal pore structure, etc.[<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0005" style="qv">5b</link>] For example, the Ni‐doped PB structure was investigated with the different contents of Ni‐doped, and it is obvious that the optimized sample could deliver a capacity of ≈55 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.[<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0005" style="qv">5b</link>] It should be noted that the substitutions of heteroatoms (Ni, Co, and Mn substituted Fe ions of PBs) have not been systemically investigated on the effect of the physical–chemical traits in details.</p><p>Alternatively, restricted by weak Na‐storage capacity of traditional anodes, the explorations of new materials with both satisfactory capacity and ultrafast rate ability are urgent.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0008"/> Meanwhile, PBs as a controllable precursor have been utilized to provide great architecture with various element component such as hollow Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> microboxes and NiS nanoframes, etc.[<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0002" style="qv">2b</link>] Notably, compared to metal‐oxide/sulfur, metal‐selenides show lower energy consumption of conversion reaction, owing to its weak electronegativity (2.5) and high electronic kinetics (1 × 10<sup>−5</sup> S m<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0002" style="qv">9</link> However, their electrochemical properties are still limited by the similar issues of conversion‐type materials, containing the volume swelling and the dissolution of polysulfide/polyselenide during cycling.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0010"/> Fortunately, in the process of transformation from cathodes to anodes of PBs, the CN groups would be effectively turned into nitrogen‐doped carbon, which boosts the alleviation of volume change and the stabilization of by‐products. Moreover, it is found that foreign cations assisted metal‐selenide would bring about more active defects and exposed edge sites.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0011"/> Bestowed by the experiences of hydrogen/oxygen evolution reactions (OER),<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0012"/> the NiFe‐based materials would show the fascinating properties of redox reactions, verifying that highly introduced content could induce the intrigued advantages on OER with wide potential range,<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0011"/> and is helpful to enhance pseudo‐capacitive behaviors with the improved rate properties on energy‐storage regions.</p><p>Herein, the X<sub>2</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (XCo, Ni, Mn) were well designed to explore the evolution of physical–chemical properties, containing morphology structure, electrochemical performances, and kinetic traits. Meanwhile, various core–shell metal‐selenide samples with double carbon matrix were derived from the corresponding Prussian blue analog (PBAs) as SIBs anodes. It is confirmed that introductions of heteroatoms would induce chemical modifications of derivations. With the ideal, the Ni‐Fe PBAs could deliver a high‐rate Na‐storage capacity of 81 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Even at ultrafast current densities of 10.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, note that the Ni<sub>0.67</sub>Fe<sub>0.33</sub>Se<sub>2</sub> could display a capacity of 375 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Through the construction of a full‐cell, it is observed that the excellent capacity was still kept 302.2 at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>The physical–chemical properties of PBAs were manipulated by the replacement of various elements (Co, Ni, and Mn), as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. The cubic‐structure Co‐Fe PBAs (CF)/Ni‐Fe PBAs (NF)/Mn‐Fe PBAs (MF) samples were uniformly stabilized at large scale as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0001"/>(A1, B1, and C1) and Figure S1 in the Supporting Information, and their particle sizes displayed the increased dimensions in order of CF &lt; NF &lt; MF. Supported by the energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) results of Figure S1 in the Supporting Information, the X elements with Fe are about 2:1, further confirmed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns. Moreover, some aggregations of CF were found due to its large Van der Waals′ force in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0001"/>A2,<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0013"/> while the architectures of other samples were well distributed. And the growth of NF is optimal with strongest diffraction rings in Figure S2 in the Supporting Information, indicating its highest crystalline.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0014"/> It is found that their diameters were about CF (≈49 nm) &lt; NF (≈113 nm) &lt; MF (≈542 nm), perhaps resulting from the different rates of microcrystal self‐assembly, which indicated that the homogeneity of CF/NF are better than that of MF. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0001"/>D, their characteristic peaks are well indexed to those of the standard samples Co<sub>2</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (JCPDS: 75‐0039), Ni<sub>2</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (JCPDS: 75‐0073), Mn<sub>2</sub>Fe(CN)<sub>6</sub> (JCPDS: 024–0580), respectively. Note that their peak intensities were gradually improved in sequence of CF &lt; MF &lt; NF, matching well with the results of Figure S2 in the Supporting Information. Notably, this trait reveals that the introduction of Ni would facilitate the reduction of coordinated water and stabilize the FeCNNi bonds, which could be beneficial for Na‐storage electrochemical performances.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0015"/> In Figure S3 in the Supporting Information, Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra show that PBAs were successfully prepared without any impurity. In addition, Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0001"/>E discloses that the size distribution of NF is more uniform than that of CF/MF, derived from the suitable precipitate rates. These characteristics could favor the improvement of pseudo‐capacitive contribution and the uniform production of derived materials. Meanwhile, their specific surface areas are displayed in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0001"/>F, revealing NF (48.7 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) &gt; MF (28.5 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) &gt; MF (20.3 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>). Among them, it showed that the introduction of Ni ions could bring about the uniform distribution of particles and highest crystalline, perhaps mainly derived from different energy of ionic bonding and chemical relativities.[<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0007 #adma201806092-bib-0016" style="qv">7a,16</link>]</p><figure xml:id="adma201806092-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201806092:adma201806092-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201806092-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>A–C) Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images and TEM images of CF (A1–A3), NF (B1–B3), and MF (C1–C3). D–F) XRD patterns (D), size distribution (E), and specific surface area (F) of the as‐obtained samples.</caption></figure><p>The replacement of various elements (Co, Ni, and Mn) in PBAs could serve important roles on internal structures of their derivatives, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link> and Figure S4 in the Supporting Information. Owing to the merits of PBAs, it is expected that the defined architecture was re‐assembled to form the cell–shell structure in carbon matrix. Interestingly, CF samples were aggregated to form the bulk structure with double carbon like graphitic and amorphous in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0002"/>A1, A2. Compared to the morphology of CF‐2/NF‐2, the cubic‐shape of MF was well kept but with obvious aggregation in the interior, where the relative large volume inhibited the serious sintering of MF in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0002"/>C1. In addition, the NF after carbonization exhibits a core–shell structure with large cavity, as shown in Figure S5 in the Supporting Information. Note that this advantage could be in favor of alleviating the expansion during the selenide process in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0002"/>B1,B2, showing homogeneous core–shell structure without the emerge of bulk, which could bring about the broadened distribution of energy with more electrochemical active sites.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0017"/> In high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of the as‐obtained samples, it is found that the samples were encapsulated in the high‐ordered graphitic carbon (0.37 nm), while they were embedded into the amorphous carbon (0.38).<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0018"/> The thickness of graphitic carbon layer was about CF‐2 (5.1 nm), NF‐2 (5.3 nm), and MF‐2 (9.3 nm), resulting from the catalytic characteristic of Co/Ni/Mn for carbon.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0019"/> These advantages above could give rise to the rapid rate of ion transfer and the alleviation of volume expansion. In the inset of Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0002"/>A3, B3, C3, the lattice distance of the resulted samples was defined about 0.26, 0.20, and 0.26 nm, assigning to the (hkl) planes of (121) (CoSe<sub>2</sub>, JCPDs: 09–0247), (220) (NiSe<sub>2</sub>, JCPDs: 88–1711), and (211) (MnSe<sub>2</sub>, JCPDs: 20–0722). The selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) images are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0002"/>A4, B4, C4, and the corresponding diffraction rings were observed. Despite the existence of carbon, it is confirmed that CF‐2/MF‐2 belong to mixture‐type samples, and NF‐2 is binary metal‐selenide with the carbon content of 9%, further demonstrated in Figure S8 in the Supporting Information. In Figures S6 and S7 in the Supporting Information, it is exhibited that the as‐obtained Fe of NF‐2 have a uniform distribution of elements. Moreover, it is found that NF‐2 displayed the smallest size distribution is ranged from 0.12 to 0.25 µm with the largest specific surface area (6.76 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) in Figure S9 in the Supporting Information. And the introduction of nitrogen was confirmed in FTIR and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) as shown by Figures S9c, S10, which was illustrated in the Supporting Information. As suggested by a previous report,<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0011"/> Fe‐assisted NiSe<sub>2</sub> with highly introduced content would strengthen the internal interaction of electrons, the exposure of active sites and enhance the distortion of grains lattice, which is expected to improve the Na‐storage capability.</p><figure xml:id="adma201806092-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201806092:adma201806092-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201806092-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>A–C) TEM images, HRTEM images, and SAED images of CF‐2 (A1–A4), NF‐2 (B1–B4), and MF‐2 (C1–C4).</caption></figure><p>Expectedly, the fascinated physical–chemical traits would induce the evolution of electrochemical performances as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>. At 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> between 2.0 and 4.0 V, their initial discharge capacities have reached to 103 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of CF, 91 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of NF and 84 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of MF in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>A, it is conjectured that more sodium ions could be absorbed on the surface of CF due to its more exposed active sites. However, serious capacity fading of CF was found, which resulted from the unstable crystalline structure, agreeing well with the analysis of XRD. It is anticipated that NF delivers a stable Na‐storage capacity of 84 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, higher than that of CF (78 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and MF (81 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). Meanwhile, Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>B and Table S1 in the Supporting Information exhibit the best rate ability of NF, and its capacity could be kept from 91 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> to 81 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> with the slight loss capacity of 10 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. And the capacity of CF/MF at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> were about 65 and 49 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with the loss of 40 and 35 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Through the comparison of their profiles in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>C, it is better that NF samples demonstrate the lowest overpotentials, indicating the stable electrochemical kinetics at different current densities. Moreover, the lowest voltage gap (<i>ΔV</i>) of NF is detected in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>D and Table S2 in the Supporting Information, while its <i>ΔV</i> during discharge/charge was nearly unchanged in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>E, Figure S11 and Table S3 in the Supporting Information, indicating its considerable cycling and rate kinetics.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0020"/> The Nyquist plots reveal that the pristine resistance of CF is smaller than that of other samples in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>F, derived from the rich pristine defects. With the cycling, gradually reduced resistances were found on the NF cell, as shown in Figure S12 in the Supporting Information, originating from the CE &lt; 100%, demonstrating that the residual sodium ions could improve the conductivity.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0019"/> Moreover, in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0003"/>G, the sharpest peaks of NF is observed, matching well with its high crystalline. Note that the <i>ΔV</i> is viewed in order of NF &lt; CF &lt; MF, corresponding to the sodiation/desodiation profiles. The width of CV peaks shows that their energy distributions of particles were reduced in sequence of CF &gt; NF &gt; MF. As is known, the smaller particle sizes would enlarge the exposed area with more active sites.[<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0016" style="qv">16a</link>] Captured by the excellent electrochemical properties of NF, its detailed kinetics were further explored, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-bib-0016" style="qv">3</link>H and Figure S13 in the Supporting Information. It is clear that the similar shapes of curves at various sweep rates were well kept, suggesting its reversible redox reaction. Utilizing the equations in Table S4 in the Supporting Information, its pseudo‐capacitive contributions could achieve up to 85.1% at 0.9 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, which confirms the results of excellent rate capability.<link href="#adma201806092-bib-0021"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201806092-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201806092:adma201806092-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201806092-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Cathode: The electrochemical properties of the as‐obtained samples (CF, NF, MF): A) cycling properties, B) rate properties, C) discharge/charge platforms at different densities, D) their 2nd platforms at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, E) the profiles at different cycling, F) their Nyquist plots, G) their cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, H) the CV curves at different sweep rates and the fitting of the capacitive contribution in the inset.</caption></figure><p>Also, the electrochemical properties of their corresponding derivate were explored in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>. Notably, the NF‐2 samples show the best cycling ability and deliver a Na‐storage capacity of 382 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is greater than that of NF‐1 (310 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) without a carbon matrix and other samples, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201806092-fig-0004"/>A and Figure S14, Table S5 in the Supporting Information. For PBAs: various transition‐metal elements could induce the evolution of the crystalline degree, particle size distribution, and specific surface area. These advantages brought about the improvement of Na‐storage properties due to the reduced coordinated water, the enlarged contacting area of electrolyte, and the reinforced pseudo‐capacitive behaviors. Utilized as SIBs cathodes, Ni‐Fe PBAs could retain a capacity of 81 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. For the derivative, it is found that NF‐2 is the binary transition‐metal selenides (Ni<sub>0.67</sub>Fe<sub>0.33</sub>Se<sub>2</sub>). Interestingly, NF‐2 shows the most uniform core–shell structure and dual carbon layer, effectively alleviating matrix and conducting network. It is obvious that the synergistic effect of Ni, Fe metals induced more lattice defects, high intrinsic conductivity, and faster electrochemical activities. Also, the full‐cell with NF versus NF‐2 can deliver a capacity of 302.2 at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>.
