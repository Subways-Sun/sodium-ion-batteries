Now, an abnormal layered‐tunnel heterostructure Na<sub>0.44</sub>Co<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material induced by chemical element substitution is reported. By virtue of beneficial synergistic effects, this layered‐tunnel electrode shows outstanding electrochemical performance in sodium half‐cell system and excellent compatibility with hard carbon anode in sodium full‐cell system. The underlying formation process, charge compensation mechanism, phase transition, and sodium‐ion storage electrochemistry are clearly articulated and confirmed through combined analyses of in situ high‐energy X‐ray diffraction and ex situ X‐ray absorption spectroscopy as well as operando X‐ray diffraction. This crystal structure engineering regulation strategy offers a future outlook into advanced cathode materials for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>An abnormal layered‐tunnel</b> heterostructure Na<sub>0.44</sub>Co<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material induced by chemical element substitution is described. xml:id="anie201912101-sec-0001"><p>Owing to the exhaustion of traditional fossil fuels and the growing concern of aggravated environmental problems such as air pollution as well as global warming, renewable and clean energy sources (wind, solar, wave, and geothermal) are urgent to be smoothly integrated into the grid via electrical energy storage technology today.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0001"/> Considering the natural abundance and low cost of sodium resources in the earth's crust, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are regarded as an ideal candidate for next‐generation large‐scale stationary electrochemical energy storage systems (ESSs).<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0002"/></p><p>Development of advanced ESSs techniques has accelerated advancements in exploiting novel Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion compounds in recent years.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0003"/> Sodium‐based layered transition metal oxide cathode materials Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MeO<sub>2</sub> (Me=Ni, Co, Mn, etc.) for SIBs have received significant attention because of the large theoretical specific capacity, low cost, and simple analogies from the successful application of LiMeO<sub>2</sub> in Li‐ion battery systems.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0004"/> However, conventional single‐phase oxide cathodes commonly face the challenge of simultaneously achieving long cycle life and excellent rate performance while realizing high specific capacity.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0005"/> For instance, although Mn‐based tunnel structure Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> cathode material displays superior cycling stability and rate capability, the specific capacity is insufficient to meet the energy storage need of the rapid development of modern society.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0006"/> Recently, according to previous reports, some pioneers have confirmed that attractive layered‐tunnel heterostructure could successfully integrate advantages of tunnel structure with excellent structure stability and those of layered structure with high reversible specific capacity.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0007"/> Meanwhile, the hot concept of heterostructure has been widely applied in the areas of energy storage and conversion, electrocatalytic, materials science, and spectroscopy technology.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0008"/> Even though some reports have attempted to obtain layered‐tunnel composite structure for sodium oxide cathode, design of an abnormal layered‐tunnel heterostructure at a low sodium content is deficient. Besides, it is of great importance to decipher the inherent synergistic effect mechanisms, formation process, structural evolution, and sodium ions storage electrochemistry during battery cycling.</p><p>Hence, we present a chemical‐substitution‐induced abnormal layered‐tunnel heterostructure Na<sub>0.44</sub>Co<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material through an optimal crystal‐structure engineering modulation strategy. Benefiting from the synergistic advantages of intergrowth structures, which are clearly verified by systematic X‐ray diffraction (XRD) techniques, high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HR‐TEM), advanced spherical aberration‐corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) with an atomic scale, and ex situ X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS), the cathode exhibits high reversible specific capacity and specific energy of 173.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 470.7 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C, superior cycling stability and rate performance in sodium half‐cell system, and excellent full‐cell electrochemical performance. This study demonstrates the performance–structure relationship in the layered‐tunnel intergrowth system and provides a firm platform to design high‐performance sodium oxide cathode materials.</p><p>The structure of Na<sub>0.44</sub>Co<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material (hereafter denoted as LT‐NaCM) was analyzed through various advanced characterization techniques. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0001"/> a, the XRD pattern of LT‐NaCM cathode material confirms that all the diffraction lines can be indexed to the hexagonal P2‐type phase with the space group <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i> (JCPDS: 27‐0752) and the tunnel phase with the space group <i>Pbma</i> (JCPDS: 27‐0750), respectively.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0009"/> The observed pattern of LT‐NaCM is highly consistent with the calculated pattern (<i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> 8.66 % and <i>R</i><sub>p</sub> 6.50 %), and the detailed crystallographic parameters as well as phase ratios of layered and tunnel structures are listed in the Supporting Information, Table S1. Furthermore, the crystal structures of layered and tunnel phases are presented in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0001"/> b and the Supporting Information, Figure S1. Elemental cobalt substitution not only uniformly incorporates into the transition‐metal layer, but also induces a novel abnormal layered‐tunnel intergrowth oxide cathode based on the tunnel structure Na<sub>0.44</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> cathode material. Layered‐tunnel intergrowth structure is usually observed for sodium manganese oxide that contains relatively high sodium content. Chemical substitution may engineer crystal structure and change the formation energy as well as thermodynamic energy, which allows the formation at a low sodium content.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0010"/> Moreover, the structural features of LT‐NaCM cathode material characterized by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) show two kinds of morphologies including well‐defined layered plate‐like structure and typical tunnel rod‐like structure (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0001"/> c,d; Supporting Information, Figure S2). Meanwhile, HR‐TEM images and line profiles further confirm the layered‐tunnel heterostructure (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0001"/> e–g; Supporting Information, Figure S3). Besides, the precursors of LT‐NaCM cathode material were executed by in situ high‐energy X‐ray diffraction (HEXRD) in air atmosphere to record the formation process (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0001"/> h,i; Supporting Information, Figures S4–S6). Furthermore, the distributed elements in the LT‐NaCM cathode material and corresponding chemical compositions were analyzed by energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) and inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP‐MS), respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0001"/> j; Supporting Information, Figure S7 and Table S2).</p><figure xml:id="anie201912101-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912101:anie201912101-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912101-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structure of the LT‐NaCM cathode material. a) Powder XRD pattern and Rietveld refinement plot. b) Crystal structures of layered and tunnel phases viewed along the [010] crystallographic direction. c) SEM image. d) TEM image. e) HR‐TEM image. f),g) Enlarged HR‐TEM images at different sites. h),i) In situ high‐energy XRD patterns of precursors at different temperatures and corresponding intensity contour maps of the characteristic diffraction peaks. j) EDS maps.</p></caption></figure><p>Detailed structural and chemical information on an atomic scale of layered‐tunnel LT‐NaCM cathode material were experimentally supported by advanced spherical aberration‐corrected STEM.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0011"/> The atom column position of transition metal is revealed by the bright spot in HAADF‐STEM and the dark spot in ABF‐STEM. Meanwhile, the faint dark spots within the interlayer position in the ABF‐STEM represent the Na and O light elements.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0012"/> As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0002"/> a–h, HAADF and ABF‐STEM images and the colored patterns correspond to the atomic model of P2 layered structure projected along the [001] and [010] crystallographic directions, respectively. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0002"/> i–l, the detailed atomic arrangements match well with tunnel‐type atomic packing models.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0013"/> Moreover, the FFT images of HAADF and ABF‐STEM images and adjacent atomic spacing further demonstrate the layered and tunnel structures (Supporting Information, Figures S8–S11). Furthermore, the valence state and atomic composition of layered‐tunnel LT‐NaCM cathode material via electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) are shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0002"/> m–o and the Supporting Information, Figure S12. The energy loss peaks in the range of 770–810 eV, 630–670 eV, and 520–550 eV are indexed to the Co L‐edge, Mn L‐edge, and O K‐edge, respectively. Meanwhile, the valence states of transition metal and oxygen from a macro‐perspective have been further confirmed by X‐ray photoelectron spectra (XPS; Supporting Information, Figure S13).</p><figure xml:id="anie201912101-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912101:anie201912101-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912101-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structure of LT‐NaCM cathode material. a)–h) HAADF and ABF‐STEM images and the corresponding coloured patterns of layered structure viewed along the [001] and [010] crystallographic directions. i)–l) HAADF and ABF‐STEM images and the corresponding colored patterns of tunnel structure viewed along the [010] crystallographic direction. m)–o) EELS spectra of Co, Mn L‐edges, and O K‐edge, respectively.</p></caption></figure><p>The electrochemical intercalation/deintercalation behavior of LT‐NaCM electrode was tested in a Na half‐cell system. The LT‐NaCM electrode delivers high reversible specific capacity and specific energy of 173.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 470.7 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0003"/> a; Supporting Information, Figure S14). The cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> at different cycles overlap well, demonstrating excellent kinetics and small polarization (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0003"/> b). The LT‐NaCM electrode could exhibit excellent specific capacity and energy density, high Coulombic efficiency and energy efficiency, and stable midpoint voltage at each current density (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0003"/> c–e; Supporting Information Figure S15), which could be showed by the excellent rate performance of 81.29 % capacity retention (1 C compared to 0.2 C) and the remarkable specific capacity of 108.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at 5 C with an energy density of 296.5 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>. Furthermore, detailed electrochemical parameters of LT‐NaCM electrode and the comprehensive electrochemical performance comparison of various systems are listed in the Supporting Information, Tables S3, S4. When the rate comes back to 1 C, the capacity could recover to 136.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating the excellent reversibility (Supporting Information, Figure S16). Moreover, the kinetic properties of LT‐NaCM electrode are calculated from the CVs at various sweeping rates, and the results of linear fitting as well as voltage polarization are presented in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0002"/> f–h and the Supporting Information, Figures S17, S18 and Table S5, respectively. Furthermore, a mixed contribution of diffusion and capacitive controlled process is confirmed based on the equation <i>i</i>=<i>av</i><sup><i>b</i></sup>.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0014"/> Besides, after various rates performance tests, the capacity retention is still as high as 81.97 % after 100 cycles at 5 C with little voltage polarization, a high Coulombic efficiency (&gt;99.81 %), a high energy efficiency (&gt;82.03 %), and a stable midpoint voltage (&gt;2.667 V) (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0002"/> i,j; Supporting Information, Figures S19, S20). On one hand, the P2 layered structure could provide high reversible specific capacity and stable prismatic crystalline frameworks as well as fast Na‐ion diffusion between open prismatic Na sites. On the other hand, the tunnel structure could take advantages of its abundant S tunnels and unique 3D crystal structure to further improve the rate capability as well as cycle stability.</p><figure xml:id="anie201912101-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912101:anie201912101-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912101-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performance of LT‐NaCM electrode. a) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves versus specific capacity at 0.2 C in the voltage range of 2.0–4.0 V. b) Cyclic voltammograms at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> at different cycles. c),d) Rate performance at various rates and galvanostatic charge/discharge curves versus specific capacity. e) Midpoint voltage and energy efficiency at various rates. f)–h) Cyclic voltammetry at different scan rates and linear fitting of peak current versus square root of the scan rate as well as log (<i>i</i>) versus log (<i>v</i>) plots at different oxidation and reduction peaks. i) Galvanostatic charge/discharge curves versus specific capacity in different 1st, 10th, 20th, 50th, 80th, and 100th cycles at 5 C. j) Cycling performance during 100 cycles at 5 C after various rates performance tests.</p></caption></figure><p>Ex situ XAS and operando XRD were carried out to better understand the charge compensation and structural evolution mechanisms of LT‐NaCM cathode material during charge/discharge process.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0015"/> Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0004"/> a,b display the normalized ex situ XANES at Co and Mn K‐edges of LT‐NaCM electrode at different electrochemical states. During the charge process, the Co K‐edge and Mn K‐edge of XANES spectra clearly shift to the higher‐energy region, suggesting that cationic cobalt and manganese are involved in the charge compensation. Note that the two pre‐edge peaks of Mn K‐edge are positioned at high energies after discharge to 2.0 V, which implies that the manganese has a high average oxidation state, and the substituted chemical element may effectively refrain the Jahn–Teller distortion to a certain degree.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0016"/> Moreover, operando XRD experiment was executed during the charge/discharge process (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0004"/> c). When Na<sup>+</sup> is being extracted, the (002)/(004) peaks of P2 phase shift toward a lower angle, and the (020) peak of P2 phase shifts to a higher angle, indicating that the <i>ab</i> plane is contracting while <i>c</i> axis is expanding.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0017"/> As Na<sup>+</sup> is further extracted, the (002) peak of P2 phase could split into two, demonstrating that P2 phase transformed into an orthorhombic P2′ phase with space‐group <i>Cmcm</i> symmetry.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0018"/> As Na<sup>+</sup> is being inserted during the discharge process, the highly reversible phase transition P2′–P2 could be observed. When Na<sup>+</sup> is being further inserted, the peaks experience an exact opposite evolution. Furthermore, it is worth noting that no obvious shift of (140) peak of tunnel phase occurs during Na ions intercalation/deintercalation, which further confirms that tunnel structure plays a very important role in stabilizing structure.</p><figure xml:id="anie201912101-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912101:anie201912101-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912101-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Charge compensation mechanism and crystal structural evolution of LT‐NaCM electrode upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion. a),b) Ex situ XANES spectra at the Co K‐edge and Mn K‐edge collected at different charge/discharge states. c) In situ XRD patterns during the charge/discharge process in the voltage range of 2.0–4.0 V and corresponding intensity contour maps of the characteristic diffraction peaks.</p></caption></figure><p>Electrochemical test of full‐cell system of LT‐NaCM electrode was executed. Hard carbon anode without the pre‐sodiation process is difficult to achieve excellent cycle stability when matched with cathode in full‐cell system due to the formation of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer forming on the anode surface.<link href="#anie201912101-bib-0019"/> Hence, before assembling the full battery, the pre‐sodiation process of the hard carbon anode was conducted (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0005"/> a; Supporting Information, Figure S21). The full cell delivers high reversible specific capacity of 171.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with specific energy of 453.9 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0005"/> b; Supporting Information, Figure S22). Meanwhile, a reversible specific capacity is 106.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even tested at 5 C with the calculated energy density of 276.8 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, and excellent energy efficiency with high Coulombic efficiency as well as stable midpoint voltage are obtained at different rates (Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0005"/> c–e; Supporting Information, Figure S23). Furthermore, as the rate comes back to 1 C, the discharge capacity retention could recover to 96.69 % after the various rates tests (Supporting Information, Figure S24). Other detailed electrochemical parameters are comprehensively summarized in the Supporting Information, Table S6. Besides, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912101-fig-0005"/> f,g and the Supporting Information, Figures S25, S26, the capacity retention is 83.92 % after 100 cycles at 5 C after various rates performance tests.
