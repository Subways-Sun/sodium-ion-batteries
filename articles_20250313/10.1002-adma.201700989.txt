xml:lang="en"><p>Uniform Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> hollow spheres assembled from N‐doped carbon‐coated ultrathin nanosheets are synthesized. A unique multilayer structure of nanosheets is presumed to significantly reduce energy consumption during the diffusion process of sodium ions, while the carbon‐coated structure can increase the overall conductivity. The as‐prepared sample used as an anode in sodium‐ion batteries exhibits the best rate performance ever reported for Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, delivering more than 60 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 continuous cycles at the high rate of 50 C, which was achieved due to its unique structure.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en" xml:id="adma201700989-abs-0001"><p><b>Uniform Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> hollow spheres assembled from N‐doped carbon‐coated ultrathin nanosheets</b> are synthesized. A unique multilayer structure of nanosheets is presumed to facilitate the diffusion of sodium ions, while the carbon‐coating increases the conductivity. The as‐prepared sample delivers more than 60 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 C, which is achieved by its unique structure.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201700989-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201700989-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201700989-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries have attracted a lot of attention due to the significantly reduced cost of the entire electrical energy storage system.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0001 #adma201700989-bib-0002 #adma201700989-bib-0003 #adma201700989-bib-0004 #adma201700989-bib-0005 #adma201700989-bib-0006"/> Among all kinds of anode materials employed in sodium‐ion batteries, the Ti‐based materials attracted a great attention due to their unique properties assuring high activity, low cost, and environmental friendliness.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0007 #adma201700989-bib-0008 #adma201700989-bib-0009 #adma201700989-bib-0010"/> Taking into account the lowest voltage of the Ti‐based materials, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> has been considered as an ideal anode material with high energy density.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0011 #adma201700989-bib-0012 #adma201700989-bib-0013 #adma201700989-bib-0014 #adma201700989-bib-0015 #adma201700989-bib-0016"/> Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> features a zigzag layered framework of TiO<sub>6</sub> octahedra, which are linked by edges. A large amount of energy is required for travel of sodium ions through a layer, which is much higher than that needed for travel along the layer.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0012 #adma201700989-bib-0013"/> Additionally, poor electron conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> associated with its large bandgap (3.7 eV) creates another barrier for migration of electrons. Therefore, these factors are responsible for sluggish Na reaction kinetics, which limits the rate performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> as an anode in sodium‐ion batteries.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0012 #adma201700989-bib-0017"/></p><p>In order to improve the performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, numerous efforts have been undertaken to reduce the length of diffusion paths of sodium ions and consequently, to enhance the sluggish Na reaction kinetics with Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. Zhang et al. reported a 3D spider‐web architecture of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. This unique structure reduces the diffusion length of sodium ions, which guarantees its superior electrochemical performance.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0018"/> Meanwhile, besides the structure optimization, surface engineering has been considered as an effective way to improve the poor electronic conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> and, hence, enhance its performance.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0007 #adma201700989-bib-0012 #adma201700989-bib-0017"/> However, the lack of rationally designed nanostructured Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> for anodes in sodium‐ion batteries limits its rate performance, especially under high current density, which is still unsatisfactory.</p><p>Hollow structures have been widely applied for the rational design of anode materials for lithium‐ion batteries because they can offer short diffusion paths for transport of ions, high specific surface area accessible to the electrolyte, and a lot of active sites enhancing electrochemical activity under high current density.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0009 #adma201700989-bib-0010 #adma201700989-bib-0019 #adma201700989-bib-0020 #adma201700989-bib-0021 #adma201700989-bib-0022 #adma201700989-bib-0023 #adma201700989-bib-0024"/> Additionally, the large voids in hollow structures are beneficial to release the strain during charging/discharging processes.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0019 #adma201700989-bib-0025"/> Meanwhile, previous research shows that sodium ions migrate much easier along the Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> layers than through these layers.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0012"/> Therefore, the hollow architectures assembled from nanosheets seem to be more desirable for Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> anodes in sodium‐ion batteries because their unique structure can reduce the number of layers and consequently the energy consumption during the diffusion process of sodium ions travelling through these layers. As compared to the hollow spheres composed of nanoparticles, the hollow spheres assembled from Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets may possess additional advantages, but their synthesis is more challenging.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0026"/></p><p>Besides optimization of the structure, carbon coating is considered as an effective strategy to improve the performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> because it can serve as both the conducting additive to promote the electron transport in the poorly conductive Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> and the elastic buffer improving the stability of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0015 #adma201700989-bib-0027 #adma201700989-bib-0028"/> As compared with bare carbon materials, N‐doped carbon might further enhance both ion and electron diffusion, resulting in enhanced electronic and ionic conductivity.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0029"/> However, despite the merits brought by N‐doped carbon, to the best of our knowledge, there is lack of reports on the surface modification of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> with N‐doped carbon.</p><p>Herein we report the first synthesis of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> hollow spheres assembled from N‐doped carbon‐coated ultrathin Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets. The hollow structure provides a large specific surface area, better access to the electrolyte, and shorter diffusion paths for both ions and electrons. The ultrathin nanosheets significantly decrease the number of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> layers and, hence, provide preferable diffusion paths for sodium ions. Finally, the ultrathin carbon coating meaningfully enhances the conductivity and facilitates transport of electrons.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0015"/> As expected, at the high current density of 50 C, the as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> hollow spheres assembled from carbon‐coated nanosheets could deliver a reversible capacity of more than 60 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is twice larger than the reversible capacity (≈26 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) of the materials assembled from nanoparticles and nearly twice larger than the reversible capacity (≈32 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) of the materials assembled from bare nanosheets, demonstrating the superiority of the sheet‐like structure and the N‐doped carbon coating.</p><p>The synthesis procedure of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C hollow spheres is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. First, highly uniform silica spheres were synthesized by Stöber method.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0030 #adma201700989-bib-0031"/> Second, the uniform amorphous titanium hydroxide was coated on the surface of silica template to obtain the titanium hydroxide‐coated silica. Subsequently, hollow spheres composed of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> ultrathin nanosheets were produced under hydrothermal conditions in sodium hydroxide aqueous solution probably due to the formation of a lamellar sodium titanate structure resulting from the intercalation of sodium cations between TiO<sub>6</sub> octahedral layers.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0032"/> Next, dopamine was employed to form polydopamine on the surface of nanosheets and create the polymer‐coated nanosheets. Finally, the as‐prepared sample was placed into a tube furnace and calcined to acquire the final product: hollow spheres composed of carbon‐coated Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> ultrathin nanosheets. The resulting Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C hierarchical hollow spheres are denoted as Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs. For the purpose of comparison, hollow spheres assembled from Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> ultrathin nanosheets without carbon coating (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> hierarchical hollow spheres denoted as Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs, Figure S1, Supporting Information) and hollow spheres assembled from Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanoparticles without carbon coating (denoted as Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs, Figure S2, Supporting Information) were synthesized and the rate performance of those materials was tested, too.</p><figure xml:id="adma201700989-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201700989-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Illustration of the route for synthesis of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C hollow spheres.</caption></figure><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link> shows the scanning electron microscope (SEM) images of the silica spheres before (panel a) and after (panel b) coating with titanium hydroxide. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>a, the highly uniform silica templates with a smooth surface and a diameter of about 500 nm could be obtained by the Stöber method. After coating, the surface of as‐prepared particles became rough (Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>b), indicating that titanium hydroxide was coated on the surface of silica spheres. The diameter of as‐prepared particles increases to about 560 nm, which implies that the average thickness of Ti‐based coating layer is about 30 nm. It is worth noting that the particles remained uniform after the aforementioned coating process (Figure S3, Supporting Information). Additionally, the roughness and 30 nm thickness of the coating layer are clearly visible on the transmission electron microscope (TEM) images (Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>c). The sample morphology after hydrothermal reaction with sodium hydroxide and the subsequent calcination was investigated by SEM (Figures S1 and S2, Supporting Information). It should be noted that the composition of reaction solvent has a significant impact on the morphology of final products, especially in relation to the building blocks of hollow structures. When the reaction takes place in pure water, hollow spheres assembled from nanosheets are obtained (Figure S1, Supporting Information), while the hollow spheres assembled from small particles are formed in the water/ethanol mixed solution (Figure S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201700989-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201700989-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM images of silica spheres; b) SEM and c) TEM images of Si@Ti(OH)<i><sub>x</sub></i> spheres; d) SEM, e) TEM, and f) HRTEM images of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs.</caption></figure><p>The final particles (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs) were studied by SEM and TEM imaging analysis. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>d and Figure S4 (Supporting Information), the Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs particles are highly uniform spheres assembled from nanosheets. Their morphology is almost the same as that of the particles after treatment with sodium hydroxide but without carbon coating, indicating that the dopamine‐derived carbon coating is ultrathin and highly uniform. As shown in the inset in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>d,e, the hollow structure is clearly visible. The thickness of shell is estimated to be about 40 nm, while the nanosheets have the width of tens of nanometers. The detailed morphology can be further observed on high‐resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) images. The HRTEM images show 2D ultrathin nature of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> sheets. It should be noticed that these ultrathin nanosheets with the thickness of less than 5 nm are highly uniform and consist of a few layers of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>f and Figure S5, Supporting Information). Meanwhile, the detailed crystal structure of these nanosheets is revealed by HRTEM images of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs (Figure S6, Supporting Information). As can be seen from the corresponding fast Fourier transform (FFT) pattern shown in Figure S6 (Supporting Information), the lattice spacings of 0.207 and 0.206 nm with an interfacial angle of 88° can be indexed as <inlineGraphic href="graphic/adma201700989-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-math-0001"/> and (104), revealing that the enclosed facets of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets are <inlineGraphic href="graphic/adma201700989-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-math-0002"/>. This agrees with the <inlineGraphic href="graphic/adma201700989-math-0003.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-math-0003"/> sharp peak visible on the X‐ray powder diffraction (XRD) patterns (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a). As can be seen from Figure S6 (Supporting Information), the exposed facets can provide large tunnels for the diffusion of sodium ions.</p><figure xml:id="adma201700989-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201700989-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The structural analysis of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs. a) XRD patterns of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs. b) Raman spectra of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs. c,d) XPS survey and high‐resolution N1s spectra of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs.</caption></figure><p>The surface modification of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> with carbon has been demonstrated as an effective way to improve the conductivity and, hence, the electrochemical performance.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0007 #adma201700989-bib-0015 #adma201700989-bib-0033"/> Due to the large bandgap of 3.7 eV, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> is considered as an insulator, which causes the sluggish kinetics of sodium ions and the poor rate performance of the pristine material. The carbon coating layer was employed to solve this issue. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0002"/>f, the ultrathin and highly uniform carbon layer has been achieved and its thickness has been estimated to be below 2 nm. Additionally, the high angle annular dark field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) elemental mapping images (Figure S7, Supporting Information) also demonstrate the presence of carbon and its even distribution in the hollow spherical structures. Thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S8, Supporting Information) shows that the content of carbon in this composite material is about 24%. Furthermore, based on the Raman spectra (Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0003"/>b), it can be found that the intensity of G band located at around 1600 cm<sup>−1</sup> (indicating the sp<sup>2</sup>‐hybridized graphitic carbon atoms) is much higher than the intensity of D band related to the disordered carbon atoms.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0034 #adma201700989-bib-0035 #adma201700989-bib-0036"/> The Raman spectra indicate the highly graphitic nature of the carbon coating. The X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) reveals the N‐doped nature of this thin carbon coating (Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0003"/>c). According to Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0003"/>d, the high‐resolution spectra of N 1s region can be fitted with two peaks: the one located at 400.0 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be assigned to the secondary amine nitrogen (RNHR) and the one located at 398.1 cm<sup>−1</sup> is attributed with tertiary/aromatic (NR) amine functionalities, demonstrating the N‐doped nature of the carbon coating.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0037"/> The N content in this composite material estimated from the survey XPS spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0003"/>c) is 2.97%. Furthermore, Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area increases from 99 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs), 168 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs) to 307 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs) due to the 2D structure of nanosheets and the micropores present in the coated carbon layer (Figure S9, Supporting Information).</p><p>The cycling performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs was examined on the basis of galvanostatic discharge–charge measurements. The initial profiles for Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a, which were recorded in the voltage range of 0.01–2.5 V versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup> at a current density of 1 C, corresponding to 177 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The first discharge capacity is 647 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> while the first charge capacity is 278 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, resulting in a low Coulombic efficiency of 43.0% in the first cycle. This low Coulombic efficiency can be generally attributed to the decomposition of electrolyte and the formation of solid‐electrolyte interfacial (SEI) film, which is also reflected by the initial cycles of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs and Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs with the corresponding Coulombic efficiencies of 53.8% and 57.4% (Figure S10, Supporting Information), respectively.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0018"/> The low Coulombic efficiency is also suggested by the large void between the first cycle and the second cycle in the initial cyclic voltammetry (CV) profiles (Figure S11, Supporting Information). After five cycles, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0004"/>c, the current density increased to about 50 C, corresponding to about 8.8 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The corresponding Coulombic efficiency increased to over 90%, which implies the highly reversible nature of the as‐prepared sample. It is noteworthy that the capacity fading for almost 1000 cycles is just 6.5%, corresponding to the initial cycle charging capacity of 72.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and the final cycle charging capacity of 68 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. This small capacity fading indicates a good durability of this uniquely structured material. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on the use of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> under 50 C and it is also the best rate performance ever reported. The good durability and high reversible capacity, which is about 40% of the theoretical capacity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, verify the possibility of using this material as a high‐performance anode for sodium‐ion batteries. To evaluate the rate capability, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs were cycled at various current densities, while the charge capacities are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0004"/>b and the discharge/charge profiles are shown in Figure S12 (Supporting Information). Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs exhibited reversible capacities of 210, 179, 142, 120, 94, 82, and 63 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the current densities of 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, 30, and 50 C. First, at the current density of 50 C, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs (no carbon coating) can only deliver about 50% charge capacity of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs, indicating a significant enhancement of the sodium‐ion storage performance resulting from N‐doped carbon coating. Second, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs exhibit about 33% higher charge capacity at the current density of 10 C in comparison to Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs, supporting the idea that the ultrathin nanosheets can improve the rate performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>. Also at higher current densities, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs show smaller polarization than Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs, which supports the idea of better sodium‐ion transport in Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs (Figure S13, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0038"/> In conclusion, it has been demonstrated that this unique structure of N‐doped carbon‐coated ultrathin nanosheets can not only improve the conductivity but also facilitate the diffusion of sodium ions.</p><figure xml:id="adma201700989-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201700989-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical sodium‐storage properties of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs: a) Initial discharge–charge voltage profiles of the first, second, and fifth cycles in the current density of 1 C. b) Rate performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs, and Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs. c) Cycling performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs under the high current density of 50 C. d) CV curves of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs at various sweep rates. e) Diagram of capacitive contribution to the total capacity.</caption></figure><p>To further investigate the origin of the superior electrochemical performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs, the sodium‐ion storage mechanism was investigated by CV techniques.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0017 #adma201700989-bib-0033 #adma201700989-bib-0039 #adma201700989-bib-0040"/> Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0004"/>d shows the CV curves at various sweep rates from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. At the higher sweep rates (higher than 2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>), an obvious distortion from the basic shape is visible on the CV curves. This distortion is originated from several sources such as increased Ohmic contribution and/or diffusion constraints.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0017"/> The contribution to the total charge stored resulting from the redox pseudocapacitance and intercalation can be analyzed by the following equations <displayedItem type="mathematics" numbered="yes" xml:id="adma201700989-disp-0001"><mediaResource href="graphic/adma201700989-math-0004.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-math-0004"/></displayedItem>or<displayedItem type="mathematics" numbered="yes" xml:id="adma201700989-disp-0002"><mediaResource href="graphic/adma201700989-math-0005.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201700989:adma201700989-math-0005"/></displayedItem>where <i>a</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> represents the contribution of redox pseudocapacitance and double layer capacitance while <i>a</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> reflects the contribution originating from the insertion processes.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0017 #adma201700989-bib-0033 #adma201700989-bib-0039"/> If <i>a</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>a</i><sub>2</sub> are determined, the current response from the capacitive and insertion processes at the specific potential can be determined. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201700989-fig-0004"/>e, the contribution from the capacitive process to the total charge stored increases with increasing sweep rates. The pseudocapacitive contribution to the total capacity increases from 37.9% (the specific capacitive contribution is shown in Figure S14, Supporting Information), corresponding to the scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, to 88.3% at the scan rate of 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (the specific capacitive contribution is shown in Figure S15, Supporting Information) while the capacitive contributions obtained for Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs and Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs show similar trend (Figure S16, Supporting Information). Thanks to the high ratio of pseudocapacitive contribution in the total charge stored and the unique structure of hollow spheres assembled from N‐doped carbon‐coated nanosheets, Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs exhibit an excellent performance as an anode for sodium‐ion batteries under extremely high current density.</p><p>The above results clearly show that the unique structure of hollow spheres assembled from N‐doped carbon‐coated nanosheets can significantly enhance the sodium‐ion storage performance of Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> as compared to the hollow spheres assembled from Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets alone and the hollow spheres assembled from nanoparticles. Specifically, the hollow structure provides large surface area for the contact of the electrode material with electrolyte, which is beneficial for the transport of sodium ions. It is worth to mention that the hollow structure significantly reduces the diffusion length of sodium ions in Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0019"/> Furthermore, the N‐doped carbon coating with micropores provides a high surface area for electrode/electrolyte interface, which reduces the diffusion length of electrons and ions, and provides relatively high conductivity to improve the transport of electrons, while the aforementioned N‐doping also results in the large number of reactive sites for sodium ions.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0029 #adma201700989-bib-0041"/> Additionally, the hollow spheres assembled from nanosheets can also provide more electrochemical active sites than those formed from nanoparticles due to the higher surface‐to‐volume ratio.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0026"/> Most importantly, the prior theoretical studies show that the diffusion coefficient of sodium ions along Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> layers is about 15 orders of magnitude larger than that related to the transport of these ions through the layers.<link href="#adma201700989-bib-0012"/> Therefore, the 2D structure with fewer layers is preferable for the transport of sodium ions due to the large energy barrier associated with their migration through the layers, which is matched with the improved performance of hollow structure assembled from nanosheets (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>@C HHSs and Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HHSs) in comparison to the hollow structure assembled from nanoparticles (Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> HSs) in this work. Our results prove that the significantly reduced dimension along the layers of ultrathin nanosheets can facilitate the diffusion of sodium ions in Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, resulting in greatly improved sodium kinetics and, hence, the excellent rate performance as an anode in sodium‐ion batteries.</p><p>In summary, a novel nanostructure of hollow spheres assembled from N‐doped carbon‐coated ultrathin Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> nanosheets has been successfully synthesized. These well‐defined Na<sub>2</sub>Ti<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> hollow spheres exhibit excellent sodium storage performance under high current density, due to the reduced diffusion length originating from their unique structure assembled from ultrathin nanosheets and the improved electronic conductivity of the N‐doped carbon coating. All these features enhance the electrochemical performance of this anode material for sodium‐ion batteries, leading to the excellent performance under the high current density. This study demonstrates that reducing the number of layers in nanosheets can enhance the electrochemical performance of such layered material. Due to the wide application of layered material in batteries, electrocatalysis, and other areas, this study indicates a new avenue for the design of layered materials for electrochemical applications.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201700989-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>F.X. and L.Z. contributed equally to this work.
