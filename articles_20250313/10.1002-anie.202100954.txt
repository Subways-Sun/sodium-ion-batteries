Herein, we develop a facile polymer stabilized droplet template strategy to synthesize porous single crystal structured NVP. The pore structures (macrostructures, hierarchically meso/macrostructures, and mesostructures), pore sizes (5–2000 nm), and specific surface areas (26–158 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) of the samples can be readily controlled by tuning the sizes of droplet templates. The resultant hierarchically meso/macropores NVP demonstrates superior sodium storage performances, because its porous single crystal structure owns solid–liquid Na<sup>+</sup> transmission mode, shortens ion diffusion distance and provides large electrode–electrolyte contact area, greatly facilitating fast ionic transport. We believe the presented method will supply a novel avenue to prepare porous single crystal structured materials for anticipative applications.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A facile polymer stabilized droplet template strategy allows the synthesis of porous single crystal structured NVP with tunable pore structure. The resultant hierarchically meso/macropores NVP demonstrates superior sodium storage performances, because its porous single crystal structure shortens ion diffusion distance and provides large electrode–electrolyte contact area, greatly facilitating fast ionic transport.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="anie202100954-ntgp-0003"><note xml:id="anie202100954-note-1001"><label>+</label><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202100954-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>As a typical sodium super ionic conductor structured material, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) is popularly regarded as the potential alternative cathode material for sodium‐ion batteries due to its high ionic conductivity and pronounced structural stability.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> However, the inherent low electronic conductivity and large bulk structure with low surface area limit its electrochemical performances.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> To address these obstacles, the main approach is devoted to the synthesis of nanostructured NVP coated with conductive materials and constituted of building units with a diameter of 100–500 nm.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0009"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Basically, this strategy is able to increase the interfacial area of electrode/electrolyte, shorten the ionic diffusion distance, and improve the electronic conductivity; thus is a promising approach to improve the electrochemical performances. But this strategy inevitably causes some adverse effects such as the low tap density and energy density.</p><p>Recently, rational synthesis of single crystalline electrode material opens a new avenue to understand the underlying intercalation chemistries at the atomic and molecular levels.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0014"><sup>[4]</sup></link> This strategy not only provides anisotropy directed morphological modulation with controlled volume‐change‐induced intragrain stress, but also enables a fast ionic transportation without crossing the grain boundaries, characteristics in the nanoscale polycrystalline materials. In addition, as we all known, the specific surface area between the active material and electrolyte is essential to achieve superior performance because of the much higher ionic conductivity in electrolyte than that within the solid materials. Porous materials are featured as the large specific surface area, high pore volume, and interconnected pore structure, which can provide a fast channel for ion‐diffusion, improve the contact area between electrolyte and electrode, and enable effective penetration of electrolyte into the active material.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0018"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Along this way, the construction of porous NVP single crystals is a powerful approach for achieving the excellent electrochemical performance with exceptional rate performance and cycle stability.</p><p>To the best of our knowledge, the relative research has rarely been reported, because simultaneous formation of single crystal structure and porous structure is incompatible in terms of reaction thermodynamics and kinetics for NVP materials.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0025"><sup>[6]</sup></link> As for an ionic crystal of NVP, anions and cations are likely to gather together driven by the strong electrostatic interaction, and then grow into large micrometer‐scale particles at ultra‐high crystallization temperature (≥700 °C), resulting in the nonporous bulk products, because of its inherent physical and chemical properties.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0026"><sup>[7]</sup></link> It is also extreme suffering to trigger the self‐assemble of precursor and pore forming agent in the ion rich complex synthesis system to form a porous structure.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0031"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Even achieving a porous structure, the crystallization process will cause the collapse of the designed pore structure at elevated temperatures, since the presence of interconnected pores induces serious strains in the crystalline NVP framework.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0035"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Until now, many methods including sol‐gel method,<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0039"><sup>[10a]</sup></link> carbothermal reduction method,<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0040"><sup>[10b]</sup></link> pyro‐synthesis method,<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0041"><sup>[10c]</sup></link> and others<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0042"><sup>[10d]</sup></link> have been utilized to downsize the sizes of NVP particles to increase the specific surface area, and usually a NVP nanocrystals stacked porous structure is obtained instead of porous single crystal structured NVP. Consequently, the development of an effective method for the synthesis of porous NVP single crystals is highly desirable but remains a great challenge.</p><p>Herein, we develop a versatile polymer stabilized droplet template strategy for the synthesis of porous single crystal structured NVP with tunable pore structure. In this approach, droplets templates are formed through the hydrogen bonding of NVP precursor and the mixed solvents composed of tetrahydrofuran (THF), ethanol (EtOH) and H<sub>2</sub>O (Scheme <link href="#anie202100954-fig-5001">1</link>). During the process of solvent evaporation, polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP) not only acts as stabilizer to control the sizes of the droplet templates in both the meso‐ and macroscopic ranges, but also as compatibilizer for minimizing the thermodynamically unfavorable contact area between droplet templates and NVP precursor, which does not affect the aggregation and growth of NVP precursor. So, during the crystallization process at high temperature, NVP maintain its porosity structure without collapse of pores. By adjusting the various reaction parameters to change the sizes of droplet templates, our method enables a high degree of control over pore structures (macrostructures, hierarchically meso/macrostructures, and mesostructures), pore sizes (5–2000 nm), and specific surface area (26–158 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) of NVP. Due to the three‐dimensional‐interconnected pore structure and single crystal structure providing a brilliant solid–liquid Na<sup>+</sup> transferring mode, large electrode–electrolyte contact area and shorten ion diffusion distance, the obtained hierarchically meso/macropores NVP single crystals (HP‐NVP) exhibits the optimal sodium storage performances: an outstanding rate capability of 61 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 C and a prominent cycling stability at 20 C for 10000 cycles (≈0.005 ‰ fading per cycle). The HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP symmetric full cells also show excellent rate performance and reversibility. And the relatively high energy density and power density indicate the promising application of HP‐NVP.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-5001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Tentative formation mechanism of hierarchically porous NVP single crystals through the polymer stabilized droplet template strategy.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202100954-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>In this polymer stabilized droplet template synthetic system, various reaction parameters including selective mass and molecular weight of PVP, evaporation temperature, and solvent composition can affect the sizes of droplet templates, and thus determining the pore sizes and pore structures of the samples. The effect of mass of PVP (M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub>) (M<sub><i>w</i></sub>=13×10<sup>4</sup> kg mol<sup>−1</sup>) on the pore structure of NVP was first studied by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). With increasing M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub> from 0 to 125 mg, the pore structures of the samples change from macrostructures (M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub>&lt;50 mg), to hierarchically meso/macrostructures (50 mg ≤ X ≤ 100 mg), and then to wormlike mesostructures (X&gt;100 mg) (Figure S1, Supporting Information). When M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub> is 100 mg, high angle annular dark‐field‐scanning TEM (HAADF‐STEM) images and TEM images show the product possesses hierarchical meso/macroporous structure (abbreviation as HP‐NVP) with the homogeneous distribution of macropores (100–200 nm) and mesopores (5–30 nm) (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0001">1 a</link>–e), indicating the superior ability of PVP to stabilize droplet templates. Selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns taken from the area of complete particles show the discrete diffraction spots, confirming the nature of single crystals rather than random aggregations of nanocrystals for HP‐NVP (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0001">1 f</link>; Figure S2, Supporting Information). High‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of HP‐NVP further display a highly ordered single‐crystal character, the distinct lattice fringe with a d‐spacing of 0.62 nm can be observed, which corresponds to (012) atomic plane of rhombohedral NVP (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0001">1 g</link> and h). Moreover, the surface of HP‐NVP is coated with a ca. 2.0 nm carbon layer (Figure S3, Supporting Information), originating from the carbonization of PVP during the crystallization process. STEM and energy dispersive spectrometry (EDS) elemental mapping are used to investigate the element distribution of HP‐NVP and reveal the uniform distribution of C, Na, O, P, and V elements (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0001">1 i</link>).</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Porous structure characterization of HP‐NVP. a–c) HAADF‐STEM images, d–e) TEM images, f) SAED pattern, g–h) HRTEM images, and i) Scanning TEM image and EDS elemental mapping of Na, V, P, O and C of HP‐NVP.</p></caption></figure><p>The effect of solvent composition on pore structure of NVP was then investigated while keeping the volume of solvent (≈40 mL) and M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub> (≈100 mg) unchanged. In the absence of THF, the as‐obtained product shows unordered wormlike mesoporous structure composed of aggregated NVP nanoparticles (denoted as WMeso‐NVP) with the size of 18 nm (Figure S4a, Supporting Information). The continuous sharp circled on SAED pattern confirms the polycrystalline nature of the sample. With varying the volume ratio of THF to EtOH (<i>V</i><sub><i>THF:EtOH</i></sub>) from 0:20 to 5:15, the resultant samples have intermediate structure composed of unevenly distributed mesopores and macropores (Figure S4b, Supporting Information). When <i>V</i><sub><i>THF:EtOH</i></sub> is 15:5, all the macrostructure disappears, instead by pure spherical mesoporous structure (denoted as SMeso‐NVP). TEM image of SMeso‐NVP reveals the diameters of mesopores are about 5–30 nm (Figure S4c, Supporting Information). When THF and H<sub>2</sub>O are used as solvent, the fast evaporation of THF affects the uniformity of droplet templates, leading to the formation of ellipsoid‐like mesoporous NVP (Figure S4d, Supporting Information).</p><p>Besides the solvent composition and mass of PVP, the molecular weight of PVP (M<sub><i>w</i></sub>) and evaporation temperature (<i>T</i>) also have tremendous influences on the pore structure of NVP. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0002">2 a</link> and Figure S5 (Supporting Information), with increasing <i>T</i> from 40 to 100 °C (M<sub><i>w</i></sub>=130×10<sup>4</sup> kg mol<sup>−1</sup>), the rapid solvent evaporation tends to form larger droplets and the pore structures of samples gradually change from hierarchically meso/macroporous structure (40 ≤ <i>T</i> ≤ 50), to intermediate structures (50 &lt; <i>T</i> ≤ 70), and to macroporous structure (70 &lt; <i>T</i> ≤ 100). SEM image of the sample prepared with <i>T</i> at 100 °C shows a macrostructure with the ultra‐large pore sizes about 500–2000 nm (denoted as Macro‐NVP). For a constant <i>T</i>, with decreasing M<sub><i>w</i></sub>, the macroporous samples are inclined to obtain, which may be attributed to the weak ability of low molecular weight PVP to stabilize droplets.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Control of pore structure of NVP. a) Schematic diagram of the pore structure of NVP as a function of <i>T</i> and M<sub><i>w</i></sub>. Synthesis conditions: M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub>=100 mg, V<sub><i>THF:EtOH</i></sub>=10:10, <i>T=</i>40 ∼100 °C, M<sub><i>w</i></sub>=2.4×10<sup>4</sup>–130×10<sup>4</sup> kg mol<sup>−1</sup>, calcination: 700 °C for 8 h in nitrogen atmosphere. b) Ternary diagram of pore structure of porous NVP. Note that this diagram is varied depending on V<sub><i>THF:EtOH</i></sub> and <i>T</i>, M<sub><i>w</i></sub> is fixed at 130×10<sup>4</sup> kg mol<sup>−1</sup>. TEM images of c) WMeso‐NVP, d) intermediate structure, e) SMeso‐NVP, f) ellipsoid‐like mesoporous NVP, g–i) hierarchically meso/macroporous NVP, and j) Macro‐NVP. The insets in (c), (e), (f), (g), (h), and (j) are the corresponding SAED patterns.</p></caption></figure><p>Based on the results above, we map out the pore structure on meso‐ and macroscales for various reaction condition in a phase diagram, and try to give the design criteria for the preparation of different pore structure of NVP (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0002">2 b</link>). Macroporous NVP single crystals are prepared by fast solvent evaporation or using low molecular weight PVP with poor droplet stability. On the contrary, only when the solvent is slowly volatilized and PVP with high molecular weight is used, can the samples with hierarchically meso/macroporous structure be obtained. The synthesis of spherical mesoporous NVP single crystals with uniform pore sizes should not only meet the synthesis condition of hierarchical meso/macroporous PVP, but also maintain the uniformity of rate of solvent evaporation. In the absence of THF, the unordered wormlike mesoporous NVP composed of nanocrystalline grains were obtained, which may be due to the inability to form stable droplets in the mixture of H<sub>2</sub>O and EtOH. Therefore, the pore structures of NVP can be precisely controlled by tuning the sizes of droplet templates (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0002">2 c</link>–j).</p><p>Detailed information of pore structure for NVP was characterized by N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption measurements. N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption isotherms of HP‐NVP and SMeso‐NVP display typical type‐IV curves, suggesting the existence of abundant mesopores (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0003">3 a</link>). The adsorption isotherm of HP‐NVP shows a capillary condensation step at relative pressure of 0.4–0.9, corresponding to pore size of 10 nm based on density functional theory (NLDFT) model (Figure S6, Supporting Information). The uptake at high relative pressure (<i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub>&gt;0.9) indicates rich macropores. The specific Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller (BET) surface area and pore volume of HP‐NVP are calculated to be 104 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and 0.173 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. For SMeso‐NVP, the adsorption isotherm exhibits two capillary condensation steps at relatively pressure over 0.4, indicating the presence of two types of mesopores of 10 and 25 nm. The BET surface area and pore volume are as high as 158 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and 0.365 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. It is worth noting that this value of surface area is almost 2.5 times as high as the largest specific surface area for NVP reported in the literatures (Table S1, Supporting Information). The isotherms of Macro‐NVP show a pore condensation step at a relative pressure over 0.9, reflecting the existence of macropores, and the BET surface area is only 46 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>. We also investigate the effect of reaction parameters on the BET surface areas of NVP. The results show the BET surface areas of samples increase linearly with the mass and molecular weight of PVP and the volume ratio of THF to EtOH, and decrease linearly with the evaporation temperature (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0003">3 b</link>–d; Tables S2–5, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Pore structure characterization of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP and SMeso‐NVP. a) Nitrogen adsorption isotherms of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP, and SMeso‐NVP. b) Relationships among BET surface area, pore volume and M<sub><i>PVP</i></sub>. c) Relationships among BET surface area, <i>T</i>, and M<sub><i>w</i></sub>. d) Relationships among BET surface area, pore volume, and V<sub><i>THF:EtOH</i></sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>The crystal structure and phase purity of as‐prepared samples were evaluated by wide‐angle X‐ray diffraction (WXRD) analysis and Raman spectroscopy. The X‐ray diffraction peaks of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP and SMeso‐NVP are readily indexed to highly crystalline rhombohedra phase (<inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100954-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-math-0001"/> space group), indicating the inexistence of other phases (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0004">4 a</link>). Raman spectra also show the characteristic modes of NVP ranging from 50 to 1200 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0004">4 b</link>). The inconspicuous fingerprint for these samples may be attributed to carbon species covering the surface of samples.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0043"><sup>[11]</sup></link> There are two types of carbon bands, including the graphite band (G band) and disorder‐induced phonon mode (D band) centered at 1596 and 1341 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The peak intensity ratios of D band to G band of Wacro‐NVP, HP‐NVP and SMeso‐NVP are similar, with a value of 0.84, indicating the high graphitization degree of carbon layers.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Structural characterization of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP, and SMeso‐NVP. a) XRD patterns, b) Raman spectra, c) V 2p, and d) C 1s high‐resolution XPS spectra of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP, and SMeso‐NVP. The black line in (a) exhibits the standard patterns of rhombohedral NVP structure (JCPDS No. 53‐0018).</p></caption></figure><p>The chemical composition and structural properties of the products were characterized by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), Fourier transform infrared (FT‐IR) spectra, and thermogravimetric (TG) analysis. XPS survey spectra reveal the existence of V, P, O, Na, and C signals in the products (Figure S7, Supporting Information). Two binding energy values at 516.7 and 523.9 eV can be observed in V 2p spectra for porous NVP, which can be assigned to V 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and V 2p<sub>1/2</sub> of V<sup>3+</sup>,<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0044"><sup>[12]</sup></link> respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0004">4 c</link>). The C 1s spectra display four binding configurations, namely, C−O, O−C=O, C−C, and C=O (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0004">4 d</link>).<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0048"><sup>[13a]</sup></link> FT‐IR spectra of HP‐NVP and SMeso‐NVP show the C−H stretching vibration at 3435 cm<sup>−1</sup> and C=O stretching vibration at 1603 cm<sup>−1</sup> that exhibit a distinct blue shift as compared with pure PVP (Figure S8, Supporting Information). It may be ascribed to the donation of oxygen loan pair electrons of PVP to the vacant orbits of V ions, confirming the C=O group can coordinate with V ions to form PVP‐NVP composites.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0049"><sup>[13b]</sup></link> Determined from CHN elemental analysis and TG analysis, the carbon contents of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP, and SMeso‐NVP are 4.0, 4.4 and 3.8 wt %, respectively (Figure S9, Supporting Information).</p><p>Based on the above discussion, we propose a polymer stabilized droplet template strategy for the preparation of porous single crystal structured NVP with controllable pore structure (Scheme <link href="#anie202100954-fig-5001">1</link>). In a preliminary experiment without PVP, the large droplet templates (≈100 nm) are formed through the hydrogen bonding of H<sub>2</sub>O, EtOH, THF, and NVP precursor (Figure S10a, Supporting Information).<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0051"><sup>[14a]</sup></link> And then macroporous NVP single crystals are obtained (Figure S10b–d, Supporting Information). After introducing PVP into the reaction system, taking HP‐NVP as a representative, the dynamic light scattering (DLS) experiments display the diameters of droplet templates decrease to 52 nm, indicating that PVP can effectively stabilize and control the sizes of droplet templates (Figure S11, Supporting Information). The carbonyl groups in PVP can interact with V ions by coordination interaction, as confirmed by FT‐IR spectra (Figure S8, Supporting Information). Na ions and PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup> gradually adsorb onto the surface of PVP and interact with V ions by electrostatic interaction. The forming PVP molecules/NVP precursor are positioned at the surface of droplets and self‐assemble into a core–shell structure. In this case, PVP molecules not only serve as stabilizer to maintain the stability of droplets and control the sizes of droplets in both the meso‐ and macroscopic ranges, but also as compatibilizer for minimizing the thermodynamically unfavorable contact area between droplet templates and NVP precursor. Along with the evaporation of mixture at 40 °C in a fume hood, due to the high reactivity of metal ions (Na<sup>+</sup> and V<sup>3+</sup>) and PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>, these core–shell structures rapidly flock together to form the micrometer‐scale particles driven by the interfacial force of THF, EtOH and water (Figure S12a, Supporting Information).<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0052"><sup>[14b]</sup></link> The prolonged evaporation process at 100 °C thoroughly removes the droplet templates, leaving abundant pores (Figure S12b, Supporting Information). During the volatilization process, PVP does not affect the aggregation and growth of NVP precursor, so the micrometer‐scale particles are obtained instead of small nanoparticles. By pyrolysis at 700 °C in N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere, NVP precursor crystallizes without the structure collapse, resulting in porous NVP single crystals.</p><p>The pore structures of porous NVP are highly controlled by adjusting the ability of PVP to stabilize the droplet templates. With increasing the volatilization temperature, the rapid solvent evaporation weakens the ability of PVP to stabilize droplet templates, and tends to form larger droplets and larger pore structures (Figure S12d–f, Supporting Information). Increasing the molecular weight of PVP decreases the surface tension of reaction system and enhances the ability of PVP to stabilize the droplet templates.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0051"><sup>[14a]</sup></link> Smaller sized droplets are formed, leading to the formation of NVP single crystals with smaller pore sizes (Figure S12g–i, Supporting Information). By adjusting the solvent composition, the formation process of droplet templates can be controlled and the volatilization rate of the solvent can be influenced, and thus, porous NVP with different pore structure and crystal structure can be prepared. In order to further prove the role of PVP, we use other polymers such as triblock copolymer (Pluronic F127) or amphiphilic poly(ethylene oxide)‐<i>b</i>‐polystyrene diblock copolymer (PEO‐<i>b</i>‐PS) instead of PVP. SEM images of the samples display interconnected porous structure and the porous skeleton is composed of aggregated NVP nanocrystals, which indicates that these polymers are only coated on the surface of nanoparticles to control the sizes of nanoparticles, rather than used to stabilize the droplet templates (Figure S13, Supporting Information). The above results further prove that PVP has strong ability to stabilize the droplet templates.</p><p>The Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance of as‐prepared NVP were evaluated in the voltage window of 2.3–3.9 V. Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 a</link> displays the galvanostatic charge‐discharge profiles at 0.5 C (1 C=117 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for cathode). HP‐NVP delivers the highest initial reversible capacity of 100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, followed by SMeso‐NVP (90 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), Macro‐NVP (84 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), and then WMeso‐NVP (78 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), which suggests the highest utilization ratio of HP‐NVP. The abnormal voltage decay at 3.2 V may be related to the reactions on the sodium metal surface, which has also been observed in the other literatures.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0053"><sup>[15]</sup></link> All the samples demonstrate an excellent capacity retention over 97 %, and HP‐NVP achieves the highest reversible capacity (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 b</link>). The main difference in the performance also manifested as the rate performance (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 c</link>). As the current rate increasing, HP‐NVP exhibits a robust rate capability with less capacity loss from 0.5 to 100 C. A high specific capacity of 61 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained at 100 C rate, much higher than those of other structural NVP materials (Table S6, Supporting Information). Figure S14 (Supporting Information) exhibits the representative discharge voltage profiles of Macro‐NVP, HP‐NVP, SMeso‐NVP, and WMeso‐NVP at various rates. Even at the current rate of 100 C, the plateau of HP‐NVP is still clearly visible. In comparison with the other samples, the lowest plateau voltage difference at different current rates for HP‐NVP suggests the least polarization of it, as well as superfast kinetics property. The cycle stability of HP‐NVP was also evaluated at a high current rate of 20 C (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 d</link>). After activation at 1 C and 10 000 cycles at 20 C, HP‐NVP still can deliver a high reversible capacity of 81 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with 0.005 ‰ loss per cycle and high Coulomb efficiency nearly 100 % on the whole cycle process.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical performance and Na<sup>+</sup> transport properties of Macro‐NVP, SMeso‐NVP, HP‐NVP and WMeso‐NVP. a) Initial charge‐discharge profiles. b) Cycle performance at 0.5 C rate. c) Rate performances from 0.5 to 100 C. d) Long‐term cyclability at 20 C rate. e) Linear fits for the anodic and cathodic peak currents of CV curves versus scan rates. f) The transport process of Na<sup>+</sup> in different NVP crystal structures.</p></caption></figure><p>The superior electrochemical performance of HP‐NVP can be ascribed to the unique architectural feature. Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction kinetics was studied via CV measurements at different scanning rates ranging from 0.2 to 4.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 e</link> and S15 (Supporting Information), HP‐NVP possesses the highest Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients (<inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie202100954-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-math-0002"/>) of 2.21×10<sup>−10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> S<sup>−1</sup> and 2.31×10<sup>−10</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> S<sup>−1</sup> in charge and discharge process (Table S7, Supporting Information). Compared with the macroporous structure and mesoporous structure, the hierarchically porous structure has a three‐dimensional interconnected channel, which can provide shorter ion diffusion distance and large electrode–electrolyte contact area, greatly facilitating fast ionic transport. Furthermore, the crystalline state of material is also a vital factor affecting the electrochemical activity. WMeso‐NVP is composed of aggregated nanocrystals that enables Na<sup>+</sup> migrate through many solid‐solid grain boundaries with sluggish kinetics (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 f</link>). The lattice near the boundaries and interfaces is usually disordered, hence the lattice is mismatched between neighboring primary grains, which need a higher activation energy of migration across solid‐solid interfaces than that in the bulk crystal phase.<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0057"><sup>[16a]</sup></link> On the contrary, HP‐NVP single crystals do not contain grain boundaries inside one particle. Owing to the unique hierarchically porous single crystal structure, Na<sup>+</sup> can enter HP‐NVP single crystals through solid–liquid interfaces instead of through many solid‐solid interfaces and quickly diffuse inside micrometer‐scale particles along the long‐range ordered lattice, which greatly improves Na<sup>+</sup> transfer rate (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0005">5 f</link>).<link href="#anie202100954-bib-0058"><sup>[16b]</sup></link> Besides, HP‐NVP exhibits higher tap density of 1.02 g cm<sup>−3</sup> as compared with WMeso‐NVP (≈0.74 g cm<sup>−3</sup>), because of its micrometer‐scale particle (Table S8, Supporting Information). Therefore, HP‐NVP delivers the best rate performance and the highest reversible capacity than those of other NVP samples.</p><p>To further demonstrate the versatility of HP‐NVP, symmetric full cells were assembled with HP‐NVP as both the cathode and anode, as illustrated in the schematic working principle (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0006">6 a</link>). The electrochemical properties of HP‐NVP in the voltage range of 1.0–2.5 V are presented in Figure S16 (Supporting Information), showing excellent sodium storage performance as well. Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0006">6 b</link> shows the typical charge‐discharge profiles with a working potential about 1.7 V. Due to the superior kinetic properties of HP‐NVP material, HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP symmetric full cells show remarkable rate performance. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0006">6 c</link>, the full cells exhibit final discharge capacities of 73, 72, 70, 65, 58, and 54 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at current rates of 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, and 30 C, respectively. Even at a high rate of 50 C, it can still show a capacity of 47 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, approximately 65 % of reversible capacity at 1 C. It is worth noting that the rate performance of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP full cells significantly exceed other recently reported NVP‐based symmetric full cells (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0006">6 d</link>; Table S9, Supporting Information), which can be ascribed to the hierarchically porous single crystal structure of HP‐NVP. Based on the mass of cathode, Ragone plot depicts the energy densities of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP full cells at different power densities calculated from the discharge profiles. Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0006">6 e</link> exhibits a specific energy of ≈125 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C and a specific power of ≈9017 W kg<sup>−1</sup>, clearly indicating the outstanding energy density and power capability of HP‐NVP in symmetric full cells system. Finally, the cycling performance is tested at a high current rate of 10 C (Figure <link href="#anie202100954-fig-0006">6 f</link>). After activation at 1 C, there is no apparent degradation in capacity over 700 cycles at 10 C. All the results fully demonstrate the promising application of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP symmetric full cells.</p><figure xml:id="anie202100954-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202100954:anie202100954-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202100954-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical behavior of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP symmetric full cells. a) Schematic illustration of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP. b) Typical charge‐discharge profiles at 1 C rate in the voltage range of 1.0–2.3 V. c) Rate capability and d) comparison of the rate performances of NVP symmetric full cells to the previous results in the literature. e) Ragone plot of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP. f) Long‐term cycle performance of HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP at 10 C rate.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202100954-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we develop a facile polymer stabilized droplet template strategy for the synthesis of porous NVP single crystals with highly interconnected pore structure. PVP is used as stabilizer to control the sizes of droplet templates. By adjusting the sizes of the droplet templates, we succeed in controlling the pore structures of the samples from macrostructures to hierarchically meso/macrostructures and to mesostructures. The resulting HP‐NVP possesses hierarchically porous single crystal structure that can provide a solid–liquid Na<sup>+</sup> transmission mode, short ion diffusion distance and large electrode–electrolyte contact area, and thus exhibits the excellent sodium storage performances with an outstanding rate capability and a prominent cycling stability. Meanwhile, the HP‐NVP∥HP‐NVP symmetric full cells deliver excellent rate performance and reversibility, clearly indicating the promising application of HP‐NVP. We envisage that this approach can inspire new studies aimed at the preparation of porous single crystal materials in materials science.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202100954-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Young Thousand Talented Program and the National Natural Science Foundation of China (21671073, 21621001, 51972142 and 1972142), the “111” Project of the Ministry of Education of China (B17020), Program for JLU Science and Technology Innovative Research Team.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202100954-sec-0005" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie202100954-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="anie202100954-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="anie202100954-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie202100954-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Jian</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.-S.</givenNames> <familyName>Hu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>X.</givenNames> <familyName>Ji</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>W.</givenNames> <familyName>Chen</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
