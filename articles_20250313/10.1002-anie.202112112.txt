Herein, N‐heteropentacenequinone (TAPQ) is investigated to reveal the solvation effect on the performance of sodium‐ion batteries in different electrolyte environment. TAPQ cycled in diglyme‐based electrolyte exhibits superior electrochemical performance, but experiences a rapid capacity fading in carbonate‐based electrolyte. The function of solvation effect is mainly embodied in two aspects: one is the stabilization of anion intermediate via the compatibility of electrode and electrolyte, the other is the interfacial electrochemical characteristics influenced by solvation sheath structure. By revealing the failure mechanism, this work presents an avenue for better understanding electrochemical behavior and enhancing performance from the angle of solvation effect.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>N‐heteropentacenequinone (TAPQ) is studied as electrode material to investigate the solvation effect in different sodium‐ion battery electrolytes. By revealing the failure mechanism of TAPQ in carbonate‐based electrolytes, we discuss how the solvation effect influences interfacial electrochemical characteristics and attributed the electrolyte compatibility to the stabilization effect of reaction intermediate via solvation effect.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112112:anie202112112-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112112-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202112112-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Building green battery with renewable electrode material is crucial for the future sustainable development of energy.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> In this regard, developing sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) with organic electrode is an appropriate choice.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0002 #anie202112112-bib-0003"><sup>[2, 3]</sup></link> Organic electrode materials (OEMs), which are characterized by renewability, structural diversity and designability, are emerging as promising candidates for developing sustainable energy storage devices.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0004 #anie202112112-bib-0005 #anie202112112-bib-0006"><sup>[4–6]</sup></link> Nevertheless, the applications of Na‐organic batteries are still terribly plagued by many factors.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0007 #anie202112112-bib-0008"><sup>[7, 8]</sup></link> A suitable electrolyte is indispensable for the application of OEMs in SIBs. Currently, most of the non‐aqueous electrolytes for SIBs are based on a single, or mixtures of carbonate solvents, such as ethylene carbonate (EC), propylene carbonate (PC) and diethyl carbonate (DEC).<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0009"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Each electrode material needs to couple with its optimized electrolyte. There is no universal electrolyte which suitable for any electrode material. Besides the sodium salt, the solvent environment has a significant effect on the fundamental electrochemical reaction process. Essentially, Na<sup>+</sup> solvation sheath structure and desolvation process play a significant role in the stability of electrolyte and the reversibility of electrochemical reaction. In the electrolyte, Na<sup>+</sup> is solvated by solvent molecules and exists in the form of cation‐solvent complex. Meanwhile, anion would be separated from the primary solvation sheath in the Na<sup>+</sup>‐solvated electrolyte. The redox behavior of electrode material is affected by the composition and structure of solvation shell.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0010 #anie202112112-bib-0011"><sup>[10, 11]</sup></link> Compared to carbonate‐based solvent, the chelating nature of linear ether‐based solvent makes it tend to form much stronger binding with alkali cation, and thus exhibits an entirely different electrochemical behavior.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0012 #anie202112112-bib-0013 #anie202112112-bib-0014 #anie202112112-bib-0015 #anie202112112-bib-0016 #anie202112112-bib-0017"><sup>[12–17]</sup></link> Although considerable efforts have been devoted to investigating the reasons for the improved cell performance in ether‐based electrolyte.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0018 #anie202112112-bib-0019 #anie202112112-bib-0020 #anie202112112-bib-0021 #anie202112112-bib-0022 #anie202112112-bib-0023 #anie202112112-bib-0024"><sup>[18–24]</sup></link> There is still lack of a systematic investigation to reveal the correlation of solvation effect and electrochemical performance. Not to mention how to enhance the performance of OEMs from the perspective of solvation effect.</p><p>In the liquid electrolyte environment, all the components including active materials and sodium salt are affected by solvent molecules. It has been proposed that dielectric environment promotes the interactions between Na<sup>+</sup> and the redox‐active sites.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0010"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Through the solvation, the electronic polarization and binding energy would be enhanced, thus indicating the strengthened Na<sup>+</sup>‐binding thermodynamics.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0010"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Therefore, the performance of OEMs are closely related to the solvation effect, which should receive considerable attention but has been rarely considered. Another important point to be considered is the correlation between electrolyte and solid electrolyte interphase (SEI).<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0025 #anie202112112-bib-0026 #anie202112112-bib-0027"><sup>[25–27]</sup></link> The competitive reactions of cation‐solvent‐anion that occur on the interphase between electrode/electrolyte play a dominant role in determining the components and properties of the SEI. The Na<sup>+</sup> solvation complex serves as a reaction precursor during the formation of SEI. Using the same sodium salt, different solvents would result in different microstructures and compositions of SEI.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0009"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Hence, it is essential to clarify how the solvation effect has impact on interfacial electrochemical characteristics.</p><p>Herein, N‐heteropentacenequinone (TAPQ) is employed as the electrode material to investigate the solvation effect in different SIBs electrolytes. It is found that TAPQ experiences structure damage when operated in the carbonate‐based electrolyte. Amazingly, TAPQ cycled in diglyme (DEGDME)‐based electrolyte exhibits extraordinary performance, including a high capacity (400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), good rate capability (242 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and long‐term stability (capacity retention of 72 % over 1000 cycles). Examining the solvation structures and the corresponding interphase properties, the failure mechanism of TAPQ in carbonate‐based solvents has been revealed. The solvation effect mechanism discussed here is feasible for various battery systems, which not only provides reliable approach to optimize performance, but also enrich the understanding of the electrolyte design principle.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202112112-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>When the electroactive functional groups synergically integrated into the backbone of pentacene, the obtained functionalized pentacene derivatives are important building blocks for multi‐electron redox OEMs.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0028 #anie202112112-bib-0029 #anie202112112-bib-0030"><sup>[28–30]</sup></link> Under these circumstances, 5,7,11,14‐tetraaza‐6,13‐pentacenequinone (TAPQ) is synthesized according to our previous report.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0031"><sup>[31]</sup></link> Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0001">1 a</link> shows the discharge/charge profiles of TAPQ in the common carbonate‐based electrolyte (1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> EC/DEC). The initial discharge profile is featured by evident and multiple voltage plateaus, which correspond to the multi‐step redox process. Unfortunately, nearly all the evident electrochemical characteristics disappear in the next cycle and the capacity declines to 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> from the 10th cycle, which is also verified by the cyclic voltammetry (CV) test (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0001">1 c</link>, Figure S1). The initial discharge/charge capacity of TAPQ in EC/DEC‐based electrolyte is 410/177 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, corresponding to a terrible Coulombic efficiency of 43 %. Notably, the ultra‐low initial Columbic efficiency and capacity fading can also be found in other carbonate‐based electrolytes (Figure S2). Different sodium salts (NaPF<sub>6</sub>, NaClO<sub>4</sub> and NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub>) are tested in various single or mixed of carbonate solvents; however, similar charge/discharge behavior can be observed, implying that the type of sodium salts and carbonate solvents does not bring a significant impact in the carbonate‐based system (Figure S2).</p><figure xml:id="anie202112112-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112112:anie202112112-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112112-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a),b) Discharge/charge profiles of TAPQ∥Na cell in 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> EC/DEC and DEGDME electrolyte, respectively. c) CV curves of TAPQ in EC/DEC‐based electrolyte. d) The CV curves of TAPQ in various cell architectures. e) FTIR spectra of TAPQ using DEGDME‐based electrolyte.</p></caption></figure><p>In comparison to carbonate, an entirely different electrochemical behavior can be observed in ether‐based electrolyte. The reversible and clear voltage plateaus are well retained in the successive cycling process (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0001">1 b</link>). Unexpectedly, TAPQ can deliver an initial discharge and charge capacity of 520 and 425 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a Columbic efficiency of 82 %, which is much higher than that of carbonate‐based electrolyte. To figure out whether the reversible redox reaction in DEGDME is contributed by the sodium storage in TAPQ and exclude the worry of instability of electrolyte, a series of CV tests are performed (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0001">1 d</link>). The CV against the copper current collector verifies that no undesired side reactions contribute to the assumed cell reaction. Without sodium salts, there is no electrochemical reaction occurring, indicating the reversible electrochemical behaviors in ether‐based electrolyte are attributed to sodium storage in TAPQ. To gain insight into the reaction mechanism, ex‐situ Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) are employed to unravel the structural evolution of TAPQ in the DEGDME‐based electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0001">1 e</link>). As the discharge depth increases, the stretching vibration of carbonyl located at about 1695 cm<sup>−1</sup> gradually decreases, which indicates the reaction between Na<sup>+</sup> and carbonyl (C=O). Meanwhile, the vibrations of imine group (C=N) at 1537, 1515, and 1483 cm<sup>−1</sup> gradually diminish during the discharging process, and recover to pristine level during the charging process. The weakening of C=O group is accompanied with the weakening of C=N group, indicating a simultaneous coordination of N and O to Na<sup>+</sup> happens during the reduction process (Scheme S2). Meanwhile, the gradual weakening/strengthening of other characteristic vibrations further supports the reversible sodiation/desodiation behavior of TAPQ.</p><p>Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0002">2</link> shows the electrochemical performance of TAPQ in EC/DEC and DEGDME electrolytes. In the case of DEGDME, TAPQ electrode could deliver a stable capacity of 400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and retain 360 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 225 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0002">2 a</link>); while in the EC/DEC electrolyte, the capacity declines rapidly from the beginning of cycling and maintains lower than 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the next 100 cycles. As the current increases to 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the advantage of DEGDME electrolyte can be evidently revealed (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0002">2 b</link>). The reversible capacity of TAPQ remains at 253 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles, meaning a capacity retention of 72 %. Compared with the EC/DEC system, batteries based on DEGDME electrolyte display an exceptional rate capability. Relative to the reversible capacity of 400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity retention is 74 %, 69 %, 65 %, 62 % and 59 %, at a current of 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, 4.0 and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0002">2 c</link>). Whereas the corresponding values for EC/DEC are only 48 %, 43 % and 40 %, at current of 1.0, 2.0 and 3.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure S1b). To reveal the superior reaction kinetics of TAPQ in DEGDME‐based electrolyte, CV measurements at various scan rates are performed (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0002">2 d</link>). Consistent with the charge/discharge profiles with multiple voltage plateaus, CV curves display three evident voltage regions at about 0.01–0.6 V, 1.0–1.94 V and 1.94–3.1 V. In different voltage regions, the amount of charge stored by capacitive and battery‐type processes is rather different. Parameter b of Ox 1, Re 1 and Re 2 is around 0.8, while the others lie between 0.55 and 0.74, implying a mixed capacitive and battery‐type reaction (Figure S3). By analyzing the relationship between peak current response and sweep rate, the contributions of capacitive behavior can be differentiated from battery‐type reaction.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0033 #anie202112112-bib-0034"><sup>[33, 34]</sup></link> Obviously, the capacity is primarily stemmed from the diffusion‐controlled process even during fast discharge/charge process. It is unusual for OEMs based upon diffusion‐controlled process to exhibit fast kinetics behavior.</p><figure xml:id="anie202112112-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112112:anie202112112-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112112-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Cycling performance of TAPQ∥Na cell in different electrolytes. b),c) Cycle stability and rate performance of TAPQ∥Na cell in 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> DEGDME electrolyte. d) CV curves at various scan rates of TAPQ∥Na cell in DEGDME‐based electrolyte. e) Capacitive and battery contributions of charge storage at different scan rates.</p></caption></figure><p>It should be noted that the unique electrochemical behavior can not only be found in DEGDME but also in other ether‐based solvents, such as ethylene glycol dimethyl ether (DME) (Figure S4) and tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) (Figure S5). Undoubtedly, there exist some commonness in these ether‐based electrolyte. Under the same concentration and sodium salt, the big difference of electrochemical behavior between carbonate‐ and ether‐based electrolytes should be attributed to the solvation effect which arises from the interfacial electrochemical characteristics and solvation sheath structure (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0003">3 a</link>). Firstly, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of cycled electrode are collected to reveal the morphology of SEI. Obviously, the SEI on the surface of electrode in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte is much thicker than that of DEGDME system (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0003">3 b,c</link>). The similar phenomenon can also be found in other carbonate‐based electrolyte system (Figure S7). To further reveal the components of SEI, X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) is performed (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0003">3 d,e</link>, Figure S8). The F 1s spectrum contains two peaks at ≈687 eV and ≈684 eV, belonging to P‐F and Na‐F, respectively. According to the C 1s spectrum, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> derived from the decomposition of the carbonate solvent can only be detected in EC/DEC‐based electrolyte (Figure S8b). As shown in the C 1s and O 1s spectrum, the content of organic species in the DEGDME system, which originate from the decomposition of the solvent, is a relative minority (Figure S8a, S8c). Compared with EC/DEC‐based electrolyte, DEGDME shows a high solvation energy and reduction stability. Hence, there is a preferential reduction of NaPF<sub>6</sub>, forming an inorganics‐dominant SEI. Different from DEGDME system, the carbonate solvents will undergo a severe decomposition to form a thicker SEI, due to its weak solvation energy and low reduction stability. In the electrode/electrolyte interphase, desolvation process plays a crucial role in determining the properties of SEI, which is correlate with the variation of solvation sheath in the bulk electrolyte and electrode/electrolyte interphase. When the solvated [Na<sup>+</sup>(solvent)<sub><i>y</i></sub>]anion approaches the electrode/electrolyte interface, there exists a competitive reduction reaction between the solvent molecule and anion. The species easy to decompose would become the subsequent SEI precursors. Consequently, the solvation sheath structure could be used to analyze the properties of interphase protection layer.</p><figure xml:id="anie202112112-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112112:anie202112112-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112112-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Schematic illustration showing the function of solvation effect on TAPQ. SEM images of the cycled electrode in different electrolyte: b) 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in EC/DEC, c) 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in DEGDME. XPS spectra of the F 1s region for the cycled electrode: d) 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in EC/DEC, e) 1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in DEGDME.</p></caption></figure><p>In the ether‐based electrolyte, Na<sup>+</sup> is preferentially coordinated with six oxygen atoms to reach the most stable configuration.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0035 #anie202112112-bib-0036"><sup>[35, 36]</sup></link> Ethers will strongly solvate Na<sup>+</sup> through its multiple oxygen atoms in the rotatable linear structure.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0026 #anie202112112-bib-0037"><sup>[26, 37]</sup></link> As the DEGDME owns three ether‐oxygen atoms, two molecules are required to achieve a stable solvation shell (Scheme S1). In comparison, each carbonate solvent molecule mainly provides one carbonyl oxygen for coordination. To achieve the same coordination number, more carbonate solvent molecules should participate to form a similar Na‐O hexa‐coordinate environment.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0020"><sup>[20]</sup></link> However, the solvation shell consisted of one Na<sup>+</sup> surrounded by several carbonate solvent molecules will result in a stronger steric repulsion.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0013 #anie202112112-bib-0016"><sup>[13, 16]</sup></link> Due to the steric hindrance of solvents and the weak Lewis base properties of carbonyl oxygen, the solvation structure of Na<sup>+</sup> in carbonate‐based solvent is not characterized by a definite geometry.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0038 #anie202112112-bib-0039"><sup>[38, 39]</sup></link> Unlike carbonate‐based electrolyte, the steric hindrance existing in the ether‐based system is much less due to its multidentate chelation feature. Owing to the strong electron‐donating properties of ether oxygen atoms, the solvation shell of [Na<sup>+</sup>(DEGDME)<sub><i>x</i></sub>] complex prefers a rigid structure, whereas that of [Na<sup>+</sup>(EC/DEC)<sub><i>y</i></sub>] is much more flexible (Scheme S1). A rigid solvation structure implies that desolvation of one solvent molecule from [Na<sup>+</sup>(DEGDME)<sub><i>x</i></sub>] requires more energy, which is indicated by a high solvation free energy. This conclusion is verified by the DFT calculations, which show that the solvation free energy of DEGDME (−122.40 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) is far greater than that of EC (−59.08 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) and DEC (−75.29 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 a</link>). Consequently, carbonate‐based solvation sheath would become more unstable and prone to accept electron because of the weak solvation energy and reduction stability. When the solvation interaction becomes strong enough, anion is within a certain distance from the inner solvation shell to achieve the electroneutrality. In the ether‐based electrolyte, solvent molecules intervene between the Na<sup>+</sup> and PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> and prevent their direct contact. Hence, anion would preferentially become the subsequent SEI precursors, and thus form an inorganic‐dominant interphase. Whereas carbonate‐based solvation shell will experience a severe decomposition of both solvent and anion.</p><figure xml:id="anie202112112-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202112112:anie202112112-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202112112-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The solvation energy of TAPQ in different electrolytes. b) Diffusion coefficient (<i>D</i>) estimated from GITT method. c),d) EIS of TAPQ∥Na cell in EC/DEC‐based and DEGDME‐based electrolyte, respectively. e) Arrhenius plots of the temperature dependence of EIS resistance. f) FTIR of the cycled electrode in different electrolytes. g) <sup>1</sup>H NMR spectra of TAPQ electrode.</p></caption></figure><p>The remarkable enhancement of electrochemical performance in ether‐based electrolyte cannot be attributed solely to the properties of SEI. There exist two types of area on the surface of electrode: fresh area and active area (Scheme S3). The fresh area is mild and stable, which exhibits compatibility with the electrolyte. In this case, electrolyte decomposition would not take place in the fresh area and there is no SEI formation. Whereas the active area refers to the lively and unstable space, which shows incompatibility with the electrolyte. In this case, electrolyte decomposition would occur and lead to the formation of SEI on the electrode surface. The active area will become stable when the SEI covers it and alleviates its further decomposition. Unfortunately, in the incompatible electrolytes, serious electrolyte decomposition would happen in both the active and fresh areas. However, the fundamental cause for the electrolyte compatibility is unclear and deserves investigation. To reveal the compatibility of electrolyte, comparative experiments were performed (Figure S9). Firstly, the cell was assembled by using TAPQ cathode and sodium metal anode with DEGDME‐based electrolyte. After the formation of SEI in the initial cycles, the battery was disassembled in an argon‐filled glovebox. Then, the cycled TAPQ electrode was used to assemble a new battery with carbonate‐based electrolyte (1.0 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in EC/DEC). It can be found that the cycled TAPQ electrode in DEGDME system almost lost the activity when re‐used in the EC/DEC system (Figure S9c). Even the SEI derived from DEGDME‐based electrolyte has formed, the cycled TAPQ electrode in EC/DEC electrolyte cannot exhibit normal behavior. In comparison, the typical characteristics of DEGDME‐based electrolyte system can also be maintained even after cycled in EC/DEC system. When the cycled TAPQ electrode in EC/DEC‐based electrolyte is re‐used in the DEGDME system, the evident voltage plateaus can also be found (Figure S9d). It can be deduced that the protective SEI formed on the surface of electrode cannot be fully responsible for the stable operation of electrode materials.</p><p>Herein, we proposed to explain the compatibility between electrolyte and electrode materials on the molecular level. The reaction activity of TAPQ is correlated to the vibration frequency of characteristic functional groups (carbonyl, imine and aromatic ring), which determined by its intrinsic electronic configuration as well as the forces exerted by the surrounded solvents. The charge/discharge process of TAPQ is accompany with the intramolecular and intermolecular charge modulation. In the liquid electrolyte environment, the solvation energy can affect the conformation change of molecule as the state of charge varied, which is effective in facilitating the subsequent charge transfer dynamics. Compared with ether, carbonate‐based solvent could not yield stable Na<sup>+</sup> solvated complexes. Different from carbonates, in which solvent‐shared triple ions ([Na<sup>+</sup>(EC/DEC)<sub><i>y</i></sub>TAPQ<sup>.−</sup>]) are formed, it is energetically favorable to form solvent‐separated triple ions ([Na<sup>+</sup>(DEGDME)<sub><i>y</i></sub>][TAPQ<sup>.−</sup>]) in compatible electrolyte (Scheme S1).<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0040"><sup>[40]</sup></link> Electrolyte composition is considered as the key to reduce the activation barrier of the phase transformation during the sodium storage. DEGDME‐based electrolyte appears to be the most efficient in decreasing the kinetic barrier for the structure evolution of organic materials.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0040"><sup>[40]</sup></link> In the charge process, the strong Coulombic force in solvent‐shared triple‐ions multiples does not facilitate the Na<sup>+</sup> removal from the solvent‐ion‐complex during desodiation.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0041"><sup>[41]</sup></link> Thanks to the DEGDME molecules intervened between the cations and TAPQ anions, and eliminating their direct contact, it would be suitable to crystallize thermally stable complexes. Owing to the joint involvement of both electrostatic ion‐dipole interaction and electronic chemisorption effect, TAPQ anions tend to be stabilized by solvation, and thus exhibits evident voltage plateaus for the multiple reaction intermediate during discharge/charge process.</p><p>Based upon the above analysis, it is time to reveal the cause for the failure of TAPQ in carbonate‐based electrolyte. To clarify the diffusion properties of Na<sup>+</sup> in the electrode, galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurement was performed (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 b</link>, Figure S10, S11). Noticeably, the diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> in EC/DEC‐based system is lower than that of DEGDME‐based system. Due to the severe decomposition of EC/DEC, a large amount of decomposed organic products precipitate to form a thicker SEI layer, leading to a higher electrochemical impedance (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 c</link>). In comparison, the corresponding value for ether‐based electrolyte is negligible (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 d</link>, Figure S6). To elucidate the reaction kinetics at interphase, temperature‐dependent EIS was performed to evaluate the charge transfer energy barrier (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>,<sub>ct</sub>) and the activation energy across SEI layer (<i>E</i><sub>a</sub>,<sub>SEI</sub>) (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 e</link>, Figure S12).<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0044 #anie202112112-bib-0045 #anie202112112-bib-0046 #anie202112112-bib-0047"><sup>[44–47]</sup></link> A sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> transfer across interface will definitely exert an adverse effect on the rate capability.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0009"><sup>[9]</sup></link> The activation energies obtained from the slope in Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 e</link> illustrate that a higher activation energy would be required to transport Na<sup>+</sup> across the SEI layer in carbonate‐based electrolyte. Additionally, the activation energy for charge transfer (80.02 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte is 2.62 times of that in DEGDME‐based electrolyte (30.49 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>). The charge‐transfer process is correlated with the properties of SEI.<link href="#anie202112112-bib-0048 #anie202112112-bib-0049 #anie202112112-bib-0050 #anie202112112-bib-0051 #anie202112112-bib-0052"><sup>[48–52]</sup></link> A thick SEI layer increases the diffusion distance. The organic species in the EC/DEC‐derived SEI exhibits high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion energy barrier. Whereas the NaF‐riched SEI derived from ether‐based electrolyte usually exhibits a reduced energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion. Consequently, the transportation of Na<sup>+</sup> across the interphase formed in DEGDME system will be faster and more convenient. As shown by the SEM image, carbonate electrolyte produces not only a less conductive layer but also a thicker SEI layer which would increase the diffusion difficulty of Na<sup>+</sup>. In contrast, thin SEI layer formed in the ether‐based electrolyte ensures an easier diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup>(Figure S13). As discussed above, the solvation stability of carbonate‐based electrolyte is inferior to the ether counterpart. More seriously, the thick SEI layer will be the blocking layer in the formation of solvation stability complex between the anions and solvent molecules, thus weakening the stabilization effect. Consequently, the structure of TAPQ in the carbonate‐based solvent cannot remain well and has been destroyed after cycling. To confirm this conclusion, FTIR and <sup>1</sup>H NMR of the cycled electrode was performed (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 f,g</link>). The FTIR vibration peaks of electrode in DEGDME are well consistent with the pristine sample (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 f</link>). However, in the carbonate system, the typical signal peak cannot be matched with pristine sample, which means that the structure of TAPQ has been destroyed after the initial cycle. To obtain more information about the structure change of TAPQ, <sup>1</sup>H NMR of the cycled electrode was conducted (Figure <link href="#anie202112112-fig-0004">4 g</link>). Due to the high symmetry of TAPQ molecule, only a couple of quartets can be found within 8.1–8.5 ppm. In the DEGDME electrolyte, the entire characteristic signals are identical with the pristine sample. However, these resonance signals disappear in the EC/DEC electrolyte and are replaced by complex proton information, showing that the molecule structure has been broken.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202112112-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, the big difference between electrochemical behavior of carbonate‐ and ether‐based electrolytes is interpreted from the angle of solvation effect. By systematically investigating how the solvation impact the foundational electrochemical process, it is found that ether‐based electrolyte displays unique solvent‐ion‐complex sheath structure, thus producing an efficient stabilization of TAPQ anions and facilitating the multi‐electron redox reaction that occurs at different potentials. The much‐improved interfacial characteristics are derived from the unique SEI properties in DEGDME‐based electrolyte. In comparison, the activation energy for charge transfer and Na<sup>+</sup> transport across the SEI layer is much higher in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte. The failure cause of TAPQ in carbonate‐based electrolyte is related to the weak solvation stability and thick SEI layer, thus leading to a degradation of material structure. This finding not only complements the existing knowledge on the unexpected high performance in SIBs with ether‐based electrolyte, but also presents a new insight into electrolyte optimization.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202112112-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge the financial support of the National Natural Science Foundation of China (21771013, 51925202 and 51822201).
