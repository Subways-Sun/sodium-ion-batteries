Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>The pseudo‐layered sulfide NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> exhibits outstanding electrochemical performance as anode material in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs). The Na storage mechanism is investigated by synchrotron‐based X‐ray scattering and absorption techniques as well as by electrochemical measurements. A very high reversible capacity in the 500th cycle of 489 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is observed at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the potential window 3.0–0.1 V. Full discharge includes irreversible generation of Ni<sup>0</sup> and Cr<sup>0</sup> nanoparticles embedded in nanocrystalline Na<sub>2</sub>S yielding shortened diffusion lengths and predominantly surface‐controlled charge storage. During charge, Ni<sup>0</sup> and Cr<sup>0</sup> are oxidized, Na<sub>2</sub>S is consumed, and amorphous Ni and Cr sulfides are formed. Limiting the potential window to 3.0–0.3 V an unusual nickel extrusion sodium insertion mechanism occurs: Ni<sup>2+</sup> is reduced to nanosized Ni<sup>0</sup> domains, expelled from the host lattice, and is replaced by Na<sup>+</sup> cations to form O3‐type like NaCrS<sub>2</sub>. Surprisingly, the discharge and charge processes comprise Na<sup>+</sup> shuttling between highly crystalline NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> and NaCrS<sub>2</sub> enabling a superior long‐term stability for 3000 cycles. The results not only provide valuable insights for the electrochemistry of conversion materials but also extend the scope of layered electrode materials considering the reversible nickel extrusion sodium insertion reaction as new concept for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202101576-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Results of an extensive study of pseudo‐layered NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> as an anode material in sodium‐ion batteries are reported. xml:id="adma202101576-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202101576-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable batteries are an essential part of electrical energy storage (EES) for our modern society as the technological demand rises rapidly since the last two to three decades.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0001 #adma202101576-bib-0002 #adma202101576-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The climate change and the pollutant impact of fossil fuels as prime power supply raised a major interest in large‐scale EES of clean, renewable and sustainable, in short, green energy from, e.g., solar, wind or geothermal sources to overcome the variation in energy production and consumption.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0004 #adma202101576-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Commercialized full cell rechargeable battery types are commonly Li‐based (lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs)) and widely utilized for mobile electric applications like portable electronics and electric vehicles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0006 #adma202101576-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Owing to the uneven distribution of Li in the earth crust with the highest abundances in geopolitically critical areas of the planet and relatively high costs for Li production make LIBs less suitable for large‐scale EES applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0008 #adma202101576-bib-0009 #adma202101576-bib-0010 #adma202101576-bib-0011"><sup>8–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This is one important reason why an indispensable research topic is dedicated to identify abundant, economic, environmentally friendly and efficient alternatives. Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have received enormous attention in the last few years to address this matter due to the high abundance of Na and a promising high theoretical energy density of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0002 #adma202101576-bib-0011 #adma202101576-bib-0012 #adma202101576-bib-0013 #adma202101576-bib-0014 #adma202101576-bib-0015 #adma202101576-bib-0016 #adma202101576-bib-0017 #adma202101576-bib-0018"><sup>2,11–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The electrochemical and physical properties of Na are comparable to Li. But in contrast to Li<sup>+</sup> or K<sup>+</sup>, the electrochemical insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> into untreated graphite is hindered and new anode materials for SIBs with high capacity, cycle stability and fast charge–discharge capability must be discovered.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0019 #adma202101576-bib-0020 #adma202101576-bib-0021 #adma202101576-bib-0022"><sup>19–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the various anode materials investigated for the application in SIBs, transition metal sulfides (TMSs) attracted high interest due to their abundance, low costs and high theoretical capacities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0023 #adma202101576-bib-0024 #adma202101576-bib-0025 #adma202101576-bib-0026 #adma202101576-bib-0027"><sup>23–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Beyond the intercalation/deintercalation mechanism using layered electrode materials, which is operating in most commercial LIBs, the so‐called conversion reaction has become a research focus due to the promising high energy densities and specific capacities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0004 #adma202101576-bib-0028 #adma202101576-bib-0029"><sup>4,28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During the electrochemical Na uptake in TMSs, the metal cations are reduced to metallic nanoparticles embedded in a Na<sub>2</sub>S matrix.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0006 #adma202101576-bib-0029"><sup>6,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, conversion reactions are accompanied by large volume changes leading to contact loss to the current collector often resulting in poor cyclability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0002 #adma202101576-bib-0029 #adma202101576-bib-0030"><sup>2,29,30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Different strategies are pursued to compensate the unbeneficial volume change, e.g. heterostructure design and organic coatings (such as SnSe<sub>2</sub>/ZnSe@polydopamine),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanoengineering or design of carbon hybrid materials using reduced graphene oxide (rGO), carbon nanofibers, or carbon nanotubes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0032 #adma202101576-bib-0033 #adma202101576-bib-0034 #adma202101576-bib-0035 #adma202101576-bib-0036 #adma202101576-bib-0037 #adma202101576-bib-0038"><sup>32–38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> But most of these carbon‐based additives are too expensive for large‐scale applications.</p><p>Ternary TMSs demonstrated good performances as anode materials in SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0024 #adma202101576-bib-0039 #adma202101576-bib-0040"><sup>24,39,40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> But to continue development, prescient concepts might be necessary to face the obstacles of conversion‐type and intercalation‐based materials in SIBs. Recently, we discovered the formation of crystalline Na<sub>0.7</sub>Cu<sub>0.15</sub>CrS<sub>2</sub> during the sodiation process in the pseudo‐layered compound CuCrS<sub>2</sub> via copper extrusion sodium insertion mechanism.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hereby, nanosized Cu particles are expelled from a [CrS<sub>2</sub>] host lattice dramatically enhancing the cycle stability of CuCrS<sub>2</sub> compared to mere NaCrS<sub>2</sub>. This discovery raised two indispensable questions: i) do other ternary layer‐like Cr sulfides exhibit comparable electrochemical properties and ii) can a transition metal extrusion from a pseudo‐layered host material be exploited to reversibly shuttle Na<sup>+</sup> cations with structural retention of the host lattice? This could provide a new concept for SIBs using particular transition metal atom sites for Na insertion/extraction in layer‐like electrode materials. During our search for pseudo‐layered TMSs we identified NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> as a suitable candidate exhibiting a high theoretical capacity (736.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) assuming a full conversion reaction of Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Cr<sup>3+</sup> ions to their elemental states as formally shown in Equation (<link href="#adma202101576-disp-0001">1</link>)<displayedItem NaCrS<sub>2</sub> and NaCr<sub>2/3</sub>Ti<sub>1/3</sub>S<sub>2</sub> as cathode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0041 #adma202101576-bib-0042"><sup>41,42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The electronic, structural, magnetic and thermoelectric properties of NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> have been studied,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0043 #adma202101576-bib-0044 #adma202101576-bib-0045 #adma202101576-bib-0046 #adma202101576-bib-0047 #adma202101576-bib-0048 #adma202101576-bib-0049 #adma202101576-bib-0050"><sup>43–50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> but this is the first report about the electrochemical performance of this material as an anode material for SIBs. The investigation of fundamental electronic and structural changes during the Na uptake and release is a prerequisite topic to understand and improve electrochemical alkali‐metal storage properties. Thus, we performed synchrotron‐based ex situ high‐energy powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD), total‐scattering X‐ray experiments to calculate pair distribution functions (PDFs), X‐ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) and extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectroscopy during the electrochemical Na uptake and release in NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>. Additionally, the electrochemical performance and electrokinetic characteristics were analyzed via galvanostatic discharge–charge (GDC) cycling, cyclic voltammetry (CV) and galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT).</p><p>Herein, we report the discovery of an unusual nickel extrusion sodium insertion mechanism in the early stage of Na uptake in NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>: Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions can be reversibly extruded/inserted from vacancy layers followed by redox processes to form nanocrystalline fcc‐Ni and NaCrS<sub>2</sub>. This Na<sup>+</sup> shuttle via crystalline phases yields an outstanding long cycle life for 3000 cycles at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. On the other hand, higher reversible capacities can be obtained by sacrificing cycle stability if Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> cells are discharged to full conversion accompanied by irreversible decomposition of the host lattice via reduction of Cr<sup>3+</sup> ions to generate ultra‐small Cr<sup>0</sup> domains and nanocrystalline fcc‐Ni embedded in nanocrystalline Na<sub>2</sub>S.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101576-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adma202101576-sec-0030"><title type="main">Characterization of Pristine NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub></title><p>The stoichiometry of the title compound was determined with a scanning electron microscope (SEM) by energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX) spectroscopy and elemental analysis (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Note S1, Tables <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S1</link> and <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information), yielding the composition of Ni<sub>1.00(7)</sub>Cr<sub>1.95(8)</sub>S<sub>4.03(5)</sub>. xml:id="adma202101576-sec-0040"><title type="main">Cycle and Rate Performance in SIBs</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> shows selected GDC profiles recorded during the first 100 cycles applying a current rate of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (0.14C) for the first and 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (0.68C) for all subsequent cycles. During the first discharge, the voltage drops linearly to 0.9 V and subsequently to 0.3 V until about 3 Na are inserted in NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>, followed by an extensive pseudo‐plateau located between 0.3 and 0.25 V. After uptake of about 8 Na per formula unit, the voltage decreases steadily to the cut‐off potential of 0.1 V and a high total initial discharge capacity of 900 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈9.8 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>) is obtained. A capacity of 647 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈7.0 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>) is recovered in the first charge process. The irreversible capacity loss of ≈28% is caused by chemical side‐reactions and formation of a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI, see also Section <link href="#adma202101576-sec-0050">2.3</link>), which was also observed for, e.g., CuV<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0040"><sup>40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and NiCo<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0032 #adma202101576-bib-0053"><sup>32,53</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The GDC profiles are very similar in every subsequent cycle after the second one and demonstrate a highly reversible process. The cycle performance of Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> at 0.5 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> (2.71C) is shown in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002">2b</link>. Applying 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity drops by 11% from 606 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈6.6 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>) in the second to 539 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈5.9 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>) in the 100th cycle, presumably due to contact loss to the current collector resulting from volume changes, which are inevitable for conversion‐type materials especially microcrystalline compounds. Subsequently, a slight increase of capacity to 558 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈6.1 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>) is observed until cycle 200. Applying a higher current rate of 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the electrode shows a tremendous long‐life cycle performance and delivers a capacity of 489 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈5.3 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>) even after the 500th cycle, before the capacity steadily drops during subsequent cycling. The outstanding high capacities are accompanied with excellent Coulombic charge efficiencies of &gt;99% after the third cycle applying 0.5 or 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The rate capability of a Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> cell was also tested at various current rates between 0.1 and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002">2c</link>,<link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002">d</link> display selected GDC curves at various current rates and the rate performance test, respectively. The highly reversible character of the electrochemical reaction even at high current rates is demonstrated by unchanged voltage profiles during the GDC processes (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002">2c</link>). In the first cycles and at low current rates (0.1 to 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>), the capacity decreases slightly as already observed at a constant current (CC) rate of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002">2b</link>). However, the Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> cell demonstrates a superior rate‐stability especially at higher current rates delivering tremendously high capacities (<b>Table</b> <link href="#adma202101576-tbl-0002"><b>2</b></link>) and the Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> cell even retains 65% (399 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> compared to the capacity obtained at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. After high rate testing, when current density is reduced to the initial value of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> electrode delivers a capacity of 583 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a loss of only 5% resulted by the rate capability test. Hence, the Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> cell is in good condition and reveals an excellent reversibility between high and low current rates.</p><figure type="main">Sodium Storage Mechanism</title><p>Discharge and charge processes were stopped at distinct points in the first cycle as shown in GDC profile in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0003"><b>3</b>b</link> and electrodes were recovered at these points for PXRD, PDF analysis (Section <link href="#adma202101576-sec-0060">2.3.1</link>) and X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS, Section <link href="#adma202101576-sec-0070">2.3.2</link>) to elucidate the Na storage mechanism schematically shown in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0003">3a</link>. Differing capacities observed in the first cycle during electrochemical performance tests (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0002">2a</link>, ≈9.8 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> at  0.14C) and in the present curve (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0003">3b</link>, ≈8.1 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> at  0.05C) is caused by usage of polyvinylidene difluoride (PVDF) in the former tests, while the latter was recorded without PVDF. PVDF and/or additives like fluoroethylene carbonate contribute to SEI formation by decomposition to NaF in SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0011 #adma202101576-bib-0062 #adma202101576-bib-0063"><sup>11,62,63</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This is evidenced by a PXRD pattern collected for a discharged sample containing only PVDF and Super C65 carbon (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information) exhibiting mainly Bragg reflections of NaF.</p><figure xml:id="adma202101576-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202101576:adma202101576-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202101576-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the Na storage mechanism elucidated for NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> by ex situ investigations at distinct points highlighted in (b), the GDC profile of the first cycle recorded with powder cells.</caption></figure><section xml:id="adma202101576-sec-0060"><title type="main">Investigation of the Storage Mechanism with PXRD and PDF</title><p>Results of high‐energy PXRD (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>) and PDFs (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0004">4b</link>,<link href="#adma202101576-fig-0004">d</link>) allow a detailed study of changes in the short‐ and long‐range order occurring during the GDC processes. The first discharge can be divided into two regions I and II (compare Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0003">3b</link>). During uptake of 2 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> (region I) corresponding to the steady voltage drop to about 0.3 V in the GDC profile (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0003">3b</link>), Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions are reduced to fcc‐Ni (green dashed lines in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0004">4a</link>) and simultaneously a new intermediate phase is formed. Interatomic Ni–Ni distances in fcc‐Ni can be clearly assigned to appearing peaks in the PDFs for 0.5 to 2 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> (green dashed lines in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0004">4b</link>). The transformation of NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> into an intermediate phase is indicated by appearance of additional reflections, most clearly seen at 1.81° 2θ (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0004">4a</link>). measurements of the first and second cycle (see Figure <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S20</link> in the Supporting Information for cycle 1–5, Supporting Information) were performed following the method reported in<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0099"><sup>99</sup></link><sup>]</sup> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0008"><b>8</b>a</link>). A comparison to a CC test without relaxation steps applying the same current density (multiplied by a factor of 7 in time domain) clearly demonstrates that identical chemical processes occur indicated by a good agreement of electrochemical features. Evaluation of the GITT data allows the estimation of trends and changes of chemical diffusion coefficients <i>D</i><sub>chem</sub> and overpotentials during cycling, although we note that relaxation to total equilibria takes much longer than the applied 3600 s per step. The values of <i>D</i><sub>chem</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0008">8b</link>, first, second, and fifth GITT cycle) are smallest during the first discharge (black solid data points in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0008">8b</link>) between 1.0 to 0.8 V (≈3 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>) and around 0.25 V (minimum at 6 × 10<sup>−13</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>). Slow chemical diffusion at these stages of discharge result from the corresponding electrochemical processes discussed above: first Ni<sup>2+</sup> extrusion and reduction to Ni<sup>0</sup>, formation of int‐NaCrS<sub>2</sub> in region I and subsequently conversion and generation of Na<sub>2</sub>S around 0.25 V in region II. The beginning of Na insertion in NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> is accompanied by a large overpotential (inset in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0008">8b</link>). The lowest diffusion during the subsequent cycles can be observed around 1.3 V during charge with <i>D</i><sub>chem</sub> ≥ 7 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> and at 0.4 V during discharge with <i>D</i><sub>chem</sub> ≥ 8 × 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>. This decrease of diffusion velocity during charge and discharge originates from consumption and reformation of nanocrystalline Na<sub>2</sub>S as evidenced by PXRD (Section <link href="#adma202101576-sec-0060">2.3.1</link>). In these compounds Cu adopts the oxidation state +1 and in the first three compounds charge neutrality is achieved by the presence of valence band holes. Hence, the first electron transferred to the host material fills the hole, and further electrons reduce Cu<sup>+</sup>. Here we highlight that uptake of 2 Na<sup>+</sup> ions accompanied by extrusion of 1 Ni<sup>2+</sup> ion from NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> leads to a high theoretical capacity of 184.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> compared to host materials based on intercalation/deintercalation. The cycle performance test (current rates: 0.5 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009"><b>9</b>a</link>) reveals an outstanding long cycle life with a capacity retention of about 152 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈1.7 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>, 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>) after 1200 cycles and 88 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (≈1.0 Na/NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>, 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) after 3000 cycles. The GDC profiles (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S21</link>, Supporting Information) subsequently change, indicating an alteration of electrochemical reactions. PXRD and total‐scattering investigations after discharge to 0.3 V and charge to 3.0 V yield very similar results in the 50th compared to the first cycle (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009">9b</link>,<link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009">c</link>). Charge compensation for this phase may be similar to that reported for Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation of O3‐NaCrS<sub>2</sub> and O3‐NaCr<sub>2/3</sub>Ti<sub>1/3</sub>S<sub>2</sub> involving redox of S<sup>2−</sup> anions through formation of electron holes and dimerization to S<sub>2</sub><sup>2−</sup> species.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0041 #adma202101576-bib-0042"><sup>41,42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> On the other hand, some residual Ni<sup>2+</sup> ions may remain for charge compensation, i.e., Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Ni<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>/2</sub>CrS<sub>2</sub>, comparable to findings for Na/CuCrS<sub>2.</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101576-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The results of the first cycle can be summarized as follows: i) during discharge Ni<sup>2+</sup> cations are removed from the host lattice and are simultaneously reduced, ii) Na<sup>+</sup> cations replace Ni<sup>2+</sup> in the vacancy layers and layer‐like int‐NaCrS<sub>2</sub> is generated, iii) during charge NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> crystallizes, reflections of elemental Ni disappear and tiny amounts of Na<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>CrS<sub>2</sub> survive. The PDFs (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009">9c</link>) after the first and 50th cycle exhibit peaks at nearly identical positions with peak positions matching those of O3‐NaCrS<sub>2</sub> and fcc‐Ni (discharge: Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009">9c</link>, top) or of NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> (charge: Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009">9c</link>, bottom), respectively. We note that no significant differences are seen for the mid‐range order up to 50 Å (Figure <link href="#adma202101576-supitem-0001">S22</link>, Supporting Information). The PXRD and PDF investigations highlight the impressive structural reversibility particularly in the short‐ to mid‐range order during Na shuttle in the reduced voltage range and indicate a reversible nickel extrusion sodium insertion mechanism in region I (compare inset in Figure <link href="#adma202101576-fig-0009">9a</link>) described by Ni<sup>2+</sup> + 2 Na ↔ Ni<sup>0</sup> + 2 Na<sup>+</sup>. Our multi‐method approach including monitoring structural, electronic and electrokinetic alterations, allowed detailed elucidation of the Na storage mechanism and relations to the electrochemical performance: Full discharge to 0.1 V generates finally fcc‐Ni<sup>0</sup> and ultra‐small Cr<sup>0</sup> nanoparticles embedded in a nanocrystalline Na<sub>2</sub>S matrix and no crystalline products are obtained during charge. As a result, the Na<sup>+</sup> shuttle is predominantly controlled by capacitive contributions and less diffusion‐limited, thus boosted by short chemical diffusion lengths yielding an excellent rate capability and high capacity retention, e.g. a superior capacity of 489 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the 500th cycle. Limiting the potential window to 3.0–0.3 V, capacity is sacrificed for the benefit of cycle stability (100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for 3000 cycles at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and a compromise in applications must be found whether a long‐term stability or large capacity is preferred. Reducing the current density to 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> delivers a considerably high capacity of 152 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even after the 1200 cycle in the small potential window. The enormous stability can be explained by the unusual reversible nickel extrusion sodium insertion mechanism including highly crystalline phases int‐NaCrS<sub>2</sub> and NiCr<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> (Equation (<link href="#adma202101576-disp-0011">11</link>))<displayedItem xml:id="adma202101576-disp-0011" type="mathematics"><label>11</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202101576:adma202101576-math-0012" wiley:location="graphic/adma202101576-math-0012.png"><mrow><mtable><mtr><mtd><mrow><msub><mtext>NiCr</mtext><mn>2</mn></msub><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">S</mi><mn>4</mn></msub><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mn>2</mn><mtext> </mtext><msup><mtext>Na</mtext><mo>+</mo></msup><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mn>2</mn><mtext> </mtext><msup><mi mathvariant="normal">e</mi><mo>−</mo></msup><mo>⇄</mo><mtext> </mtext><mn>2</mn><mtext> </mtext><msub><mtext>NaCrS</mtext><mn>2</mn></msub><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><msup><mtext>Ni</mtext><mn>0</mn></msup></mrow></mtd></mtr></mtable></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><p>The shuttle of Li<sup>+</sup> between crystalline phases is the basis of commercial LIBs. Here we demonstrated that a similar reaction is possible for SIBs reducing the volume change during cycling compared to full conversion. The difference to electrode materials in LIBs is that Ni plays an important role occupying vacancy layer positions and generating metallic Ni nanoparticles during extrusion, which keeps the crystallites in close contact. Such materials can only be identified understanding charge storage properties, structural and electronic variations as well as the electrokinetic as a prerequisite for advanced battery development.
