Herein, B substituted Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) as stable cathode materials for Na‐ion battery is presented. A combined experimental and theoretical investigations on Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) are undertaken to reveal the evolution of crystal and electronic structures and Na storage properties associated with various concentration of B. X‐ray diffraction results indicate that the crystal structure of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3) consisted of rhombohedral Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with tiny shrinkage of crystal lattice. X‐ray absorption spectra and the calculated crystal structures all suggest that the detailed local structural distortion of substituted materials originates from the slight reduction of V–O distances. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> significantly enhances the structural stability and electrochemical performance, giving remarkable enhanced capacity of 100 and 70 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when the C‐rate increases to 5 C and 10 C. Spin‐polarized density functional theory (DFT) calculation reveals that, as compared with the pristine Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, the superior electrochemical performance of the substituted materials can be attributed to the emergence of new boundary states near the band gap, lower Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion energy barriers, and higher structure stability.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en" xml:id="advs176-abs-0001"><p><b>Electrochemical performance and local crystal strucure</b> of B substituted Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> are investigated experimentally and theoretically. Compared to pristine Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, B substituted Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> cathode offers better cycling stability and rate capability thus improves the electrochemical performance significantly.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs176:advs176-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs176-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup><note xml:id="advs176-note-0001" numbered="no"><p>The acknowledgements section of this manuscript was updated December 14th, 2016, after online publication.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body xml:id="advs176-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs176-sec-0010"><label role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are attracting a significant attention as promising alternative to dominant lithium‐ion batteries for the potential application in large‐scale grid storage and electrical vehicles due to the abundant reserves and cost advantages of Na as compared with Li.<link href="#advs176-bib-0001 #advs176-bib-0002"/> However, electrochemical performances in terms of rate capability and cycling life associated with SIBs did not come up to those of the lithium counterparts for their practical application. As compared with lithium‐ion, ionic radius of sodium‐ion is much larger, which severely restricts the ion diffusion kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> in host material, especially in cathode materials.<link href="#advs176-bib-0003 #advs176-bib-0004 #advs176-bib-0005 #advs176-bib-0006"/> Therefore, how to efficiently improve the diffusion kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> is a significant issue for development of high performance electrode materials for SIBs.</p><p>In the past few years, many compounds including layer‐structured oxide and polyanionic phosphates have been investigated to find a suitable cathode material for reversible and rapid intercalation/deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#advs176-bib-0007 #advs176-bib-0008 #advs176-bib-0009 #advs176-bib-0010 #advs176-bib-0011 #advs176-bib-0012 #advs176-bib-0013 #advs176-bib-0014"/> Among them, sodium super ion conductor (NASICON) structured Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with 3D open framework and large tunnels for Na<sup>+</sup> migration has aroused an extensive interest as cathode materials for SIBs owing to its superiority of good stability, moderate potential plateau, high energy density, and so on.<link href="#advs176-bib-0014 #advs176-bib-0015 #advs176-bib-0016 #advs176-bib-0017 #advs176-bib-0018"/> However, the poor intrinsic electronic conductivity and structural stability of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> severely retard the cycling stability and rate capability of electrode in its practical application. To overcome such fundamental demerits, several strategies, including coating various carbon materials, reducing the particle size to nanoscale, and doping with the foreign ion, have been widely proposed in literature.<link href="#advs176-bib-0019 #advs176-bib-0020 #advs176-bib-0021 #advs176-bib-0022 #advs176-bib-0023"/></p><p>Ion‐doping can be considered as an effective way to enhance the electronic conductivity of electrode material for improving the electrochemical performance of lithium and/or sodium ion batteries. Various metallic ions with different valence states and ionic sizes (such as K<sup>+</sup>, Fe<sup>2+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, and so on)<link href="#advs176-bib-0023 #advs176-bib-0024 #advs176-bib-0025"/> were introduced to partly substitute Na and/or V of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> units, and hence a modified Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with high capacity and excellent rate property can be achieved. So far, research on nonmetal substitution of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> at P‐site and/or O‐site is rare, except some cases on Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3–</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>F<sub>3</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>, in which F atoms were utilized to replace the whole PO<sub>4</sub><sup>3−</sup>.<link href="#advs176-bib-0026 #advs176-bib-0027 #advs176-bib-0028"/> Actually, the strategy of doping nonmetal element in polyanion cathode materials of LIBs to improve the electrochemical property has been studied.<link href="#advs176-bib-0029 #advs176-bib-0030 #advs176-bib-0031 #advs176-bib-0032 #advs176-bib-0033 #advs176-bib-0034"/> For example, Sin et al. reported that boron polyanion substituted LiFe<sub>0.4</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> showed a high initial capacity and low over potential during cycling.<link href="#advs176-bib-0035"/> Similar enhancement of electrochemical performance can also be observed in olivine LiMnPO<sub>4</sub> and LiFePO<sub>4</sub> cathode, NASICON‐type Li<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode and solid‐state electrolyte LiTi<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with an optimum amount of B.<link href="#advs176-bib-0031 #advs176-bib-0036"/> They believed that replacement of B not only introduces the p‐type conductivity, but also significantly increases Li<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics and structure stability.</p><p>Inspired by the positive effects of B doped compounds with enhancement of electrochemical performance in LIBs, we herein presented B substituted Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) as cathode materials for SIB. A combined experimental and theoretical study on Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) were undertaken to reveal the evolution of crystal and electronic structure and Na‐ion diffusion properties associated with various amount of B. Electrochemical properties of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> were optimized by adjusting B contents. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> shows an obvious enhancement in cycling stability, fast rate capability, and Na‐ion diffusion kinetics. DFT calculations revealed that the enhancement of Na<sup>+</sup> migration derives from the optimized crystal structure and the decreases of the bad gap are due to the appearance of the new bound states by B substitution.</p></section><section xml:id="advs176-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Phase purity of the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤1) with various B concentrations was examined by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs176-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>, A series of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub>(0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3) with various amount of B can be observed in the rhombohedral crystal structure, which could be well‐indexed into the NASICON Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with R‐3c space group, indicating the substituted B in the range of 0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3 did not change the crystal framework of parent material. Moreover, the continuous shift of peak position to higher angle with increased <i>x</i> in Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3) implies a solid solution over this composition range, and a shrink of crystal size affected by the substitution of B. This observation is in agreement with previous report concerning variations of crystal volume of the B doped Li<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="advs176-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs176:advs176-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs176-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (x = 0, 1/10, 1/6, 1/3) powders, b) magnified view, and c) calculated crystal structure of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (<i>x</i> = 1/6).</caption></figure><p>Moreover, the half‐height width of the peak changes to broaden with the increasing of B contents. The average crystallite sizes of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<i><sub>3–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> with x = 0, 1/10, 1/6, and 1/3 calculated by Scherrer's equation are 205, 110, 80, and 64 nm, respectively, indicate that doping B can prevent the growth of nanoparticles, which is favorable for improving the kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in bulk materials. Nevertheless, substantial changes in the B substitution in a Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) procure the formation of impurities of Na<sub>2</sub>V(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub> while <i>x</i> &gt; 1/2 (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The particle size and morphologies of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub>(0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3) were explored by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Figure S2, Supporting Information), showing sphere‐like morphologies with similar particle size range of 0.5–2 μm. The high resolution transmission electron microscope (HR‐TEM) images (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs176-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>) of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> with <i>x</i> = 0 and <i>x</i> = 1/6 shows that both particles are coated uniformly by carbon layer with around 3 nm. It is believed that modification with carbon could endow the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> higher electronic conductivity. Interplanar spacing of 3.7 Å corresponded to the (113) planes of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="advs176-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs176:advs176-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs176-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>HR‐TEM images of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub>: a) <i>x</i> = 0 and b) <i>x</i> = 1/6.</caption></figure><p>According to the reports on the ion doping, the incorporation of impurity is more likely to replace those ions which have the similar valence states and the closer ionic radii.<link href="#advs176-bib-0037"/> Therefore, the boron prefers to substitute phosphorus or vanadium. To explore the effect of B doped Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, a spin‐polarized density functional theory (DFT) calculation was performed to investigate the crystal structure change while phosphorus or vanadium is replaced by boron. Figure <link href="#advs176-fig-0001"/>c shows the lowest total energy configuration of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub>, in which boron atom occupies the tetrahedral interstitial site. It can be clearly observed that a small amount of boron doping does not alter the framework of the material (Figure S3, Supporting Information). The corresponding lattice constants before and after doping are listed in Table S1 in the Supporting Information. The changes in the lattice parameter of the calculated Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> with reference to the experimental value are less than 2%. The optimization results show that the lattice constant of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3–x</sub>B<sub>x</sub>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3) decreases with the increase of boron concentration and a slight deformation of initial rhombohedral structure, which is owing to the smaller ion radius of B<sup>3+</sup> (0.27 Å) relative to P<sup>5+</sup> (0.38 Å). This is in good agreement with the XRD results. In addition, due to the weaker Coulomb interaction, the strength of the BO bond is weaker than that of the PO bond which can provide larger Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion channel. However, the lattice parameter <i>c</i> elongates and volume expands significantly when the boron substitutes vanadium site, which are not conform to the experimental results, indicating that boron prefers to substitute for phosphorus.</p><p>To identify the local structural change and enhance the comprehension of the shrink of crystal volume affected by B substitute, X‐ray absorption spectra including X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure (XANES) and extend X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) were carried out. According to XANES (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs176-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a) at V K‐edge, the edge of B substituted sample shifts to higher energy compared to pristine sample, indicating a higher oxidation state of V in Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>‐</sub></i><sub>1/3</sub>B<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>12</sub>, which is consistent with the fact that replacing P<sup>5+</sup> with aliovalent ion B<sup>3+</sup> accompanies partly charge compensation of V in the compound. X‐ray photoelectron spectroscope (XPS) of V 2p further confirms the formation of V<sup>4+</sup> (Figure S4, Supporting Information) and demonstrates that boron successfully a substitute for phosphorus as expected. As Park et al. noted,<link href="#advs176-bib-0038"/> V<sup>4+</sup> usually exhibits a short vanadyl VO bond length, which trends toward a distorted VO<sub>6</sub> octahedron. XANES spectra at V K‐edge reveal the local environment surrounding V atom. The variation of peak intensity for the samples may be caused by the distortion of VO<sub>6</sub> octahedron and other local structural changes after incorporation of B in crystal. Moreover, the V–O distances of the B substituted sample Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/3</sub>B<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>12</sub> and pristine Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> determined from the EXAFS data (Figure <link href="#advs176-fig-0003"/>b) was 1.964 Å and 1.971 Å, suggesting that V–O distances were slightly reduced by substitution of B.</p><figure xml:id="advs176-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs176:advs176-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs176-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XANES and b) Fourier transformed EXAFS spectra of the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (<i>x</i> = 0 and <i>x</i> = 1/3). In addition, as compared with the undoped samples, the V1O1 and V3O3 bond length of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> is significantly shortened, while other VO bond length only slight shrinkage (Table S2, Supporting Information), showing the same tendency with the experimental results. For the pure Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> or NaV<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, the calculated local magnetic moments of V ion are the same (1.98 or 1.103 μ<sub>B</sub>). Therefore, the strong Coulomb interaction leads to the shrinkage of V1‐O1 and V3‐O3 when the oxidation state of V1 and V3 changes from V<sup>3+</sup> to V<sup>4+</sup>. The change of local element valence induces the adjacent polyhedron geometry distortion.</p><p>The effects of substitution on intrinsic electronic conductivity were explored by calculating the density of state of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub>. The spin up density of states (DOS) of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and the projected DOS of atoms as labeled in Figure <link href="#advs176-fig-0003"/>c are displayed in Figure S5 in the Supporting Information. It clearly shows that Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> is a semiconductor with a large band gap of 2.57 eV and the high energy occupied regions in valence band are mainly ascribed to the V‐3d states with little contribution of O‐2p states, while the low energy occupied regions in conduction band are mainly ascribed to the O‐2p states with little contribution of V‐3d states, which are in agreement with the other theoretical calculation result using HSE06 hybrid function.<link href="#advs176-bib-0039"/> All the V‐3d states and O‐2p states are nearly the same. In order to identify the possible electron conduction mechanism after B doping Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, the electronic structures of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (<i>x</i> = 0, 1/6, 1/3) were calculated. The band gap of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (as listed in Table S1 in the Supporting Information) decreases with the increase of boron doping due to the occurrence of new boundary states, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs176-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a. However, although the band gap of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> (<i>E</i><sub>g</sub> = 1.633 eV) is significantly narrower than Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, the change is not obvious when continuing to increase the amount of doping. The partial spin up density of states of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> as presented in Figure <link href="#advs176-fig-0004"/>b illustrates that the new energy states in the band gap near the bottom of conduction band are mainly ascribed to the 3d states of V1 and V3 atoms, while those near the top of the valence band are mainly ascribed to the 3d states of V2 and V4 atoms. There are two holes when B<sup>3+</sup> substituted P<sup>5+</sup>, which may be transferred to the vicinity of the V1 and V3 atoms and induce the new energy states near the bottom of conduction band. Then the oxidation state of V1 and V3 changes from V<sup>3+</sup> to V<sup>4+</sup>, resulting the VO<sub>6</sub> octahedral distortion. The change of the local structure also affects the electronic structure of the adjacent atoms, such as V2 and V4, and the appearance of the new energy states may improve the electrical properties of the materials.</p><figure xml:id="advs176-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs176:advs176-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs176-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Calculated a) total density of states (DOS) of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (x = 0, 1/6, 1/3) and b) partial spin up density of states of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub>. The XRD and XAS characterizations demonstrated that B doping did not change the crystal framework of parent material in the range of 0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1/3, but aroused the local structure distortion with tiny shrink of crystal lattice. Confirmed from the results of DFT calculation, doping of B in material could change local element valence, resulting in the adjacent polyhedron geometry distortion, which narrow the band gap and facilitate the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup>. B substituted Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> significantly enhacnes the structure stability and electrochemical performance. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> exhibits the best cycling stability and rate capability. The capacity retention of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>12</sub> (98.7%) is much higher than that of pristine materials (91.8%) after 200 cycles at 5 C.</p></section><section xml:id="advs176-sec-0040"><label role="interim">4</label><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Computation Methods</i>: First‐principles spin‐polarized density functional theory (DFT) calculations<link href="#advs176-bib-0041"/> have been performed to investigate the Boron doping to the NASICON structured Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode materials using the Vienna ab initio simulation package<link href="#advs176-bib-0042"/> with the projector augmented waves (PAW) pseudopotentials<link href="#advs176-bib-0043"/> and the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof (PBE) exchange‐correlation functional.<link href="#advs176-bib-0044"/> The plane‐wave basis set was determined with a cutoff energy of 520 eV and the k‐point sampling of Brillouin‐zone integrals used a 2 × 2 × 1 Gamma grid. The simplified rotationally invariant approach introduced by Dudarev<link href="#advs176-bib-0045"/> to the Hubbard model corrections was used with a U‐J parameter of 4.2 eV for vanadium atom, whereas the correction was not used for the other species. The U value of vanadium is a little higher than the experimental formation enthalpy of vanadium oxides as there are high inductive effects in vanadium phosphates.<link href="#advs176-bib-0046"/> The calculations were performed in a rhombohedral chemical cell consisting 6‐f.u. Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and a sodium‐ordered form was chosen in our models which had been demonstrated in other literatures.<link href="#advs176-bib-0047 #advs176-bib-0048"/> Different boron substitution positions of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (<i>x</i> = 1/6 and <i>x</i> = 2/6) and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2‐1/6</sub>B<sub>1/6</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> were calculated, and the lowest energy structure was selected. The Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion hopping barriers were obtained using the climbing image nudged elastic band (CINEB) method, which can determine the pathway for structure changes efficiently.</p><p><i>Sample Preparation</i>: The NASICON structured Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) was synthesized by a sol–gel process and followed by solid‐state reaction. Typically, 2 mmol NH<sub>4</sub>VO<sub>3</sub> and 4 mmol citric acid were added to 70 mL deionized water maintaining at 80 °C with continuous stirring to obtain a clear solution, and then stoichiometric amount of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NH<sub>4</sub>H<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>, and H<sub>3</sub>BO<sub>3</sub> were added. After evaporation of water at 80 °C, the solution transforms from sol to gel. The gel was dried in an oven at 150 °C for 4 h, and heat‐treated at 400 °C for 5 h under nitrogen atmosphere to remove CO<sub>2</sub>, H<sub>2</sub>O, and NH<sub>3</sub>. Afterward, the powder was grounded and annealed at 800 °C under Ar flow for 12 h to produce the final compound.</p><p><i>Structure Characterization</i>: X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the as prepared samples were collected on a Bruker D8 diffractometer using Cu Kα radiation. The morphology of the materials was observed by Hitachi S‐4800 field emission scanning electron microscope (FE‐SEM). High resolution transmission electron microscope (HR‐TEM) images were taken on a JEOLJEM‐2010F microscope (JEOL, Japan) at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) was measured at the V k‐edge, at 12‐BM at the advanced photon source (APS), Argonne national laboratory.</p><p><i>Electrochemical Characterization</i>: The work electrode was prepared by coating a mixture of 80 wt% Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>P<sub>3</sub><i><sub>–x</sub></i>B<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>12</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1), 10 wt% PVDF and 10 wt% Super P onto Al current collector. The electrode was then dried at 120 °C in vacuum for 12 h. The test cells were assembled into coin cell in an argon‐filled glovebox. Sodium metal foil was used as the counter electrode and a glass fiber filter (Whatman) was used as the separator. A 1 <sc>m</sc> solution of NaClO<sub>4</sub> in EC/DMC (1:1 by volume) was prepared and used as the electrolyte. The charge/discharge, C‐rate capacity and cycling ability of cells were recorded on a LAND battery test system. Cyclic voltammograms (CVs) were performed using a CHI 440A instrument (CHI Instrument Inc.) at a scanning rate from 0.2 to 0.6 mV s<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="advs176-sec-0050" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>P.H and X.F.W. contributed equally to this work.
