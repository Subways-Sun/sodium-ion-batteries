xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Metals such as Sb and Bi are important anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries because they feature a large capacity and low reaction potential. However, the accumulation of stress and strain upon sodium storage leads to the formation of cracks and fractures, resulting in electrode failure upon extended cycling. In this work, the design and construction of Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub> bimetallic alloy films with a compositional gradient to mitigate the intrinsic structural instability is reported. In the gradient film, the top is rich in Sb, contributing to the capacity, while the bottom is rich in Bi, helping to reduce the stress in the interphase between the film and the substrate. Significantly, this gradient film affords a high reversible capacity of ≈500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and sustains 82% of the initial capacity after 1000 cycles at 2 C, drastically outperforming the solid‐solution counterpart and many recently reported alloy anodes. Such a gradient design can open up the possibilities to engineering high‐capacity anode materials that are structurally unstable due to the huge volume variation upon energy storage.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs3877-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The design of structurally durable bimetallic alloy films with a compositional gradient is demonstrated to mitigate the intrinsic structural instability. This gradient film features a high density, a high capacity of ≈500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and sustains 82% of the initial capacity upon 1000 cycles at 2 C, drastically outperforming the solid‐solution counterpart and many alloy anodes.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3877-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Metals such as Sn, Sb, and Bi are important for sodium‐ion batteries because they are capable of accommodating a large amount of Na ions through a reversible (de)alloying process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among these alloy anodes, Sb is considered a particularly competitive option because it offers a high capacity of 660 or 4400 mA h cm<sup>−3</sup> (to form Na<sub>3</sub>Sb) at an average potential of 0.5 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This alloying potential avoids the possibility of sodium plating but does not significantly compromise the working voltage and therefore the energy of full cells. A major challenge lies with the huge volume variation, which could be up to 296% upon the accumulation of Na ions. Such an expansion induces large stress and strain inside electrodes, thus resulting in possible electrode fracture and loss of electrical connection.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a result, cycling instability has been commonly reported in Sb anodes.</p><p>Given this challenge, two common engineering strategies, i.e., incorporating a carbon matrix and designing a porous structure, have been proposed. Carbon is a common material to buffer the volume swelling of alloying electrodes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> while the pores provide additional space to accommodate the volume expansion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> While both designs help to minimize or even diminish the cumulative stress within the Sb electrode, they inevitably lead to a reduction in the volumetric capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, introducing a less swelling element to construct bimetallic alloys that represents another plausible approach. The introduced element can be either Na inactive or Na active. In the former, inactive components, such as Cu, Ni, Zn, serve as a conductive framework to buffer swelling upon Na addition.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the latter scenario, the introduced element is electrochemically active to Na but exhibits a lower degree of volume variation. A representative example is Bi, which undergoes a swelling rate of 223% when Bi is transformed into Na<sub>3</sub>Bi (Table <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To date, this design has been extensively implemented, as Bi and Sb share a similar structure and are miscible at any proportion in principle.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For instance, Gao et al. reported a Bi<sub>2</sub>Sb<sub>6</sub> alloy anode that retained a capacity of 429 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 100 cycles and 258 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 2000 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Previously, our group reported a durian‐like Sb<sub>0.25</sub>Bi<sub>0.75</sub> alloy electrode achieving capacity retention of 284 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 2000 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is noted that a high degree of stability has only been attainable in the high‐Bi‐ratio phases (low capacity) of Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub>; the high‐Sb‐ratio phases, however, are still plagued by the electrochemical instability caused by the large strain and stress developed.</p><p>One plausible way to bypass this dilemma is to engineer a compositional gradient. In this structure, the perfect combination of multiple functional components at the nanoscale offers a possibility to regulate the dissipation of the stress induced by the volume variation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, electrode integrity could be principally preserved regardless of swelling. The conceptual design of gradient structures has been previously pursued in lithium‐ion batteries,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> photoelectrochemical devices,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and electrocatalysis.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A case in point is that Sun et al. proposed a compositional gradient structure in the outer layer of Ni‐Mn‐Co oxide particles, which offer high energy and superior thermal stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recently, a full concentration‐gradient design has been demonstrated, endowing lithium‐rich oxide with excellent voltage retention and rate capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To the best of our knowledge, however, the gradient design has not been well fulfilled in sodium alloy anodes.</p><p>In this work, we demonstrate that electrochemical stability can be readily realized in Sb‐rich alloy anodes of compositional gradient Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> (G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>) films without further engineering. This gradient Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> film was fabricated by controlled electrodeposition from solutions of varying Bi:Sb ratios. A gradient variation in the concentration of Bi and Sb is presented in the vertical direction from the film bottom to the top. The top is rich in the high‐capacity component of Sb, which contributes to the majority of the capacity, while the bottom is rich in the low‐capacity Bi, which helps to reduce the stress in the interphase between the film and the substrate. Compared with the solid solution of Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> (S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>), the gradient design significantly reduces the stress developed within the electrode film upon sodiation, as confirmed by finite element analysis (FEA). Electrochemical (de)sodiation test results show that G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> retains a stable capacity of 328 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at a rate of 0.5 C (1 C is arbitrarily taken as 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). It also displays durable sodium storage at a higher rate of 2 C, thereby demonstrating its great potential in sodium‐ion batteries for renewable and grid storage.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The failure of sodium‐ion batteries is often caused by electrode fracture as a result of the accumulation of stress and strain during electrochemical cycling. Upon the uptake and release of Na<sup>+</sup> ions, alloy anodes will accordingly experience volume expansion and shrinkage. Once the stress induced by the volume variation surpasses the elastic limit, the electrode structure will undergo permanent deformation and even cracks and fractures. On the one hand, some fractures will lose electrical connection with the current collector, making them inactive in the sequential process. On the other hand, electrode fracturing leads to a fresh surface and continuous generation and growth of the solid‐electrolyte interface (SEI), resulting in augmented resistance and sodium consumption. As this process continues, the failure of electrodes and batteries will eventually occur. Hence, efficient reduction and dissipation of stress are critically desirable while designing durable and sustainable battery electrodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The design of gradient structures could dissipate the accumulative stress within electrodes. To unveil the effectiveness of such a design, FEA was implemented on varied sodiation stages for both gradient structure and solid solution. The simulated results of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> and S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> are presented in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3877-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a,b</link>, respectively. In general, with the storage of Na<sup>+</sup> ions (sodiation), stress is continuously developed in both electrodes as a direct result of volume swelling. A close observation reveals two distinct features related to stress evolution. First, stress is developed and accumulated preferentially on the electrode bottom. This is because the swelling of the film bottom, not the surface, is primarily restricted by the current collector. Second, the stress gathered in G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> is consistently lower than that in S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>, regardless of the sodiation state. This can be explained by the unique composition gradient where less‐swelling Bi is enriched at the bottom of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>. Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0001">1c</link> illustrates the cross‐sectional stress distribution in both electrodes. The maximal stress arising in G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> is 35% less than that in S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> at the same sodiation stages (Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Remarkably, the accumulation of stress near the bottom is much slower in G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> versus in S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>, thus more effectively preventing the former from possible cracking and fracturing. Therefore, designing a compositional gradient could theoretically address the instability issue relative to bimetallic alloy anodes.</p><figure xml:id="advs3877-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3877-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Stress distribution in the solid solution or the gradient structure of bimetallic alloy films simulated by FEA. Stress profiles at different sodiation voltages for a) S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> and b) G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>. c) Comparison of the geometric distribution of stress from the bottom to the top for both samples. The electrode thickness is set at 600 nm.</caption></figure><p>Guided by the simulation results, we constructed gradient films of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> by galvanostatic electrodeposition. As schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S2</link> (Supporting Information), a compositional gradient was realized involving varying Bi:Sb ratios in the electrolyte. The initial electrolyte consists of 50 mM Bi<sup>3+</sup> and Sb<sup>3+</sup>. With the processing of deposition, a 50 mM solution of Sb<sup>3+</sup> was continuously poured into the electrolyte, leading to gradual dilution of Bi<sup>3+</sup> species. The concentration of Sb remains unchanged because of the supply of Sb<sup>3+</sup> cations. Such a variation in the Bi:Sb ratio leads to the generation of compositional gradients. Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images reveal a dense film deposited on Cu foil with a rough surface for the as‐deposited films (Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). The film, ≈630 nm in thickness, contains 33% Bi and 67% Sb as a whole, as determined by inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry. As a comparison, solid‐solution films were deposited from an electrolyte of 25 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc> Bi<sup>3+</sup> and 50× 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc> Sb<sup>3+</sup>.</p><p>After mild annealing at 200 °C, the gradient film retains its original morphology (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3877-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). To verify the gradient structure, we conducted compositional analysis by energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS). The composition was recorded at equidistant positions in the cross‐section of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0002">2b,c</link>). The results present a vertical gradient distribution of Bi and Sb from the film bottom to the top. The bottom is rich in Bi, and the top is rich in Sb. Both elements exhibit a gradient and reciprocal transition in the film. This gradient is closely linked with the variation in ion concentrations and electrode potentials during deposition. In the initial stage, the phase deposited from the solution of 50 mM Bi and Sb is dominated by Bi because the standard electrode potential of Bi<sup>3+</sup>/Bi (0.32 V vs SHE) is more positive than that of Sb<sup>3+</sup>/Sb (0.21 V vs SHE).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> With the processing of deposition, the dilution of electrolyte Bi species results in a gradual shift of the redox potential of Bi<sup>3+</sup>/Bi toward lower values. In contrast, the electrolyte concentration of Sb remains unchanged because of the continuous replenishment of the Sb<sup>3+</sup> solution. When the redox of Bi<sup>3+</sup>/Bi is more negative than that of Sb<sup>3+</sup>/Sb in the electrolyte, the deposition of Sb species would be dominant. As a result, a vertical gradient structure of Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub> is created. If no solution of Sb<sup>3+</sup> flows into the electrolyte, both Bi and Sb will be consumed, and a solid solution of Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub> will be deposited. As seen in Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information, the film deposited from an electrolyte of 25× 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc> Bi and 50× 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc> Sb<sup>3+</sup> discloses a solid solution with a ratio of 33% Bi and 67% Sb. In addition, the atomic composition of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> films with depth was inspected by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) combined with Ar<sup>+</sup> ion etching. As presented in Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0002">2d–f</link>, the signal intensity of Sb gradually decreases with etching, while that of Bi correspondingly increases. Again, the compositional gradient is detected for both Bi and Sb, thus validating the construction of gradient films of Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="advs3877-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3877-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Morphology and compositional gradient of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>. a) Top‐view and b) cross‐sectional SEM images. The numbers in the cross section indicate the sites where the EDS signals were recorded. c) Atomic ratios of Bi and Sb showing the compositional gradient. Data acquisition by EDS was repeated three times. Core‐level XPS spectra of d) Bi 4f and e) Sb 3d recorded at different depths by Ar<sup>+</sup> ion etching. f) Atomic ratio of Bi and Sb by XPS, again showing the compositional gradient.</caption></figure><p>The gradient structure was further corroborated by high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images observed at distinct sites of the film. The TEM images in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a–c</link> reveal a good crystalline state with lattice fringes corresponding to the (012) plane of Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub> alloys.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The interplanar spacings show some variation, ranging from 0.313 (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003">3a</link>) to 0.318 nm (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003">3b</link>) and 0.325 nm (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003">3c</link>). As the spacings are in the midst of those of Sb (0.310 nm) and Bi (0.328 nm), this variation could be used to support the composition gradation in the film. In the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern in Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003">3d</link>, the G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> film can be indexed into a hexagonal structure with the space group of R<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/advs3877-math-0001.png"><semantics><mover accent="true"><mn>3</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">$\bar{3}$</annotation></semantics></math>m. Two additional diffraction peaks at 24.7° and 26.1° can be assigned to Cu<sub>3</sub>Sb (PDF #11‐0072), which functions as a metal glue to consolidate the film and the substrate.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, XPS was adopted to probe the surface state of the gradient film (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003">3e,f</link>). Both metal elements exhibit two sets of doublets, which can be attributed to trivalent and metallic Bi (or Sb), respectively. The appearance of Bi<sup>3+</sup> and Sb<sup>3+</sup> is a direct result of the exposure of the film in the ambient atmosphere, as corroborated by the abnormal signal intensity of O 1s in Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0003">3f</link>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Interestingly, the portion of Sb<sup>3+</sup> in Sb species is overwhelmingly greater than that of Bi<sup>3+</sup>, suggesting that the former is more sensitive to oxidation.</p><figure xml:id="advs3877-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3877-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural analysis of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>. a–c) High‐resolution TEM images showing <i>d</i>‐spacings of a) 0.313 nm, b) 0.318 nm and c) 0.325 nm, correlating to the (012) plane of the hexagonal alloy phase with varying Bi:Sb ratios. d) XRD pattern. Core‐level XPS spectra of e) Bi 4f and f) Sb 3d.</caption></figure><p>To unravel the superiority of the gradient structure, sodium storage of both Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> films was electrochemically evaluated by galvanostatic charge and discharge (GCD) and cyclic voltammetry (CV). <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3877-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link> compares the GCD curves of Na half cells in the third cycle. G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> affords a higher reversible capacity of 447 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> versus S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>, which only affords 412 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Remarkably, both films present a high sodium activity in the initial stage, exhibiting a high desodiation capacity of up to ≈500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). However, the solid solution undergoes a more significant irreversible loss and capacity decay during the initial cycling, possibly caused by the larger stress and strain. In the CV profile shown in Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0004">4b</link>, three cathodic peaks located at 0.48, 0.39, and 0.24 V represent stepwise alloying with Na.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The extra peak at 0.67 V might be attributed to the formation of an SEI, which causes initial capacity loss. By analogy, desodiation in Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub> films proceeds stepwise. The two anodic peaks at 0.61 and 0.77 V reflect the two‐step dealloying of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub> to Na(Bi, Sb) and Bi<sub>x</sub>Sb<sub>1−x</sub>, in good agreement with previous reports.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0010 #advs3877-bib-0011"><sup>10,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="advs3877-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3877-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of bimetallic alloy films for Na<sup>+</sup> storage. Comparison of a) typical GCD and b) CV curves of both films<sub>.</sub> c) Electrochemical cycling of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> at a rate of 0.5 C. d) Comparison of electrochemical cycling of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> and S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> at a rate of 2 C. This assumption has been well manifested by the superior cycling stability of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>, as shown in Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0004">4c–e</link>. The gradient film affords 328 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at a rate of 0.5 C, and 253 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles at a high rate of 2 C. The capacity decay per cycle is only 0.018%. Electrode durability at higher rates (such as 5 C) and extended cycles (2000 cycles) is also attainable (Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). In contrast, the solid‐solution counterpart only sustains ≈250 cycles, after which it experiences a capacity plummet. Moreover, G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> shows reproducible GCD curves at various cycling stages, highlighting the acquisition of electrode stability through such a unique gradient design. Remarkably, this gradient film electrode is fully comparable or even outperform some nanoscale Bi and Sb anodes (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0004">4f</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Also, our dense film electrodes feature a much higher packing density (6.8 g cm<sup>−3</sup>) versus those of composite electrodes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which is essential and unique for microbatteries to power microelectronics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To monitor the evolution of electrodes upon sodium cycling, cross‐sectional SEM imaging was conducted on the electrode at various cycling stages. While G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> almost retains the original state regardless of cycling (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3877-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a–c</link>), S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> reveals substantial evolution in the film thickness and morphology (Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0005">5d–f</link>). The augmentation of the thickness of S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> is accompanied by the generation of pores and fractures. Delamination of the film from the substrate is also visible, which is believed to be responsible for the sudden capacity drop observed in Figure <link href="#advs3877-fig-0004">4d</link>. This result indicates that the gradient structure could effectively dissipate the stress and thus avoid possible electrode failure. In addition, XRD analysis reveals that no evident structural change occurs, even after 250 continuous cycles (Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information). Furthermore, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy confirms the electrode durability during cycling (Figure <link href="#advs3877-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). While the impedance spectrum of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> shows little evolution, that of S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> constantly evolves with cycling. The marked evolution of charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) could be linked with the formation of pores and fractures that facilitate the permeation of electrolyte ions in S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>. In addition, the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> of G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> is consistently less than that of S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub>, implying that the former is more efficient for the charge transfer reaction.</p><figure xml:id="advs3877-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3877:advs3877-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3877-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Comparison of electrode evolution upon electrochemical cycling. In the gradient structure, the film top is rich in Sb, contributing to the capacity, while the bottom is rich in Bi, helping to reduce the stress developed in the interphase between the film and the substrate. Significantly, the gradient film affords a capacity of ≈500 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and sustains 82% of the initial capacity over 1000 cycles at 2 C, drastically outperforming the solid‐solution counterpart and many reported alloy anodes. Such superior stability could be correlated to the specific material design where the accumulation of the stress inside the film could be essentially suppressed and dissipated. The gradient film also exhibits excellent rate capability, affording 319 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a high rate of 10 C. Significantly, such films feature a high packing density of 6.8 g cm<sup>−3</sup>, which is essential and unique for microbatteries to power microelectronics. We believe that the gradient design could open up a broad avenue to engineering high‐capacity anode materials that are plagued by the poor structural integrity caused by the huge volume variation upon energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> Films</title><p>G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> film electrodes were directly grown on Cu foil through galvanostatic electrodeposition. Prior to deposition, Cu foil (0.1 mm, 99%) was ultrasonically cleaned in oxalic acid (1 <sc>m</sc>), deionized water, and ethanol, respectively. Two electrolytes were prepared for electrodeposition. Electrolyte <b>A</b> consists of BiCl<sub>3</sub> (50 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>), SbCl<sub>3</sub> (50 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>), H<sub>3</sub>BO<sub>3</sub> (60 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>), and C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>10</sub>Cl<sub>2</sub>N<sub>2</sub> (240 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>) dissolved in a mixture solvent of ultrapure water (20 mL), ethanol (30 mL), ethylene glycol (50 mL), and HCl (3 mL). Electrolyte <b>B</b> has a similar composition without BiCl<sub>3</sub>. Electrodeposition was carried out in a three‐electrode setup in electrolyte <b>A</b> with Ag/AgCl as the reference electrode and platinum gauze as the counter electrode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3877-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During deposition, electrolyte <b>B</b> was continuously fed into electrolyte <b>A</b>. The gradual dilution of Bi<sup>3+</sup> cations in the electrolyte led to the formation of a compositional gradient. G‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> films were electrodeposited at a current of 5.7 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for 200 s with constant stirring. The mass loading of the film is 0.43 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. As a comparison, S‐Bi<sub>0.33</sub>Sb<sub>0.67</sub> films were electrodeposited on Cu foil from an electrolyte of 25 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc> Bi<sup>3+</sup> and 50 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc> Sb<sup>3+</sup>. The resulting films were thoroughly washed with water and ethanol, dried, and finally annealed at 200 °C for 60 min in a tube furnace under argon flow.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0080" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This research was supported by grants from the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 52172219, 52025028, 51872192, 51772197), the Natural Science Foundation of Jiangsu Province (Grant No. BK20180002), the Natural Science Foundation of the Jiangsu Higher Education Institutions of China (Grant No. 19KJA170001), and the Priority Academic Program Development (PAPD) of Jiangsu Higher Education Institutions.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0090" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0100" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3877-sec-0110" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="advs3877-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="advs3877-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="advs3877-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>J.
