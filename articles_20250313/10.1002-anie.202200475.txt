The commercial hard carbon demonstrates superior rate performance with 212 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at an ultra‐high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the electrolyte with weak ion solvation/desolvation, which is much higher than those in common electrolytes (nearly no capacity in carbonate‐based electrolytes). sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The rapid development and growth of electrochemical energy storage in our society calls for next‐generation techniques that combine high energy, high power and low cost.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Various post‐lithium‐ion batteries such as sodium/magnesium/zinc/potassium‐ion batteries have been intensively investigated due to their unique advantages in resource, cost and safety.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0005"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Among them, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted growing attention and been regarded as the promising supplement of lithium‐ion batteries in terms of energy storage due to the low cost and naturally abundant Na resources.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0016"><sup>[3]</sup></link> However, the energy market requires continued improvement in energy density and sustained high power discharge at high specific energy and low temperature.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0019"><sup>[4]</sup></link> The imbalance between energy and power density in SIBs is inherently attributed to redox reaction energy and charge transport kinetic limitations, which are even more severe at the anode because of the large ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> and the resistance of the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI).<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0023"><sup>[5]</sup></link></p><p>During the past few decades, a huge amount of experimental and theoretical research has focused on the micro‐nano structure design to accelerate the redox reaction rate of electrode materials via enhancing the ion/electron conductivity in second‐ion batteries, thus boosting their electrochemical performance.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0004 #anie202200475-bib-0027"><sup>[1c, 6]</sup></link> However, other potential factors that may be beneficial to the further enhancement of performance were always ignored. As well known, in addition to the redox reaction rate at the electrode, the rate capability of the anode is greatly dependent on the charge transfer process from electrolyte to anode, which involves the ion solvation/desolvation at the SEI, ion transport through the SEI, ion–solvent diffusion and so on.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0024 #anie202200475-bib-0034"><sup>[5a, 7]</sup></link> However, which is the most limited process has not been well elaborated. For instance, Xu's group proposed that ion–solvent desolvation was the most sluggish process among these multiple steps.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0037"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Y.‐M. Chiang and his group pointed out that the ion transport at the cathode–electrolyte interface which greatly depends on salt solvation is much slower than that at the bulk material.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0026"><sup>[5c]</sup></link> T. R. Jow found that ion charge transfer kinetics is mostly dominated by the SEI and only in the case when the SEI is very conductive and of low activation energy for Li<sup>+</sup> conduction, is the Li<sup>+</sup> charge transfer kinetics dominated by the desolvation process.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0036"><sup>[7b]</sup></link> Although the charge transfer process may vary for different electrode material systems, the electrode–electrolyte interfacial chemistry, including the ion solvation/desolvation at the SEI/electrolyte interface and ion transport through SEI, should be critical for the rate capability of the anode material.</p><p>It is understood that the interfacial chemistry (SEI layer) on electrodes relates to the electrolyte solvation structure, which also determines the solvation/desolvation energy of Na<sup>+</sup>. In conventional electrolytes, Na<sup>+</sup> is solvated in different polar solvents to form a solvent‐derived SEI on the electrode.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0038"><sup>[9]</sup></link> For example, in a propylene carbonate (PC)‐based electrolyte, the solvent was preferentially reduced to generate a unique PC‐derived SEI film, which adopts a hybrid structure with Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> as inorganic salts, and a larger amount of sodium alkyl carbonates as organic ones.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0039"><sup>[10]</sup></link> In contrast to ester‐based electrolytes, the ether‐based electrolytes were preferentially chosen to lower the Gibbs free energies of solvation/desolvation energy to generate a unique ether‐derived SEI to support excellent cycling performance.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0042"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Because solvent and anion can both serve as ligands to coordinate with Na<sup>+</sup> through ion–dipole or ion–ion interactions, the actual solvation structure depends on the competitive coordination between them.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0043"><sup>[12]</sup></link> The weaker solvating energy of the ion–solvent pair can theoretically permit more anions to coordinate with Li<sup>+</sup>/Na<sup>+</sup>, finally forming the anions‐derived SEI with excellent interfacial ion transport kinetics and high stability.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0044"><sup>[13]</sup></link> Therefore, the exploration of electrolytes will be an efficient route to simultaneously modulate the solvation energy and interfacial properties of anode/electrolyte, which are crucial to tune the charge transfer process in the interfaces.</p><p>This work aims to demonstrate the critical effect of electrode–electrolyte interfacial chemistry on the performance of a commercial hard carbon anode operating at the rate and temperature extremes. A new weakly solvating electrolyte (WSE) consisting of a cyclic ether (tetrahydrofuran, THF) as solvent and NaPF<sub>6</sub> as the electrolyte salt was proposed to optimize the interfacial properties of anode/electrolyte. Surprisingly, the lower ion desolvation energy and homogeneous SEI are simultaneously achieved. Benefiting from the low Na<sup>+</sup> desolvation energy and fast transport at the anode/electrolyte interface, the commercial hard carbon anode demonstrates ultra‐high rate capability and remarkable low‐temperature performance.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>As previous studies indicated, the ion desolvation process dominates the charging/discharging process at room and low temperatures.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0037 #anie202200475-bib-0045 #anie202200475-bib-0047"><sup>[8, 13a, 14]</sup></link> We hypothesize that the desolvation behavior is a direct result of the inherent solvation structure of electrolytes, which holds the key to describing the performance discrepancy between different electrolytes including 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/tetrahydrofuran (THF), 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/1,2‐dimethoxyethane (DME), 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/ethylene carbonate (EC)/diethyl carbonate (DEC) and 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/propylene carbonate (PC).</p><p>The solvation structures of these four electrolytes were investigated by spectroscopic characterization combined with classical molecular dynamics (MD) simulations in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0001">1</link>a–d. The coordination number of Na<sup>+</sup> with different solvent molecules can be obtained by analyzing the radial distribution function (RDF) of O around Na<sup>+</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0001">1</link>e). As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0001">1</link>f, the electrolytes of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF and DME exhibit average coordination numbers of 3.3 THF and 2.4 DME per Na<sup>+</sup>, respectively, which are higher than that of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in DEC/EC (1.5 DEC/EC per Na<sup>+</sup>) and 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in PC (1.4 PC per Na<sup>+</sup>). The binding energy of Na<sup>+</sup> solvent complexes is mainly determined by the chemical structure of the ligands. Firstly, ethereal O exhibits lower nucleophilicity than carbonyl O; therefore, ethers usually have lower solvating energy than carbonates.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0046"><sup>[13b]</sup></link> Secondly, ligands with multiple coordination sites (also named chelating effect) exhibit a stronger interaction with Na<sup>+</sup> than monodentate ligands. In addition, multiple oxygen atoms in the same solvent molecule occupy more coordination space of Na<sup>+</sup>, resulting in lower coordination numbers between Na<sup>+</sup> and multi‐dentate solvents than single‐dentate solvents. For example, DME has two O atoms to coordinate with Na<sup>+</sup> (Supporting Information Table 1), and therefore exhibits higher binding energies but lower coordination numbers than THF. First‐principles calculations were further conducted to probe the molecular interactions between Na<sup>+</sup>(solvent)<sub><i>n</i></sub> and Na<sup>+</sup>, which yielded binding energies of −79.214, −88.785, −93.367 and −134.82 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0001">1</link>g) for the Na<sup>+</sup>(THF)<sub>3.3</sub>, Na<sup>+</sup>(DME)<sub>2.4</sub>, Na<sup>+</sup>(DEC/EC)<sub>1.5</sub> and Na<sup>+</sup>(PC)<sub>1.4</sub> complexes (average coordination numbers from MD), respectively. It is also concluded that Na<sup>+</sup> is more easily solvated by the cyclic carbonate ester solvents to form a solvation–Na<sup>+</sup> complex compared with the linear carbonate ester solvents and the combination of THF and Na<sup>+</sup> shows a weakly solvated structure.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0048"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Hence, the binding energies provided for Na<sup>+</sup>(THF)<sub>3.3</sub>, Na<sup>+</sup>(DME)<sub>2.4</sub>, Na<sup>+</sup>(DEC/EC)<sub>1.5</sub> and Na<sup>+</sup>(PC)<sub>1.4</sub> complexes can be regarded as a qualitative indication of their divergent desolvation barriers. The more positive solvation binding energy between Na<sup>+</sup> and THF indicates a lower desolvation energy of Na<sup>+</sup>(THF)<sub>3.3</sub>, that is, the energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup> to desolvate on the electrode surface is lower, which accelerates the transport kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup>. The balance between cation/solvent and cation/anion binding in each solvation shell is of particular importance when considering Na<sup>+</sup> charge‐transfer mechanism at the anode interphase because different solvation structures may undergo different dynamics processes in the presence of the electric field.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0045"><sup>[13a]</sup></link> For the weakly solvated Na<sup>+</sup>(THF)<sub>3.3</sub>, anions are more likely to enter the solvated sheath and reach the electrode surface to form a stable anion‐derived SEI, which is beneficial to the electrochemical performance.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200475-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200475:anie202200475-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200475-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Theoretical and experimental analysis of electrolyte structure. a–d) Snapshots obtained from classical molecular dynamics simulations of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in a) THF, b) DME, c) EC/DEC and d) PC. e) Na<sup>+</sup> radial distribution function of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC. f) Coordination numbers of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC. g) Corresponding Na<sup>+</sup>‐solvent binding energies obtained from quantum chemistry simulations in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC. h) Raman spectra of pure solvents, salts and the electrolytes.</p></caption></figure><p>Experimental evidence was obtained from Raman spectra of the electrolytes (1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/DME, 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF, 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/(EC : DEC=1 : 1), and 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/PC) and their components (NaPF<sub>6</sub> salt, DME, THF, EC/DEC and PC solvents). These spectra are displayed in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0001">1</link>h. In a solvent system, the peaks associated with the anion of the salt undergo a significant red‐shift due to the reduced coordination between cation and anion and increased coordination between cation and solvent.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0045"><sup>[13a]</sup></link> As seen in the spectrum of NaPF<sub>6</sub>, the P−F stretching vibration peak of the PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−1</sup> at 765 cm<sup>−1</sup> has a significant shift to 742 cm<sup>−1</sup> when dissolved in different solvents, indicating a strong dissociation of the Na<sup>+</sup>/PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> interactions. In addition, new vibration bands of DME and EC/DEC solvents arise at 868 and 903 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, signifying the abundant Na<sup>+</sup>–DME and Na<sup>+</sup>–EC/DEC complexes. On the contrary, vibration bands at 916 cm<sup>−1</sup> of pure THF and NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte are almost identical, indicating that the interaction between THF and Na<sup>+</sup> is extremely weak. Similar results can also be found in the Fourier transform infrared spectra (FT‐IR) for ether electrolyte systems (Figure S1), where the C−O−C peak of THF (1065 cm<sup>−1</sup>) undergoes a reduced shift compared with that of DME (1106 cm<sup>−1</sup>) after the introduction of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>, indicating that THF–NaPF<sub>6</sub> displays a weak binding energy. However, Na<sup>+</sup> is bonded to the O of C=O in ester solvents and the coordination numbers are small, so the characteristic peak position of the C−O−C functional group is basically unchanged. Besides, the low viscosity of the THF electrolyte system (Supporting Information Table 1) is beneficial to the ion conductivity of the electrolyte, thus enhancing the rate performance.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0047 #anie202200475-bib-0051"><sup>[14, 16]</sup></link></p><p>To reveal the influence of the ion–solvent structure on the performance of the anode, commercial hard carbon (HC), which is the most promising anode material, was employed as an example. The morphology and structure details of commercial hard carbon are demonstrated in Figures S2 and S3. Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0002">2</link>a shows the rate capability of HC in different electrolytes. As seen, it delivers capacities of 304 and 300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in NaPF<sub>6</sub>/PC and NaPF<sub>6</sub>/EC/DEC electrolytes, respectively. However, they sharply decrease to &lt;100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when the current density is above 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Interestingly, the rate capability of HC in NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF and NaPF<sub>6</sub>/DME electrolytes is much superior. As seen, the HC electrode demonstrates the best rate capability in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte with 305, 277, 266, 260, 250 and 238 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 20, 100, 200, 500, 1000 and 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Even at 5000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (20 C, 1 C=250 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), a high discharge capacity of 212 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is still maintained for the THF solvent, which is much higher than those of DME (193 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), PC (24 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and EC/DEC (5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) solvents. In fact, as displayed in Figure S4, the HC here exhibits much higher reversible capacity at high current density in comparison with the previously reported results.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0021 #anie202200475-bib-0028 #anie202200475-bib-0056"><sup>[4b, 6a, 17]</sup></link> The charge/discharge curves of the HC electrode in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0002">2</link>b) show that a high platform (below 0.1 V) capacity is well maintained at various current densities, which is much better than those in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/EC/DEC and 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/PC electrolytes, in which the plateau region below 0.1 V almost disappears (Figure S5, S6). Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0002">2</link>c further compares the cycling performance of HC anodes in different electrolytes at 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The HC in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte exhibits the most stable specific capacity with a retention ratio of 91 % after 100 cycles. Moreover, as seen in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0002">2</link>d, it also shows superior long‐term cycling stability in the THF‐based electrolyte with a high specific capacity of 206 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, approximately fourfold the value observed in the EC/DEC‐based (54 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and PC‐based (37 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) electrolytes. When the current density is increased to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, HC in the THF‐based electrolyte can maintain a remarkable capacity of 199 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0002">2</link>e) and a capacity retention of up to 90 % after 1000 cycles. In addition, as shown in Figure S7, HC electrodes can also maintain considerable stability in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF at a higher current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. When increasing the load weight of HC electrode to 3.9 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, the THF electrolyte system also retains superior rate performance in Figure S8. Repeated tests were carried out to ensure the good reproducibility of the electrochemical properties (Figure S9).</p><figure xml:id="anie202200475-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200475:anie202200475-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200475-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Systematic comparison of the electrochemical performance of commercial hard carbon electrodes in different electrolytes. a) Rate performance of HC cycled in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC at current densities ranging from 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Initial galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles of HC in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF at different current densities. c) Cycling performance of HC in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC at a current density of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for 100 cycles. d) Cycling performance of HC in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. e) Cycling performance of HC in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>The huge difference between the electrochemical properties of the given hard carbon electrodes in different electrolyte systems inspires us to further investigate the nature of the SEI on the electrode surface, which determines the ion transport through interphase. High‐resolution XPS of C1s, O1s, Na1s and F1s spectra were collected to reveal the detailed structure and component of SEI in different electrolytes (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0003">3</link>a–d, Figure S10–13). It can be deduced from Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0003">3</link>a–d that the SEI films formed in the ether‐based and ester‐based electrolytes contain both organic (R−O‐Na in ether‐based and ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na in ester‐based electrolytes) and inorganic (Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, NaF) components, which are consistent with previously reported results.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0025 #anie202200475-bib-0065"><sup>[5b, 18]</sup></link> The detailed discussions are demonstrated in Figure S10.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200475-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200475:anie202200475-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200475-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The electrode surface composition analyzed by XPS etching. XPS a) C 1s, b) O 1s, c) F 1s and d) Na 1s spectra of the HC electrodes after 3 cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> when using the electrolytes of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in THF, DME, EC/DEC and PC. The atomic fractions of Na, C, O, F as a function of etching time in HC electrodes after 3 cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> when using the electrolytes of 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in e) THF, f) DME, g) EC/DEC and h) PC.</p></caption></figure><p>To gain more insights into the SEI composition, the depth profiles were further analyzed by conducting an in‐depth Ar<sup>+</sup> sputtering for 0 s, 10 s, 20 s, 30 s, 40 s, and 50 s with an etching rate of approximately 0.4 nm s<sup>−1</sup>. The etching depth corresponds to the standard thermal oxidation of Ta<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> samples (see Experimental Section in the Supporting Information for more details). As shown in the compared spectra at 0 s and 50 s (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0003">3</link>e–h), there is a difference between the SEIs composition formed in these electrolytes.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0046 #anie202200475-bib-0049"><sup>[13b, 15a]</sup></link> For EC/DEC‐based and PC‐based electrolytes, the C and O concentrations decrease, while the F and Na contents increase significantly with the etching time (etching depth). These results agree well with the classic multilayers SEI model, in which the outer layer mainly consists of a large number of organic species, such as ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na, caused by the degradation and instability of the electrodes during cycling and the inner layer consists of various inorganic compounds such as NaF and Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0025 #anie202200475-bib-0068"><sup>[5b, 19]</sup></link> In contrast, with the sputtering time increasing, no obvious change of elements can be observed in the SEI of THF‐based and DME‐based electrolytes, especially the O and F elements keep constant, indicating that the SEI compositions in the ether‐based electrolyte are highly homogeneous along with its depth <b>(</b>Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0003">3</link>e, f). It should be emphasized that NaF in the SEI is conducive to Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion through the SEI.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0002 #anie202200475-bib-0022 #anie202200475-bib-0069"><sup>[1a, 4c, 20]</sup></link> As observed in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0003">3</link>e–h and Figure S12, the NaF content in the SEI of ether‐based electrolyte is constant with the depth. However, the NaF content in the SEI of ester‐based electrolytes is relatively low at the exterior and increases with depth, which is not good for the ion conductivity. Note that, in the THF electrolyte system, due to the weak solvation energy for THF, PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> groups are more likely to enter the solvated sheath and arrive at the HC surface together with Na<sup>+</sup>, involving the reduction process to form NaF in SEIs.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0046 #anie202200475-bib-0072"><sup>[13b, 21]</sup></link> For the EC/DEC and PC electrolyte, the strong coordination ability of solvent molecules will restrain the anions to coordinate with Na<sup>+</sup>. In addition, during the discharge process (SEI formation process), with the potential of the anode decreasing, more and more electrons gather in the anode, which will repulse anions and decrease the number of free anions. Therefore, the content of NaF (decomposed from PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>) increases with etching time in EC/DEC and PC electrolytes.</p><p>Moreover, as reported, the organic components in the SEI possess higher elasticity and are more buffered to mechanical deformations, therefore can offer good protection for the hard carbon electrode.<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0067 #anie202200475-bib-0073"><sup>[18b, 22]</sup></link> It is easy to understand that a homogeneous organic component is more beneficial to the interface stability, that is why hard carbon electrodes demonstrate superior cycling stability in THF and DME‐based electrolytes. The better sodium storage performances of HC in THF‐based electrolyte than DME should be due to the lower solvation/desolvation energy as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0001">1</link>g.</p><p>To better confirm the benefits of low solvation/desolvation energy and homogeneous SEI layer, more dynamic properties were investigated. Figure S14 shows the typical CV curves of HC in different electrolytes. In EC/DEC and PC‐based electrolytes, notable cathodic peaks are observed below 1.0 V, corresponding to the decomposition of electrolytes and the formation of SEI film on the electrode surface in the first cycle. On the contrary, irreversible cathodic peaks of THF and DME‐based electrolytes shift negatively to almost overlapping with the sodiation peak, meaning that THF and DME are harder to be reduced, which is consistent with the higher LUMO levels of ether solvents than ester solvents as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>a. In fact, as seen in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>b, although the LUMO energy decreases after Na<sup>+</sup> and solvents forming solvated sheaths in electrolytes (Figure S15), the LUMO levels of Na<sup>+</sup>[ether][PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>] sheaths are also higher than those of Na<sup>+</sup>[ester][PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>] complexes.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200475-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200475:anie202200475-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200475-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Molecular orbital energies of NaPF<sub>6</sub> and several solvents. b) Molecular orbital energies of Na<sup>+</sup>‐solvents complexes. Schematic diagrams of Na<sup>+</sup> migration in c) THF and d) PC‐based electrolytes and SEI. e) Potential polarization between the cathodic and anodic peaks in the CV curves as a function of scan rate. f) EIS after 3 cycles at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. g) Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients (<i>D</i><sub>Na+</sub>) of HC in THF and PC‐based electrolytes obtained from GITT tests.</p></caption></figure><p>To further compare the Na storage kinetics of HC in different electrolytes, CV curves with different scan rates were tested. It can be seen from Figure S16 that the HC electrodes cycled in EC/DEC and PC‐based electrolytes display much higher polarization than those in THF and DME electrolytes, and the polarization becomes more severe as the scan rate increases. The polarization remains small with the increase of scanning rate in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte, indicating quite favorable sodiation dynamics. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) tests were employed to analyze the ionic transport properties of the SEI films and electrodes in different electrolytes (Figure S17). The results indicate that the resistances originated from the SEI film (<i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub>) and charge transfer (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) in THF‐based electrolyte are both far lower than those in EC/DEC‐based and PC‐based electrolytes. Moreover, the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> of HC electrode in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte is very low and hardly changed as the number of cycles increases. Therefore, these results imply the facilitation of Na storage kinetics in the 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte. In fact, this is because Na<sup>+</sup> in THF electrolyte can be rapidly desolvated on the electrode surface and the uniform SEI facilitates the rapid diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> through the SEI interface, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>c. On the contrary, in the ester electrolytes such as PC, the Na<sup>+</sup> can be slowly desolvated and the thick and heterogeneous SEI hinders the Na<sup>+</sup> transport (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>d).</p><p>In addition, it can be seen from Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>e that, although the resistance in THF‐based electrolyte is much smaller than that in ester electrolyte, it is still slightly larger than that in DME electrolyte. The polarization curves in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>f indicate that the polarization of HC in THF‐based electrolyte is slightly larger than that in DME. This is because the SEI film formed by HC in THF‐based electrolyte has more R−O−Na organic composition, which makes its kinetic performance slightly worse than that in DME. However, due to the advantages of the weak solvation structure of THF‐based electrolyte, the HC electrode demonstrates much better electrochemical performance than the DME‐based electrolyte. Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was used to evaluate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients (<i>D</i><sub>Na+</sub>) and further explain the differences in kinetics behavior during the discharging process in different electrolytes (Figure S18) and it was found that the average <i>D</i><sub>Na+</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>g) of HC electrolyte in the THF‐based electrolyte is higher than that in the PC‐based electrolyte, which further confirms that the kinetics are effectively accelerated in the THF‐based electrolyte.</p><p>It is well known that low‐temperature performance is also significant for some special scenarios in practical applications. However, there are few reports on the low‐temperature performance of the HC anode for SIBs. As previous studies have indicated that the solvation structure of the electrolyte defines the charge‐transfer behavior at low temperature,<link href="#anie202200475-bib-0045"><sup>[13a]</sup></link> the low‐temperature performance of HC in THF‐based electrolyte was further investigated. When the ambient temperature drops to −5 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0005">5</link>a), the specific capacities of HC in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte at 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> are 282 and 155 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, indicating superior rate capability. Even at −20 °C, a high capacity of 269 and 175 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be achieved at 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The charge‐discharge profile in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0005">5</link>b, c) still maintains a high low‐plateau capacity at different densities, indicating favorable kinetics. Moreover, HC anode in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte also possesses superior long‐term cycling stability with a specific capacity of 203 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles at a large current density of 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> at −5 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0005">5</link>d). Even at a lower temperature of −20 °C, it still delivers high capacity retention up to 95 % with a specific capacity of 181 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0005">5</link>e). Such excellent cycling stability at low temperature with a high plateau capacity ratio of 70 % (below 0.1 V) is rarely reported for sodium‐ion batteries previously.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200475-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200475:anie202200475-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200475-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Systematic comparison of the electrochemical performance of HC electrodes in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte at low temperatures. a) Rate performance in the 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF electrolyte at −5 and −20 °C and the corresponding galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles at different current densities at b) −5 °C and c) −20 °C. Long‐term cycling performance in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>/THF at d) −5 °C and e) −20 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>According to the above discussions, as illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie202200475-fig-0004">4</link>c, d, the electrode–electrolyte interfacial chemistry can be well‐modulated in the THF‐based electrolyte. Firstly, the Na<sup>+</sup>/solvent desolvation energy at the interface is very low, which is beneficial to the fast diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> between the electrolyte/electrode interface. Secondly, the SEI formed in the THF‐based electrolyte possesses a homogeneous composition, in which the well‐distributed NaF component greatly contributes to the fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion through the SEI layer and the uniform organic components can keep the interface stable. As a consequence, the commercial HC electrode can demonstrate significantly improved electrochemical properties in THF‐based electrolyte in comparison to that in ester‐based electrolytes.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we have demonstrated that electrode–electrolyte interphase chemistry including ion desolvation and SEI interphase are crucial to the electrochemical properties of the anode at the ultra‐high rate and low‐temperature extremes via the comparative study of different electrolytes. The THF‐based electrolyte enables the commercial HC anode superior rate capability (up to 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and ultra‐low‐temperature performance (operating stably at −20 °C). Kinetic analysis reveals that the remarkable performance is attributed to weakly Na<sup>+</sup>‐solvent desolvation energy and uniform SEI composition achieved in the THF‐based electrolyte, which is beneficial to the ion diffusion between the electrolyte–electrode interface and interface stability. This work not only proposes an advanced low‐temperature electrolyte, but also provides new insights into the interfacial modulation for next‐generation high‐rate and low‐temperature batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This study was financially supported by the National Nature Science Foundation of China (21905306, 21975289 and U19A2019), Hunan Provincial Science and Technology Plan Project of China (2017TP1001 and 2020JJ2042), Open sharing Fund for the Large‐scale Instrument and Equipment of Central South University (CSUZCC202112) and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central South University (2020zzts060).</p></section><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0005" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0006" type="openResearch"><section xml:id="anie202200475-sec-0000" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available on request from the corresponding author.
