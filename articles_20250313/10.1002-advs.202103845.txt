Herein, a novel metallic sodium/sodium–tin alloy foil anode (Na/NaSn) with 3D interpenetrated network and porous structure is prepared through facile alloy reaction. The strong sodiophilic properties of sodium–tin alloy can lower the nucleation energy, resulting in smaller depositing potential and strong adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup>, while synergistic effect of porous skeleton and additional potential difference (≈0.1 V) between Na and Na–Sn alloy (Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>) can alleviate volume expansion, redistribute the Na‐ion flux and regulate electronic field, which favors and improves homogeneous Na deposition. The as‐fabricated Na/NaSn electrode can endow excellent plating/stripping reversibility at high areal capacity (over 1600 h for 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> at 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> and 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> at 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>), fast electrochemical kinetics (500 h under 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> at 4 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) and superior rate performances. A novel strategy in the design of high‐performance Na anodes for large‐scale energy storage is provided.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs3388-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>An Na anode integrating Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> and metallic Na is prepared through facile alloy reaction between Na and Sn, enabling superior rate performances and uniform Na deposition under high capacity.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3388:advs3388-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3388-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs3388-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as a promising alternative to lithium ion batteries (LIBs) to integrate reproducible energy sources for scale stationary grids owing to the advantages of low cost, earth abundance, availability of sodium source.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, conventional graphite anode with low theoretical specific capacity (&lt;300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) can't meet the requirements of high energy and power density, limiting the large‐scale application of sodium batteries in energy storage field.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The metallic sodium possessing a theoretical specific capacity (1166 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and low redox potential (–2.714 V vs standard hydrogen electrode), has attracted great interests as anode of sodium metallic batteries (SMBs). Whereas, several challenges remain to be addressed before their application of SMBs, such as the uncontrolled sodium dendrite growth, unrestrained volume expansion and unstable solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer owing to high activity of metallic sodium, which contributes to the inferior cycling stability and safety hazards.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Various strategies have been proposed to handle aforementioned issues, including electrolyte optimization (high concentration electrolyte,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> electrolyte additives,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> solid state electrolyte<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) and interface engineering (artificial SEI,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> alloy interface<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) for stabilizing solid electrolyte interface, 3D scaffold engineering<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> for accommodating the volume expansion. Although those methods alleviate corresponding problems through investigating formed Na deposition morphology at low areal capacity (&lt;1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) and areal current densities (&lt;2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>), they are rarely focusing on regulation of intrinsic factors responsible for the dendrite growth, including sodiophobic properties, highly inhomogeneous electric field distribution as well as nonuniform Na<sup>+</sup> flux, especially under condition of high rate and high areal capacity. The sodiophilic materials, such as transmission metal of Au<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and carbon‐based materials with specific active group,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> are able to obtain the uniform plating through guiding initial nucleation behavior and growth during Na deposition. Whereas, these materials are just used as surface heterogeneous seeds to guide the Na deposition, the nonuniform Na<sup>+</sup> flux and uncontrollable electric field remain to be addressed since they are crucial for Na planar growth. For example, metal tin is sodiophilic material, which can benefit Na deposition. However, some reported Na‐X alloy only acts as seeds for guiding Na ions deposition on the substrate<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0008"><sup>8b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> or as a protective layer,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which is hard to obtain high deposition capacity for ultralong cycling life owing to repeated expansion and contraction of volume during Na plating and stripping.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, achieving stable sodium metallic anode with high areal capacities at high current densities, is still a great challenge for high‐rate SMBs.</p><p>Herein, we report a metallic sodium foil (Na/NaSn) with 3D connected network integrating metallic sodium and Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> alloy through an in situ alloy reaction. The 3D alloy network can not only provide an interpenetrated channel for fast sodium diffusion and act as stable skeleton for high areal capacity of Na deposition, but its porous structure is beneficial to accommodating the volume expansion and regulating Na<sup>+</sup> flux during Na deposition. The alloy Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> with ≈0.1 V potential difference can create a secondary electric field to drive the Na<sup>+</sup> transfer, which benefits decreased ion concentration difference. In addition, the strong affinity between metallic Na and Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> can help and favor the reduced Na deposition barrier, and the high sodium ion diffusion coefficient of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> enable fast kinetics for remarkable rate performances. The Na/NaSn anode can deliver an ultralong stripping/plating life for high deposition capacity (1600 h at 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>; 1600 h at 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>) with low deposition potential (24.5 mV) and fast kinetics (500 h at 4 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> for 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>). In addition, the Na/NaSn|NVP full cell can stably operate for 90 cycles with capacity retention of 95% at a current density of 1C and display superior rate performances. This work provides a promising potential for practical application of high rate SMBs with high capacity at high current densities.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3388-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The Na/NaSn foil was prepared through repeated folding and calendaring of Sn powder (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information) and Na foil for 30 times, where the Na–Sn alloy can be simultaneously formed at room temperature under inert atmosphere, as illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3388-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. The metallic Na foil with bright silver luster immediately becomes greyish white after alloy reaction (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). The atomic ratios of Na to Sn are set to be 30:4, 45:4, 90:4, which are based on the equation of (<i>x</i> + 15)Na + 4Sn = Na<sub>(</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>+15)</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> demonstrated by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy （XRD） patterns (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0001">1b</link>). The surplus Na is filled in the pores of alloy particles (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information) and interstice between interconnected Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> particles. The X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectroscopy of pure Sn has two main peaks centered at 484.9 and 493.3 eV for Sn3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Sn3d<sub>3/2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> respectively, and then shifted to lower positions after alloyed with Na, indicative of the formation of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> alloy (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0001">1c</link>). The morphologies of metallic Na foil and Na/NaSn foil were observed by scanning electron microscope (SEM, Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0001">1d</link>). Compared to morphology of metallic Na (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information), the Na/NaSn foil exhibits more rough surface with some exposed micro‐size Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> alloy particles demonstrated by energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) mappings (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). The morphology of dealloyed Sn particles (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S6a</link>, Supporting Information) shows interconnected network with specific surface area of 10 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S6b</link>, Supporting Information), and the Sn particles can be closely connected to form sheets (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0001">1e</link>). This special interconnected skeleton can act as stable intrinsic host for uniform Na nucleation and optimize the electric field distribution owing to the potential difference (0.1 V)<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> between Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> and Na, which contributes to reduced Na dendrite and alleviation of volume expansion during Na deposition. The Na/NaSn foil discussed as followed mainly refers to the one with mass ratio of 45:4 (Na:Sn) owing to its good electrochemical performances (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information). Since the thickness of Na/NaSn electrode is set as 100 µm, the calculated specific capacity is about 11 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><figure xml:id="advs3388-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3388:advs3388-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3388-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The synthesis process and physicochemical properties. a) The scheme of Na/NaSn material. b) The XRD patterns of Na/NaSn material. c) The XPS spectroscopy of Sn for Sn power and Na/NaSn, respectively. d) The SEM image of Na/NaSn. e) The optical image of Na/NaSn sheets after soaking in ethanol.</caption></figure><p>To evaluate the structural advantages for metallic sodium deposition, the stripping and plating measurements for Na|Na and Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn symmetrical batteries were carried out. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3388-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> shows the charge/discharge voltage profiles for Na/NaSn and metallic Na electrode with a high areal capacity of 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> (≈34 µm thickness) at a current density of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>. The Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn symmetrical battery can keep ultralong cycling stability for 1600 h without potential drift, while the metallic Na|Na battery appears soft short circuit at first stripping/plating circles, indicating that the framework of Na–Sn alloy is favorable for stable Na deposition. Meanwhile, it can be seen that lower voltage hysteresis of 24.5 mV at initial charge stage is observed for Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn battery, but the Na|Na battery shows a near 5 V voltage hysteresis (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0002">2b,c</link>), implying that the Na–Sn alloy skeleton is qualified with excellent sodiophilic property, which is beneficial to lowering nucleation barrier. Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0002">2d,e</link> displays the corresponding enlarged voltage profiles ranging from 100 to 140 h and from 1240 to 1280 h for Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn and Na|Na batteries, respectively. The Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn batteries has lower stripping/plating potentials (8 mV at 100 h and 9 mV at 1240 h, Figures <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S8a,b</link>, Supporting Information) and more flat stripping/plating curves compared to those of the Na|Na batteries (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S8c,d</link>, Supporting Information), suggesting the homogeneous and stable Na deposition on surface Na–Sn alloy framework. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) displays that the Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn battery has a smaller diameter of half cycle than that of Na|Na battery, suggesting the improved charge transfer capability owing to the excellent sodiophilic property of Na–Sn alloy (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0002">2f</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After 50 cycles, the diameter for Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn battery decreases, indicative of the reduced charge resistance, further demonstrating the outperformed cycling stability and low stripping/plating potential (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information). The early stage of Na nucleation and growing process on the surface of electrodes were investigated by chronoamperometry method, as shown in Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0002">2g</link>. The Time–current curve derived at an overpotential of 0.2 V for Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn battery shows higher current density (68 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) than that (26 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) of Na|Na battery, which indicates that Na–Sn alloy framework exhibits excellent ion conductivity for good rate performances.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To further evaluate the structural advantages for fast electrochemical kinetics, the measurement of higher areal current density was carried out for Na|Na and Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn symmetrical batteries, as shown in Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0002">2h</link>. The wettability of electrolyte on the surface of Na/NaSn and Na electrode is characterized by contact angles (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0003">3d</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Notably, the contact angle (21°) of Na/NaSn is smaller than that (51°) of Na electrode, further implying lower nucleation barrier, which contributes to the excellent rate performances. Second, the potential difference between the Na and Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> can offer additional driving force for Na ion transfer along the pathway.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0015 #advs3388-bib-0019"><sup>15,19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Third, the alloy Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> is less reactive with the electrolyte due to its higher electrochemical potential than metallic sodium (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information),<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which displays ultralong cycling life owing to less electrolyte consumption.</p><p>In order to explore the morphological evolution of Na deposition on Na and Na/NaSn electrode (The electrodes have been firstly stripped with a capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>), the ex situ SEM was carried out (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3388-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>). After a capacity of 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> of Na deposition, the Na/NaSn electrode exhibits a smooth surface without obvious Na dendrite, while Na electrode shows porous structure (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0004">4a,c</link>). When further depositing with additional 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> on the Na/NaSn electrode, the planar surface is still preserved (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0004">4b</link>). In contrast, the Na dendrite protrusions with messy morphologies covered on surface were observed for metallic Na electrode (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0004">4d</link>), which indicates that Na–Sn alloy skeleton can not only favor uniform Na nucleation and Na growth, but accommodate ultrahigh deposition capacities. Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0004">4e–g</link> give the EDX mappings of Na and Sn elements for Na/NaSn electrode with a deposition capacity of 8 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. Obviously, the Na–Sn alloy particles gather closely and ultrahigh‐capacity Na can uniformly deposit on the Na–Sn alloy.</p><figure xml:id="advs3388-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3388:advs3388-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3388-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The exploration of mechanism of Na deposition on the Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> electrode. a,c) The SEM images for Na and Na/NaSn electrode with deposition of 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> and b,d) 8 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> after an initially stripped capacity of 2 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>. e) The SEM image of Na/NaSn electrode after removal of part deposited Na and corresponding EDX mappings of Sn f) and Na g), respectively. h) The mechanism of Na deposition on Na/NaSn alloy framework.</caption></figure><p>To demonstrate the structural merits during cycling process, the Na|Na and Na/NaSn|Na/NaSn batteries were stripped/plated for ten cycles at different current densities (1, 4, 8 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>) at a capacity of 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> and their corresponding depositing appearances were further observed. The Na/NaSn electrode remains planar surface at 1 and 4 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S13a,b</link>, Supporting Information), explaining its superior rate performances and cycling stability, while the metallic Na electrode shows Na dendrite and porous structure, which would contribute to dead Na when operated for long stripping/plating cycles (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S13d,e</link>, Supporting Information). Even measured at 8 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, the surface of Na/NaSn electrode covered by intimately connected small particles is still flat, while the Na electrode displays serious Na protrusions and aggravated porous structure (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S13c,f</link>, Supporting Information). These phenomena imply that Na–Sn alloy can act as favorable framework for stable and high‐capacity Na deposition at high current densities. Therefore, the mechanism of Na deposition on the Na–Sn alloy can be included that (Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0004">4h</link>): sodium ions firstly adsorb on surface of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> through solid‐liquid interface, then penetrate entire Na–Sn alloy network into Na substrate, and finally form Na planar. In order to study favorable framework, XPS measurements are carried out at different depths, such as 0, 10, 20, and 30 nm. The full spectra (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S14</link>, Supporting Information) mainly contain five elements of Na, P, C, O, F. The fitted spectra of element F show two peaks assigned to NaF bond and PF bond, and the PF bond is related to remained electrolyte (NaPF<sub>6</sub>). As an increase of etching depth, the intensity of NaF bond of Na/NaSn electrode becomes weaker compared to those of Na electrode, indicating that Na/NaSn electrode has more thinner SEI film (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S15a</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> From the fitted curves of Na (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S15b</link>, Supporting Information), peak intensity of NaF bond for Na/NaSn electrode is similar at different testing depth, which is far lower than those of pure Na electrode at 0, 10, 20 nm. This result further demonstrates the lower activity of Na–Sn alloy compared with pure Na metal. In the XPS spectra of P (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S15c</link>, Supporting Information), there are two peaks assigned to PF<sub>6</sub> and OP═O,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and the total contents of P are 2.4% for Na electrode and 0.8% for Na/NaSn electrode, respectively. As for element O (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S16a</link>, Supporting Information), the spectra of Na anode can be deconvoluted into four peaks of CCO, CONa, Na<sub>2</sub>O, Na kll, while Na/NaSn anode exhibits less functional groups, which could be attributed to the different reactivity. From the fitted curves of element C (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S16b</link>, Supporting Information), it can be deconvoluted into four peaks of CC, CO, Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, CF.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is notably that Na/NaSn electrode exhibits less peaks and weaker peak intensity than those of Na electrode, indicating that the formation of alloy can weaken the reaction between electrode and electrolyte.</p><p>To further explore the intrinsic reasons of the Na/NaSn anode with excellent electrochemical performances, the first principle calculation (DFT) was carried out.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3388-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The adsorption properties of single Na on metallic Na and Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> alloy surfaces have been investigated, where the calculated crystal faces of Na and Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> are 100, 111, 110 (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S17</link>, Supporting Information). The calculated binding energy <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> is 0.58 eV on Na (100), 0.46 eV on Na (111), and 0.35 eV on Na (110), while the calculated <i>E</i><sub>b</sub> for Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> is 1.45 eV on (110), 1.17 eV on (100), and 0.19 eV on (111). Clearly, Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> alloy can remarkably strengthen Na adsorption since both (110) and (100) can offer binding capability as strong as that in bulk sodium, which indicates that (110) and (100) of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> is preferred crystal plane for uniform Na deposition, demonstrated by XRD patterns of depositing metallic Na (Figure <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S18</link>, Supporting Information). The images of differential charge density (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3388-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>–<link href="#advs3388-fig-0005">f</link>) for different crystal faces (110, 100, 111) of Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> show that more electrons accumulate around Sn compared to Na, which is confirmed by calculated Mulliken charge (Table <link href="#advs3388-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information), indicating that Na<sup>+</sup> can be intimately adsorbed by Na–Sn bonds. We also noticed that Na favors the bonding with Sn on the surface, especially at active sites when Sn is presented at the surface and subsurface as in (110) and (100).</p><figure xml:id="advs3388-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3388:advs3388-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3388-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>The theoretical calculation and numerical simulation for exploring uniform Na deposition. a–f) The differential charge density of a) Na(110), b) Na(100), c) Na(111), d) Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>(110), e) Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>(100), f) Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub>(111). g) The exterior and section view h) of numerical simulation of Na<sup>+</sup> flux.</caption></figure><p>Numerical simulations of the Na<sup>+</sup> flux distribution for Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> electrode were carried out to understand deposition behavior. In addition, Figure <link href="#advs3388-fig-0005">5h</link> shows the Na<sup>+</sup> flux in pylome is more homogeneous and enhanced, suggesting that porous architecture with additional electronic field can improve the magnitude of Na<sup>+</sup> flux for planar Na deposition. Owing to the good Na affinity, Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> can decrease the Na nucleation barrier for lower deposition potential, while the auxiliary potential difference between Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> and Na can create an additional driving force to regulate the Na‐ion flux for uniform Na deposition. The 3D Na–Sn alloy network with high Na‐ion diffusion coefficient can provide a penetrated pathway for fast Na diffusion over the entire framework, and its porous structure can accommodate the volume expansion during Na deposition. Through regulation of ion flux, electric field and sodiophilic property, the Na/NaSn anode with free dendrite can be realized at a high areal capacity of 4 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> for ultralong stripping/plating cycles and enable fast kinetics. The Na/NaSn|NVP full cell can present a remarkable cycling stability and an excellent rate performance. This work provides a novel strategy to facilely design metallic Na anode for high areal capacities and excellent rate performances, giving the potential application of energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3388-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grants: BX20200307, 2020M672268) and Zhengzhou University Analytical and Testing Center.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3388-sec-0050" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3388-sec-0060" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3388-sec-0070" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available in the supplementary material of this article.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="advs3388-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="advs3388-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="advs3388-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>Q.</givenNames> <familyName>Jin</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>W.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Wang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>H.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>P.</givenNames> <familyName>Feng</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Z.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>W.</givenNames> <familyName>Wang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Jiang</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
