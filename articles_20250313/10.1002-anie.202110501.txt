To address this issue, a novel desolvation‐free sodium dual‐ion battery (SDIB) has been proposed by using artificial graphite (AG) as anode and polytriphenylamine (PTPAn) as cathode. Combining the cation solvent co‐intercalation and anion storage chemistry, such a SDIB operated with ether‐based electrolyte can intrinsically eliminate the sluggish desolvation process. Hence, it can exhibit an extremely fast kinetics of 10 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (corresponding to 100C‐rate) with a high capacity retention of 45 %. Moreover, the desolvation‐free mechanism endows the battery with 61 % of its room‐temperature capacity at an ultra‐low temperature of −70 °C. This advanced battery system will open a door for designing energy storage devices that require high power density and a wide operational temperature range.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:id="anie202110501-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Rechargeable lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have witnessed widespread applications in portable electronics, electric vehicles and grid energy storage systems owing to their high energy, long life and low self‐discharge.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0001 #anie202110501-bib-0002 #anie202110501-bib-0003"><sup>[1–3]</sup></link> However, the increasing demand is gradually pushing LIBs to their limits, especially in fast charge and low temperature applications.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0004 #anie202110501-bib-0005"><sup>[4, 5]</sup></link> For conventional LIBs, solvated lithium ions experience the desolvation step at the solid electrolyte interphase of electrolyte/electrode prior to the intercalation into bulk electrodes.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0006 #anie202110501-bib-0007 #anie202110501-bib-0008 #anie202110501-bib-0009 #anie202110501-bib-0010"><sup>[6–10]</sup></link> The high energy barrier for the desolvation step would be the rate‐determining step that leads to slow kinetics,<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0011 #anie202110501-bib-0012 #anie202110501-bib-0013 #anie202110501-bib-0014 #anie202110501-bib-0015 #anie202110501-bib-0016"><sup>[11–16]</sup></link> resulting in the failure of current graphite‐based LIBs in fast charge of 80 % capacity acquired within 15 minutes.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0004 #anie202110501-bib-0017"><sup>[4, 17]</sup></link> Moreover, the sluggish desolvation might cause undesired Li‐plating on the graphite anode during charge process at high rate or low temperature.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0017 #anie202110501-bib-0018"><sup>[17, 18]</sup></link> Even worse, current LIBs suffer from severe energy/power loss at sub‐zero temperature and even cease to operate below −20 °C, greatly hindering their deployment at low temperature scenarios.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0016 #anie202110501-bib-0019 #anie202110501-bib-0020"><sup>[16, 19, 20]</sup></link> Therefore, it is vital to develop alternative battery chemistry that could accelerate the desolvation process or even avoid it so as to circumvent these challenges.</p><p>Different from LIBs, dual‐ion batteries (DIBs) introduce the intercalation behavior of anions in the cathode, which might offer a solution for desolvation at cathodes by virtue of the weak solvation of anions.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0020"><sup>[20]</sup></link> Such a battery chemistry generally shows a dual‐graphite configuration in organic electrolyte where the graphite‐based cathode stores anions (such as PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>, BF<sub>4</sub><sup>−</sup>, TFSI<sup>−</sup>, etc.) and the other graphite‐based anode simultaneously stores cations (like Li<sup>+</sup>, Na<sup>+</sup>, etc.).<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0021 #anie202110501-bib-0022 #anie202110501-bib-0023 #anie202110501-bib-0024 #anie202110501-bib-0025 #anie202110501-bib-0026 #anie202110501-bib-0027"><sup>[21–27]</sup></link> High working voltage can be achieved owing to the anion intercalation reaction at around 5 V (vs. Li<sup>+</sup>/Li).<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0021 #anie202110501-bib-0026"><sup>[21, 26]</sup></link> However, conventional organic electrolyte tends to continuously decompose at such high potential, resulting in low Coulombic efficiency and severe capacity decay of DIBs during cycling.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0020 #anie202110501-bib-0021 #anie202110501-bib-0025 #anie202110501-bib-0026 #anie202110501-bib-0028 #anie202110501-bib-0029"><sup>[20, 21, 25, 26, 28, 29]</sup></link> Given the fact that developing a practical advanced electrolyte with oxidative stability higher than 5 V (vs. Li<sup>+</sup>/Li) is still hard to achieve at present, utilizing alternative cathode materials that store anions at relatively low potentials would be a good strategy.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0025 #anie202110501-bib-0030"><sup>[25, 30]</sup></link> As a kind of anion‐storing positive electrodes, organic polymers are undoubtedly excellent candidates with anion‐doping mechanism for their low‐cost, large‐scale production and sustainability.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0019 #anie202110501-bib-0025 #anie202110501-bib-0030 #anie202110501-bib-0031"><sup>[19, 25, 30, 31]</sup></link> A representative polytriphenylamine (PTPAn) has proved its feasibility to reversibly store anions both in aqueous and organic electrolytes owing to its achievable cut‐off potential of 4.3 V (vs. Li<sup>+</sup>/Li), exhibiting fast kinetics via charge storage on the surface groups or in the large interstitial space.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0019 #anie202110501-bib-0025 #anie202110501-bib-0031 #anie202110501-bib-0032"><sup>[19, 25, 31, 32]</sup></link> The desolvation process of anions can be much improved by its surface‐controlled anion‐doping mechanism, which can be considered as an approximate desolvation‐free process. Undoubtedly, this candidate cathode rebuilds confidence in developing practical DIBs with high Coulombic efficiency and acceptable working potential. Nevertheless, the cation intercalation in graphite anodes is still the limiting factor for fast charge owing to the undesired desolvation process, which diminishes the advantages of cathode with fast kinetics. To make a compensation, some trials have been proposed in the view of electrolyte engineering. Applying weakly polar solvents or regulating solvation structure have been demonstrated feasible to facilitate the desolvation process, which improved rate capability and/or low temperature performance of batteries to some extent.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0012 #anie202110501-bib-0013 #anie202110501-bib-0014 #anie202110501-bib-0015 #anie202110501-bib-0016"><sup>[12–16]</sup></link> However, there lies a dilemma that weak solvation solvents would also be weak for salt dissociation, which might cause low ionic conductivity for such “salt‐splitting” mechanism of DIBs. It seems difficult to make a compromise only in the electrolyte formulation. To tackle this problem, an ideal solution would be if the desolvation process could be avoided at the graphite anode. Here it comes to the co‐intercalation of ions with solvent molecules, as it can be accomplished via intelligently using the special mechanism of Na<sup>+</sup>‐ether insertion into the graphite anode.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0033 #anie202110501-bib-0034 #anie202110501-bib-0035"><sup>[33–35]</sup></link> To be specific, Na<sup>+</sup> would be intercalated with ether solvents into graphite to form ternary graphite‐intercalation compounds (GICs),<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0033 #anie202110501-bib-0034 #anie202110501-bib-0035"><sup>[33–35]</sup></link> where the desolvation process can be intrinsically circumvented. Based on these thorough considerations, it can be expected to fabricate a desolvation‐free DIB with unique features of fast‐charge and low‐temperature capability.</p><p>Herein, cation‐solvent co‐intercalation chemistry and anion storage chemistry were combined to create a novel system that effectively circumvents the sluggish desolvation process. Such a proposed desolvation‐free sodium dual‐ion battery (SDIB) would allow the PTPAn cathode to capture PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> and the artificial graphite (AG) anode to receive a Na<sup>+</sup>‐solvent complex during charge, fulfilling fast kinetics at high rate and low temperature. Such SDIB could amazingly maintain 45 % of its capacity at super‐high current density of 10 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, and exhibit remarkable stability over 800 cycles with high capacity retention of 98 % at 1 Ag<sup>−1</sup> and room temperature. Moreover, this desolvation‐free chemistry was capable of cycling for 400 cycles with a slight decay of 0.075 % per cycle at −40 °C. Even at ultra‐low temperature of −70 °C, a high capacity of 61 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be delivered, accounting for 61 % of the room‐temperature capacity. The interesting strategy put forward herein can offer a train of thought towards the development of energy storage systems for high‐power output within a wide temperature range.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202110501-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0001">1 A,B</link> illustrates the divergence in the working mechanism between desolvation‐involved conventional “rocking chair” LIBs with lithium metal‐oxide (LMO) cathode∥AG anode and desolvation‐free SDIB based on PTPAn cathode∥AG anode. Li<sup>+</sup> would be solvated after being extracted from LMO during the charge process, while it can only be embedded into AG to form a LiC<sub>6</sub> compound after overcoming the huge energy barrier originating from the desolvation process.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0007 #anie202110501-bib-0008 #anie202110501-bib-0009 #anie202110501-bib-0010 #anie202110501-bib-0011"><sup>[7–11]</sup></link> The reverse is similar for discharge, suggesting that the desolvation process is also required while Li<sup>+</sup> goes back into the cathode.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0020"><sup>[20]</sup></link> Therefore, it is reasonable that traditional LIBs encounter kinetics hindrance at low temperatures and high rates.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0004 #anie202110501-bib-0005 #anie202110501-bib-0036"><sup>[4, 5, 36]</sup></link> By contrast, Na<sup>+</sup>–ether co‐intercalation in SDIB would occur in the AG anode to generate ternary GICs during the charge process,<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0033 #anie202110501-bib-0034 #anie202110501-bib-0035"><sup>[33–35]</sup></link> while the PTPAn could capture anions from the electrolyte by the triphenylamine radical cation.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0019 #anie202110501-bib-0025"><sup>[19, 25]</sup></link> As for the discharge process, PTPAn would release anions back into the electrolyte, while Na<sup>+</sup>–ether complexes inside the AG anode would return to the electrolyte. In this case, all processes of cation desolvation, the usual rate‐determining steps, are intrinsically circumvented. Predictably, such a SDIB system will display superior rate and low‐temperature performance.</p><figure xml:id="anie202110501-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110501:anie202110501-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110501-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic diagrams of A) a conventional LIB of LMO∥AG, and B) desolvation‐free SDIB of PTPAn∥AG. C) Cycling stability of Na∥PTPAn and Na∥AG half cells at 25 °C. D) Performance of Na∥PTPAn at −40 °C. The electrolyte is 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME and current density is 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>PTPAn was obtained through the oxidation polymerization of monomer TPAn and exhibited the shape of micron‐sized sheets (Figure S1 and S2).<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0019 #anie202110501-bib-0025 #anie202110501-bib-0031 #anie202110501-bib-0032"><sup>[19, 25, 31, 32]</sup></link> The electrochemical performance of PTPAn was firstly revealed by Na∥PTPAn half‐cells operated with a conventional sodic ether‐based electrolyte (1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diethylene glycol dimethyl ether (DEGDME), denoted as 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME). As presented in Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0001">1 C</link> and S3A, PTPAn can deliver a reversible capacity of around 105 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (close to the theoretical capacity of 109 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) with an average discharge voltage of 3.7 V for 100 cycles.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0032"><sup>[32]</sup></link> As for the AG anode, it exhibits a typical Na<sup>+</sup>–solvent co‐intercalation profile with an average voltage of 0.6 V (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0001">1 C</link> and S3B),<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0033 #anie202110501-bib-0034 #anie202110501-bib-0035"><sup>[33–35]</sup></link> showing a stable capacity of 137 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> that lasts for 100 cycles. Benefiting from circumventing the desolvation process, PTPAn and AG are thought to be able to show their excellent rate and low‐temperature capabilities. Unexpectedly, PTPAn in 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME encountered severe capacity decay within 10 cycles at −40 °C, despite almost 90 % of its room‐temperature capacity for the initial cycle (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0001">1 D</link> and S4).</p><p>To probe the incentives for such capacity degradation at low temperature, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were carried out for PTPAn at both 25 and −40 °C. The presented EIS results in Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 A</link> indicate that it is the huge increase in electrolyte/electrode interfacial impedance at low temperature that should take the responsibility. Through further cryogenic storage experiments of electrolytes, partial solidification of electrolyte could be found for 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at −40 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 B</link>). Given the fact that DEGDME will not solidify until the temperature is below −60 °C, we ascribed the occurring solid phase to recrystallization of NaPF<sub>6</sub>, which could be evidenced by the differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) curve shown in Figure S5. Raman spectra of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at various temperatures further support the view (Figure S6). To address this issue, the concentration of NaPF<sub>6</sub> was reasonably reduced to a level of 0.5 <sc>m</sc>, which brought several benefits (e.g., reducing cost and viscosity, enhancing interfacial wettability, expanding operational temperature range) shared by previously reported low‐concentration electrolytes.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0037 #anie202110501-bib-0038"><sup>[37, 38]</sup></link> More importantly, the recrystallization of NaPF<sub>6</sub> is indeed effectively suppressed, as demonstrated in Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 B</link>, S5 and S6C, thus leading to decreased interfacial impedance at −40 °C in 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 A</link>). Moreover, 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME exhibits a comparable electrochemical stability window and even higher ionic conductivity than 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at low temperature (Figure S7 and S8).</p><figure xml:id="anie202110501-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110501:anie202110501-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110501-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>A) EIS investigation of PTPAn performed with 1 <sc>m</sc> and 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME in a specific three‐electrode device at 25 and −40 °C. B) Photographs of 1 <sc>m</sc> and 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at 25 and −40 °C. C) Rate capability of a Na∥PTPAn half cell operated in 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at −40 °C. D) Cycling stability of Na∥PTPAn and Na∥AG half cells operated in 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME with a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> at 25 °C. Discharge or charge curves of E) Na∥PTPAn and F) Na∥AG half cells performed in 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME with various current densities at 25 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>By virtue of low interfacial impedance (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 A</link>), PTPAn collaborating with 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME can finally show its superior performance at −40 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 C</link> and S9), delivering a capacity of 95 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and 84 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 and 0.4 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The AG anode also delivers a considerable capacity with 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at −40 °C, which will be discussed later in details. Moreover, both Na∥PTPAn and Na∥AG half‐cells exhibit similar stability in 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 D</link> and S10) to those in 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME at 25 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0001">1 C</link> and S3), which further demonstrates the feasibility of low concentration electrolyte. The kinetics of the electrochemical reactions are then investigated in Figure S11, where both PTPAn and AG possess fast kinetics not limited by the diffusion process.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0039 #anie202110501-bib-0040"><sup>[39, 40]</sup></link> Even when the current density is 30 times higher (3 Ag<sup>−1</sup>), a high capacity of 63 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be output, accounting for 62 % of its capacity (102 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) at 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 E</link>, S12A). It is worth mentioning that the obvious capacity loss at high rate can be attributed to the intrinsically poor electronic conductivity of the PTPAn polymer (Figure S13). Meanwhile, the AG anode can retain 86 % of its capacity (116 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> vs. 135 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) after increasing the current density from 0.1 to 10 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0002">2 F</link>, S12B). This excellent rate capability can be attributed to the desolvation‐free characteristic of the anion‐doping mechanism in PTPAn and Na<sup>+</sup>–solvent co‐intercalation behavior in AG.</p><p>After the electrochemical investigation for single electrodes, PTPAn and AG are then utilized as cathode and anode, respectively, to configure desolvation‐free SDIBs based on 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME. The mass ratio of PTPAn and AG is accordingly set to be 1:1 with a slight excess of the AG anode in capacity to avoid Na‐plating during the following low‐temperature investigations. The operation voltage of the full cell was firstly monitored via a specific three‐electrode cell using Na metal as the reference electrode (Figure S14). It can be observed from voltage–time curves that setting the voltage range from 1 to 3.9 V is able to exactly charge PTPAn to 4.1 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) and thus deliver its full capacity within the half cell (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0003">3 A</link>). In addition, it is worth noting that the specific capacity of the full cell and applied current density shown below are both based on the mass of PTPAn. The AG∥PTPAn full cell can deliver a stable discharge capacity of around 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with an average voltage of 2.95 V after 10 cycles at 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (corresponding to 1C‐rate, Figure S15), outputting a considerable energy density of 148 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> (based on total mass of PTPAn and AG). Furthermore, an AG∥PTPAn SDIB that successfully circumvents the desolvation process shows a remarkable rate performance (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0003">3 B,C</link>). At the current density of 2 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge capacity of the AG∥PTPAn full cell can reach 82 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, revealing that such SDIB is capable of charging more than 80 % of the capacity in 150 s, which is far beyond state‐of‐the‐art LIBs.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0004 #anie202110501-bib-0017"><sup>[4, 17]</sup></link> Even at an extremely high current density of 10 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (about 100C), a reversible capacity of 45 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can also be obtained within 17 s, corresponding to a high retention up to 45 % of its capacity at 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>. Besides, such SDIB can also exhibit excellent cycling stability. As demonstrated in Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0003">3 D</link>, the AG∥PTPAn full cell still maintains 98 % of its initial capacity with a high average Coulombic efficiency of 99.3 % after 800 cycles at 1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>. The outstanding performance demonstrates the advantage of the desolvation‐free configuration with high rate feature and long‐term stability.</p><figure xml:id="anie202110501-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110501:anie202110501-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110501-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>A) In situ monitoring of an AG∥PTPAn full cell during charge/discharge with a three‐electrode device. The investigations are conducted with 0.5 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>–DEGDME electrolyte at 25 °C.</p></caption></figure><p>Low‐temperature performance loss of graphite‐based LIBs is an issue that researchers are pursuing to address all the time.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0005 #anie202110501-bib-0012"><sup>[5, 12]</sup></link> The limited charge capacity of LIBs (or discharge capacity of graphite) at subzero temperature is mainly attributed to a sluggish desolvation process at the graphite interphase, the dominant impedance contributor.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0007 #anie202110501-bib-0012"><sup>[7, 12]</sup></link> Therefore, utilizing co‐intercalation of solvents is undoubtedly a straightforward way to circumvent such a big obstacle. To prove the strategy, the low‐temperature operation of the AG anode with Na<sup>+</sup>–ether co‐intercalation mechanism is further investigated, whose capacity maintains 50 % at 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (1C‐rate) and 43 % with 0.3 Ag<sup>−1</sup> at −40 °C when compared to the capacity at 25 °C (Figure S16). The relatively poor low‐temperature rate capability of AG compared to PTPAn might arise from the almost half loss of ion‐diffusion coefficients (<i>D</i><sub>Na+</sub>) in the AG from 25 to −40 °C (Figure S9, S16 and S17). Even so, such performance still surpasses most conventional systems (Table S1). A further step shows that the AG∥PTPAn SDIB delivers a capacity of 53 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at −40 °C with 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, which is 53 % of that obtained at 25 °C with the same cut‐off voltage of 3.9 V. More capacity of 77 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained when increasing the cell voltage to 4.0 V (Figure S18). The abrupt increase of the cut‐off voltage might drive the anode potential across the threshold of Na‐plating and thus lead to the impairment of cycle life and even safety issues.<link href="#anie202110501-bib-0041"><sup>[41]</sup></link> To clarify this, in situ monitoring has been conducted with a three‐electrode device. As demonstrated in Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0004">4 A</link>, the discharge curves of AG are always kept above 0 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na), which perfectly avoids the plating of Na during the charge process of the full cell with the upper voltage of 4.0 V at −40 °C. Moreover, AG∥PTPAn full cell maintains 70 % of its initial capacity after 400 cycles with a high average Coulombic efficiency of 99.8 % at 0.1 Ag<sup>−1</sup>, exhibiting a remarkable cycling stability at −40 °C (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0004">4 B</link>). Considering its high capacity at low temperature, the rate capability of the desolvation‐free cell is studied from 0.01 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (0.1C‐rate) to 0.4 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (4C‐rate) at −40 °C. At a low current density of 0.01A g<sup>−1</sup>, such desolvation‐free chemistry can even possess a high capacity of 89 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0004">4 C</link> and S19), accounting for 89 % of its room‐temperature capacity. Excellent capacity retention can be realized up to 37 % at a high rate of 4C, where conventional rechargeable batteries can hardly be charged/discharged at such temperature (Table S2). Surprisingly, the full cell can operate well under an ultra‐low temperature of −70 °C with a reversible capacity of 44 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.01 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0004">4 C</link>), despite the kinetics limitation from the AG anode (Figure S20). When the cut‐off voltage is further increased to 4.1 V, the full cell can even deliver a reversible capacity as high as 61 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, the AG∥PTPAn full cell can restore its original capacity at 25 °C after low‐temperature operation (Figure S21). Given the remarkable rate performance and low‐temperature endurance of the desolvation‐free SDIB, the energy/power densities are calculated to estimate its practical application based on the total mass of cathode and anode (Figure <link href="#anie202110501-fig-0004">4 D</link>). Such a desolvation‐free AG∥PTPAn SDIB can deliver a considerable energy density of 148 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and a high power density of 10 526 W kg<sup>−1</sup> at 25 °C, also keeping an energy density of 117 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and power density of 450 W kg<sup>−1</sup> at −40 °C. Considering the electrolyte‐involved mechanism of the SDIB, the energy/power density based on the total mass of electrodes and consumed electrolyte is also estimated in Figure S22. The achieved performance demonstrates that the as‐fabricated desolvation‐free system possesses excellent kinetics and can find extensive applications where both high power and wide temperature range are required.</p><figure xml:id="anie202110501-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110501:anie202110501-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110501-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>A) In situ monitoring of an AG∥PTPAn full cell during charge/discharge with a three‐electrode device at −40 °C. The operational mechanism is based on the anion‐doping in the PTPAn cathode and the co‐intercalation of a Na<sup>+</sup>–ether complex in the AG anode. Such a chemistry can effectively circumvent the cation‐desolvation process that kinetically hinders conventional “rocking chair” batteries (e.g., LIBs) from achieving excellent rate and low‐temperature performance. As‐fabricated SDIB is capable of charging more than 80 % of the capacity within 150 s and even demonstrates super‐fast kinetics of 10 Ag<sup>−1</sup> (100C‐rate). Moreover, the absence of desolvation facilitates the easy operation at low temperature, maintaining 89 % and even 61 % of its room‐temperature capacity at −40 °C and an ultra‐low temperature of −70 °C. The system provides an alternative pathway towards developing future energy storage devices that require super‐high power density and wide operational temperature range.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202110501-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Key Research and Development Program of China (2018YFE0201702), Shanghai Science &amp; Technology Committee (19DZ2270100, 19DZ1205500), Chenguang Program supported by Shanghai Education Development Foundation and Shanghai Municipal Education Commission (19CG01).</p></section><section xml:id="anie202110501-sec-0005" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie202110501-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie202110501-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Winter</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>B.</givenNames> <familyName>Barnett</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Xu</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
