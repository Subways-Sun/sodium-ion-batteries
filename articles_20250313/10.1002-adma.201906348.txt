Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted incremental attention as a promising candidate for grid‐scale energy‐storage applications. To meet practical requirements, searching for new cathode materials with high energy density is of great importance. Herein, a novel Na superionic conductor (NASICON)‐type Na<sub>4</sub>MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> is developed as a high‐energy cathode for SIBs. The Na<sub>4</sub>MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> nanoparticles homogeneously embedded in a carbon matrix can present an extraordinary reversible capacity of 160.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with three‐electron reaction at ≈3.53 V during the Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion process, realizing an unprecedentedly high energy density of 566.5 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> in the phosphate cathodes for SIBs. It is intriguing to reveal the underlying mechanism of the unique Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup>, Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup>, and Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup> redox couples via X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure spectroscopy. The whole electrochemical reaction undergoes highly reversible single‐phase and biphasic transitions with a moderate volume change of 7.7% through in situ X‐ray diffraction and ex situ high‐energy synchrotron X‐ray diffraction. Combining density functional theory (DFT) calculations with the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique, the superior performance is ascribed to the low ionic‐migration energy barrier and desirable Na‐ion diffusion kinetics. The present work can offer a new insight into the design of multielectron‐reaction cathode materials for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201906348-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A novel NASICON‐type Na<sub>4</sub>MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode presents an unusual reversible Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup>, Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup>, and Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup> three‐electron reaction with a moderate volume change during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion, enabling an unprecedentedly high energy density in polyanionic cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201906348:adma201906348-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201906348-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201906348-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201906348-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>The growing demands for renewable clean energy have aroused an urgent need to develop large‐scale energy‐storage systems. Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been recognized as one of such ideal next‐generation energy‐storage technologies, owing to the low cost and abundant resources of sodium compared with its counterpart of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs).<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0001 #adma201906348-bib-0002 #adma201906348-bib-0003">1–3</link> Since the weight and radius of Na<sup>+</sup> are much larger than those of Li<sup>+</sup>, undesirable sluggish reaction kinetics and irreversible phase transition (even structural failure) of Na‐storage electrode materials commonly occur. It is particularly crucial to exploit promising host lattices with fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion channels and stable frameworks.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0004 #adma201906348-bib-0005 #adma201906348-bib-0006">4–6</link> Especially, designing high‐energy‐density cathode materials is of great significance to meet the practical requirements.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0007">7</link> Currently, various interesting cathode materials have been reported for SIBs, such as layered/tunnel‐type transition‐metal oxides,<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0008 #adma201906348-bib-0009 #adma201906348-bib-0010">8–10</link> polyanionic compounds,<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0011 #adma201906348-bib-0012 #adma201906348-bib-0013 #adma201906348-bib-0014 #adma201906348-bib-0015 #adma201906348-bib-0016 #adma201906348-bib-0017">11–17</link> Prussian blue analogues,<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0018 #adma201906348-bib-0019">18,19</link> and organic salts.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0020 #adma201906348-bib-0021">20,21</link> Among them, the polyanion‐type phosphates have always been a research hotspot by virtue of the strong binding energy of PO bonds, the 3D open frameworks, and the structural diversity.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0002 #adma201906348-bib-0004 #adma201906348-bib-0011 #adma201906348-bib-0012">2,4,11,12</link> For example, NaFePO<sub>4</sub> has long been studied as a cathode for SIBs considering the great success of its counterpart of LiFePO<sub>4</sub> in LIBs, the NaFePO<sub>4</sub> features a high theoretical specific capacity of 154 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and an average operating potential of 2.5–2.8 V.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0022">22</link> Afterward, pyrophosphates Na<sub>2</sub>MP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (M = Fe, Co, Mn) were developed as high‐voltage cathode materials for SIBs on account of the inducing effect of [P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>]<sup>4−</sup>.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0023 #adma201906348-bib-0024">23,24</link> Nevertheless, their specific capacity was restricted by the one‐electron reaction process. In recent years, Na superionic conductor (NASICON)‐structured Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> and its derivatives have been extensively investigated as two‐electron reaction SIB cathode materials,<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0025 #adma201906348-bib-0026 #adma201906348-bib-0027 #adma201906348-bib-0028 #adma201906348-bib-0029 #adma201906348-bib-0030 #adma201906348-bib-0031 #adma201906348-bib-0032">25–32</link> the Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> can deliver a reversible capacity of ≈110 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a relatively high voltage plateau positioned at 3.4 V, realizing a promising energy density of about 370 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0026 #adma201906348-bib-0027 #adma201906348-bib-0028 #adma201906348-bib-0029">26–29</link> Furthermore, the introduction of inductive F<sup>−</sup> into Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> to form Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>O<i><sub>x</sub></i>F<sub>3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 0–2) was identified to be an effective approach to elevate the working potential,<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0033 #adma201906348-bib-0034 #adma201906348-bib-0035">33–35</link> thereby the energy density could be significantly improved to a high level. Though very encouraging progress has been made, up to date, for most phosphate cathode materials, the number of electrons involved in the electrochemical reaction is still limited to one or two per formula unit, rendering the energy density less than 500 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0012 #adma201906348-bib-0036">12,36</link> It is highly desirable but remains a great challenge to seek a new cathode material that enables three‐electron reaction as well as high operating voltage for a higher energy density.</p><p>Herein, a hitherto‐unknown NASICON‐type Na<sub>4</sub>MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NMCP) has been successfully synthesized by a facile sol–gel method and developed as a three‐electron reaction cathode for SIBs. The as‐prepared NMCP/C nanocomposites demonstrated an intriguingly reversible three‐electron process corresponding to the Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup> (3.6 V), Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> (4.2 V), and Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup> (4.4 V) couples, respectively. Consequently, an admirable reversible capacity of 160.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 C together with a high average discharge voltage of 3.53 V has been achieved, realizing the highest energy density of 566.5 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> among all the phosphate Na‐storage cathodes reported so far, which is even higher than that of the commercialized LiFePO<sub>4</sub> in LIBs. It is interesting that the synergetic effect of Mn and Cr can not only stabilize the crystal structure along with the Mn<sup>2+/4+</sup> and Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup> transitions, but also improve the specific capacity, and increase the working voltage through the high‐potential redox centers.</p><p>The crystal structure of NMCP was determined by both high‐energy synchrotron X‐ray diffraction (SXRD, Figure S1, Supporting Information) and neutron powder diffraction (NPD). <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> presents the Rietveld refinement of the NPD pattern of NMCP/C, in which a good agreement has been obtained between the experimental and the calculated data (<i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> = 8.76%). The NMCP crystallizes in a rhombohedral structure (<i>R</i>−3<i>c</i>). The cell parameters of NMCP are <i>a</i> = <i>b</i> = 8.919 Å, <i>c</i> = 21.522 Å, and <i>V</i> = 1482.993 Å<sup>3</sup>, which enable a merit of larger lattice than that of the prototype composition of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (<i>a</i> = <i>b</i> = 8.738 Å, <i>c</i> = 21.815 Å, and <i>V</i> = 1442.480 Å<sup>3</sup>),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0037">37</link> providing sufficient diffusion channels for Na<sup>+</sup>. NPD is an essential method to investigate the possible cation ordering existence in the Mn/Cr octahedral sites. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0001">1a</link>, no additional characteristic peaks of long‐range ordering between Mn and Cr can be observed in the diffraction pattern, suggesting that Mn and Cr ions are randomly distributed in the lattice of NMCP.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0032 #adma201906348-bib-0038">32,38</link> The detailed information of atomic occupations and lattice parameters determined from NPD and SXRD are listed in Tables S1 and S2, Supporting Information, respectively. It needs to note that the Rietveld refinement results of NPD and SXRD are in good coincidence with each other, verifying the validity of the present crystal structure. As illustrated by the inset of Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0001">1a</link>, NMCP consists of corner‐shared Mn/CrO<sub>6</sub> octahedra and PO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra to establish a 3D framework of [MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>]. Due to the different oxygen coordination environments, two types of crystallographic Na sites are located in the interstitial structure, where the 6b (Na1) sites and the 18e (Na2) ones have six‐ and eightfold coordination, respectively. Considering the stronger bond energy of Na1O, it is more facile for Na2 to be extracted during electrochemical reaction.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0026 #adma201906348-bib-0039">26,39</link> The Rietveld refinement results of NPD further reveal that the occupancies for Na1 and Na2 are 1.0 and 0.945, respectively, resulting in 23.01 (1.000 × 6 + 0.945 × 18) sodium ions in each unit cell (<i>Z</i> = 6). There is a good agreement between the refined composition and the nominal chemical formula.</p><figure xml:id="adma201906348-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201906348:adma201906348-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201906348-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Rietveld structural refinement of neutron powder diffraction, b) SEM, c) HRTEM, and d) EDS element mapping images of NMCP/C.</caption></figure><p>The morphology and microstructure of NMCP/C were characterized by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). As displayed in Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0001">1b</link>, the as‐synthesized NMCP featuring monodispersed nanoparticles (with average sizes of 100–200 nm) are homogeneously embedded in carbon matrix. High‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image shows that the carbon layer with a thickness of about 6 nm is evenly coated on the nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0001">1c</link>). The regular lattice fringes with an interplanar spacing of 0.38 nm can be well assigned to the (113) plane of crystalline NMCP with a NASICON rhombohedral structure. The present sample of NMCP/C nanocomposite contains 12.4 wt% of carbon by thermogravimetric analysis (Figure S2, Supporting Information). For comparison, the samples with lower (7.2 wt%) and higher (18.2 wt%) carbon contents were also prepared. Energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) was performed to further analyze the chemical composition of NMCP/C. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0001">1d</link>, Na, Mn, Cr, P, O, and C elements are uniformly distributed within the particles, and the corresponding atomic ratio of elements is close to the stoichiometric configuration (the EDS spectrum in Figure S3, Supporting Information). The carbon structure in the as‐prepared NMCP/C sample exhibits amorphous character with abundant defects and vacancies, as indicated by the intensity ratio (1.1) of D (disorder‐induced) band to G (graphitic‐induced) band (I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub>) of Raman spectrum (Figure S4, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0040">40</link> As shown in the deconvoluted Mn 2p spectrum of X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy, a small amount of Mn<sup>3+</sup> can be found to coexist with the main valence state of Mn<sup>2+</sup> (Figure S5a, Supporting Information), which is similar to the recently reported Na<sub>3</sub>MnTi(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0041">41</link> The valence state of Cr is trivalent in the NMCP as indicated by the two peaks at ≈577.8 eV (Cr 2p<sub>3/2</sub>) and ≈587.1 eV (Cr 2p<sub>1/2</sub>) of the Cr 2p spectrum (Figure S5b, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0042">42</link> The N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms of NMCP/C exhibit a typical type‐IV isotherm hysteresis loop (Figure S6, Supporting Information). Correspondingly, the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) specific surface area is calculated to be 72.75 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) pore size distribution is centered at 7.5 nm.</p><p>The electrochemical properties of NMCP/C were evaluated by assembling CR2025 coin cells. The electrodes were charged/discharged under two different voltage windows of 1.4–4.3 V and 1.4–4.6 V, respectively. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> plots the galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles of NMCP/C within the voltage range of 1.4–4.3 V at a current density of 0.1 C (1 C = 111 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, based on two‐electron transfer). It is worth mentioning that the carbon matrix contributes negligible capacity to the composite (Figure S7, Supporting Information), thereby the specific capacity is calculated based on the mass of active NMCP in this study. The as‐prepared NMCP/C can deliver an initial charge/discharge capacity of 129/114 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 88.3% in the first cycle. The irreversible capacity is mainly attributed to the decomposition of electrolyte at high voltage and the detrimental Jahn–Teller effect of Mn<sup>3+</sup>.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0032">32</link> Noticeably, the initial CE of NMCP/C is higher than those of most Mn‐based phosphate cathodes for SIBs, such as Na<sub>3</sub>MnTi(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (70%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0043">43</link> Na<sub>4</sub>Mn<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (84%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0044">44</link> Na<sub>2</sub>MnPO<sub>4</sub>F (80%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0045">45</link> Na<sub>2</sub>MnP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (69%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0023">23</link> and Na<sub>3</sub>MnZr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (87%).<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0032">32</link> After three cycles, the discharge capacity still maintains 113.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, attesting the superior reversibility of the present NMCP/C. The obtained reversible capacity is slightly higher than the theoretical value of 110.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> based on 2e<sup>−</sup> transfer, which is caused by the Mn<sup>2+/3+</sup>, Mn<sup>3+/4+</sup>, and minor Cr<sup>3+/4+</sup> reactions (as will be discussed later). The cyclic voltammetry (CV) was performed within 1.4–4.3 V at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0002">2b</link>), there are two pairs of redox peaks located at about 3.45/3.75 V and 4.10/4.25 V, respectively, which are ascribed to the stepwise intercalation/deintercalation of two sodium ions and agree well with the charge/discharge profiles. In addition, the CV curves are well overlapped, further validating the highly reversible Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction process. The long‐term cyclic stability of NMCP/C was examined at a current density of 5 C (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0002">2c</link>). The electrode affords an initial discharge capacity of 55.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. Even after 600 cycles, a discharge capacity of 47.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can still be sustained, leading to a desirable capacity retention of 86.5%. The rate capability of NMCP/C was investigated by measuring coin cells under different current densities from 0.1 C to 15 C. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0002">2d</link>, the NMCP/C can deliver discharge capacities of 114.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 106.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 99.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 86.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 73.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 60.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 43.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, and 26.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C, 0.2 C, 0.5 C, 1 C, 2 C, 5 C, 10 C, and 15 C, respectively. The fascinating rate performance of NMCP/C is further highlighted in the inset of Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0004">4b</link>, which compares favorably with those of the previously reported Mn‐based phosphate cathodes for SIBs.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0023 #adma201906348-bib-0032 #adma201906348-bib-0043 #adma201906348-bib-0045">23,32,43,45</link> For comparison, the electrochemical performance of NMCP with a lower carbon content (7.2 wt%) was examined (Figure S8, Supporting Information). Only a reversible capacity of 60 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained at 0.1 C, demonstrating that the carbon coating plays a critical role in improving the electrochemical activity of the present NMCP. The rate capability of NMCP/C with a higher carbon content of 18.2 wt% was also measured (Figure S9, Supporting Information). Although the electrode exhibits excellent high‐rate tolerance, the energy density is not comparable to that of NMCP/C (12.4 wt%) when taking the total mass loading into account. Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were carried out to assess the electronic/ionic conductivity of the electrodes with different carbon contents (Figure S10, Supporting Information). The fitting results reveal that the electrodes with higher carbon contents display lower charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) and faster Na‐ion diffusivity, manifesting the great significance of carbon to the rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="adma201906348-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201906348:adma201906348-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201906348-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles at a current density of 0.1 C, and b) CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> of the NMCP/C cathode within the voltage window of 1.4–4.3 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. c) Cycling performance at 5 C and d) rate capability of the NMCP/C electrode. e) Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique measurement of NMCP/C and f) the corresponding calculated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients at different charge/discharge states.</caption></figure><p>Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) measurements and CV curves at different scan rates were performed between 1.4 and 4.3 V in order to estimate the electrode process kinetics of NMCP/C. Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0002">2e</link> depicts the typical charge/discharge GITT curves at the second cycle. The cell was charged and discharged at 0.1 C for 30 min and relaxed for 120 min to make the voltage reach equilibrium. The calculated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients (<i>D</i><sub>Na</sub>) are about 10<sup>−11</sup> to 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0002">2f</link>; Figure S11, Supporting Information), which are comparable to those of the NASICON‐type Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode materials.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0028">28</link> From the CV curves at various scan rates (0.2–1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>), the <i>D</i><sub>Na</sub> values of NMCP/C electrode are calculated to be around 10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S12a,b, Supporting Information), in good accordance with the GITT result. In general, faradaic intercalation and surface‐controlled adsorption (pseudocapacitance) both exist in carbon–coated nanostructured materials.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0014 #adma201906348-bib-0046">14,46</link> Specifically, the pseudocapacitive contribution in NMCP/C is identified to be ≈32% at a scan rate of 0.6 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S12c,d, Supporting Information). The moderate pseudocapacitance is essential to the outstanding rate performance.</p><p>It is interesting to find that when the present NMCP/C is charged/discharged within a wider potential range of 1.4–4.6 V, three distinct voltage plateaus occur at 3.6 V, 4.2 V, and 4.4 V, respectively (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>). The electrode can deliver an initial charge capacity of 196 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 C (1 C = 160 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, based on three‐electron transfer). Intriguingly, an extraordinary reversible discharge capacity of 160.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and a decent average working voltage of 3.53 V are achieved during the following Na<sup>+</sup> insertion process. With the elevation of charge cut‐off voltage, the NMCP/C enables an unprecedentedly high energy density of 566.5 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> among the phosphate Na‐storage cathodes reported so far which will be discussed later in detail. In accordance with the charge–discharge profiles, the CV curves at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> within the wide voltage range of 1.4–4.6 V (Figure S13, Supporting Information) display three distinct pairs of redox peaks located at about 3.43/3.75 V, 4.11/4.26 V, and 4.34/4.43 V, respectively. As for the cyclic stability, a promising capacity retention of 74% is attained after 50 cycles at 0.2 C (Figure S14, Supporting Information). The capacity fading is mainly caused by the decomposition of electrolyte at high voltage and the detrimental Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller distortions. As is well known, fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) is recognized as a suitable additive to restrict the decomposition of electrolyte and benefit the formation of a stable solid electrolyte interface film on electrodes.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0047 #adma201906348-bib-0048">47,48</link> In this scenario, the electrolyte containing 10 vol% FEC additive (5% more) was employed, and the cycling performance of the NMCP/C cathode has been significantly improved (Figure S15, Supporting Information). The voltage profiles of NMCP/C at 0.05 C in the electrolyte with 10 vol% FEC are plotted in Figure S16, Supporting Information, the charge/discharge capacity of 176.2/152.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> in the first cycle corresponds to an enhanced initial Coulombic efficiency with a lower irreversible capacity, further verifying our assumption that FEC can effectively restrict the electrolyte decomposition and relieve the capacity decay. Moreover, the rate capability of NMCP/C between 1.4 and 4.6 V along with the charge–discharge curves is provided in Figure S17, Supporting Information. The electrode can display respectable discharge capacities of 151.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 143.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 133.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 121.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 108.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 89.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, 70.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C, 0.2 C, 0.5 C, 1.0 C, 2.0 C, 5.0 C, 10.0 C, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="adma201906348-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201906348:adma201906348-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201906348-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles at a current density of 0.05 C of NMCP/C within the voltage window of 1.4–4.6 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. b,c) XANES spectra of Mn K‐edge (b) and Cr K‐edge (c) of the NMCP/C electrode collected at different charged and discharged states. d) Fourier‐transformed Mn K‐edge EXAFS spectra during the charge process. e) In situ XRD patterns of NMCP/C during the first cycle, along with the selected (300) diffraction profiles.</caption></figure><p>In order to unveil the valence variations of Mn and Cr in NCMP/C during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion, ex situ X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure (XANES) spectroscopy was conducted. Before the measurements, all samples were disassembled and sealed directly into Capton film in a glovebox to avoid exposure to air. Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0003">3b</link> presents the Mn K‐edge spectra at different charged/discharged states during the first cycle. Upon charging to 3.8 V, the energy of Mn K‐edge shifts rightward, indicating the valence change from Mn<sup>2+</sup> to Mn<sup>3+</sup>. As further charged to 4.3 V, the energy shows a significant shift to a higher level, manifesting further oxidation from Mn<sup>3+</sup> to Mn<sup>4+</sup>. When the charge cut‐off voltage is continuously raised to a higher value of 4.6 V, no difference occurs in the energy shift of Mn K‐edge compared with that at 4.3 V, suggesting that Mn is fully oxidized at 4.3 V. To reveal the reversibility of Mn valence under different charge depths, two electrodes were discharged to 1.4 V after finishing the charge measurements at 4.3 and 4.6 V. Both of them can recover to the energy state which is close to the pristine material, attesting the highly reversible Mn valence change. Considering the different coordination environments of phosphate and oxides, the standard oxides of MnO, Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and MnO<sub>2</sub> were not used as the references for comparing the oxidation states of Mn.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0015">15</link> Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0003">3c</link> presents the Cr K‐edge absorption spectra at different states. It is very interesting to observe that there is also a shift in the energy of Cr K‐edge upon charging, especially, the pre‐edge peak of Cr displays a dramatic change (the inset of Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0003">3c</link>). When charged to 3.8 V, the pre‐edge peak remains unchanged at 5990 eV, similar to the pristine electrode. However, upon charging to 4.3 V, the pre‐edge peak splits into two peaks located at 5990 eV and 5992 eV, respectively, which can be attributed to the 1s to 3d transition due to the Cr oxidation. The small pre‐edge peak at 5992 eV is exactly the indication of Cr<sup>4+</sup>. Such phenomenon has also been observed in other Cr‐based cathode materials for SIBs.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0049 #adma201906348-bib-0050">49,50</link> When the electrode was further charged to 4.6 V, the intensity of the pre‐edge peak at 5992 eV is significantly increased but not the energy. It means a continuous oxidation from Cr<sup>3+</sup> to Cr<sup>4+</sup>. Therefore, we can conclude that a small amount of Cr<sup>3+</sup> undergoes valence variation to Cr<sup>4+</sup> when charged to 4.3 V, and more Cr<sup>3+</sup> can be oxidized to Cr<sup>4+</sup> upon charging to 4.6 V. More impressively, after being discharged to 1.4 V, the pre‐edge peak recovers back to the pristine state, corroborating the excellent reversibility of the whole reaction. Based on the above discussions, it can be concluded that the three reversible plateaus, which are located at 3.6 V, 4.2 V, and 4.4 V, correspond to the Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup>, Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup>, and Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup> redox couples, respectively. The extraordinary specific capacity in the present NMCP material is highly correlated with the unique three‐electron transfer process, namely, Na<sub>4</sub>Mn(II)Cr(III)(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> – 3Na<sup>+</sup> – 3e<sup>−</sup> ⇄ NaMn(IV)Cr(IV)(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>. To deeply understand the local environment of Mn upon sodiation/desodiation, the extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) analyses have been performed. Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0003">3d</link> exhibits the Fourier‐transformed Mn K‐edge EXAFS spectra of NMCP during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction. When the electrode is charged to 3.8 V, the peak of MnO bonds splits into two peaks with decreasing intensity, implying that the MnO<sub>6</sub> octahedra experience positive Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller distortions with four lengthened MnO bonds and two shortened MnO bonds. Such a phenomenon was also observed in the previous study on LiMnPO<sub>4</sub>.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0051">51</link> Moreover, with further charging to 4.3 V, the two peaks emerge into one peak again, which suggests another valence transformation from Mn<sup>3+</sup> to Mn<sup>4+</sup>.</p><p>For the purpose of illuminating the structural evolution in NMCP/C during the Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion process, in situ XRD has been carried out. The in situ XRD patterns were collected within the voltage range of 1.4–4.6 V using Al foil as both current collector and monitoring window (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0003">3e</link>). In general, all the diffraction peaks undergo significant shift and progressively recover to the initial state, demonstrating that the full charge/discharge reaction is highly reversible. The gradual shift of some peaks signifies a single‐phase solid‐solution reaction, whereas the emergence of new peaks or disappearance of existing peaks represents phase transition. Specifically, the (211) profile shifts rightward, and completely disappears upon charging. At discharging, the peak gradually reappears. Furthermore, upon desodiation, the (300) peak experiences rightward shift, splits into two peaks, and subsequently continues to shift rightward. During the sodiation process, the peak of (300) completely restores back to the pristine state. To elucidate the detailed structural change, we conducted the Rietveld refinement of ex situ high‐energy SXRD on the samples at two different charged states of 4.3 and 4.6 V. As shown in Figure S18 and Tables S2 and S3, Supporting Information, there is a systematical change in lattice parameters as follows: the pristine Na<sub>4</sub>MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (<i>a</i> = 8.912 Å, <i>c</i> = 21.493 Å), Na<sub>2</sub>MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (4.3 V, <i>a</i> = 8.646 Å, <i>c</i> = 21.741 Å), and NaMnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (4.6 V, <i>a</i> = 8.510 Å, <i>c</i> = 21.760 Å). The <i>a</i>‐axis decreases, while the <i>c</i>‐axis increases. The unit cell volume expands (Δ<i>V</i>/<i>V</i><sub>pristine</sub>) at 4.8% and 7.7% for the two charged states of 4.3 V and 4.6 V, respectively. The increased <i>c</i>‐axis and decreased <i>a</i>‐axis manifest that the Na2 sites gradually become empty, while the Na1 sites are still highly occupied.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0043">43</link> It needs to be noted that the volume expansion of the current three‐electron reaction in NMCP is similar to those of the one‐electron reaction or two‐electron reaction materials such as Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (8.3%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0037">37</link> Na<sub>3</sub>MnZr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (5.5%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0032">32</link> Na<sub>4</sub>Mn<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> (7%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0044">44</link> Na<sub>3</sub>MnTi(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (4.8%),<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0043">43</link> and LiFePO<sub>4</sub> (6.8%).<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0052">52</link> This indicates that the high‐potential Cr redox center can not only contribute to the reversible capacity, but also stabilize the structure by alleviating the detrimental impact from the Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller effect.</p><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link> summarizes the discharge voltage as a function of specific capacity for the present NMCP material and the available polyanion‐type cathodes. Not only higher discharge voltage but also higher specific capacity is demanded toward excellent performance of energy density. In the present work, it is very interesting to achieve an energy density as high as 566.5 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> (specific capacity of 160.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with operating voltage of 3.53 V). It is the first realization of energy density more than 550 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> for the phosphate SIB cathode materials, which is even higher than that of the commercialized LiFePO<sub>4</sub>/Li batteries.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0052">52</link></p><figure xml:id="adma201906348-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201906348:adma201906348-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201906348-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Comparisons of energy density between this work and other developed polyanionic cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries.</caption></figure><p>To deeply figure out the origin of the superior Na‐storage behavior of NMCP/C, density functional theory (DFT) calculations were performed to elucidate the Na<sup>+</sup> migration paths and barriers. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>, four possible migration pathways are rationally built following Na1→Na2 (P1), Na2→Na1 (P2), Na1→Na1 (P3), and Na2→Na2 (P4). Based on the climbing‐image nudged elastic band method,<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0053">53</link> the calculated energy barriers for the P1, P2, P3, and P4 paths are 0.38 eV, 0.38 eV, 3.65 eV, and 1.59 eV, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0005">5b</link>). Therefore, one can see that the Na1–Na2–Na1 channels (depicted by the blue isosurfaces in Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0005">5a</link>) in parallel with the [MnCr(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>] framework are the most energy favorable pathways for Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion. The corresponding migration barrier (0.38 eV) is comparable to that of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>, and is much smaller than those of most reported SIB cathode materials.<link href="#adma201906348-bib-0014 #adma201906348-bib-0016 #adma201906348-bib-0046">14,16,46</link> In addition, the projected density of states (DOS) of NMCP exhibit some metallic properties especially for the spin up electrons (Figure <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0005">5c</link>, the band structure is provided in Figure S19, Supporting Information). The excellent reaction kinetics and desirable rate performance of NMCP are ascribed to the low ionic‐migration energy barrier and decent electronic conductivity.</p><figure xml:id="adma201906348-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201906348:adma201906348-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201906348-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Possible Na<sup>+</sup> migration pathways and b) corresponding migration energy barriers in the NMCP crystal. c) Density of states of NMCP.</caption></figure><p>The fascinating Na‐storage performance of NMCP/C inspires us to explore its practical feasibility for full batteries. The hard carbon anode was matched with the NMCP/C cathode to assemble Na‐ion full cells, as schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201906348-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link>. The galvanostatic charge/discharge curves and cycling performance of the hard carbon anode are provided in Figure S20, Supporting Information. The crystal structure with <i>R</i>‐3<i>c</i> space group was verified by the NPD and high‐energy SXRD Rietveld refinements. The NMCP nanoparticles homogeneously distributed in carbon matrix could deliver an initial discharge capacity of 114 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage window of 1.4–4.3 V with a prominent capacity retention of 86.5% after 600 cycles. When the charge cut‐off voltage was extended to 4.6 V, the cell could display an extraordinary reversible capacity of 160.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with an average discharge voltage of 3.53 V, harvesting the energy density record of 566.5 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> for the phosphate Na‐storage cathode materials. Confirmed by the ex situ XANES measurements, the uncommon three‐electron reaction with Mn<sup>2+</sup>/Mn<sup>3+</sup>, Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup>, and Cr<sup>3+</sup>/Cr<sup>4+</sup> redox couples is responsible for the ultrahigh specific capacity. The DFT computations and GITT measurements manifest that the low ionic‐migration energy barrier and facilitated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusivity account for the outstanding rate capability of NMCP/C. In situ XRD and ex situ synchrotron XRD further elaborate that the structure experiences highly reversible single phase and biphasic transitions with small volume changes of 4.8% and 7.7% over the two‐electron and three‐electron processes, respectively. Future studies on metal‐ion doping, morphology design, and surface engineering may further improve the cyclic stability of the present interesting three‐electron reaction cathode material. The concept of designing reversible multielectron reaction cathode materials can be extended to the family of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>M<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (M = transition metal), which opens a new window for the development of high‐energy‐density cathodes for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201906348-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (21825102, 21805007, and 21731001), the Young Elite Scientists Sponsorship Program by CAST (2018QNRC001), and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (FRF‐TP‐18‐001C2 and FRF‐TP‐19‐029A2).
