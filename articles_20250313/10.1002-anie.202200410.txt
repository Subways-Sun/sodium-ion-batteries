To compensate for the shortage of energy density, the most accessible solution is developing a high‐voltage anode‐free configuration comprising a lightweight Al current collector on the anode and a high‐voltage sodiumized cathode. However, it imposes stringent Na reversibility and high‐voltage stability requirements on the electrolyte. A 3A zeolite molecular sieve film is rationally designed, and a highly aggregated solvation structure is constructed through the size effect. It suppresses the trace but continuous oxidative decomposition and extends the oxidative stability to 4.5 V without sacrificing the Na reversibility of the anode (99.91 %). Thus, we can make anode‐free cells with high energy density of 369 and 372 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> for 4.0 and 4.25 V class cells, respectively. Furthermore, this strategy enables a long lifespan (250 cycles) for 4.0 V‐class anode‐free cells.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A highly aggregated ether electrolyte is rationally constructed by introducing a 3A zeolite molecular sieve film. Benefitting from the highly aggregated electrolyte configuration, it enables a dramatically improved oxidative stability. Under the extremely harsh anode‐free conditions, the high‐voltage anode‐free Na battery configuration has an ultrahigh energy density of 369 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> for 4.0 V class cathodes.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie202200410-sec-0001"><p>Sodium (Na) has larger atomic mass and radius compared with lithium (Li). The subtle difference leads to a higher redox potential for the anode and a lower storage capacity for the cathode in Na‐ion batteries, resulting in a significantly lower energy density than Li‐ion batteries.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> In addition, the cost‐effective graphite in Li‐ion batteries is not suitable for the storage of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0006"><sup>[2]</sup></link> The generally used hard carbon anodes in Na‐ion batteries not only have lower capacity than graphite,<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0009"><sup>[3]</sup></link> but also cost more than 1.5 times that of graphite.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0012"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Although Na‐ion batteries are regarded as a promising candidate for Li‐ion batteries, it would be difficult to develop practical applications even in the field of large‐scale energy storage if the anxiety in energy density and cost cannot be well resolved.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0013 #anie202200410-bib-0015"><sup>[4a, 5]</sup></link> The high‐voltage anode‐free configuration provides the possibility for Na batteries to compete or even replace conventional Li‐ion batteries in terms of energy density and cost.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0018"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Compared with conventional Na‐ion batteries, the anode‐free configuration remarkably increases the energy density by 60 % and reduces the cost by 40 %. In this case, the energy density and cost are more competitive than traditional LiFePO<sub>4</sub>‐graphite‐based Li‐ion batteries due to the elimination of negative active materials and the use of lightweight Al current collectors.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0013 #anie202200410-bib-0020"><sup>[4a, 6b]</sup></link> The high element abundance and low cost of Na and Al would greatly improve the sustainability of this battery technology. Another advantage that cannot be ignored is that the safety problems can be completely addressed. During practical industrial cell fabrication procedure, the Na metal is eliminated, which provides a more sustainable path towards advanced energy storage devices. However, gaining the highest benefit from an anode‐free (zero Na excess) configuration demands the relentless challenge of ultra‐high Na reversibility (both anode and cathode).</p><p>The generally used carbonate electrolytes have excellent oxidation stability, and the optimized ester electrolytes have been successfully adopted in high‐voltage anode‐free Li batteries.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0023"><sup>[7]</sup></link> However, ester electrolytes are highly reactive with Na metal, resulting in the continuous release of flammable gases.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0027"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Furthermore, ester electrolytes fail to develop a robust solid‐electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer and promote uniform Na deposition. The efficiency of Na stripping‐plating is less than 90 %, which makes it unfeasible to build up an anode‐free battery.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0032"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Fortunately, a series of electrolytes were reported gaining a high Coulombic efficiency (CE) of over 99.9 %.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0035"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Nonetheless, there is little literature on high‐voltage anode‐free Na batteries using ether electrolytes, which may be attributed to its poor anti‐oxidation property. Even using a cathode with low cut‐off voltage below 4.0 V (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>), its lifespan in anode‐free cells is still very limited.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0020 #anie202200410-bib-0022"><sup>[6b,d]</sup></link> The poor oxidative stability of ether electrolytes indeed restricts the choice of high‐voltage cathodes (≥4.0 V cut‐off voltage) and undermines the energy‐density of anode‐free Na batteries. Increasing the salt concentration<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0036"><sup>[11]</sup></link> or using metal organic frameworks (MOFs)<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0043"><sup>[12]</sup></link> to construct a highly concentrated electrolyte has been demonstrated to be effective in reducing the solvent activity and extending the oxidative stability window in Li‐ion batteries. However, the generally used Na salts such as sodium hexafluoride phosphate (NaPF<sub>6</sub>) and sodium tetrafluoroborate (NaBF<sub>4</sub>) favor high Na reversibility but have low solubility in ether solvents.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0046"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The sodium bis(fluorosulfonyl)imide (NaFSI) has a considerable solubility (5.2 M) in 1,2‐dimethoxyethane (DME), but it has a CE below 98 %.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0049"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Even an optimized co‐solvent is introduced to construct a localized high concentration electrolyte, its CE is only increased to 99 %, and there are no reports of increased oxidative stability. There is no such electrolyte yet to reach the high reversibility of Na metal at anodes while maintaining the high‐voltage stability.</p><p>In this work, a novel electrolyte with solubility beyond the limit is first achieved by a 3A zeolite molecular sieve film in Na batteries. The well‐defined pore size (3.2 Å) in zeolite gives a unique de‐solvation process due to the size effect and highly aggregated solvation structures are trapped in its pores. Benefitting from the highly aggregated electrolyte configuration, it enables a dramatically improved oxidative stability for ether electrolytes (from original 2.5 V to 4.5 V vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). Therefore, the unique electrolyte enables an ultra‐long lifespan of 600 and 800 cycles for O3‐NaCu<sub>1/9</sub>Ni<sub>2/9</sub>Fe<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathodes (4.0 V cut‐off voltage) and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> cathodes (4.25 V cut‐off voltage), respectively. At the same time, the high reversibility at Na anodes is well preserved. Therefore, even under an extremely harsh condition of anode‐free that is zero excess of Na metal, NNCFM‐based anode‐free Na battery possesses the highest energy density of 369 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> and an ultralong lifespan of 250 cycles is achieved for the first time.</p><p>Low‐cost and sustainable 3A zeolite molecular sieves have unique and well‐defined channel, which are widely used for gas separations by the size effect.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0050"><sup>[15]</sup></link> Similarly, solvation structures with different sizes can be sieved by the fixed channel size (Figure S1). This change in solvation structures can be well observed in Raman and Fourier‐transform infrared spectroscopy (FT‐IR). The pure diglyme (G2) solvent, NaPF<sub>6</sub> and a series of electrolytes with different concentrations are also tested for comparison. As shown in Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0001">1</link>b and S2a, a Raman peak located at about 742 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be identified for all electrolytes except pure solvents. This peak is assigned as A1 g mode for P−F stretching in PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup>,<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0048 #anie202200410-bib-0054"><sup>[13b, 16]</sup></link> which well interprets the peak absence in pure G2 solvents. Its shift is a distinctive feature of the bonding formation between the anion and Na<sup>+</sup> through fluorine atoms.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0055"><sup>[17]</sup></link> As the concentration increases, a blue‐shift can be seen for this peak, which is usually attributed to ion association interaction transformation. Ion association interactions are generally classified as solvent‐separated‐ion‐pair (SSIP), contact‐ion‐pair (CIP) and aggregate (AGG) coordination according to the number of coordination bonds between anions and cations. In SSIP, it is coordinated to a pair of diglyme molecules through six oxygen atoms. A PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> anion is coordinated to a single Na<sup>+</sup> cation through three fluorine atoms (CIP‐III), which dominate the stable CIP structure. AGG‐I generally exists as the PF<sub>6</sub><sup>−</sup> anions coordinated with two Na<sup>+</sup> cations. Unfortunately, the peak position difference between SSIP and CIP‐III is less than 1 cm<sup>−1</sup>,<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0054 #anie202200410-bib-0055"><sup>[16, 17]</sup></link> which cannot be distinguished as the spectral resolution is 1 cm<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0055"><sup>[17]</sup></link> However, the AGG‐I peak has a well‐defined position at about 750 cm<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0056"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Compared with saturated electrolytes, the A1g mode in 3A zeolite modified electrolytes shows a remarkably stronger AGG peak, which is impossible to achieve by conventional chemical methods. The G2 solvent is polarized by Na<sup>+</sup> under the strong electric field, providing lone pair electrons to Na<sup>+</sup>, thereby lowering the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) energy level.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0057"><sup>[19]</sup></link> And the higher the coordination number between the solvent and the Na<sup>+</sup>, the lower the HOMO energy level, thus showing better oxidative stability (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0001">1</link>d).<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0042 #anie202200410-bib-0057"><sup>[11f, 19]</sup></link> Therefore, the 3A zeolite modified electrolyte is expected to exhibit good oxidative stability as the AGG dominates the solvation structure. The function of the molecular sieve in transforming solvation structure can also be seen from the vibration of G2 solvents. For pure G2 solvent, three peaks located at 806.9, 827.5 and 853.5 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be distinguished, which correspond to the CH<sub>2</sub> rock and C−O−C stretching of the free solvent (Figure S2b). Two additional coordinated peaks at 847.6 and 879.2 cm<sup>−1</sup> emerge due to the coordination between Na<sup>+</sup> cation and the G2 solvent.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0056 #anie202200410-bib-0058"><sup>[18, 20]</sup></link> A vibrational bands undergo a slight blue‐shift due to the ion‐dipole attraction between the dissociated Na<sup>+</sup> ions and oxygens of the solvent molecule. Interestingly, the peak of coordinated solvents is stronger in 3A zeolite modified electrolytes compared to the saturated electrolyte. Correspondingly, peaks associated with free solvent in the modified electrolyte decreased significantly. It can also be seen for CH<sub>2</sub> wagging with FT‐IR activity.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0059"><sup>[21]</sup></link> The free solvent peak at 851.9 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be seen in pure G2 solvents (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0001">1</link>c). As the salt concentration increases, a red‐shift can be observed due to the improved interaction between solvents and Na<sup>+</sup> ions. The electrolyte in the zeolite reaches a state where the coordinated solvent dominates the solvation structure. In this case, there is almost no free solvent in the modified electrolytes. The dramatically improved aggregation and largely reduced free solvents enabled by 3A zeolite is expected to give considerable oxidative stability.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0037 #anie202200410-bib-0038 #anie202200410-bib-0039 #anie202200410-bib-0041"><sup>[11a–c,e]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202200410-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The construction path of high‐voltage anode‐free Na batteries. a) A high‐voltage anode‐free Na battery is constructed by optimizing the electrolyte aggregation through using a 3A zeolite molecular sieve. b, c) Raman (b) and FT‐IR (c) spectra of various electrolytes. d) Schematic diagram of the relationship between electrolyte solvation structure and oxidative stability.</p></caption></figure><p>The oxidative stability of the electrolyte was systematically analyzed to evaluate its practical application performance under high voltage. Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0002">2</link>a shows the stability of the 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> in G2 electrolytes at different potential regions through linear sweep cyclic voltammetry (LSV) tests at a scan rate of 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The decomposition occurs at about 2.5 V and severe decomposition can be identified after 4.5 V. In previous reports, the oxidative stability was tested at a high scanning rate. At the same time, the scale of the coordinate axis is very large, which obscures the weak oxidation current at low potential and only the obvious oxidation current after 4.5 V can be identified. This improper operation gives the wrong conclusion that the electrolyte is stable until 4.5 V. In fact, when a high‐voltage NVPF cathode (4.25 V cut‐off voltage) is used for evaluating, it can only run for 4 cycles, suggesting that the neglected weak decomposition dominates the actual stability of the battery. When the electrolyte was modified by introducing a 3A zeolite molecular sieve film on the cathode side, almost negligible oxidative current could be identified before 4.5 V, implying a significant improvement in oxidation stability. Note that zeolite film does not replace the separator, we only apply it on the cathode side. The oxidation of electrolyte is always accompanied by the continuous accumulation of gases and oxidation species. Then, the gas evolution was monitored by using differential electrochemical mass spectroscopy (DEMS) (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0002">2</link>b). Even at low potential of 2.8–3.5 V, methane (CH<sub>4</sub>, <i>m</i>/<i>z=16</i>) displays a fast production rate, and the evolution rate continues to improve with increasing applied voltage in pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes. In contrast, in the 3A zeolite modified electrolytes, CH<sub>4</sub> remains at a very low and stable level throughout the entire scanning voltage range. Under a constant potential of 4.0 V, its oxidative current remains at low level within 12 h (Figure S3). The electron transfer and gas evolution were then stimulated by the integration of oxidation current, indicating that the gas production in the 3A zeolite modified electrolyte was significantly reduced (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0002">2</link>c). In addition, the oxidation by‐products were clearly identified in <sup>1</sup>H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) results (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0002">2</link>d). After holding at 4.0 V for 12 h in pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes, two peaks marked with squares can be identified, which can be attributed to alcohol‐related and alkenyl‐related species induced by unstable EG<sub>2</sub><sup>−</sup> and C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>O intermediate products (Table S1). In contrast, for the 3A zeolite modified electrolytes, almost no obvious by‐product signal was observed after holding 12 h at 4.0 V. Based on the above results, the gas evolution reaction can be inferred as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0002">2</link>b. The Gibbs free energy (Δ<i>G</i>) of this reaction is 121.5 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup> (detailed data shown in Table S1), which theoretically occurs at 3.34 V. This value is slightly higher than the 2.5 V observed in our study, which may be due to the catalytic activity of the current collector used for testing.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200410-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Oxidative stability evaluation of the electrolytes. a) Interpreting the stability of pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes in different voltage regions. b) DEMS results of CH<sub>4</sub> signal (<i>m</i>/<i>z</i>=16) observed during LSV in pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes. c) The evolution of gas with time in those two electrolytes. d) By‐product analysis based on <sup>1</sup>H NMR results after holding for 12 h at 4.0 V.</p></caption></figure><p>The oxidative stability of electrolytes was then examined by galvanostatic cycling of 4.0 V Na half‐cells with NNCFM cathodes. The usage of Na metal at anode far exceeds consumption, which ensures exclusive failure factor of oxidative stability of electrolytes and its structural damage to cathodes. The detailed physical characterizations of NNCFM are shown in Figures S4–6. In pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes, the Na‐NNCFM half‐cell can only operate stably for about 70 cycles at 1.0 C based on two sets of parallel cells (1.0 C=100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). Severe capacity degradation can be observed, and the battery is damaged by overcharge at the 68th cycle (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0003">3</link>a). The decomposition products can also be identified from the NMR results marked with circles (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0003">3</link>e). It suggests that G2 solvents in pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes is involved in parasitic oxidative side reactions, the complete consumption of which would lead to cell failure. After attaching a 3A zeolite film on the NNCFM cathode, the lifespan is increased to 600 cycles while maintaining a high CE (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0003">3</link>c), with little capacity fading for the first 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0003">3</link>b). After 600 cycles, a high capacity of 76.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is still maintained. In addition, negligible by‐product signals can be detected from the <sup>1</sup>H NMR results of 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (after 220 cycles) (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0003">3</link>e). When the mass loading increased to 8.4 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, the cycling stability was well maintained within 350 cycles at 0.5 C. (Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0003">3</link>d and S7). Furthermore, the SEM image of 3A zeolite film implies a good structural maintenance after long‐term cycling (Figure S8), which ensures its function in suppressing electrolyte decomposition and maintaining a neat NNCFM surface (Figure S9).</p><figure xml:id="anie202200410-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Na‐NNCFM half‐cell performance. a, b) Selected discharge/charge curves of Na‐NNCFM batteries with pristine electrolytes (a) and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (b). c) The cyclic stability of Na‐NNCFM batteries in those two electrolytes. d) The cyclic stability of Na‐NNCFM batteries under high mass loading. e) The <sup>1</sup>H NMR results of cycled electrolytes.</p></caption></figure><p>To elucidate the mechanism of the improved oxidative stability, the cycled NNCFM electrodes were analyzed by Transmission electron microscope (TEM) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). For the NNCFM cathode cycled in pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (after 68 cycles), an amorphous cathode electrolyte interphase (CEI) layer was unevenly coated on the surface of NNCFM crystals due to the decomposition of electrolytes under high voltage (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>a). Besides, the phase transition can be clearly observed from TEM images (Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>a and S10), which exhibits a larger lattice spacing compared with the bulk NNCFM crystal. The phase transition may be induced by the high reactivity of the electrolyte and is responsible for the rapid capacity decay of the battery. An opposite result was observed for 3A zeolite modified electrolyte group, where cycled NNCFM cathodes maintained typical ordered lattice fringes and no CEIs and phase transitions were observed (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>b and S11). In addition to the structural differences in CEIs, their components were further investigated by XPS. For the NNCFM cathode extracted from pristine electrolyte group, the Ni signal cannot be detected due to the presence of the CEI on the surface. A weak Ni 2p signal was detected (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>c) only after 7 min of Ar<sup>+</sup> etching. By contrast, for the cycled NNCFM cathode obtained from the 3A zeolite‐modified electrolyte, clear Ni signals were always detected at different etching times (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>d). The same results can also be seen from Mn 2p and Fe 2p signals (Figure S12). For the C 1s spectrum located at 289.5 eV, it is generally attributed to C=O species in CEIs,<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0042"><sup>[11f]</sup></link> which maintains a high intensity even after 9 min etching for the NNCFM cathode cycled in pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>e). Moreover, there is no C−Fx bond (291 eV) of PVDF binders, which entirely demonstrates the formation of a thick CEI layer originating from electrolyte decomposition. According to the fitting results of C1s on cycled NNCFM cathodes, 10.1 % C−Metal bonds can be observed (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4 f</link>), which may be due to the formation of metal carbides or similar C‐metal interactions when the electrolyte decomposes on the surface of NNCFM. The O=C−O and C=O bonds also occupy a high proportion, which are the main components of a typical CEI. After etching for 9 min, the proportion of C−Metal bonds increased to 17.3 % and the proportion of O=C−O bonds also increased significantly. This is due to the increased reactivity as it gets closer to the NNCFM cathode, resulting in the formation of more metal carbides or species with C‐metal interactions. The existence of this C‐metal chemical bond may be related to the phase transition observed from TEM images. For the C1s signal of NNCFM cathode harvested from 3A zeolite modified electrolytes, a weak C−Fx peak is observed at about 191 eV, and no clear C=O bond can be detected due to the absence of CEIs (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>g). Detailed information can be obtained from the fitting results of C1s (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0004">4</link>h). Before and after Ar<sup>+</sup> etching, the content of C=O bonds is basically unchanged, and the proportion is always less than 10 %. No C−M bonds were detected, further verifying that the surface of the NNCFM cathode is clean without the formation of CEIs and phase transitions.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200410-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Characterization of the NNCFM cathode. a, b) The TEM images of NNCFM cathodes harvested from 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (a) and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (b). XPS spectra of Ni 2p and C 1s with different etching time (0–9 min) collected from the NNCFM cathode cycled in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (c, d) and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (f, g). C1s fitting results at etching time of 0 min and 9 min for NNCFM cathodes harvested from 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (e) and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (h).</p></caption></figure><p>In pursuit of high‐voltage Na batteries and to highlight the excellent anti‐oxidation properties of the 3A zeolite‐modified electrolyte, a NVPF cathode (Figures S7 and S13) with higher cut‐off voltage of 4.25 V was further investigated. In the pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte, the cell survived only 4 cycles, followed by overcharging. (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>a). By contrast, the lifespan is significantly improved to 400 cycles with a stable CE of 99.55 % at 1.0 C when introducing a 3A zeolite film on the cathode (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>b). Note that, the cathode exhibits inherently lower CE compared to NNCFM cathodes in the initial stage (inserted in Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>b), which is unfavorable to reach a high capacity and stability in anode‐free cells. Further increasing the current density to 2.0 C, the Na‐NNCFM half‐cell harvested an ultra‐long lifespan of over 800 cycles with almost overlapped voltage profiles (Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>c, d). The excellent stability at such a high cut‐off voltage reflects the universality and practical applicability of this strategy, as most cathode materials operate at voltages not exceeding 4.25 V. At such high operating voltage, continuous electrolyte decomposition leads to the formation of thick CEIs up to 17 nm on the NVPF cathode after 4 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>e). The thick CEI results in a significantly increased interfacial resistance, which leads to overcharging of the battery. Different from the clean surface of NNCFM cathodes in 3A zeolite modified electrolytes, a rough surface can be identified for cycled NVPF cathodes due to the formation of thick CEIs on its surface (Figure S14). In 3A zeolite modified electrolytes, a thin CEI with a thickness of 3 nm can be observed after 400 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>f). During long‐term operation, although weak decomposition was detected, it did not affect its stability in view of its excellent cyclic stability. The composition and structure of CEIs were further investigated by XPS depth profiles. The binding energy between 288 and 292 eV normally dominates the organic composition of CEIs. By normalizing the intensity of the C−C peak, the content and composition changes of the CEI can be analyzed with the etching time. For the NVPF cathode harvested from 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes, it always maintains strong intensity under different etching depths (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>g). A relative intensity of 0.4 was still observed even after 12 min of etching. However, for NVPF cathodes harvested from 3A zeolite‐modified electrolytes, the peak intensity is lower and fades away with etching time. (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>h). Apparently, the formation of thick CEI seriously affects the battery performance. Next, COMOSOL simulation is used to visually present the influence of CEI thickness on battery performance. CEIs with interfacial impedances of 0, 0.2 and 0.6 Ω cm<sup>−2</sup> on spherical NVPF cathodes are considered in the simulation (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>k). As the CEI impedance increases, the Na ion concentration on the surface of the NVPF particles fades and leads to internal polarization, although the concentration gradient appears to be negligible in the bulk cell (Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>j and S15). When CEIs with impedances of 0.2 and 0.6 Ω cm<sup>−2</sup> are formed on the NVPF surface, the polarization voltage increases by about 0.5 V and 1.5 V, respectively, compared with the case without CEI (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>i). This increase in polarization is manifested as the frequent overcharging phenomena and a large reduction in efficiency.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200410-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Na‐NVPF half‐cell performance and simulations about CEI thickness on battery performance. a) Selected discharge/charge curves of Na‐NVPF batteries with pristine electrolytes and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes. b) The cyclic stability of Na‐NVPF batteries in 3A zeolite modified electrolytes at 1.0 C (1.0 C=100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). c) Selected discharge/charge curves of Na‐NVPF batteries with 3A zeolite modified electrolytes at 2.0 C. d) The cyclic stability of Na‐NVPF batteries at 2.0 C. e, f) The TEM image of the cycled NVPF cathode harvested from pristine 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (e) and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (f). g, h) XPS spectra of C1s with etching time from 0–12 min collected from the NVPF cathode cycled in 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolytes (g) and 3A zeolite modified electrolytes (h). i) The simulated discharge curve under different CEI impedance. j) Simulation results of electrolyte concentration distribution in the whole battery. k) Mantle diagram of spherical NVPF cathode composition analysis.</p></caption></figure><p>While achieving high voltage stability, the ultra‐high CE is also retained. An extremely high average CE of 99.91 % is obtained over 320 cycles at current density of 1 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> and areal capacity of 1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure S16). The durable high‐voltage stability and highly reversible Na deposition‐dissolution performance allow us to explore anode‐free full cells. In the anode‐free cell configuration, the 3A zeolite modified electrolyte was sandwiched between NNCFM/NVPF cathodes and bare Al foil current collectors were used as anodes. As shown in Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0006">6</link>a, b, at a mass loading of 8.52 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, the NNCFM cathode still maintains a capacity of 63.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 250 cycles. Based on the anode CE (99.91 %) and cathode capacity retention (73.1 %) obtained from the half‐cell, the anode‐free cell is expected to obtain a capacity of 68.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 250 cycles, which is close to the actual value (63.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) in our study. This further demonstrates that the high voltage stability and high CE of the Na anode can be simultaneously obtained. The high Na reversibility in this electrolyte can also be reflected by the uniform Na deposition morphology (Figure S17). In this cell configuration, no additional Na metal is used, which substantially promotes the energy density of the full cell to 369 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> based on the mass of active electrode materials (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0006">6</link>c). For the NVPF‐based anode‐free cells, it shows a high initial energy density of 372 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>. However, due to the inherently low CE exhibited in the half‐cell (Figures <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0005">5</link>b and S18), it is relatively less stable compared to NNCFM‐based anode‐free cells. This demonstrates that the CE on the cathode side is also critical for the performance of anode‐free cells.</p><figure xml:id="anie202200410-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202200410:anie202200410-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202200410-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Anode‐free full cell performance and energy density and cost analysis. a) Selected discharge/charge curves of Al‐NNCFM full cells with 3A zeolite modified electrolytes. b) The cyclic stability of anode‐free Al‐NNCFM full cells and Al‐NVPF full cells with 3A zeolite modified electrolytes. c) The galvanostatic discharge curves versus the gravimetric energy density. d) Energy density (based on the active materials of both cathodes and anodes) contour profile with traditional Na batteries, Li‐ion batteries and our anode‐free Na batteries. e) Cost comparison of traditional Li‐ion batteries, Na‐ion batteries and anode‐free Na batteries.</p></caption></figure><p>Compared with the hard carbon anode commonly used in Na‐ion batteries, the energy density of the anode‐free cell is increased by 60 %. The energy density is increased by 21 % compared with the Na metal battery with 100 % Na excess (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0006">6</link>d). A more appealing assessment on the energy density is its much higher value than the state‐of‐art LiFePO<sub>4</sub>‐graphite Li‐ion batteries (320 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>) and slightly lower than the NCM811‐graphite Li‐ion batteries (408 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>). To the best of our knowledge, this energy density (372 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>) reaches the highest value among reported works. It is expected to achieve a higher energy density by using a cathode with high‐capacity and high operating voltage, thus competing with the NCM811‐graphite batteries. Furthermore, the use of lightweight Al current collectors makes it more advantageous compared to Li‐ion batteries.</p><p>In addition to the high energy density, the anode‐free cell also favors a reduced cost from the perspective of sustainable battery fabrication. Compared with traditional Na‐ion batteries, the anode‐free cells do not require the use of active materials at anodes. Only a bare Al foil is used at anode side, which reduces costs by approximately 26 %. Besides, no conductive carbons and binders are needed in anode side for anode‐free configuration. By replacing the expensive and heavyweight Cu current collector used in Li‐ion batteries with cheap and lightweight Al, the cost is reduced in half. Therefore, the anode‐free cell have significant advantages in cost compared with traditional Li‐ion batteries and Na‐ion batteries (Figure <link href="#anie202200410-fig-0006">6</link>e). When the same energy density is reached, the anode‐free Na batteries cost 73.6 % of Li‐ion batteries and 56.2 % of Na‐ion batteries, respectively. The costs are calculated from a cost and resource analysis by Christoph Vaalma et al.<link href="#anie202200410-bib-0013"><sup>[4a]</sup></link> Detailed calculation methods are shown in Supporting Information.</p><p>In summary, we bypass the Na salt solubility limitation and develop a highly aggregated electrolyte configuration by using a 3A zeolite film with well‐defined pore sizes and narrow channels. The modified ether electrolyte not only inherits its good compatibility with the Na deposition‐dissolution process, but also remarkably improves the oxidative stability through sieving stable CIP and AGG ion‐pairs stored in the narrow channels of zeolites. Consequently, the modified electrolyte enables an ultra‐long lifespan over 800 cycles at a high cut‐off voltage of 4.25 V. More impressively, the assembled high‐voltage anode‐free full cell reaches an ultra‐high energy density of 369 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> and ultra‐long lifespan of 250 cycles for the first time. At the same time, with the same energy density, its cost is 26.4 % and 43.8 % lower than that of traditional Li‐ion batteries and Na‐ion batteries, respectively. In terms of energy density, cost, and sustainability, the strategy is expected to facilitate the practical application of high‐voltage anode‐free Na cells and replace conventional Li‐ion batteries.</p><section xml:id="anie202200410-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>We thank Dr. Yong Guo from Tianjin University for his help in XPS testing and analysis.
