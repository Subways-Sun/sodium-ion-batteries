Now, the long‐ and short‐range structural evolution of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F during cycling is studied by in situ high‐energy X‐ray diffraction (XRD), ex situ solid‐state nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and first‐principles DFT calculations. DFT calculations suggest that the intermediate phase, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, adopts the space group of <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>c</i>, which is a subgroup (<i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>b</i>11, No. 14) of <i>Pbcn</i> (No. 60), the space group of the starting phase, Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, and this space group provides a good fit to the experimental XRD and NMR results. The two crystallographically unique Na sites in the structure of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F behave differently during cycling, where the Na ions on the Na2 site are electrochemically active while those on the Na1 site are inert. This study determines the structural evolution and the electrochemical reaction mechanisms of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F in a Na‐ion battery.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Revealing the structural evolution</b>: Structural changes of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F cathode for a sodium‐ion battery during the cycling are elucidated by tracking the local environment of the Na sites.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201805555:anie201805555-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201805555-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201805555-sec-0001"><p>The quest for batteries that are low‐cost, safe, and environmentally benign to be used in portable devices, electric vehicles, and large‐scale energy storage systems continues unabated.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0001"/> While Li‐ion batteries (LIBs) provide reliable energy sources for most of the above applications, the limited lithium resources cannot meet the fast growing demand for increasingly larger devices.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0002"/> Therefore, the development of Na‐ion batteries (SIBs) has become an alternative strategy, especially in large‐scale energy storage systems, owing to the naturally abundant and thus lower cost sodium resources.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0003"/></p><p>To enhance the competitiveness of SIBs, screening cathode materials with high discharge voltages is an important approach. Among the high‐voltage candidate cathode materials, polyanionic compounds have attracted great attention owing to their structural diversity and stability. Different types of polyanionic compounds used in SIBs have been reported in the past years, including phosphates, NaFePO<sub>4</sub><link href="#anie201805555-bib-0004"/> and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>,<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0005"/> pyrophosphates, Na<sub>2</sub>MP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub><link href="#anie201805555-bib-0006"/> (M=Fe, Co, Mn), as well as fluorophosphates, Na<sub>2</sub>MPO<sub>4</sub>F<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0007"/> (M=Fe, Co, Mn) and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2<i>x</i></sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>F<sub>3−2<i>x</i></sub>.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0008"/> These materials have good structural stability but poor electronic conductivity, which can be compensated by carbon coating and/or cation substitution to improve their electrochemical performances.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0009"/></p><p>Among these polyanionic compounds, a layered iron‐based fluorophosphate, Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, with a space group of <i>Pbcn</i>, has been proved to be a promising cathode material for SIBs.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0010"/> Ellis et al. in 2007 reported the extraction of Na ions from Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, in a LIB, showing solid‐solution‐like electrochemical behavior, where transitions between two closely related phases (Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F to Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F and Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F to NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F) were observed and the intermediate phase Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, obtained by chemical desodiation, adopted a space group of <i>P</i>2/<i>c</i>, which is not a subgroup of <i>Pbcn</i>.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0007a"/> Recham et al. first published the use of nano‐Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F in a SIB with a discharge capacity of 100 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in the first cycle.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0010c"/> Deng et al. further improved the cycling performance of carbon‐coated Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F with about 85 % capacity retention after 1000 cycles.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0010d"/> Tripathi et al. calculated the migration barriers of Na ions in Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, demonstrating high Na mobility between [FePO<sub>4</sub>F] layers through a 2D network in the <i>ac</i> plane.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0011"/> Recently, Tereshchenko et al. combined XRD and DFT calculations to determine the intermediatephase Na<sub>1.55</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F with a space group of <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>b</i>, and showed that Fe<sup>II</sup>/Fe<sup>III</sup> charge ordering was coupled with Na/vacancy ordering.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0012"/> However, Smiley et al. employed ex situ <sup>23</sup>Na solid‐state nuclear magnetic resonance (ssNMR) to characterize Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F at different states of charge, showing: a) one single‐phase transition between Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F and NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F during the charge process, without detection of intermediate phase Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F; and b) no evidence of Na ions in a mixed Fe<sup>II</sup> and Fe<sup>III</sup> environment,<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0013"/> which are in contradiction to other reported results.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0007a #anie201805555-bib-0012"/> Thus, the structural evolution and the electrochemical reaction mechanisms of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F during the cycling are still under debate.</p><p>Herein, we have elucidated the structural evolution and determined how the Na ions in the structure of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F (de)intercalate during the cycling through in situ high‐energy X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns, ex situ ssNMR spectra, and first‐principles DFT calculations. The results reveal that the whole cycling process of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F cathode is dominated by two two‐phase reactions between structures at different states of charge. The intermediate phase, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, with a space group <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>c</i>, is determined by DFT calculations and agrees with the XRD and ssNMR results. Additionally, we find that the Na ions on the Na2 site are electrochemically active, while the Na ions on the Na1 site are inert and remain in the structure.</p><p>Pristine Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, synthesized by solid‐state methods, crystallized in the space group <i>Pbcn</i> (Supporting Information, Figure S1a). As shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S1b, the structure of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F comprises [FePO<sub>4</sub>F] layers formed by face‐sharing FeO<sub>4</sub>F<sub>2</sub> octahedra, connected by bridging F atoms and PO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedra. Two different Na sites, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> a and Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> b, are located between the [FePO<sub>4</sub>F] layers.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0007a"/> While each Na site has 4 Fe ions in their second coordination shell, the local environment are quite different for these two sites as listed in the Supporting Information, Table S1: a) the Na1 site has two Na−Fe distances of approximately 4.55 Å and two Na−Fe distances around 3.45 Å, while the Na−Fe distances for the Na2 site are all around 3.33 Å; b) the angles of two Na1−F−Fe bonds are near 180° and other angles of Na1−O/F−Fe bonds are close to 90°, while all the angles of Na2−O/F−Fe bonds are around 90°. In terms of the electron‐nuclear dipolar (hyperfine) interaction between the paramagnetic Fe electron and the Na nucleus (<i>I</i>=3/2), this implies a more symmetric interaction tensor for the Na spins on the Na2 site, which has important implications as discussed below.</p><figure xml:id="anie201805555-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201805555:anie201805555-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201805555-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>The local environment of the a) Na1 and b) Na2 sites in Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. c) <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectrum (black) of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F at 55 kHz with the cumulative spectrum (red) and deconvolution of resonances at −180 ppm (light blue) and 440 ppm (light red), respectively. The local environment of the h) Na1′′ site in NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F. i) <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectrum (black) of NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F at 60 kHz with the cumulative spectrum (red), deconvolution of resonances at 345 ppm (light red) and around 0 ppm (gray), respectively. The peak around 0 ppm is the diamagnetic impurity. j) The first charge curve of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F electrode with corresponding marks for Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, and NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F samples.</p></caption></figure><p>Since ssNMR is very sensitive to the local environment, two resonances are expected in the <sup>23</sup>Na magic angle spinning (MAS) NMR spectrum of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. As shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S1c, two main resonances, the isotropic shifts of which are 440 ppm and −180 ppm at 60 kHz, were observed in the <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectrum of pristine Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, along with a weak resonance around 0 ppm, which most likely arose from NaF from the synthesis process. Given that the isotropic peaks and the spinning sidebands are severely overlapped at 60 kHz, a deconvolution (Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> c) was performed on a spectrum acquired at 55 kHz. As paramagnetic NMR shifts depend strongly on the sample temperature and frictional MAS heating, all the shifts of isotropic resonances are labeled with those obtained at 60 kHz for consistency. The intensity ratio between the two resonances at 440 and −180 ppm is about 1.04:0.96, which is in reasonable agreement with the 1:1 occupancy ratio between the two Na sites in the structure.</p><p>Furthermore, comparing the two resonances, we observe more symmetric sideband manifolds that span a narrower range for the resonance at −180 ppm. In contrast, the resonance at 440 ppm shows asymmetric sideband manifolds in which the spinning sidebands positioned at −62 ppm and −572 ppm have even stronger signal intensity than the isotropic resonance (Supporting Information, Figure S1d). Considering that the major contribution to the sideband manifolds of the two Na resonances is the electron–nuclear dipolar interaction between Na and Fe ions,<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0014"/> we tentatively assign the resonances at −180 ppm and 440 ppm to the Na2 and Na1 site, respectively.</p><p>However, accurate characterization of spinning sideband manifolds in paramagnetic NMR can be difficult due to bulk magnetic susceptibility of powders; this is further complicated by the possible interplay between the interaction between the electron–nuclear hyperfine tensor and quadrupolar tensor.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0015"/> Hence, first‐principles DFT calculations were also employed to estimate the isotropic shifts of the two Na sites in Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. Detailed information from the DFT calculations are shown in the Supporting Information. The contour maps of electron spin density around the two Na sites in Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F obtained from projected DFT wave functions are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S5, where the spin density with opposite sign is transferred to the two Na sites, resulting in positive and negative shifts for the Na1 and Na2 resonances, respectively, in the <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectra. The calculated isotropic shifts <i>δ</i><sub>iso</sub> and the asymmetry parameter <i>κ</i> are in agreement with our experimental results, which lie between the Hyb20 and Hyb35 calculated values listed in Table <link href="#anie201805555-tbl-0001"/>. However, our results contradict with the previous assignment by Smiley et al.,<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0013"/> where the resonance at 450 ppm with broader sideband manifolds was assigned to the Na2 site, and the resonance at −175 ppm with narrower sideband manifolds was assigned to the Na1 site. [b] <i>Ω</i> gives the span of anisotropy and <i>κ</i> gives the asymmetry.</p></note></noteGroup></tabular><p>The electrochemical performance of the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F cathode in a Na‐ion battery (Supporting Information, Figure S6) clearly reveals two two‐phase processes and the formation of an intermediate phase Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F at current rates from 0.1 C to 1 C. Considering this intermediate phase, we have generated numerous possible configurations for the intermediate phase Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F by extracting half of the Na ions from the Na2 site in the Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F unit cell; DFT calculations were then performed on each configuration, optimizing each structure, and ranking the energies of the different structures. The XRD patterns generated from possible configurations which have different Na vacancies are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S2a, and the experimental XRD pattern of electrochemically prepared Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F matches the lowest‐energy phase, indexed as <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>c</i>, which is quite different with the previous reported space group of <i>P</i>2/<i>c</i> (No. 13) reported by Ellis et al.,<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0007a"/> owing to their different crystallographic setting, but it agrees with that determined by Tereshchenko et al.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0012"/> Detailed information concerning the proposed configurations and Rietveld refinement are shown in the Supporting Information. <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>c</i> is a subgroup (<i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>b</i>11, No. 14) of <i>Pbcn</i> (No. 60), the space group observed for the starting material Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, resulting from a symmetry reduction arising from the loss of orthogonality between the <i>b</i>‐ and <i>c</i>‐axis in Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. Note that the earlier reported space group of <i>P</i>2/<i>c</i> for Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F is not a subgroup of <i>Pbcn</i> (subgroup of <i>Pbcn</i> is <i>P</i>2/<i>a</i> related to its setting).<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0007a"/> Bond valence sum (BVS in valence unit or vu) calculations<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0016"/> listed in the Supporting Information, Table S4, suggest that Fe1 and Fe2 correspond to Fe<sup>II</sup> and Fe<sup>III</sup> ions, respectively. Therefore, an additional Na1 site and an extra Fe site are obtained in this lowest‐energy phase of Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, resulting in different Fe ion configurations in the second coordination shell of the two Na1 sites, labeled as Na1a′ and Na1b′ in Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> d and Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> e. Thus, the six bonds (Supporting Information, Table S5) connecting Na1 to Fe via O/F are further divided to two classes: a) 4 bonds with the distances between Na1a′ to Fe<sup>II</sup> being less than 4 Å and angles nearing 90° while other 2 bonds with the distances between Na1a′ to Fe<sup>III</sup> nearing 4.5 Å and angles nearing 180°; b) 4 bonds with the distances between Na1b′ to Fe<sup>III</sup> being less than 4 Å and angles nearing 90° while other 2 bonds with the distances between Na1b′ to Fe<sup>II</sup> being nearing 4.5 Å and angles nearing 180°, which implies different paramagnetic shifts of the Na1a′ and Na1b′ sites in Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F we will discuss below.</p><p>The almost identical <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectra of Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F prepared by chemical and electrochemical desodiation are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S3a, indicating the same final product for both processes. Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> g shows the deconvolution spectra of electrochemically prepared Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F with three isotropic resonances located at 615, 320, and −130 ppm. According to the local environment of the Na sites mentioned above and the Hyb20 and Hyb35 calculated values listed in Table <link href="#anie201805555-tbl-0001"/>, these three resonances at 615, 320, and −130 ppm are assigned to the Na1b′, Na1a′, and Na2′ sites, respectively. DFT predictions of the shifts of the other configurations of Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F (Supporting Information, Table S7) do not agree with these shifts, further confirming the existence of this phase. Of note, our DFT and NMR results show no vacancy on Na1a′ and Na1b′ sites and there is only one site for Na2′, while the result from Tereshchenko et al. show that there is 9 % vacancy on the second Na1 sites, and around 18 % Na partially occupied the second Na2 sites in Na<sub>1.55</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F. The difference of the Na occupancy most likely arises from the process of refining the light atom Na in XRD patterns, resulting in a better fitting but a slight mismatch for the real occupancy distribution.</p><p>Now moving to NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F, with all the Fe<sup>II</sup> oxidized to Fe<sup>III</sup>. This gives four Fe<sup>III</sup> ions in the second coordination shell of the Na1′′ site (Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0001"/> h), and the space group returns to <i>Pbcn</i>. Such higher symmetry arises from all the Fe and Na sites being identical again, which also manifests in a single isotropic <sup>23</sup>Na resonance at 345 ppm of both electrochemically and chemically prepared NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F (Supporting Information, Figure S3b). This resonance is assigned to the Na1′′ site in NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F, which is in agreement with our DFT calculation results and also with the previous assignment by Smiley et al.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0013"/></p><p>To investigate the structural evolution of cathode Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F further, ex situ ssNMR was employed to characterize samples prepared at different states of charge, with their corresponding amount of Na remaining in the structure listed in the Supporting Information, Tables S8 and S9. To deconvolute the severely overlapped isotropic resonances from the spinning sidebands, again, two spectra at 60 kHz and 55 kHz were measured.</p><p>In the charge process (Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0002"/> a–c), from pristine, Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, to C6, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, the Na1 resonance gradually decreases, while the resonances at 615 ppm (Na1b′), and 320 ppm (Na1a′), start to appear and gradually increase, with the total amount of these three resonances remaining constant. Meanwhile, the Na2 resonance gradually decreases until completely disappearing at C6, while another resonance at −130 ppm (Na2′) appears and gradually increases. The intensity of the Na2′ resonance in sample C6 is half of the intensity of Na2 resonance in pristine sample, meaning half the Na ions on the Na2 site are deintercalated in this process. These observations imply a two‐phase reaction process from pristine to C6, which agrees with our electrochemical measurements and in situ HEXRD results in the Supporting Information.</p><figure xml:id="anie201805555-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201805555:anie201805555-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201805555-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Selected different states of charge and the normalized <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectra of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F during first charge process at b) 60 kHz and c) 55 kHz, respectively. d) Selected different states of charge and the normalized <sup>23</sup>Na MAS NMR spectra of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F during first discharge process at e) 60 kHz and f) 55 kHz, respectively. Spinning sidebands are marked with an asterisk (*). g) The distribution of Na ions on different Na sites during first cycle with the electrochemical profile shown as red solid line.</p></caption></figure><p>From C6, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, to C8, NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F, Na1a′ and Na1b′ resonances decrease then disappear, while a new resonance at 345 ppm (Na1′′) appears and gradually increases. Additionally, the intensity of the Na1′′ resonance in sample C8 is almost equal to that of the Na1 resonance of pristine Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F as well as the sum of the Na1a′ and Na1b′ resonances in sample C6. The Na2′ resonance decreases until disappearing at C8, indicating the Na ions on the Na2′ site are totally extracted at the end of charge.</p><p>The discharge process (Figure <link href="#anie201805555-fig-0002"/> d–f) shows reversible behavior as compared to the charge process, which indicates Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F shows highly reversible capacity when used as a cathode for a SIB. Indeed, Deng et al. has demonstrated the excellent cycling performance of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F with about 85 % capacity retention after 1000 cycles.<link href="#anie201805555-bib-0010d"/></p><p>In summary, the whole cycling process of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F consists of two two‐phase reactions between three structures at different states of charge: Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, and NaFePO<sub>4</sub>F with space group of <i>Pbcn</i>, <i>P</i>2<sub>1</sub>/<i>c</i>, and <i>Pbcn</i>, respectively. The structure of the intermediate phase, Na<sub>1.5</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, is identified by DFT calculations and confirmed via a comparison between the experimental <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectra as well as hyperfine shifts calculated via hybrid‐DFT calculations. Na1 remains in the structure while Na2 (de)intercalates from/into the structure along the cycling. This work provides in‐depth analysis of the structural evolution and the electrochemical reaction mechanisms of cathode Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F for a SIB.</p><section xml:id="anie201805555-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 21233004, 21761132030, and 21621091) and the National Basic Research Program of China (Grant No. 2016YFB0901502 and 2018YFB0905400).
