In this work, a novel Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure is prepared on fungus‐derived carbon matrix encapsulated by 2D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene highly conductive layers, which exhibits high specific sodium ion (Na<sup>+</sup>) and potassium ion (K<sup>+</sup>) storage capacities of 610.9 and 449.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and excellent capacity retention at high charge–discharge rates. MXene acts as conductive layers to prevent the restacking and aggregation of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> sheets on fungus‐derived carbonaceous nanoribbons, while the natural fungus functions as natural nitrogen/carbon source to provide bionic nanofiber network structural skeleton, providing additional accessible pathways for the high‐rate ion transport and satisfying surface‐driven contribution ratios at high sweep rates for both Na/K ions storages. In addition, in situ synchrotron diffraction and ex situ X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy measurements are performed to reveal the mechanisms of storage and de‐/alloying conversion process of Na<sup>+</sup> in the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/carbonaceous nanoribbon heterostructure. As a result, the assembled Na/K full cells containing MXene‐supported Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>@carbonaceous anodes possess stable large‐ion storage capabilities.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202101535-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/fungus‐derived carbonaceous nanoribbon‐based sodium‐ion and potassium‐ion batteries exhibit high specific sodium‐ion and potassium‐ion storage capacities of 610.9 and 449.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and excellent capacity retention at high charge–discharge rates.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202101535:adma202101535-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202101535-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202101535-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Alkali‐metal ion batteries play an increasingly important role in energy storage applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Currently, lithium is the most widely used alkali metal; however, it will be likely replaced with other alkali elements in the foreseeable future due to the limited Li reserves in the Earth's crust.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the development of next‐generation cost‐effective sustainable energy storage system represents an urgent task. Sodium and potassium metals with relatively large ionic radii and electronic structures similar to that of Li have become promising candidates as the anode materials of alkali‐metal‐ion batteries because of their abundant reserves, high theoretical capacities, and low redox potentials close to Li<sup>+</sup>/Li.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0003 #adma202101535-bib-0004"><sup>3,4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, the high repulsive forces generated during the ion insertion and extraction processes occurring in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) and potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs) significantly limit their large‐scale commercial applications. Therefore, it is necessary to identify suitable host materials for the both the larger sodium ions (Na<sup>+</sup>) and potassium ions (K<sup>+</sup>).</p><p>The convergence of biological and synthetic materials is a cutting‐edge subject that offers significant potential to develop bionic hybrid materials with improved properties, especially in the context of energy storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0005 #adma202101535-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> With unique functions, the 1D hierarchical nanostructure is one of the most promising type of high efficiency materials. This type of material structure design enables a high conductivity and at the same time has transport properties. Materials with such design are attractive candidates for electrical energy storage systems. In this field of research, fungus‐inspired 1D nanostructure materials, although deserve more attention, have not been studied as a highly promising electrode materials for SIBs and PIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, fungus‐inspired carbonaceous materials are even more capable for energy storage than carbon‐materials due to their naturally occurring biological structures.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0008 #adma202101535-bib-0009"><sup>8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among various synthetic materials, transitional metal chalcogenides (TMCs) are advantageous for energy storage applications due to their multistep electronic conversion reactions and favorable structures.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Although they exhibit higher reversible ion storage capacities than those of carbon‐based materials, however, their intrinsically high surface energies and weak van der Waals interactions may lead to the stacking and agglomeration of materials that reduce the accessibility of a large number of electrochemically active sites.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After a development of close to decade, the 2D transition metal carbonitride, so‐called MXenes, shine in fields of electrochemical energy storage, electromagnetic shielding, and biomedical applications, due to their unique physico–chemical properties. According to previous research, the general formula of MXenes could be considered as M<i><sub>n</sub></i><sub>+1</sub>X<i><sub>n</sub></i>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>, where M, X and T<i><sub>x</sub></i> refer to the early transition metal (such as Ti, V, and Nb), carbon/nitrogen, and various terminational groups, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0012 #adma202101535-bib-0013"><sup>12,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Similar to other 2D matrix, the self‐restacking obstacles becomes inevitable during repeating ions insertion/extraction, leading to the capacity fading. Reasonable structural optimization of high‐capacity bionic hybrid materials is therefore required in order to compensate for these intrinsic limitations.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Herein, we utilize highly conducting Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXenes acting as conductive layers to support Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 2 or 3) heterostructures on fungus‐derived carbonaceous nanoribbons (FCRs) denoted as Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCRs by adopting a “step‐by‐step” assemble strategy to increase the composite conductivity, prevent stacking and aggregation processes, and expose a larger number of electrochemically active sites from Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructures. This approach increases the efficiency of electrochemical reactions and produces strong pseudocapacitive effects, while the surface‐driven contribution ratios exceed 90% at high sweep rates during Na ions storage. Moreover, the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene conductive layers supply an adequate number of deposition sites for the electrostatic self‐assembly and the growth of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructures with full exposure, while the natural fungus functions as natural nitrogen/carbon source to provide bionic nanofiber network structural skeleton, yielding additional accessible pathways for the high‐rate ion transport. Accordingly, the storage behavior of the fabricated heterostructures was investigated by in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and ex situ X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) techniques. Finally, the assembled Na/K full cells based on the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐MXene‐supported Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>@carbonaceous anodes demonstrated stable large‐ion storage capabilities. We hope that this work can shed light on the novel perspectives for the designing of bionic hybrid materials in high‐performance SIBs and PIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0030"><title type="main">Preparation and Characterization of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR Materials</title><p>The preparation of 1D hierarchical Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCRs bionic hybrid materials is schematically illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. An <i>Aspergillus niger</i> (<i>A. Niger</i>) nanoribbon (ANR) was first encapsulated by a well‐dispersed few‐ or monolayered 2D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanosheets intercalated by organic tetramethylammonium hydroxide (TMAOH) (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information), due to the formation of hydrogen bonds between hydroxyl groups to develop a highly conductive “shell.” A wrinkled surface is clearly observed in the MXene/ANR image (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S2a</link>, Supporting Information). Acting as a highly conductive shell with a robust structure, this undulated rough surface contains a large number of chemically active sites, facilitating the interlinking of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene terminal hydroxyl groups with Fe<sup>3+</sup> ions during electrostatic assembly.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Meanwhile, the unique 1D fibrous architecture of ANRs can be used as building blocks with a high surface‐to‐volume ratio, providing an ultrastable structural skeleton. Therefore, interfacial assembly was successfully performed due to the electrostatic attraction between Fe<sup>3+</sup> ions and the negatively charged Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanosheets. Subsequently, the hybrid aerogel underwent calcination and vapor selenylation treatments. After that, the previously implanted iron ion precursor was converted to nanoplate‐like Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure nanocrystals with full exposure, which were well distributed across the crumpled surface of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐MXene‐covered FCR, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S2b</link>, Supporting Information. The limited transport of Fe<sup>3+</sup> ions into the fungus bulk due to the presence of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene support increased the electrochemical reactivity of the fully exposed Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructures. Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S2c</link>, Supporting Information, shows that without the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene conductive layers, Fe<sup>3+</sup> diffuse into the ANR interior via bioleaching during the microbial metabolism process. After the high‐temperature pyrolysis, the FCR encapsulated each Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure (Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/FCR) and thus, reduced the accessible area for ion migration. The advantages of this unique design are as follows: i) using Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene as conductive layers to fabricate fungus‐derived Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/carbonaceous bionic heterostructure for the first time, which prevents the stacking and aggregation processes and increases electrochemically active sites by a large amount; ii) the fungus functions as natural nitrogen/carbon source to provide bionic nanofiber network structural skeleton, providing additional accessible pathways for the high‐rate ion transport; and iii) the successful integration of fungus‐derived Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/carbonaceous bionic heterostructures can tailor their properties and help manufacture SIBs and PIBs anodes with desirable electrochemical characteristics that have seldom been reported.</p><figure xml:id="adma202101535-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202101535:adma202101535-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202101535-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the synthesis of 1D hierarchical Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR and Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/FCR nanoribbons. b–f) Morphological characterization: b,c) TEM images of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR nanoribbons; d,e) HRTEM images of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR; f) elemental mappings of N, Ti, O, Fe, and Se in Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1b</link> displays a typical low‐magnification transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of the prepared Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR bionic hybrid materials, which clearly shows that the fungal carbonaceous nanoribbon‐like matrix is uniformly encapsulated by the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanoflakes with a rough surface, which resulted from the interfacial partial oxidation of the outer Ti atoms in the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene structure. In particular, the monodispersed Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure nanoplates with an average crystal size of ≈300–500 nm were homogenously distributed on the wrinkled MXene@FCR matrix in a disordered manner, which was in good agreement with the corresponding scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images presented in the inset of Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1b</link>. The magnified TEM image depicted in Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1c</link> displays a detailed interface between the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene supporting layers and the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure. It shows the random growth of nanoplate‐like Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructures on the surface of stacked Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanoflakes. As a result, almost the entire Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> surface becomes exposed, suggesting the possibility of an efficient utilization of the highly conductive Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene layers. The successful integration of the heterostructure components can tailor their properties and help manufacture SIB and PIB anodes with desirable electrochemical characteristics.</p><p>The dotted line circles in the high‐resolution TEM images of an Fe–Se heterostructure on the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐MXene‐encapsulated fungal carbonaceous matrix denote the <i>d</i>‐spacing values of 0.246 and 0.256 nm, corresponding to the (120) and (111) of FeSe<sub>2</sub> lattice planes depicted in Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1d</link>. Meanwhile, the (111) diffraction peak in the Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> XRD pattern (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1e</link>) is related to the lattice spacing of 0.271 nm, indicating the coexistence of FeSe<sub>2</sub> and Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> species in the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> structure, which is consistent with the obtained XRD pattern. Additionally, the interlayer distances of 0.351 and 0.189 nm can be ascribed to the (101) and (200) lattice planes of the TiO<sub>2</sub> species generated by the partial oxidation of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene surface Ti atoms (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1e</link>). Owing to the unique heterostructure texture, the intrinsic heterogeneous nature of the Fe–Se compounds embedded into the mesoporous MXene@N‐doped carbon conductive 1D fibrous matrix can produce a strong pseudocapacitive effect, which enhances the Na/K‐ion adsorption capability. The elemental distribution of the synthesized nanohybrid was determined by energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy mapping (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information); the obtained atomic ratios of C, N, O, F, Ti, Fe, and Se elements were 56.1%, 8.8%, 10.5%, 2.6%, 8.6%, 5.4%, and 8.0%, respectively. These elements were homogenously distributed throughout the entire hybrid fibers, further confirming the integration of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> hetero‐nanoplates with the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐MXene‐based carbonaceous compounds (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0001">1f</link>).</p><p>The heterostructure textural properties determined by nitrogen adsorption/desorption isothermal analysis are displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information. The ideal type II isotherm indicates the unlimited mono‐ or multilayered adsorption behavior of the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR bionic hybrid fibers, which is in good agreement with the results of morphological analysis. The hysteresis loop of the H3 type suggests: 1) homogenous distribution of 2D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanoflakes and on the fibrous carbonaceous matrix; 2) higher exposure degree of nanoplate‐like Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> species on the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene supporting conductive layers, compared to unmodified hybrids. The specific surface area of the material calculated from the results of Brunauer–Emmett–Teller analysis was as high as 62.31 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, which is higher than that of carbon matrix encapsulating Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure without Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene layers supporting (6.72 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). Moreover, the pore size distribution indicated a complex micro‐/mesoporous structure of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐MXene‐supported hybrid fibers with the most probable aperture sizes of 0.69, 3.53, and 5.34 nm, as shown in the inset of Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information. These micro‐/mesopores facilitate the diffusion of large Na<sup>+</sup> and K<sup>+</sup> ions at the electrode/electrolyte interface by forming fast transport channels and producing sufficient electrochemically active sites to allow the storage of these ions at Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructures.</p><p>A detailed analysis of the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR nanocomposite components was performed systematically in this work. The apparent shift of the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene characteristic (002) peak from 8.2° to 6.1° indicates a uniform distribution of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> nanoflakes on the surface of fungous fibers, further demonstrating the existence of strong interactions between the microbiological strain and Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene species via biosorption. Therefore, it becomes necessary to overcome the resistance generated by electrostatic repulsion, which is a possible reason for the observed <i>D</i><sub>K</sub> minimization after the increase in the K<sup>+</sup> diffusion path length. When the anode begins to depotassiate toward higher potential (above 0.01 V), the diffusion coefficient jumps to a larger value because the previously adsorbed ions start to desorb from the surface, and the driving force progressively weakens for the deeply stored potassium ions in the interlayers, leading to a continuous decrease in <i>D</i><sub>K</sub>. The natural structural advantage of the 2D conductive layers cladding the 1D N‐rich carbonaceous nanoribbons and further decorated with fully exposed iron selenides is the presence of hierarchical ion transportation channels that lead to high diffusion coefficients.</p><p>The Nyquist plots are illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information; the semi‐circles in intermediate‐frequency region corresponds to the electrochemical polarization process; the conductivity differences of different electrodes can be concluded by comparing the radii of the semi‐circles, which presents the resistance of charge transfer during ions storage. Compared to the carbon encapsulating Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>, conductive Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene supporting carbon‐based Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructure exhibits smaller radii, indicating the enhanced conductivity and charge transfer ability, which is in consistence with our deductions. To further illustrate the structural robustness during consecutive ions insertion/extraction, the SEM and TEM images of Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR after 100 cycles were provided in Figure <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S20</link>, Supporting Information. Apparently, the main 1D fibrous structure almost remains unchanged after cycling, indicating the good structure stability and satisfying reversibility. Therefore, the prepared Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene conductive layer supported transition metal selenides exhibits better electrochemical Na/K ions storage properties, which are than that of carbon‐based conductive benchmark supporting TMCs (Tables <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S1</link> and <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0060"><title type="main">Full‐Cell Batteries</title><p>Owing to the enhanced Na/K ion storage capabilities of the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR anodes for SIBs and PIBs as compared with the paramete rs of similar anodes displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0020 #adma202101535-bib-0029 #adma202101535-bib-0030 #adma202101535-bib-0031 #adma202101535-bib-0032 #adma202101535-bib-0033 #adma202101535-bib-0034 #adma202101535-bib-0035"><sup>20,29–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> full‐cell devices were assembled using Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) and K<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (KVP) counter electrodes to further evaluate the practical applicability of the synthesized hybrid nanoribbons (Figure <link href="#adma202101535-fig-0006">6b</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202101535-bib-0036 #adma202101535-bib-0037"><sup>36,37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The morphologies and phase compositions of the NVP and KVP cathodes, investigated by the SEM and XRD techniques in Figures <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S21</link> and <link href="#adma202101535-supitem-0001">S22</link>, Supporting Information, are similar to those reported in previous studies. type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We have successfully designed and prepared fully exposed Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>@microbial‐fungus‐derived carbon nanoribbons using 2D Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene conductive supporting layers. As compared with the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> heterostructures embedded into fungal carbon, the encapsulation of MXene nanosheets generates a sufficient number of active sites for Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> deposition, leading to the formation of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> and FeSe<sub>2</sub> species with high ion‐storage capacities. The resulting 3D bionic nanofiber network structure serving as a reliable structural supporting skeleton between the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i> phase and the carbonaceous matrix enables the effective and stable storage of sodium/potassium ions. Specifically, the Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐MXene‐supported Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/FCR anode half‐cells of SIBs and PIBs exhibit outstanding pseudocapacitive effects with surface‐driven fractions reaching 93.7% at 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and 74.9% at 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Furthermore, after 2000 discharge/charge cycles conducted at a current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the anode retained a reversible Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity of 348.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, while its K<sup>+</sup> storage capacity after 80 cycles was equal to 449.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Meanwhile, in terms of K<sup>+</sup> storage at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, only a negligible capacity fade of 0.017 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> was exhibited per cycle during 900 charge/discharge processes. More importantly, the assembled Na/K full batteries based on the Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i><sub>−1</sub>Se<i><sub>x</sub></i>/MXene/FCR anodes and NVP/KVP cathodes exhibited stable sodium‐/potassium‐ion storage capacities and high working potentials. The results of this work suggest that Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanoflakes can be used as effective conductive layers and stabilizing agents for complex hybrid structures, which enhance the electrochemical properties of TMCs and bio‐inspired carbon composites for alkali‐metal‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0080"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Chemicals</title><p>Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> MAX powder (400 mesh) was purchased from 11 Technology Co., Ltd., China. Hydrochloric acid (HCl, ≈37wt%), lithium fluoride (LiF, 99%), TMAOH (25 wt% in H<sub>2</sub>O), ascorbic acid (99%), iron (III) chloride hexahydrate (ACS, 97–102%), and selenium powder (Se) were obtained from Aladdin Reagent, China and Alfa Aesar. Deionized water was self‐supplied in our laboratory.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Multilayers and Few‐Layered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXenes</title><p>First, 2.0 g of Ti<sub>3</sub>AlC<sub>2</sub> MAX powder was added into 30 mL 6 <sc>m </sc>HCl containing 3.0 g LiF gently in case of oxidation due to exothermic reaction, etching treatment going through 10 h at the temperature of 35 °C under continuously stirring. Next, 1.5 g of previously etched multilayered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> was dissolved in 35 mL of 25 wt% TMAOH thoroughly, followed by consecutive stirring at the interval of 12 h under room temperature. After first three centrifugation–rinse cycles, the obtained liquid supernatant was collected for use afterward; corresponding concentration of few‐layered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> colloidal solution was ≈5 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Culture of Biological Fungus</title><p>After on‐going incubation process in a nutrient solution with a certain weight ratio of 10:8 for glucose and peptone at 35 °C for 3 days, grown microbial ribbons—<i>A. Niger</i>—were rinsed from yellow brownish color to white, removing cultural ingredients.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Comprehensive Biosorption and Electrostatic Adsorption</title><p>The biosorption reaction between <i>A. Niger</i> ribbons and the previously prepared few‐layered Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> nanosheet supernatant (approximately 200 mL) was performed for 36–48 h, followed by the self‐assembly of Ti<sub>3</sub>C<sub>2</sub>T<i><sub>x</sub></i> MXene nanosheets and Fe<sup>3+</sup> ions from a 0.1 mol L<sup>−1</sup> FeCl<sub>3</sub> solution conducted at room temperature for another 24 h. After freeze‐drying in liquid nitrogen at −55 °C for 3 days, the resultant dark green aerogel underwent vapor selenylation at 400 °C for 2 h in an Ar atmosphere with 0.5 g of Se powder placed at the upstream location of the tube furnace inside a quartz boat. Through rough calculation, the reference ratio value of Fe<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/FeSe<sub>2</sub> was ≈1:3.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0150" numbered="no"><title type="main">Fabrication of the Batteries</title><p>The working electrodes were fabricated by homogeneously mixing the as‐obtained electrochemically active powder, Super P (conductive agents), and poly(vinylidene difluoride) (binder) in <i>N</i>‐methylpyrrolidone with the weight ratio of 8:1:1, respectively. The mass of active materials was in the range of 1.04–1.2 mg, based on the total mass of 1.3–1.5 g. Type CR2032 coin cells were used to assemble half‐/full‐devices in an Ar‐gas‐filled glovebox. Battery grade copper and aluminum foils were used as the current collectors for anodes and cathodes. The electrolytes were 1 <sc>m </sc>NaClO<sub>4</sub> and 0.8 <sc>m </sc>KPF<sub>6</sub> in ethylene carbonate (EC)/dimethyl carbonate (1:1, v/v); the sodium and potassium foils were used as the counter electrodes in half‐devices. Besides, the Whatman glass fiber (GF/D) was chosen as the separators. For full‐cell devices, the mass ratio of anode/cathode was about 1:6; both undergo Na ions pre‐intercalation processes at the sweep rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> by CV scan for several cycles before use; additionally, the capacity was calculated based on the mass of anodes; corresponding cathodes for SIB and PIB were NVP and KVP according to previous references, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202101535-sec-0160" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization</title><p>Thermogravimetric analysis–differential scanning calorimetry (TG‐DSC) was measured with a condition of temperature range from room temperature to 1000 °C at a heating rate of 10 °C min<sup>−1</sup> in Air atmosphere. Cyclic stabilities, rate capabilities, galvanostatic charge–discharge profiles, and GITT were carried on LAND CT2001A testing systems. Additionally, CV scan finished through a CHI760E electrochemical working station. XRD patterns, XPS, FTIR Spectroscopy, and Raman spectra were investigated by RIGAKU (D/MAX 2550V), Renishaw inVia, SHIMADZU (IRAffinity‐1S), and Thermo Scientific Escalab 250Xi, respectively.
