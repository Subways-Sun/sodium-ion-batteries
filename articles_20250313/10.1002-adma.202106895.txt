Foundation</fundingAgency><fundingNumber>2020M682031</fundingNumber></fundingInfo><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Because of concerns regarding shortages of lithium resources and the urgent need to develop low‐cost and high‐efficiency energy‐storage systems, research and applications of sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have re‐emerged in recent years. Herein, recent advances in high‐capacity Sn‐based anode materials for stable SIBs are highlighted, including tin (Sn) alloys, Sn oxides, Sn sulfides, Sn selenides, Sn phosphides, and their composites. The reaction mechanisms between Sn‐based materials and sodium are clarified. Multiphase and multiscale structural optimizations of Sn‐based materials to achieve good sodium‐storage performance are emphasized. Full‐cell designs using Sn‐based materials as anodes and further development of Sn‐based materials are discussed from a commercialization perspective. Insights into the preparation of future high‐performance Sn‐based anode materials and the construction of sodium‐ion full batteries with a high energy density and long service life are provided.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202106895-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are now intensively studied to complement lithium‐ion batteries in energy‐storage markets. Recent advances in various high‐capacity Sn‐based anode materials for SIBs are summarized, with an emphasis on the reaction mechanisms, and the multiphase and multiscale structural optimizations for stable sodium storage. Cathode materials and electrolyte are also discussed from the perspective of full‐cell construction.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202106895-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are important in energy‐storage systems because of their advantages of a high energy density, long service life, no memory effect, and environmental friendliness.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0001 #adma202106895-bib-0002 #adma202106895-bib-0003 #adma202106895-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the uneven distribution and limited reserves of lithium resources have resulted in price increases and restricted LIB application. Therefore, it is imperative to reduce costs and better meet the needs of large‐scale energy storage (such as grid‐level energy storage) by developing other energy‐storage systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0005 #adma202106895-bib-0006 #adma202106895-bib-0007 #adma202106895-bib-0008"><sup>5–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Elemental sodium is abundant in the earth's crust, and it has similar properties to lithium. LIB replacement with sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) could save costs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0005 #adma202106895-bib-0009 #adma202106895-bib-0010 #adma202106895-bib-0011"><sup>5,9–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, SIBs have a lower energy density compared with LIBs, which is not conducive to applications where a high energy is required. The energy density of batteries depends mainly on the potential difference between cathode and anode electrodes, and their specific capacities. Considerable progress has been made in the research of SIB cathode materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0012 #adma202106895-bib-0013 #adma202106895-bib-0014 #adma202106895-bib-0015 #adma202106895-bib-0016 #adma202106895-bib-0017"><sup>12–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> but many challenges remain for anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Because the radius of sodium ions (1.02 Å) is larger than that of lithium ions (0.76 Å), commercially available graphite anode materials in LIBs fail to be applied in SIBs. Even though some carbon materials such as hard carbon (HC) can store sodium ions, (e.g., Stevens and Dahn<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> achieved a reversible sodium capacity of 300 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> as early as 2000) the relatively low specific capacity was not well suited to achieving a high energy density. Furthermore, sodium‐intercalated HC reacts with electrolyte and causes safety problems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The standard potential of sodium is 0.3 V higher than that of lithium, which lowers the energy density after assembly into full batteries. Therefore, high‐capacity anode materials must be developed to promote the future large‐scale application of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0006 #adma202106895-bib-0021 #adma202106895-bib-0022"><sup>6,21,22</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Because of their advantages of a high capacity, abundant resources, and environmental benignity, tin (Sn)‐based materials have been studied extensively as LIB anodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0023 #adma202106895-bib-0024 #adma202106895-bib-0025"><sup>23–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unlike ion‐insertion‐type anode materials (e.g., graphite), Sn‐based materials undergo conversion and alloying during lithiation, which yields higher specific capacities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Conversion and alloying reactions occur during sodium storage, which contributes to a high theoretical specific capacity. For example, pure Sn delivers a high capacity of 847 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after being sodiated to form Na<sub>15</sub>Sn<sub>4</sub> through an alloying step;<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sn dioxide with a decent specific capacity of 667 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained after complete conversion and alloying reactions. The other Sn‐based materials also exhibit high theoretical specific capacities by similar sodiation mechanisms, as summarized and shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. Moreover, the more competitive silicon‐based materials with a higher capacity in LIBs show low reaction kinetics with Na and therefore are unsuitable as sodium‐storage materials. Considering all factors, Sn and its compounds are a desirable class of materials for Na‐ion storage.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Theoretical gravimetric and volumetric capacities for various Sn‐based anode materials in Na‐ion batteries. The black bars denote gravimetric specific capacity; the red bars denote volumetric specific capacity.</caption></figure><p>Many hurdles require clarification for Sn‐based materials during (de)sodiation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0028 #adma202106895-bib-0029 #adma202106895-bib-0030"><sup>28–30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Huge volume changes (420%) occur after Na‐ion insertion into the Sn lattice, which causes the active materials to pulverize gradually and eventually lose connection with the current collectors. Sn agglomerates spontaneously during repeated (de)sodiation cycles, which aggravates the volume‐expansion effect, and extends the Na‐ion migration distance. A large gap remains between the current achievable and theoretical specific capacity because of limitations of sluggish kinetics. In response to these problems, many effective modification methods have been applied, which can be summarized as controlling the particle size,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0031 #adma202106895-bib-0032 #adma202106895-bib-0033 #adma202106895-bib-0034 #adma202106895-bib-0035"><sup>31–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> constructing unique structures<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0036 #adma202106895-bib-0037 #adma202106895-bib-0038 #adma202106895-bib-0039 #adma202106895-bib-0040 #adma202106895-bib-0041"><sup>36–41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and forming composite materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0042 #adma202106895-bib-0043 #adma202106895-bib-0044 #adma202106895-bib-0045 #adma202106895-bib-0046"><sup>42–46</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To date, several review papers have contributed to advancements of alloy‐based materials for SIBs. However, to the best of our knowledge, no focused review exists that details the recent progress of Sn‐based materials for sodium storage. Such a review is required urgently. Herein, state‐of‐the‐art advances of Sn‐based material for stable SIBs are presented in a comprehensive and detailed manner, and the reaction mechanisms, structural optimizations, and full‐cell designs are emphasized. Further development of future Sn‐based materials is proposed from a commercialization perspective.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0020"><title type="main">Reactions between Sn‐Based Materials and Sodium</title><p>During the reaction between Sn‐based materials and Na, various amorphous intermediate phases appear because of limitations of sluggish kinetics, and it is often difficult to probe their specific composition through conventional characterization methods, which means that the sodiation mechanism is complicated. However, because of a low recrystallization temperature, Sn agglomerates spontaneously to reduce its surface energy. Therefore, the Sn particles grow gradually and cause a more serious volume expansion, which yields active material pulverization and leads to battery failure, as illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0067 #adma202106895-bib-0068"><sup>67,68</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Strategies have been used to prevent Sn particle coarsening and to suppress large volume changes during sodium storage. These include the construction of multiphase composites (Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0004">4b</link>)<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0026 #adma202106895-bib-0069 #adma202106895-bib-0070"><sup>26,69,70</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and unique structures with special morphologies. However, the achievement of Sn‐based anode materials with a superior cycling stability and rate performance is not easy, and more breakthroughs in structural designs and composition optimizations are expected to emerge.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) Schematic diagram of (de)sodiation and structural evolution after multiple cycles of pure Sn (a), and pure Sn compositing with a second phase (b).</caption></figure><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0060"><title type="main">Sn–M Alloys</title><p>As mentioned previously, spontaneous agglomeration of Sn particles occurs during repeated cycles of Sn‐based electrodes, which results in structural damage and short‐term performance degradation. Therefore, besides primary Sn particle size control, severe aggregations must be avoided. One widely developed strategy is to alloy Sn with other metal elements, at the sacrifice of the Na storage capability, to allow the Sn‐based alloys to display better long‐term cycling stabilities and rate capabilities than pure Sn.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0052 #adma202106895-bib-0054 #adma202106895-bib-0055 #adma202106895-bib-0071"><sup>52,54,55,71</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0070"><title type="main">Sn‐Active Metal (Sb, Bi, Ge) Alloys</title><p>Yi et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0072"><sup>72</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared a hollow SnSb structure using nanoscaled Sn as template and ethyl alcohol as solvent, as revealed by the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and TEM images in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a,b</link>. As highlighted above, Sn and Sb react with sodium at different potentials, and the first generated Na<sub>3</sub>Sb serves as a matrix to buffer volume expansion and suppress Sn particle agglomeration.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0052"><sup>52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The hollow structure shortened the diffusion distance of Na ions and the internal pores could release mechanical stress. As a result, the obtained composites exhibit an excellent cycle performance with a high reversible capacity of 451.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at current rates of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0005">5c</link>). In addition, an excellent Sn‐based alloy anode is obtained for SIBs through component regulation and ratio optimization. For instance, Farbod et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0073"><sup>73</sup></link><sup>]</sup> produced a series of ternary Sn–Ge–Sb alloy films by magnetron sputtering, among which the Sn<sub>50</sub>Ge<sub>25</sub>Sb<sub>25</sub> exhibited the best performance with the first reversible specific capacity of 653 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at ≈0.5 C and remained at 491 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles. Xie et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0074"><sup>74</sup></link><sup>]</sup> obtained different ratios of films that were composed of elemental Sn, Bi, and Sb by sputtering, and the Sn<sub>10</sub>Bi<sub>10</sub>Sb<sub>80</sub> sample had the most stable cycling capability with a reversible capacity of 621 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. Further investigation of the mechanism suggests that Sn and Ge enter the Sb lattice to form a single‐phase solid solution. The structural deterioration that is caused by internal stresses can be suppressed owing to solid‐solution strengthening. Another favorable factor is that Sn and Ge dissolution changes the chemical potential of Sb, which results in a higher number of distinct sodiation processes.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) Hollow SnSb. a) SEM image, b) TEM image, and c) cycling performance at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. a–c) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0072"><sup>72</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2017, Elsevier. d–f) Ni<sub>3</sub>Sn<sub>2</sub> microcages. d) SEM image, e) TEM image, and f) cycling performance at a current density of 1C. d–f) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0076"><sup>76</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2014, American Chemical Society.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0080"><title type="main">Sn–Inactive Metal (Cu, Ni, Co, Fe, Mn, etc.) Alloys</title><p>Lin et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0054"><sup>54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared Sn–Cu alloys by a surfactant‐assisted wet‐chemistry method and used the nanocomposites as SIB anode materials. As an inactive phase, Cu does not participate in the sodium‐storage reactions, but enhances the electrical conductivities, hinders Sn coarsening, and buffers volume changes. Test results showed that the Sn<sub>0.9</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub> nanoparticles could deliver a reversible capacity of more than 420 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 C with a high capacity retention of 97% after 100 cycles. The number of pure Sn microparticles and Sn nanoparticles decreased rapidly during prolonged cycles with only 13% and 49% of the initial capacity, respectively. Li et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0075"><sup>75</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized Ni<i><sub>x</sub></i>Sn (0.6 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1.9) nanoparticles with a tuned composition, in which the Ni<sub>0.9</sub>Sn‐nanoparticle‐based electrodes showed the best performance toward Na ions insertion, at 160 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over 120 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>Although Sn alloys exhibit a better cycling stability than pure Sn, capacity degradation is inevitable after a limited number of cycles. To achieve more stable cycling performance, several unique structures have been constructed based on Sn alloys. For instance, Liu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0076"><sup>76</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized Ni<sub>3</sub>Sn<sub>2</sub> microcages that were composed of tiny nanoparticles based on the Ostwald ripening mechanism and etching treatment. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0005">5d,e</link>, the final products have a hollow structure with a highly porous surface, which facilitates Na‐ion transport and mitigates volume changes. The Ni matrix that is generated during sodiation could improve the conductivity and prevent Sn particle aggregation. A decent capacity of ≈270 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was maintained at 1 C during the 300 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0005">5f</link>). Ni et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0077"><sup>77</sup></link><sup>]</sup> constructed a binder‐free electrode by depositing Sn nanowall‐shaped arrays (denoted SnNA) on a copper substrate, followed by annealing at 180 °C for 2 h. Sn–Cu alloy formed at the interface between Sn arrays and the Cu substrate after heat treatment, which enhanced the structural stability as a strong bond. Voids in the SnNA provide buffering spaces for volume expansions. Therefore, the resultant SnNA showed superior reaction kinetics and a cycle stability. When the current density varies from 0.2 to 10 C, the reaction plateaus of the SnNA remain visible. Moreover, a high reversible capacity of 501 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> could be retained after 300 cycles even at a high current rate of 5C.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0090"><title type="main">Sn(M)–C Composites</title><p>Besides Sn‐based alloy synthesis, carbon decorations are another effective way to promote the electrochemical performance of pure Sn electrode, which prevents agglomeration, reduces huge volume variations in Sn particles and improves electrical conductivities, because carbon materials have excellent mechanical and electrochemical properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0078 #adma202106895-bib-0079"><sup>78,79</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0100"><title type="main">Spherical Sn–C Composites</title><p>Electrode materials with microsized spherical particles tend to have a lower surface area but higher tap density, which is usually prepared by spraying. Ying et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0080"><sup>80</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized Sn/N‐doped carbon microcage composites (Sn/NMCs) by using NaCl as a pore‐forming agent. Carbon matrices and ultrasmall Sn nanodots suppress volume changes and improve cycling performance, and the interconnected channels provide a rapid transport path for Na ions and electrons. Therefore, the Sn/NMCs exhibit a good electrochemical performance when used as SIB anodes. At 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the composites deliver an initial reversible capacity of 439 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which was maintained at 332 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles. In comparison, Sn‐based composite oxides, which are metal oxides that are composed of Sn and other metal elements, showed a better capacity attenuation and rate performance because of the synergistic effects between the metal components. The introduction of other metal elements can serve as a matrix to buffer the volume changes, improve the conductivity and act as a second phase to prevent severe Sn particle agglomeration. Zou et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0114"><sup>114</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared CoSnO<sub>3</sub>‐MOFs composites using hollow CoSnO<sub>3</sub> nanoboxes and 2‐methylimidazole as precursors. After heat treatment at 500 °C in Ar, the final product could be harvested. Cobalt is generated as an inactive phase during sodium insertion, which allows for an excellent sodium‐storage performance. After 1000 cycles, the resultant composites remained at a high capacity of 273.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> even at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Zhang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0115"><sup>115</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized amorphous CuSnO<sub>3</sub> nanospheres as an active substance for SIBs. They revealed the enhancement in conductivity by the metallic copper that was generated during cycling. The ex‐situ XPS results show that the position of the binding energy where Cu<sup>2+</sup> is located shifted gradually toward that of Cu<sup>0</sup>, which indicates that Cu<sup>2+</sup> was reduced to Cu during the first sodiation process, and the Cu<sup>0</sup> peak remained constant in the following cycles. Compared with the initial state, the cell exhibited a lower impedance after cycling. These unique Sn‐based oxides show promising prospect, which indicates that similar structures may be constructed in other Sn‐based compounds to achieve an excellent sodium‐storage performance.</p><p>From the above discussion, Sn oxides have better cycling stability compared with Sn because they form a Na<sub>2</sub>O matrix during Na‐ion insertion, which can buffer the volume changes and prevent Sn from agglomeration. However, the low ICE is unfavorable for their applications in sodium‐ion full batteries. Constructing oxygen vacancies, heterojunctions and Sn‐based bimetallic oxides can promote the ICE, the preparation process is complicated and the improvement is limited. For Sn oxides, more attention should still be focused on reducing the irreversibility of conversion reaction and improving the ICE with simple and effective methods.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0180"><title type="main">Sn Sulfides</title><p>Sn sulfides (SnS<sub>2</sub> and SnS) have received considerable attention and have been explored extensively for sodium storage owing to their prominent advantages. They have high theoretical specific capacities of 1136 and 1022 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Their unique 2D layered structure and wide layer spacing allow for Na‐ion insertion and efficient extraction.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0062 #adma202106895-bib-0116"><sup>62,116</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The SnS bond strength is weaker than that of the SnO bond, which provides Sn sulfides with a better reversibility in conversion reactions than Sn oxides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0117 #adma202106895-bib-0118"><sup>117,118</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0190"><title type="main">SnS<sub>2</sub>–RGO Nanocomposites</title><p>SnS<sub>2</sub> has a CdI2‐type layered structure with a large interlayer spacing (<i>a</i> = 0.3648 nm, <i>c</i> = 0.5899 nm, and space group: <i>P</i>3<i>m</i>1), which is conducive to the insertion and extraction of Na ions and the withstanding of a huge volume variation. Unfortunately, pure SnS<sub>2</sub> is unsuitable as an anode for high‐performance SIBs because of its poor cycling and rate performance during (de)sodiation.</p><p>To resolve these problems, carbon materials, especially 2D graphene, are commonly introduced to compound with SnS<sub>2</sub>. Xie et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0062"><sup>62</sup></link><sup>]</sup> proposed for the first time that the nanocomposite that is composed of SnS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets and graphene was used as an SIB anode. The composite showed an improved performance with an initial reversible capacity of 725 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Qu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0119"><sup>119</sup></link><sup>]</sup> combined layered SnS<sub>2</sub> with RGO as an anode material for SIBs (denoted SnS<sub>2</sub>‐RGO). The SnS<sub>2</sub>‐RGO displayed a good rate capability with a reversible capacity of 630 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 544 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and a stable cycling performance of 500 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> that remained at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 400 cycles.</p><p>Even though graphene addition to SnS<sub>2</sub> could facilitate the electrochemical performance, because of van der Waals forces, graphene and SnS<sub>2</sub> sheets tend to restack, which reduces the contact area with the electrolyte, slows the reaction kinetics, and results in agglomeration and pulverization of the active materials during repeated cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0102 #adma202106895-bib-0120 #adma202106895-bib-0121 #adma202106895-bib-0122"><sup>102,120–122</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Tu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0123"><sup>123</sup></link><sup>]</sup> designed RGO/SnS<sub>2</sub>/RGO composite materials with a sandwich structure by a liquid‐phase exfoliation approach. RGO can prevent SnS<sub>2</sub> aggregation and SnS<sub>2</sub> inhibits RGO restack. The composites exhibited a high specific capacity of 843 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a high capacity retention of 98% after 100 cycles. A decent specific capacity of 335 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> could be achieved even at a high current density of 8.4 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Jiang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0124"><sup>124</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized sandwich‐structured SnS<sub>2</sub>/RGO/SnS<sub>2</sub> composites by a one‐step hydrothermal method. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0011"><b>11</b>a</link>, this ingenious structure design provides SnS<sub>2</sub> with a larger interlayer spacing (≈8.03 Å), which favors rapid insertion and extraction of Na ions, and provides sufficient buffering spaces. The structure could be stabilized by suppressing the restacks of RGO and SnS<sub>2</sub>. Therefore, the SnS<sub>2</sub>/RGO/SnS<sub>2</sub> composites displayed a high specific capacity of 1133 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 100 cycles, a good capacity retention of 91.2% and an impressive rate capability with a reversible capacity of 765 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> even at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0011">11b,c</link>).</p><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0011"><label>11</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0011" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0011-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) SnS<sub>2</sub>/RGO/SnS<sub>2</sub>. a) Atom diagram of sandwich‐like structure. b) Cycle performance at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and c) rate capability from 0.1 to 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. a–c) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0124"><sup>124</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2019, American Chemical Society. d–f) SnS/CNS. d) Schematic diagram of preparation process and corresponding structure. e) Rate capability of SnS/CNS at different current densities from 0.5 to 20 A g<sup>−1</sup> and f) cycling performance of SnS/CNS, SnS, and N/C at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. d–f) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0134"><sup>134</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2017, Royal Society of Chemistry.</caption></figure><p>In addition to a compound with graphene, Chen et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0083"><sup>83</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared 1D self‐supported SnS<sub>2</sub>/C nanofiber materials (denoted SnS<sub>2</sub>/CNFs) by electrospinning and vulcanization. The SnS<sub>2</sub>/CNFs delivered a reversible specific capacity of 378.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after cycling 100 times at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 247.1 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Li et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0125"><sup>125</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized SnS<sub>2</sub>@C hollow nanospheres by a solvothermal method. Compared with SnS<sub>2</sub>/C bulk materials, composites with a hollow structure exhibited a better cycle property and rate capability. Our group reported a micro–nanostructured (SnS‐SnS<sub>2</sub>‐S)/FLG (few‐layered graphene) composite synthesized by a simple and facile plasma milling (P‐milling) method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0126"><sup>126</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The multiphase structure induced the formation of a high fraction of interfaces, accommodated the volume expansion, and prevented particle aggregation during sodiation, which enabled an ultrahigh, ultrafast, and ultrastable sodium‐storage performance. Even when cycled at 1.0 and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, high reversible capacities of 556 (90.5% retention) and 484.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (88.5% retention) were maintained after 500 cycles.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0200"><title type="main">Hierarchical SnS Supported with N‐Rich Carbon</title><p>SnS has a unique layered structure and large interlayer spacing (<i>c</i> = 0.4330 nm, and space group: <i>Pnma</i>). SnS exhibits a better cyclic stability than SnS<sub>2</sub> owing to the smaller structural changes during sodium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0065"><sup>65</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To achieve a decent cycling performance for SnS, strategies for designing porous and hierarchical structures, and especially combining SnS with RGO or S, N‐doped carbon have been proposed.</p><p>Choi et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0127"><sup>127</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared macroporous SnS microspheres by spray pyrolysis and vulcanization treatment. The large pores facilitate Na‐ion migration and charge transfer. The obtained SnS showed an improved ICE of 72% at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a good cycle stability and rate capability (e.g., delivering 364 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles and 336 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 1.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.). Cho et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0128"><sup>128</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized a 3D petal‐like structured SnS by the polyol method. The hierarchical structure could shorten the diffusion path for Na ions and provide enough space to relieve volume expansion during sodiation, which contributed to a higher power density and a more durable cycle life.</p><p>Although a unique structure can improve the sodium‐storage performance, pure SnS yields structural collapse during prolonged (de)sodiation. Similar to SnS<sub>2</sub>, carbon modification revamps the electrochemical properties of SnS.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0117 #adma202106895-bib-0129 #adma202106895-bib-0130 #adma202106895-bib-0131"><sup>117,129–131</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For instance, Li et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0132"><sup>132</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared SnS/RGO nanostructures by hydrothermal and annealing treatment. The composites delivered a high initial discharge capacity of 832 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which remained at 559 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 70 cycles. A superior rate performance with a reversible capacity of 456 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was achieved even at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Veerasubramani et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0133"><sup>133</sup></link><sup>]</sup> anchored extremely fine SnS quantum dots on nitrogen‐rich carbon nanospheres by a hydrothermal method and carbonization treatment. Under the synergistic effect of a carbon matrix and SnS quantum dots, the sample showed a reversible capacity of 172 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and a good capacity retention of 75% after 500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p>Sheng et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0134"><sup>134</sup></link><sup>]</sup> obtained a composite material with oriented SnS nanoflakes bonded to S‐doped nitrogen‐rich carbon nanoflakes (denoted SnS/CNS), as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0011">11d</link>. This delicate structure design provides numerous and rapid transport channels for Na ions. N, S co‐doping could improve the conductivity and produce more active sites for Na ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0135 #adma202106895-bib-0136 #adma202106895-bib-0137"><sup>135–137</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Consequently, the SnS/CNS composites displayed a distinguished rate capacity (250.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and ultrastable cycling capacity with a high capacity retention of 98% (474 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>), as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0011">11e,f</link>. To reveal the inner mechanism of enhanced sodium‐storage performance, variable speed cyclic voltammetry tests were performed. Calculations showed that the pseudocapacitance gradually dominated the capacity contribution as the scan rate varied from 0.2 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, which contributed to the remarkable reaction kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0118"><sup>118</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Overall, Sn sulfides are a very promising class of anode materials for SIBs due to their unique layer structure and large layer spacing, which facilitates the storage and rapid migration of sodium ions. Compared with Sn oxides, they have higher reversibility in conversion reaction and better electrical conductivity. In the future, precise synthesis control and energy density improvement could be the development directions and effective strategies should be further explored for Sn sulfides.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0210"><title type="main">Sn Selenides</title><p>Sn selenides are a potential anode material for sodium storage because of their decent capacity and better conductivity than Sn oxides and Sn sulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0138"><sup>138</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sn selenides can be classified into two categories, namely, SnSe<sub>2</sub> and SnSe. SnSe<sub>2</sub> is a CdI2‐type layered material (<i>a</i> = <i>b</i> = 3.81 Å, <i>c</i> = 6.14 Å, and space group: <i>P</i>3<i>m</i>1), which delivers a theoretical sodiation capacity of 756 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. SnSe is also a layered material and belongs to a IV–VI semiconductor with a narrow bandgap of 0.9 eV, which delivers a theoretical sodiation capacity of 780 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0045 #adma202106895-bib-0063"><sup>45,63</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0220"><title type="main">SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i> Dispersed in Carbon Matrix</title><p>Pure SnSe<sub>2</sub> and SnSe undergo serious pulverization and are broken during cycling when serving as SIB anodes. SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i> modification is needed to enhance the sodium‐storage performance. Zhang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0063"><sup>63</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized an SnSe<sub>2</sub>/RGO composite by using a hydrothermal method. SnSe<sub>2</sub> laminas are dispersed irregularly in the RGO matrix, and the high‐performance carbon material improves electrical conductivity, buffers volume changes and hinders particle aggregation. As a result, the SnSe<sub>2</sub>/RGO composites showed a stable cycling capability with a reversible capacity of 515 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a good rate performance with 365 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Zhang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared a SnSe/C nanocomposite by high‐energy ball milling. The resultant SnSe/C exhibited a superior cyclic property with a reversible capacity of 324.9 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> and an excellent rate performance with 598 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In stark contrast, pure SnSe experienced a sharp capacity decay with only 25.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles. Yang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0139"><sup>139</sup></link><sup>]</sup> used RGO material as a carbon source to compound with SnSe by ball milling. The composites showed a good high‐rate cycling capability with a high capacity retention of 98% at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 120 cycles.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0230"><title type="main">SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i>‐C Composites with Strong SeC Bond</title><p>Even though carbon coating improves the cycling stability to some extent, the weak force between the Sn selenide and carbon matrix easily leads to Sn–selenide detachment from the carbon matrix, which causes particle agglomeration and pulverization in a limited number of cycles. In recent years, several studies have been reported in which SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i> is bound tightly to the carbon matrix when strong SeC and SnC bonds form, which results in an ultrastable long cycling performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0033 #adma202106895-bib-0140 #adma202106895-bib-0141 #adma202106895-bib-0142 #adma202106895-bib-0143"><sup>33,140–143</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Yang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0140"><sup>140</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized a structure with SnSe<sub>2</sub> embedded in 3D porous carbon by a facile carbothermal reduction method, where SnSe<sub>2</sub> was bound firmly to the carbon matrix by a strong SeC bond. As expected, an excellent cycling stability was achieved, with a high reversible capacity of 340 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 170 cycles even at 2.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Xiao et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0141"><sup>141</sup></link><sup>]</sup> developed a multistep templating method to prepare yolk–shell SnSe<sub>2</sub> nanospheres, which was denoted SnSe<sub>2</sub>@Se‐C. The inner SnSe<sub>2</sub> core was integrated with the outer carbon shell by a strong SeC bond. This delicate design provided the composite with a superior high‐rate stability, and delivered reversible capacities of 441 and 406 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles, respectively. Our group reported a SnSe/FLG composite for an excellent sodium‐storage anode via the p‐milling method (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0012"><b>12</b>a,b</link>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0142"><sup>142</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Under the combined effect of a high‐energy ball milling and plasma discharge, substantial defects were created on the FLG matrix, which facilitated the formation of SnC and SeC bonds, as evidenced by the XPS results in Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0012">12c,d</link>. The strong affinity between SnSe nanoparticles and the FLG matrix prevented SnSe aggregation and detachment even after long‐term cycling. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0012">12e</link>, the capacity remained at 312.2 and 250.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at high current densities of 1.0 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 ultra‐long cycles, which corresponds to exceptional capacity retentions of 91.6% and 90.8%, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0012"><label>12</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0012" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0012-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–e) SnSe/FLG. a) Schematic illustration of fabrication and b) discharge–charge of SnSe/FLG with and without SnC and SeC cobonding. c,d) XPS spectra of Sn 3d (c) and Se 3d (d); e) cycling performance tested at high current densities of 1 and 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. a–e) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0142"><sup>142</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2019, American Chemical Society. f–h) Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@C yolk–shell structure. f) SEM image, g) TEM image, and h) long cycle stability at high current density of 1 and 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. f–h) Reproduced with permission.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0147"><sup>147</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Copyright 2017, Royal Society of Chemistry.</caption></figure><p>Sn selenides are also a very prospective anode materials for sodium storage, but they have received less concerns than Sn oxides and Sn sulfides. Although they are not prominent in specific capacity, the conductivity is the best among the three Sn‐based composites. By forming SeC bonds with carbon substrates, ultralong cycle life and excellent rate performance for SIBs could be achieved. Therefore, more attention can be placed on Sn selenides in future scientific studies.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0240"><title type="main">Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@C Yolk–Shell Structure</title><p>Sn phosphides possess a high theoretical specific capacity (e.g., 1132 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>) with a suitable sodium‐storage potential (≈0.4 V), which makes them attractive SIB anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0144"><sup>144</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the intrinsic poor electrical conductivity (10<sup>−14</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>) of phosphorus (P) and huge volume variations after full sodiation restrict their practical application.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0145"><sup>145</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Kim et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0066"><sup>66</sup></link><sup>]</sup> first utilized Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub> as SIB anode material. During sodiation, Sn nanoparticles act as an electronic channel to activate the P component, and the first generated Na<sub>3</sub>P during the conversion suppressed Sn agglomeration and provided a matrix for buffering huge volume expansion. A high reversible specific capacity of 718 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> was obtained at 0.15 C and remained stable for more than 100 cycles without obvious capacity decay. Huang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0146"><sup>146</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared multi‐shell Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub> nanospheres by using a low‐temperature solvothermal method. This unique structure accommodated huge volume changes, shortened the Na‐ion migration distance, and produced more active sites for Na‐ion storage. Pure Sn phosphides face challenges of limited cycle numbers and a poor rate capability, because they suffer from more severe volume variations and poorer conductivities than other Sn‐based sodium‐storage materials. Therefore, there is an urgent need for powerful measures to overcome these problems. Liu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@C nanospheres with a unique yolk‐shell structure through carbon coating, H<sub>2</sub> reduction, and phosphating. The reserved space between the Sn phosphide and carbon material could be used to buffer large volume changes. The carbon shell improves conductivities and prevents direct SEI film generation on the Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub> surface, which avoids the repeated rupture and formation of SEI films because of significant volume changes. As a result, a stable cycling performance was achieved with a high capacity of 360 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.5 C after 400 cycles. Ma et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0147"><sup>147</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared yolk–shell structured Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@C nanocubes, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0012">12f,g</link>. The Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@C yolk–shell maintained its structural integrity after extensive cycling, which exhibited an outstanding long‐term cycle stability of 516 and 368 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles at 1.0 and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0012">12h</link>). As a comparison, the Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>/C and bare Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub> experienced structural collapse that was induced by the severe volume changes during (de)sodiation. More recently, Wang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> obtained a Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@HC co‐storage anode for SIBs by ball milling. HC can store Na ions as an active material, meanwhile, it can buffer the large volume changes of Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub> during (de)sodiation, and help to improve the cycle stability. Therefore, a decent capacity of 312 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> could be maintained at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 400 cycles.</p><p>In general, Sn phosphides are an another very interesting class of Sn‐based materials for sodium storage and deserve to be further investigated. Their moderate sodium‐storage potential and high specific capacity are beneficial to achieve high energy density for SIBs. However, at the moment, two major problems of poor electrical conductivity and large volume variation still require effective strategies to address them.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0250"><title type="main">Full‐Cell Design</title><p>The previous discussion focused on the half‐cell configurations that were composed of Sn‐based anode materials and sodium metal, whereas the full cell was rarely involved, which is important in practical applications. Because the amount of sodium ions is almost infinite in half cells, it does not really reflect the true performance of the battery and the high‐capacity anode materials. A discussion of the full cell is indispensable to evaluate the battery performance. In this section, the current status of the application of Sn‐based materials in sodium‐ion full batteries is described and a brief outlook on further developments is provided.</p><p>Hasa et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0148"><sup>148</sup></link><sup>]</sup> reported a SIB that was formed by coupling a NaFePO<sub>4</sub> cathode and Sn/C composite as anode as early as 2014. The battery delivered a maximum capacity of 150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at an average voltage of 3 V, which corresponds to a theoretical energy density of ≈450 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>. However, the cycling stability was limited to 20 cycles, which is far from the standard that is required for commercialization. Walter et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0149"><sup>149</sup></link><sup>]</sup> synthesized SnSb nanocrystals of 10–30 nm and paired them with a Na<sub>1.5</sub>VPO<sub>4.8</sub>F<sub>0.7</sub> cathode to form a full‐cell battery. The SnSb/Na<sub>1.5</sub>VPO<sub>4.8</sub>F<sub>0.7</sub> full‐cell could deliver 400 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, with an average discharge voltage of 2.7 V. Its cycling performance was still not good enough and only 60 cycles were maintained. Some Sn‐based materials based on conversion reactions have also been reported as anodes for sodium‐ion full batteries. For instance, Qu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0119"><sup>119</sup></link><sup>]</sup> developed the SnS<sub>2</sub>‐RGO as an anode for a sodium‐ion full battery, with P2 phase Na<sub>0.80</sub>Li<sub>0.12</sub>Ni<sub>0.22</sub>Mn<sub>0.66</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as a cathode. Because of the formation of SEI and some side reactions that occurred during the first charge and discharge process, which led to Na‐ion depletion, the initial capacity loss reached 0.398 mA h (36% loss compared with the first discharge capacity). After 50 cycle times at 0.334 mA, the full cell only retained 74% of the initial capacity. Sun et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0150"><sup>150</sup></link><sup>]</sup> constructed a full cell that was composed of an SnS<sub>2</sub> anode and an Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode. The cell delivered the first charge and discharge capacities of 559 and 375 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, with an ICE of 67%. Unfortunately, the cell also exhibited a poor cycling performance and further optimization is required.</p><p>During the advancing of the commercialization of sodium‐ion full batteries, the anode materials used are mainly HC owing to the advantages of abundant raw materials, a good structural stability and a low sodiation potential.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0151 #adma202106895-bib-0152 #adma202106895-bib-0153 #adma202106895-bib-0154"><sup>151–154</sup></link><sup>]</sup> More recently, the CATL Co. Ltd. released a new SIB in July 2021, with a cell energy density of 160 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> by using an HC anode and a Prussian layer cathode. However, the low sodiation potential of the HC led to sodium dendrite growth and caused a safety hazard. Increased requirements of power batteries for long endurance working implies great opportunities for high‐capacity Sn‐based materials, because the energy density of the full cell depends on the potential difference between the cathode and anode materials, and is related closely to the electrode material capacity. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0013"><b>13</b></link> shows the full‐cell model that is based on the Sn‐based anode materials (Sn, SnSb, Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>, etc.) and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode, and the corresponding charge/discharge curves. The Sn, SnSb, and Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub> delivered the first reversible specific capacities of 839.8, 403.1,, and 507.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. Based on the potential difference between the cathode and anode electrodes and the reversible specific capacity, the energy densities were calculated as 265, 183 and 224 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (energy density = voltage × specific capacity). For Sn–X (O, S, Se) negative materials, because of the higher voltage plateau during (de)sodiation, cathodes with higher voltage plateaus are needed for pairing, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202106895-fig-0014"><b>14</b></link>. The SnO<i><sub>x</sub></i>, SnS<i><sub>x</sub></i> and SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i> delivered the first reversible specific capacities of 335.8, 817 and 475.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. Similarly, the corresponding energy densities were calculated in the same way as 181, 281 and 297 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>. The data indicate that high‐capacity Sn‐based materials have the potential to achieve high energy density as anode materials for sodium‐ion full batteries, and with the development and application of new cathode materials with higher voltage and capacity, the energy density can be further promoted. A long cycle stability is equally critical for high‐performance sodium‐ion full batteries, and requires that the structural integrity of the positive and negative electrodes be maintained during repeated (de)sodiations. To achieve large‐scale applications of Sn‐based materials in sodium‐ion full batteries, high‐capacity, high‐voltage platform and structurally stable cathode materials need to be developed.</p><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0013"><label>13</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0013" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0013-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Full‐cell model composed of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode and Sn‐based anode (Sn, SnSb and Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>). b) Charge and c) discharge curves of Sn‐based materials (Sn, SnSb, and Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>) and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="adma202106895-fig-0014"><label>14</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202106895:adma202106895-fig-0014" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202106895-fig-0014-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Full‐cell model composed of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.17</sub>Co<sub>0.33</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode and Sn‐based anode (SnO<i><sub>x</sub></i>, SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i> and SnS<i><sub>x</sub></i>). b) Charge and c) discharge curves of Sn‐based materials (SnO<i><sub>x</sub></i>, SnSe<i><sub>x</sub></i> and SnS<i><sub>x</sub></i>) and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.17</sub>Co<sub>0.33</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.</caption></figure><p>The electrolyte also plays a key role in battery performance. Generally speaking, high ionic conductivity, wide electrochemical window, excellent thermal stability, and low price, etc. are basically required. In addition, the electrolyte should have good compatibility with the electrodes to ensure good structural stability during repeated cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0155 #adma202106895-bib-0156 #adma202106895-bib-0157 #adma202106895-bib-0158 #adma202106895-bib-0159 #adma202106895-bib-0160 #adma202106895-bib-0161 #adma202106895-bib-0162 #adma202106895-bib-0163 #adma202106895-bib-0164"><sup>155–164</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Currently, ester‐based (ethylene carbonate (EC), propylene carbonate (PC), diethyl carbonate (DEC), dimethyl carbonate (DMC) and ethyl methyl carbonate (EMC), etc.) electrolytes can meet the above requirements, so they get commonly used in the research of SIBs, as summarized in <b>Table</b> <link href="#adma202106895-tbl-0001"><b>1</b></link>. However, ester‐based electrolytes are less resistant against reduction, which leads to a thick SEI film on the anode and a low ICE, and is unfavorable for the applications in full batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0165"><sup>165</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In recent years, ether‐based electrolytes have received increased attention from researchers. Using ether‐based electrolytes as a replacement of ester‐based electrolytes is beneficial to obtain thinner and elastic SEI films, which contributes to more superior electrochemical performance by promoting the ICE and buffering the huge volume changes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0165 #adma202106895-bib-0166 #adma202106895-bib-0167 #adma202106895-bib-0168"><sup>165–168</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, to realize the large‐scale applications of ether‐based electrolytes in SIBs, several issues, such as self‐instability and narrow electrochemical windows, need to be carefully settled in future. Moreover, the safety problems should be considered, since the flammability, liquid leakage and corrosion have raised extensive concerns. To deal with these challenges and to construct safer full batteries, research on ionic liquids and solid‐state electrolytes has been undertaken in recent years.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0169 #adma202106895-bib-0170 #adma202106895-bib-0171 #adma202106895-bib-0172 #adma202106895-bib-0173"><sup>169–173</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides electrolytes, modifications for other components in SIBs are also very important, such as electrolyte additives, binders and separators, etc. For example, fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) addition into electrolytes contributes to a more stable SEI film formation, which could improve the cycle stability significantly.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0174 #adma202106895-bib-0175 #adma202106895-bib-0176 #adma202106895-bib-0177 #adma202106895-bib-0178"><sup>174–178</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Polyacrylic acid<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0179 #adma202106895-bib-0180 #adma202106895-bib-0181"><sup>179–181</sup></link><sup>]</sup> or carboxymethyl cellulose<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0182 #adma202106895-bib-0183 #adma202106895-bib-0184 #adma202106895-bib-0185"><sup>182–185</sup></link><sup>]</sup> as a poly(vinylidene fluoride) replacement as a binder could enhance the adhesion of active substances to the current collect and avoid the use of toxic <i>N</i>‐methylpyrrolidone as solvent. As for battery separators, glass fiber is the most used in SIBs, especially in PC solvent, because PC solvent can decompose polypropylene membrane. However, the use of glass fiber lowers the energy density based on the mass or volume of all components of sodium‐ion full batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202106895-bib-0186 #adma202106895-bib-0187 #adma202106895-bib-0188 #adma202106895-bib-0189 #adma202106895-bib-0190"><sup>186–190</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the development of thinner, lighter, more compatible The main reason is that Sn‐based materials suffer severe structural damage during sodium storage. The development of cathode materials and electrolytes that match Sn‐based anodes remains far from adequate. With increased attention and effort, the application of Sn‐based materials in sodium‐ion full batteries will provide a breakthrough in the future.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0260"><title type="main">Conclusions and Outlook</title><p>We have summarized recent advances in several Sn‐based anode materials (including Sn alloys, Sn oxides, Sn sulfides, Sn selenides, Sn phosphides, and their composites) for SIBs from the perspective of reaction mechanisms, structural designs and full‐cell construction. Compared with carbon anodes for SIBs, Sn‐based materials have a higher capacity and exhibit a good sodium‐storage performance after reasonable structural designs and component optimizations. However, Sn‐based materials undergo a severe capacity decay from the huge volume changes and sluggish reaction kinetics during sodium storage, which hinders their further application. To date, researchers have adopted many approaches for promoting cyclic properties and rate capabilities, which can be classified as controlling the particle size, constructing unique structures, and forming composites with carbon supports. <b>Table</b> <link href="#adma202106895-tbl-0001"><b>1</b></link> summarizes the structures of Sn‐based materials, and their electrochemical performance for SIBs, among which the 3D Sn/NS‐CNFs@RGO exhibited the most durable cycling performance, with a remaining specific capacity of 373 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The SnSe<sub>2</sub>@Se‐C showed the best rate capability with a decent specific capacity of 279 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> even at an extremely high current density of 100 A g<sup>−1</sup>. There is still a long way to go to realize commercial application of Sn‐based materials for SIBs, because an ideal Sn‐based anode, which can simultaneously meet the standard of a high ICE, long cycle stability and superior rate performance, has not been developed yet.</p><p>In the future, numerous efforts should focus on Sn‐based anodes to achieve high‐performance SIBs. The structural integrity must be maintained during multiple cycles. The ICE and rate capability must be facilitated. Particle size control and stress relief that are caused by violent volume changes are important and necessary. Measures, such as second‐phase addition (carbon materials or transition metal elements) and the reservation of enough buffer spaces (porous, hollow, or yolk–shell structures, etc.) during synthesis can be applied. A shortening of the migration distance and a promotion of the Na‐ion transfer rate are also urgently required. For the former, nanosized, layered, and hollow structures have been constructed to reduce the Na‐ion diffusion path. For the latter, conductivity material introduction, oxygen vacancy engineering, heteroatom doping, and heterojunction formation are feasible ways to enhance the Na‐ion diffusion rate. The sodium‐storage properties of Sn‐based materials require further improvement. It may be possible to combine these modifications through rational designs, to achieve an excellent sodium‐storage performance and reach commercial application requirements.</p><p>Meanwhile, more effort should be devoted to the development of full batteries rather than stopping at half‐cell research, because full batteries with a high energy density and long life are more significant for practical application. Simultaneous optimization of the remaining battery components, except for the anode material, should be considered. In addition, sodium storage at low temperature (low‐<i>T</i>) is another important aspect for wider applications of SIBs. However, Na‐transfer kinetics is very sluggish at room temperature, let alone at low‐<i>T</i>. To date, there are very few reports on SIBs under low‐<i>T</i> circumstance, and it is almost a gap for Sn‐based materials in this field. In future, Sn‐based materials for low‐<i>T</i> applications of SIBs should also arouse our attention, besides the preparation of advanced electrode materials, it is crucial that new electrolyte systems with low melting point, high ionic conductivity, and low desolvation energy are developed.</p><p>Besides battery performance, increased attention should focus on those methods that can be scaled‐up easily as well, such as ball milling or other facile synthetic techniques, to lower production costs and achieve large‐scale commercial adoption. An important parameter, tap density, should be considered because it is related closely to the energy density in practical application, whereas it is often ignored in laboratory research. For instance, a high ratio of carbon material is usually added in many studies to ensure a good conductivity and structural stability of Sn‐based anodes. However, too much carbon reduces the tap density and energy density significantly because it is fluffy and almost inactive to sodium. Therefore, it is important to ensure that the content of carbon material remains within a suitable range when Sn‐based composites are prepared.</p><p>Although the development of Sn‐based anode materials for SIBs faces many challenges now, with the joint efforts of relevant researchers, fundamental and technical breakthroughs will be made soon.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202106895-sec-0270" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Nos. 52071144, 51822104, 51831009, 51621001, 51925207, U1910210, and 51872277), and the Guangzhou key research and development program (202102040001), the National Synchrotron Radiation Laboratory (KY2060000173), the Joint Fund of the Yulin University and the Dalian National Laboratory for Clean Energy (Grant.
