Ultramicropores are considered essential for attaining high‐rate capacity as well as initial Coulombic efficiency by allowing the rapid diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> and inhibiting the contact of the electrolyte with the inner carbon surfaces. Herein, hard carbon nanosheets with centralized ultramicropores (≈0.5 nm) and easily accessible carbonyl groups (CO)/hydroxy groups (OH) are synthesized via interfacial assembly and carbonization strategies, delivering a large capacity (318 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup>), superior rate capability (145 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and approximately 95% of reversible capacity below 1.00 V. Notably, a new charge model favoring fast capacitive sodium storage with dual potential plateaus is proposed. That is, the deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> from graphitic layers is manifested as the low‐potential plateau region (0.01−0.10 V), contributing to stable insertion capacity; meanwhile, the surface desodiation process of the CO and OH groups corresponds to the high‐potential plateau region (0.40−0.70 V), contributing to a fast capacitive storage.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202000447-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Hard carbon nanosheets with centralized ultramicropores (≈0.5 nm), accessible functional CO/OH groups, and large graphitic layer spacings exhibit excellent sodium‐storage properties. The desodiation process from graphitic layers and CO/OH groups results in a new sodium‐storage characteristic with dual‐potential plateaus during the charge process, which favors a high output of 95%, realistic capacity, and rapidly capacitive sodium storage.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202000447-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202000447-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202000447-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are considered a promising alternative to Li‐ion batteries for large‐scale energy storage systems due to their low cost and earth‐abundant resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0001 #adma202000447-bib-0002 #adma202000447-bib-0003 #adma202000447-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The search for SIB anode materials with high reversible capacity, high rate capability, and long lifespan is a daunting task because of the more sluggish diffusion kinetics and a larger radius of Na<sup>+</sup> than Li<sup>+</sup>. Compared with titanium‐based oxides with low reversible capacity,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0005 #adma202000447-bib-0006 #adma202000447-bib-0007 #adma202000447-bib-0008 #adma202000447-bib-0009 #adma202000447-bib-0010"><sup>5–10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and metallic alloys and conversion materials with poor structural stability,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0011 #adma202000447-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> hard carbons comprising a large number of graphitic microcrystallites, defects, and pores show superior performance and have been used for practical applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Recently, hard carbons including highly graphitized hard carbons and porous hard carbons have been widely investigated, and a specific capacity of 200–500 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) in the 30–80% range, and an acceptable rate capability were achieved.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0014 #adma202000447-bib-0015 #adma202000447-bib-0016 #adma202000447-bib-0017 #adma202000447-bib-0018"><sup>14–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, these three key indicators of electrochemical performance are often contradictory. For example, the high rate capability of porous hard carbons is always accompanied by a low ICE, whereas the opposite is obtained for highly graphitized hard carbons. This phenomenon originates from the complex sodium‐storage behaviors arising from the diversity of the active sites in hard carbons. The control of homogeneous active sites of hard carbons remains a great challenge and requires ongoing research effort.</p><p>Sodium storage behaviors are mainly divided into three categories: adsorption on the edges, defects, and functional groups, insertion in the graphitic interlayers, and pore filling. In the charge curve of anode materials, highly graphitized hard carbons normally exhibit two regions: a low‐potential plateau (0.01–0.10 V) and a high‐potential slope (0.10–1.00 V). The reversible capacity of the materials is mainly due to the plateau region. However, this plateau capacity is easily affected by polarization at a high current density, resulting in a decreased sodium storage capacity. Although the correspondence of sodium storage behaviors to different potential regions has been controversial, it is known that the diffusion kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> can be improved by the introduction of porosity as an effective strategy for reducing the influence of polarization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0013 #adma202000447-bib-0019 #adma202000447-bib-0020 #adma202000447-bib-0021"><sup>13,19–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Previous studies have shown that porous hard carbons normally have high reversible capacity and high rate capability, yet low ICE due to a large irreversible capacity loss arising from numerous defects and a large specific surface area.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0013 #adma202000447-bib-0022 #adma202000447-bib-0023 #adma202000447-bib-0024"><sup>13,22–24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Their sodium storage behaviors generally exhibit a slope potential region (0.01–3.00 V), but the charge specific capacity below 1.00 V is usually lower than 50%.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0018 #adma202000447-bib-0025 #adma202000447-bib-0026"><sup>18,25,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In practical use, the charge capacity below 1.00 V of a half‐cell is considered to determine its realistic capacity, and a high charge potential lowers the operating voltage window of a practical full‐cell.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Consequently, we expect that the porous hard carbon anode should possess a high actual capacity at a low charge potential. All the abovementioned issues necessitate a breakthrough in the designed synthesis of specific hard carbons in order to improve the rate capacity while increasing the utilization of reversible capacity below 1.00 V.</p><p>As reported in previous work, ultramicropores (size of &lt;0.7 nm) can provide transport channels for Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and slow down the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film formation by reducing the contact between the carbon surface and the electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, during the first discharge process, a number of larger micropores (&gt;0.7 nm) that will promote the formation of SEI films are still present. Therefore, the synthesis of hard carbon anodes with uniform ultramicropores is highly desirable albeit quite difficult.</p><p>Furthermore, heteroatom doping (e.g., N, S, O, and P) of carbon anode materials is expected to be an effective strategy for enhancing the Na‐ion storage capacity and rate capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0029 #adma202000447-bib-0030 #adma202000447-bib-0031"><sup>29–31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In particular, CO and OH groups are involved in the redox reaction and the chemical absorption of Na<sup>+</sup>, contributing to the capacitive‐type sodium storage capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0032 #adma202000447-bib-0033 #adma202000447-bib-0034"><sup>32–34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the capacity derived from the sodium storage process is usually manifested as a slope potential profile accompanied by a low availability for a real full‐cell. To address this issue, the CO/OH groups should be easily accessible to increase the rate and homogeneity of the process.</p><p>In this study, sheet‐like hard carbons with centralized ultramicropores (≈0.5 nm) and accessible CO/OH groups that exhibit a large capacity (318 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup>), superior rate capability (145 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and high ICE (71.2%) have been synthesized. Below 1.00 V, approximately 95% of the specific capacity is available for practical use. Meanwhile, a new charge model with two potential plateaus was proposed.</p><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a−c</link>, the synthesized hard carbons show a sheet‐like morphology, with hard carbon nanosheets stacked on top of each other to form open structures. High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001">1d−f</link>) show that the microstructures of the three samples have massive crystallites stacked by graphitic layers. The gradual growth of the graphitic domains indicates that the graphitization degree of the carbon materials increases as the carbonization temperature is increased from 900 to 1100 °C. The carbon materials obtained by the carbonization at these temperatures are referred to as MPC‐900, MPC‐1000, and MPC‐1100. The graphitic interlayer spacings of MPC‐900, MPC‐1000, and MPC‐1100 were observed to be as large as 0.381, 0.395, and 0.378 nm, respectively, which may facilitate Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and deintercalation. Furthermore, the uniformly distributed ultramicropores of MPC‐1000 are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001">1e</link> and Figure S1, Supporting Information.</p><figure xml:id="adma202000447-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202000447-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a−g) Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of the hard carbon nanosheets: a,d) MPC‐900; b,e) MPC‐1000; c,f) MPC‐1100; g) XRD patterns. h) Raman spectra, and i) pore size distributions of sample MPC‐900 and MPC‐1000.</caption></figure><p>The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the three materials show the typical characteristics of amorphous carbon with two broad diffractions at 2θ of ≈23° and ≈42° representing the (002) and (100) planes, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001">1g</link>). The (002) peak of MPC‐1000 is shifted to a lower angle compared to those of MPC‐900 and MPC‐1100, indicating the existence of a large graphitic layer spacing (<i>d</i><sub>002</sub>). The <i>d</i><sub>002</sub> values for MPC‐900, MPC‐1000, and MPC‐1100 were calculated as 0.387, 0.400, and 0.379 nm, respectively, in agreement with the HRTEM observations. As the carbonization temperature increases, the graphitic layers spacing first increases and then decreases; this is usually related to the heteroatom (N, O) doping in the graphitic structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0035"><sup>35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Raman spectrum of the samples shows two typical peaks: the D peak at 1350 cm<sup>−1</sup> and the G peak at 1580 cm<sup>−1</sup> that are ascribed to the disorder or defective graphite band and the crystalline graphite band, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001">1h</link>). The calculated peak area ratio (<i>A</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>A</i><sub>G</sub>) of the D‐ and G‐bands increases from 0.34 for MPC‐900 to 0.40 for MPC‐1000 and then increases further to 0.48 for MPC‐1100, indicating that the degree of graphitization gradually increases with increasing carbonization temperature. These results are also in good agreement with the HRTEM observations (Figure S2, Supporting Information).</p><p>Nitrogen sorption isotherms of the three samples show a type I characteristics with a narrow knee at low relative pressures (<i>P/P</i><sub>0</sub> &lt; 0.01), indicating that the carbon nanosheets have a microporous structure (Figure S3, Supporting Information). The Brunauer−Emmett−Teller surface areas of MPC‐900, MPC‐1000, and MPC‐1100 were found to be 654, 762, and 567 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The micropore sizes of the three materials are accordingly concentrated at 0.59, 0.52, and 0.52 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001">1i</link>) in the ultramicropores regime (&lt;0.7 nm). It is clear that the pore size of the ultramicropores decreases slightly with increasing carbonization temperature. To the best of our knowledge, such centralized ultramicropores without the presence of pores with other sizes are rarely observed in directly synthesized porous carbons.</p><p>The electrochemical performance of the carbon nanosheets was first investigated in an ether electrolyte via galvanostatic charge−discharge tests at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). For the first time, dual potential plateaus in the charge curves are found in the ranges of 0.01–0.10 V and 0.40–0.70 V with approximately 95% of the specific capacity below 1.00 V. In addition, the charge−discharge curves of MPC‐1000 from 2<sup>nd</sup> to 5<sup>th</sup> cycles at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup> show two obvious potential plateaus, illustrating that the new sodium storage mechanism has outstanding reversibility (Figure S4a, Supporting Information). This is advantageous for increasing the realistic capacity for practical applications. The reversible charge specific capacities and ICE were found to be 238 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and 64% for MPC‐900, 319 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and 71% for MPC‐1000, and 269 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and 75% for MPC‐1100. MPC‐1100 showed a high reversible capacity of up to 75%; this performance is unexpected for a hard carbon with a specific surface area of 567 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and is due to the significant effect of ultramicropores for reducing the SEI formation. Furthermore, a conventional ester‐based electrolyte was also adopted to test the first charge−discharge properties of MPC‐1000 (Figure S4b, Supporting Information). Consequently, dual potential plateaus in the charge curve can also be observed. The reversible charge specific capacity and ICE reach 288.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and 63% and are close to the values obtained in the ether electrolyte, indicating that hard carbons with high surface area and centralized ultramicropores can achieve a high ICE. It appears that the electrochemical performance of the ether‐base electrolyte is improved relative to that obtained using an ester‐based electrolyte. As reported in a previous study, a stable and thin SEI film can be formed on the surface of the hard carbon anode in an ether electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0035 #adma202000447-bib-0036 #adma202000447-bib-0037 #adma202000447-bib-0038 #adma202000447-bib-0039"><sup>35–39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this case, the polarization phenomenon will be suppressed, enabling a high ICE and excellent sodium storage capacity.</p><figure xml:id="adma202000447-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202000447-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical characterization of MPC‐900, MPC‐1000, and MPC‐1100 in ether electrolytes. a) First galvanostatic discharge−charge profiles at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and b) rate capability from 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup> to 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c,d) Long‐term cyclability of MPC‐1000 at 0.10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (c) and 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup> (d). e) Cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of MPC‐1000 at scan rates of 0.1–2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. f,g) Linear relationship between log <i>i</i> (logarithm currents) and log <i>v</i> (logarithm scan rate) of two pairs of redox peaks at various scan rates of 0.1–2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. h) Normalized contribution ratio of capacitive capacities of MPC‐1000 at different scan rates.</caption></figure><p>The rate performance characteristics of MPC‐900, MPC‐1000, and MPC‐1100 were studied at different current densities (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2b</link>). Remarkably, MPC‐1000 delivers excellent reversible capacities of 326, 310, 294, 277, 256, 239, 214, and 145 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.02, 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.50, 1.00, 2.00, and 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Comparison to previous reports shows that the rate performance of MPC‐1000 is among the best (Table S1, Supporting Information). The high rate capability of this sample originates from its well‐developed ultramicropore structure, large graphitic layer spacing, and open structures. For hard carbon anodes of SIBs, a trade‐off between the rate and ICE is always present and both properties must be taken into account. The loss of ICE can be compensated by the available presodiation technology.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0040 #adma202000447-bib-0041"><sup>40,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After charging and discharging at 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the charging capacity of MPC‐1000 can be restored to 322 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup>. These results indicate that MPC‐1000 exhibits superior structural stability, as can be further proved by the results of the cyclic performance tests (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2c,d</link>). The capacity retention of MPC‐1000 was 97% at 0.10 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. Even after 5000 cycles at 5.00 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity was still maintained at ≈70 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a decay rate of 0.01%, which is much less than 0.027 and 0.066%.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0015 #adma202000447-bib-0042"><sup>15,42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hence, we conclude that the ultramicroporous hard carbon nanosheets with dual potential plateaus can maintain a stable structure during long‐term cycles.</p><p>Furthermore, the sodium storage characteristics of MPC‐1000 were evaluated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements in order to determine the origin of the dual potential plateaus. The CV curves were obtained at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in the range of 0.01–2.00 V for three cycles (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The large irreversible oxidation peak observed at ≈0.60 V corresponds to the formation of the SEI film during the first discharge process. However, this peak did not appear during the subsequent discharge process, indicating that a stable SEI film was generated in the first scan. Furthermore, a pair of sharp reversible redox peaks and a pair of broad reversible redox peaks located at approximately 0.10 and 0.70 V, respectively, appear in the second and third CV curves. These correspond to the presence of the dual potential plateaus of the charge curves, reflecting the existence of two different reaction mechanisms. Furthermore, the sodium storage mechanism for MPC‐1000 was investigated by CV at the scan rates varying in the 0.1−2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> range (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2e</link>). The quantitative analysis of capacitance behavior can be carried out using Equation <link href="#adma202000447-disp-0001">1</link>.<displayedItem xml:id="adma202000447-disp-0001" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma202000447-math-0001.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>a</mi><msup><mi>ν</mi><mi>b</mi></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><p>The curves are drawn by plotting ln (<i>v</i>) and ln (<i>i</i>) to determine the <i>b</i> values. The <i>b</i> values for the redox peaks located at 0.1 V were 0.86/0.76 at the scan rates in the range of 0.1–0.6 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and were 0.41/0.46 at the scan rates in the range of 0.8–2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating a diffusion‐controlled process as indicated by the <i>b</i> value approaching 0.50 in the Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2f</link> and S6, Supporting Information. The <i>b</i> value of 1.07 for another cathodic peak located at 0.70 V is close to 1.00 at the scan rates in the range 0.1–2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, indicating that the reaction is dominated by capacitive behavior (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2g</link>). This is key for achieving good rate performance and high‐energy density at high current densities. The insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> into graphitic layers and nanovoids is considered to be a diffusion process, while the adsorption of sodium ions induced by edges, defects, and functional groups is defined as capacitive behavior.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0001 #adma202000447-bib-0031 #adma202000447-bib-0043 #adma202000447-bib-0044"><sup>1,31,43,44</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Based on the studies of Dunn and Chao, the ratio of capacitive contribution can be calculated using Equation <link href="#adma202000447-disp-0002">2</link>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0045 #adma202000447-bib-0046 #adma202000447-bib-0047 #adma202000447-bib-0048"><sup>45–48</sup></link><sup>]</sup><displayedItem xml:id="adma202000447-disp-0002" type="mathematics"><label>2</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/adma202000447-math-0002.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mrow><mfenced><mi>V</mi></mfenced></mrow><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>k</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mi>v</mi><mo>+</mo><msub><mi>k</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><msup><mi>v</mi><mrow><mn>1</mn><mo>/</mo><mn>2</mn></mrow></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem>where <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> are the energy storage contributions from the capacitive and diffusion‐controlled processes, respectively. For MPC‐1000, the capacitive contribution increased to 85% at 2.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2h</link>), showing that the capacitive capacity is mainly attributed to the sodium storage behavior at 0.7 V at a high current density.</p><p>To further analyze the sodium storage behaviors in different potential ranges, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was used to calculate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient using a pulse current of 0.02 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 0.5 h accompanied by a rest interval of 2.0 h (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a,b</link>). The diffusion coefficient was computed according to Fick's second law (Equation <link href="#adma202000447-disp-0003">3</link>):<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0026 #adma202000447-bib-0049"><sup>26,49</sup></link><sup>]</sup><displayedItem xml:id="adma202000447-disp-0003" type="mathematics"><label>3</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-math-0003" wiley:location="graphic/adma202000447-math-0003.png"><mrow><mi>D</mi><mo>=</mo><mfrac><mn>4</mn><mrow><mi>π</mi><mi>τ</mi></mrow></mfrac><msup><mrow><mrow><mfenced><mrow><mfrac><mrow><msub><mi>m</mi><mtext>B</mtext></msub><msub><mi>V</mi><mtext>M</mtext></msub></mrow><mrow><msub><mi>M</mi><mtext>B</mtext></msub><mi>S</mi></mrow></mfrac></mrow></mfenced></mrow></mrow><mn>2</mn></msup><msup><mrow><mrow><mfenced><mrow><mfrac><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">Δ</mi><msub><mi>E</mi><mtext>s</mtext></msub></mrow><mrow><mi mathvariant="normal">Δ</mi><msub><mi>E</mi><mi>τ</mi></msub></mrow></mfrac></mrow></mfenced></mrow></mrow><mn>2</mn></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem>where τ (s), <i>m</i><sub>B</sub> (g), <i>V</i><sub>M</sub> (cm<sup>3</sup> mol<sup>−1</sup>), <i>M</i><sub>B</sub> (g mol<sup>−1</sup>), and <i>S</i> (m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>) are the pulse duration, active mass of the electrode, molar volume, molecular weight, and active surface area of the electrode, respectively. Additionally, ∆<i>E</i><sub>S</sub> and ∆<i>E</i><sub>τ</sub> are obtained from the GITT curves. Based on these results, the sodium storage behavior can be assigned to three regions consisting of a low‐potential plateau, a high‐potential plateau, and a high‐potential slope. For the plateau in the 0.01–0.10 V range, the diffusion coefficients of discharge show a variation at 0.06 V, demonstrating the existence of two different sodium storage mechanisms (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0003">3c</link>). During the discharge process, a single low‐potential plateau region appears. Sodium ions first insert into graphitic layers and later fill into micropores.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0044 #adma202000447-bib-0050"><sup>44,50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Further, the diffusion coefficients vary at 0.10 V during the charge process (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0003">3d</link>). Sodium ions first leave the graphitic layers (0.01–0.10 V), and then depart from the micropores (0.10–0.40 V) because of the weak interaction between sodium and the micropores, similar to Li‐ion batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0051"><sup>51</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Considering the results presented in Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0001">1h</link> and <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0002">2a</link>, the increase in the charge capacity in the range of 0.01–0.10 V is consistent with the overall trend for the degree of graphitization of the samples, indicating that sodium ions can be stored between graphitic layers.</p><figure xml:id="adma202000447-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202000447-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a−d) Galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) potential profiles and Na<sup>+</sup> apparent diffusion coefficients calculated from the GITT potential profiles of MPC‐1000 for discharge process (a,c) and charge process (b,d) during the second cycle.</caption></figure><p>Ex situ X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was used to investigate the structure of the MPC‐1000 electrode at different charge−discharge states in order to explore the sodium storage mechanism. As seen in Figure S9a, Supporting Information, the XPS spectrum of MPC‐1000 displays three peaks of C 1s, N 1s, and O 1s. For pristine poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF), the N 1s peak is inconspicuous because of the dilution effect due to the addition of carbon nanotubes and PVDF, and a new F 1s peak appears due to the −CF<sub>2</sub>− bond in PVDF. The changes in the intensity of the Na 1s and Na KLL peaks can be observed during the entire sodium storage process.</p><p>The O 1s spectra can be divided into three peaks by fitting to the C–O, OH, and CO peaks at 531.1, 532.4, and 533.8 eV, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0025 #adma202000447-bib-0052"><sup>25,52</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During the discharge to 0.40 V and even to 0.10 V, the peak intensities of CO and OH are gradually reduced, while the peak intensity of C–O increases sharply, and a new Na KLL Auger peak appears at approximately 536.0 eV (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a−c</link>). This demonstrates that Na<sup>+</sup> ions are adsorbed onto the oxygen atoms of the OH groups, and react with the CO groups, giving rise to a surface−redox reaction described by CO + Na<sup>+</sup> + e<sup>−</sup> ↔ CONa. Furthermore, the changes in the peak intensity of −C−O−Na at 1072.0 eV in the Na 1s spectrum further confirm the sodium storage behavior due to the interaction between Na<sup>+</sup> and oxygen functional groups (Figure <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0004">4d−f</link>). As shown in Figure S7a,c, Supporting Information, after charging to 0.70 V, the stable desodiation process of CO/OH give rise to a high‐potential plateau region in the charge curve that can provide a surface‐dominated capacity. When the charging process was completed, the amount of the CONa and CO groups continuously decreased due to the desorption of Na<sup>+</sup> from the OH groups and the conversion of CONa to CO (Figure S7b,d, Supporting Information). The overall surface oxygen atom content of MPC‐1000 was approximately 3.43 at% as measured by XPS, and the overall bulk oxygen atom content was approximately 7.95 at%, as measured by elemental analysis (Figure S8 and Table S2, Supporting Information). Based on the oxygen content measured by elemental analysis, the MPC‐1000 sample can deliver a theoretical capacity of approximately 122 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, while the specific capacity of the high‐potential plateau region can provide approximately 110 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, accounting for 90% of the theoretical capacity (Figure S4, Supporting Information). In addition to the oxygen on the surface that can react with Na<sup>+</sup>, the oxygen inside the material can also be largely utilized. Although the subsequent high‐potential slope region corresponds to the reactions derived from a small fraction of oxygen‐containing functional groups with sodium ions, these groups were not considered. In addition, the CO groups are involved to a great extent in the redox reaction and eventually disappear after being discharged to 0.10 V. As seen from the above results, CO/OH groups can be largely utilized during the sodiationdesodiation process because of the abundant accessible ultramicropores of this material.</p><figure xml:id="adma202000447-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202000447-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a−f) The ex situ X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra of O 1s (a−c) and Na 1s (d−f) of the MPC‐1000 electrode at the stage of pristine, discharge to 0.40 V, and discharge to 0.10 V states.</caption></figure><p>The C 1s spectrum was further analyzed throughout the chargedischarge process (Figure S9b−f, Supporting Information). It is observed that the high‐potential sloping region is related to the storage of Na ions on the surface sites including on edges, defects, and some of the CO/OH groups. The edges and defects of the hard carbons are responsible for the sodium storage behavior in a slope‐dominated performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high‐potential plateau region corresponds to the sodium storage process of other CO/OH groups. However, the peak intensity cannot be restored due to the growth of sodium‐based compounds during the formation of the SEI film. Thus, the sodium storage behaviors at different charge−discharge stages are in good agreement with the results deduced from the C 1s and O 1s spectra.</p><p>In situ XRD is highly effective for detecting the change in the interlayer spacing during the sodiation−desodiation process. The XRD pattern of the first charge–discharge cycle of MPC‐1000 at 0.05A g<sup>−1</sup> is displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202000447-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>. The large and unchanged graphitic interlayer spacings (0.400 nm) indicate that this sample can satisfy the layer spacing variation caused by the intercalation of sodium ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202000447-bib-0031 #adma202000447-bib-0046 #adma202000447-bib-0053 #adma202000447-bib-0054"><sup>31,46,53,54</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Similar results are presented in Figure S10, Supporting Information. Sufficiently large graphitic layer spacings can alleviate the volume expansion during charge and discharge, facilitate rapid diffusion of sodium ions, and expose more active sites, endowing the hard carbon nanosheets with an excellent rate performance, large reversible capacity, and long lifespan. Herein, a dual potential plateau model of hard carbon nanosheets is illustrated in Scheme S1, Supporting Information. Based on the previous analysis, it is observed that the charge process can be divided into three stages. In Stage I, sodium ions first leave the graphitic layers (0.01–0.10 V), and later depart from the micropores (0.10–0.40 V). Stage II corresponds to the surface desodiation process of the CO/OH groups (0.40–0.70 V). Stage III is due to the desodiation process on the surface sites including edges, defects, and some of the CO/OH groups (0.70–1.50 V).</p><figure xml:id="adma202000447-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202000447:adma202000447-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202000447-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>In situ XRD mapping of MPC‐1000 during the first charge−discharge process at 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> at 25 °C.</caption></figure><p>In summary, we have demonstrated that hard carbon nanosheets with uniformly concentrated ultramicropores (≈0.5 nm) and accessible functional CO/OH groups can deliver a superior rate capability, high ICE, and cycle stability for sodium storage. Remarkably, a new charge model with two potential plateaus was established based on the results obtained using CV, ex situ XPS, GITT, and in situ XRD techniques, and this charge model can deliver over 95% realistic capacity below 1.00 V. During the charging process, the sodium storage behaviors corresponding to two potential plateau regions are the Na<sup>+</sup> deinsertion from the graphitic layers in the range of 0.01–0.10 V and the surface‐dominated Na storage from the desorption of OH and the redox reaction of CO groups in the range of 0.40–0.70 V, respectively. Our findings provide an effective strategy for designing material structures and a novel perspective on Na‐ion storage behavior for the engineering of high‐performance carbon anodes.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202000447-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Science Fund for the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Nos.21776041 and 21875028), Cheung Kong Scholars Programme of China (T2015036), and Liao Ning Revitalization Talents Program (XLYC1902045).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202000447-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202000447-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202000447-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202000447-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>D.</givenNames> <familyName>Xu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>C.</givenNames> <familyName>Chen</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Xie</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>B.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>L.</givenNames> <familyName>Miao</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Cai</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Huang</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>L.</givenNames> <familyName>Zhang</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
