The advancement of NIBs depends on the exploration of new electrode materials and fundamental understanding of working mechanisms. Herein, via experimental and simulation methods, we develop a mixed polyanionic compound, Na<sub>2</sub>Fe(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)SO<sub>4</sub>⋅H<sub>2</sub>O, as a cathode for NIBs. Thanks to its rigid three dimensional framework and the combined inductive effects from oxalate and sulfate, it delivered reversible Na insertion/desertion at average discharging voltages of 3.5 and 3.1 V for 500 cycles with Coulombic efficiencies of ca. 99 %. In situ synchrotron X‐ray measurements and DFT calculations demonstrate the Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox reactions contribute to electron compensation during Na<sup>+</sup> desertion/insertion. The study suggests mixed polyanionic frameworks may provide promising materials for Na ion storage with the merits of low cost and environmental friendliness.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Mixed and matched</b>: A combined oxalate and sulfate cathode is prepared. The optimized sodium‐ion battery achieves reversible Na‐ion storage for 500 cycles with high Coulombic efficiencies of approximately 99 %, indicating that mixed polyanionic frameworks may provide promising materials for Na‐ion storage with the merits of low cost and environmental friendly.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912272:anie201912272-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912272-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="anie201912272-ntgp-0002"><note xml:id="anie201912272-note-1001"><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201912272-sec-0001"><p>To tackle climate change and fossil resources extinction, the change of an energy structure from high carbon to low carbon and carbon‐free is an inevitable trend, which calls for low cost and sustainable energy‐storage systems for renewable energies, such as solar and wind. Among rechargeable electrochemical energy‐storage devices, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) are widely used in portable electronics and electric vehicles.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0001"/> However, the uneven distribution and limited resource of lithium, especially the fast and extensive consumption of Li has restricted the further and sustainable applications of LIBs.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0002"/> As an alternative, sodium‐ion batteries (NIBs) have attracted much attention for large‐scale applications, such as smart grid and ancillary services for renewable energy, owing to the natural abundance, low cost, and chemical similarity of the sodium ion with the lithium ion.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0003"/></p><p>As in LIBs, the positive electrode plays a key role in overall performance in NIBs. The conventional oxide cathodes have exhibited high specific capacities, while they are prone to irreversible structural changes in Na deep‐desertion states, showing a low coulombic efficiency and rapid capacity decline.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0004"/> In comparison, the polyanionic cathodes have displayed stable framework structures which ensure the long‐term cycling stability.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0005"/> In particular, mixed polyanionic cathodes combining more than one kind of anion units can be useful in tuning the redox voltage by varied degree of inductive effect and providing more structural diversity.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0006"/> Meanwhile, iron‐based polyanionic compounds have further advantages due to the low cost of Fe and its environmental friendliness.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0007"/> Since the report of Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F, the combination of (PO<sub>4</sub>)<sup>3−</sup> and F<sup>−</sup> in the anion sublattice has led to a series of compounds with enhanced operating voltages.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0008"/> Several strategies, such as nano‐technology, single or multi elements doping, homogeneous mixing with nanostructured carbon, etc., have been utilized to improve the performance of these Fe‐based fluorophosphate cathodes.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0009"/> Afterward, other Fe‐based mixed‐anion cathodes were also intensively investigated, including Na<sub>4</sub>Fe<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>, NaFe<sub>2</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, NaFeSO<sub>4</sub>F, and Na<sub>3</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0010"/></p><p>Herein, we report a known magnetic material, Na<sub>2</sub>Fe(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)SO<sub>4</sub>⋅H<sub>2</sub>O,<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0011"/> as a potential low‐cost and environmentally friendly NIBs cathode for the first time. The choice of oxalate lies in the fact that the oxalic acid is a relatively strong acid, with acidity (pKα=1.25/4.14) between those of H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (pKα=−3/1.99) and H<sub>3</sub>PO<sub>4</sub> (pKα=2.15/7.20/12.32). This suggests that the electronic inductive effect of the oxalate group on the d‐states of the transition metal ions should be similar to (SO<sub>4</sub>)<sup>2−</sup> and (PO<sub>4</sub>)<sup>3−</sup>, and hence may give rise to similar redox potentials to sulfate and phosphate. On the other hand, it is found that the channels in Na<sub>2</sub>Fe(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)SO<sub>4</sub>⋅H<sub>2</sub>O are quite large. As shown in Figure S1 of the Supporting Information, the structure has a three‐dimensional framework and contains two triangular prismatic channels with cross‐section of 5.907 and 4.126 Å. Such large channels would be beneficial for Na‐migration. By in situ synchrotron X‐ray adsorption structure analysis and DFT‐based simulations, its electrochemical reaction mechanism was demonstrated to be reversible Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox reactions. After optimization, the studied cathode exhibited a capacity of its theoretical value (88 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), and can stably cycle over 500 cycles with a capacity retention of 85 %, indicating the feasibility of this mixed polyanionic compound as a low‐cost and environmentally friendly NIBs cathode.</p><p>The structure of Na<sub>2</sub>Fe(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)SO<sub>4</sub>⋅H<sub>2</sub>O (NFOS) is represented in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> a,b. In detail, it composes a three dimensional framework formulated as [FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>SO<sub>4</sub>], where Fe<sup>2+</sup> and (C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)<sup>2−</sup> construct two dimensional [FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>]<sub>∞</sub> layers, and (SO<sub>4</sub>)<sup>2−</sup> bridged adjacent [FeC<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>]<sub>∞</sub> layers together. Na<sup>+</sup> and water molecules (Ow) lie in interstitial sites. Within a unit cell, there are two Na sites, that is, two third Na1 and one third Na2. Viewing from <i>c</i>‐axis, two large channels can be detected, which may serve as Na<sup>+</sup> migration paths (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> b). On this account, single crystals of NFOS were synthesized by an optimized hydrothermal method with a yield of 95 % based on iron source. Pure crystallites were obtained by hydrothermal reaction and subsequently filtrating products with hot glycerin and drying overnight in a 60 °C vacuum oven. Orange crystallites with an average size of 0.60×0.25×0.25 mm<sup>3</sup> were clearly observed under an optical microscope (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> c). The excellent agreement between experimental and calculated X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern confirms that the obtained sample is of high purity (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> d). The state of iron was confirmed to be high spin 2+ through the Mössbauer pattern (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> e and Table S1). As the atomic site for water molecules is partially occupied in the structure, its stability against moist atmosphere was evaluated. Results indicated that the NFOS absorbed trace amount of water and formed Na<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> impurity when exposed in moist. Therefore, pristine pure samples in later tests were kept in the Ar‐filled glove box. Transmission electron microscope (TEM) and energy dispersion spectra (EDS) mappings (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> f) confirmed the uniform distribution of Na, Fe, S, C, and O in the obtained crystallites, while the sample is sensitive to intense electron beams to take high‐resolution TEM or selected area electron diffraction, similar to some fluorosulfates and metal–organic frameworks (MOFs).<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0012"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201912272-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912272:anie201912272-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912272-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Three‐dimensional crystal structure of NFOS.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0011"/> b) Structure of NFOS viewing along the <i>c</i>‐axis. c) The optical micrograph of single crystals of NFOS. d) XRD patterns of the experimental and the standard NFOS. e) Room‐temperature Mössbauer spectrum of NFOS. f) Transmission electron microscope and energy dispersion spectra mappings of NFOS.</p></caption></figure><p>To test the electrochemical properties of NFOS as the cathode material, pristine samples were ball‐milled with conductive carbon to improve the conductivity. The phase and purity of ball‐milled samples were checked under XRD and SEM (Figure S2,S3). This composite material was then applied as the cathode in coin‐type half‐cells (see Supporting Information). To explore the optimal reversible voltage window, cells were first cycled at a current density of 45 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in different voltage ranges, using diverse cut‐off charging voltage of 4.2 V, 4.3 V, 4.5 V, and 4.6 V, respectively, and the same discharged cutoff of 1.7 V. The capacity revolution of the cells is illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> a. It is observed that cells cycled in 1.7–4.5/4.6 V delivered initial specific capacity of approximately 170 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to appropriately total Na desertion/insertion of NFOS. However, these capacities faded by 50 % within the first 60 cycles. In comparison, cells cycled in 1.7–4.2/4.3 V were relatively stable with little capacity fading. After 300 cycles, the 1.7–4.2 V cycled cell delivered a higher specific capacity than that in 1.7–4.3 V. By inspecting voltage–capacity profiles of the second (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> b) and the 60th cycle (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> c), it is observed that the higher the charging cutoff voltage is, the less stable the cell is. Meanwhile, galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) curves of a 4.2 V cell in different cycles remained unchanged, while those of higher up‐limit voltages transformed a lot. This illustrates that the range of 1.7–4.2 V is the optimal voltage window for reversibility. Further tests were therefore performed in this voltage window.</p><figure xml:id="anie201912272-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912272:anie201912272-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912272-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The cyclability performance of different cut‐off voltages of the NFOS/Na half cells at a rate of 0.5C. b) Galvanostatic charge–discharge (GCD) profiles of different cut‐off voltages in the second cycle. c) GCD profiles of different cut‐off voltages in the 60th cycle. d) The corresponding <i>dQ</i>/<i>dV</i> differential curve of the NFOS/Na half cells in the window of 1.7–4.2 V at 0.2C. e) Long‐term cycling performance of the NFOS/Na half cells at 0.5C for 500 cycles. f) GCD profiles of different cycles at a rate of 0.5C. g) Galvanostatic cycling of half cells with NFOS as cathode for 500 cycles, with medium discharge voltage highlighted by red dots. h) Rate capacities and corresponding Coulombic efficiencies of the NFOS/Na half cells under different current rates from 0.2C to 5C.</p></caption></figure><p>A typical <i>dQ</i>/<i>dV</i> curve of a stabilized cell in the range of 1.7–4.2 V is displayed in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> d. It exhibited two distinctive peaks at 3.2 V and 3.8 V upon charging, and two at 3.1 V and 3.5 V upon discharging, for which the lower potential peaks are more intense than that of the higher potential. The hysteresis of 0.1 V and 0.3 V of redox peaks is reasonable compared to other cathode materials in NIBs.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0010a #anie201912272-bib-0013"/> Considering the content of the Na1 and Na2 sites in the structure and the capacity contribution from each redox peak, the 3.2/3.1 V peaks are likely corresponding to full insertion/de‐insertion of Na1, while the 3.8/3.5 V peaks shall be related to insertion/desertion of 25 % Na2.</p><p>The cyclability performance of NFOS electrode was evaluated by GCD method at 0.5C (1C=88 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) in the window of 1.7–4.2 V. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> e, after initial activation during the first few cycles, the cell delivered stable discharging capacities with coulombic efficiencies (CE) as high as around 99 %. A reversible capacity of 75 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> has been retained after 500 cycles, corresponding to 85 % of its theoretical capacity (88 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). The capacity exceeding its theoretical value in the activation period comes possibly from oxidative side reactions of electrolytes and/or carbon additives.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0014"/> GCD profiles for the 20th, 50th, 100th, 200th, 300th, 400th, and 500th cycle resembled very well, as illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> f. Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> g shows long‐term cycling curves with medium discharge voltages (MDV) highlighted in red. It can be seen that the cycles reserved very well with a stable MDV at about 3.0 V. This suggests a highly reversible Na<sup>+</sup> storage process in NFOS.</p><p>The kinetics features of NFOS were further characterized by rate capability test and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> h presents the charge and discharge capacities and corresponding CE under different rates. At the rate of 0.2C, the obtained capacity approached almost its theoretical capacity, and decreased gradually when the rate increased to 0.4C, 1C, 2C, 3C, 4C, and 5C. At 5C, 62 % of the theoretical capacity was retained (54 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). In addition, the capacity recovered when the rate returned from 5C to 0.2C. CEs for each rate were constantly above 97 %. The Nyquist plots in different cycles from EIS tests are shown in Figure S4. Each plot consists of a semicircle in the high‐medium frequency region and a sloping line in low frequency region, attributed to the charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) and Warburg impedance, respectively. It is seen that the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> of the fresh cell is the highest and descends greatly after 50 cycles. Afterwards, the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> kept almost constant. This reveals good kinetic stability of the cell.</p><p>To illustrate the working mechanism of NFOS as cathode material in NIBs, we carried out in situ synchrotron X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) characterization to track the average iron valence and its local structure evolution. The 2D contour plots of normalized XAS spectra are presented in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0003"/> b along with the corresponding voltage profile of the in situ cell (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0003"/> a). It can be seen that the evolution of the XAS data correlates well with the electrochemical process, which is reversible as the charge and discharge are alternated. More specifically, Fe <i>K</i>‐edge X‐ray absorption near edge spectra (XANES) during the charge and discharge process are shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0003"/> c. During the charging process, the Fe <i>K</i>‐edge continuously shifted to higher energy, which indicates an increase of the average valence state of iron.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0015"/> During the following discharge process, the <i>K</i>‐edge of the iron shifted back to its original higher energy. To further understand the redox mechanism of the NFOS electrode, we obtained the precise change of valences during the charge–discharge process by linear combination fitting,<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0016"/> and the results are shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0003"/> d (Table S2). In the charging process, the valence state of iron changes from 2+ to 3+, and returned to 2+ during the following discharge process. The increase and decrease in the valence state of iron correspond to the desertion and insertion of Na<sup>+</sup>, respectively, to balance the overall charge in NFOS cathode. Obviously, the variation in valence state of iron indicates the high reversibility of Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox couple. Further, the corresponding <i>k</i><sup><i>2</i></sup>‐weighted Fourier transform (FT) spectra of extended synchrotron X‐ray absorption fine‐structure (EXAFS) as plotted in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0003"/> e,f. The first peak centered around 1.5–1.7 Å corresponds to Fe–O octahedral coordination. It is seen that Fe–O distance shortened gradually along charging process, and elongated along discharging process. The well overlapped EXAFS curves for two terminal states demonstrate the good reversibility of iron environment (Figure S5). Combining XANES and EXAFS profiles, it is proved a Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> reaction happened and is quite reversible during the sodium desertion/insertion process in NFOS cathode in the window of 1.7–4.2 V.</p><figure xml:id="anie201912272-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912272:anie201912272-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912272-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The voltage profile of the NFOS/Na half cells throughout the in situ charge–discharge process. b) The 2D contour plots of normalized XAFS spectra. c) The 3D surface with a projection of iron <i>K</i>‐edge XANES spectra during the charging and discharging process. d) The valence change of iron and the voltage profiles of NFOS/Na half cells as a function of time. Fourier transform of <i>k</i><sup>2</sup>‐weighted EXAFS patterns for the Fe <i>K</i>‐edge of NFOS in e) charging and f) discharging process. g) The in situ XRD patterns of NFOS during charge and discharge process in the voltage range of 1.7–4.2 V. The enlarged patterns between h) 16.4–20.6°, and i) 25.8–36.4°. The black star mark in (g) represents the peak of the beryllium window. The bottom curve of (g)–(i) is collected on a bare electrode.</p></caption></figure><p>Further, the structural evolution is evaluated by in situ XRD (Figure S6). The similarity of the overall XRD patterns indicates the framework of the NFOS cathode is well preserved during charging and discharging process. Meanwhile, it is clear that the peaks at around 17.0, 28.0, 30.0, and 32.5°, corresponding to (−120), (002), (030), and (−122) plane, respectively, shift to higher angles during the charging process, indicating that the d‐spacings decrease during the Na ions deinsertion. After discharging to 1.7 V, the peaks return to the original positions, indicating the good reversibility. Similar phenomena have also been reported in other cathode materials.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0017"/></p><p>In searching for the microscopic origin of the stable and reversible sodium storage chemistry of the NFOS framework, we have performed density functional theory (DFT) calculations based on the DFT + U method, where the Hubbard U term is used to describe the strong onsite Coulomb repulsion of localized Fe 3d electrons.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0018"/> Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0004"/> a shows a primitive cell of NFOS after DFT structural optimization. Two different Na sites, namely Na<sup>α</sup> and Na<sup>β</sup>, were identified, along with two channels along the <i>c</i> axis for possible sodium atoms insertion/desertion. The Na<sup>α</sup> is found to be more active than the Na<sup>β</sup>. When all sodium ions are extracted, the framework would collapse and the oxalate decomposes, which is in agreement with the experiments described above. The Na<sup>α</sup> in crystallographic Na1 site of Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0001"/> b (occupancy 2/3, three‐fold symmetry) is related to the 3.2/3.1 V peaks in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0002"/> d, while the Na<sup>α</sup> in crystallographic Na2 site (occupancy 1/3, four‐fold symmetry) corresponds to the 3.8/3.5 V peaks. Further, the migration path related to active Na ions (point <i>a</i> to point <i>e</i> in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0004"/> b), and the corresponding energies were simulated (Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0004"/> c). From the results, we estimate that the sodium diffusion barriers of NFOS are about 0.5 eV, at the same level as the previously reported cathodes.<link href="#anie201912272-bib-0013 #anie201912272-bib-0019"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201912272-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201912272:anie201912272-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201912272-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Density functional theory (DFT) calculated atomic structures of pristine NFOS, Na<sup>α</sup> and Na<sup>β</sup> stands for active and non‐active sites. b) The diffusion path (points <i>a</i>–<i>e</i>) of Na<sup>α</sup> ion in NFOS during charge and discharge process and c) corresponding energies. d) The partial density of states (PDOS) of NFOS projected onto the Fe 3d orbitals. The dotted line stands for Fermi level.</p></caption></figure><p>To elucidate the electronic structure of the system, the partial density of states (PDOS) projected onto the Fe 3d states of the pristine and the charged sample were calculated, shown in Figure <link href="#anie201912272-fig-0004"/> d. Fe<sup>2+</sup> exhibits a high‐spin state with the electron configuration <i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>4</sup><i>e</i><sub>g</sub><sup>2</sup> (<i>S</i>=2), where the minority spin‐down states (<i>t</i><sub>2g</sub>) locate right below the Fermi level (EF) and therefore being redox active. As sodium ions were extracted, these spin‐down states of Fe<sup>2+</sup> gradually shift above the EF with electrons transfer to surrounding anions, and the majority spin‐up states move deeper below the EF. In the charged state, Fe<sup>3+</sup> is stabilized in the highest spin state (<i>S</i>=5/2) with half‐filled electron configuration <i>t</i><sub>2g</sub><sup>3</sup><i>e</i><sub>g</sub><sup>2</sup>, obeying the Hund's rule. This calculated Fe<sup>2+</sup> to Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox chemistry during the charging process agrees well with the in situ XANES measurements.</p><p>In summary, a Fe‐based mixed polyanionic material, Na<sub>2</sub>Fe(C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>)SO<sub>4</sub>⋅H<sub>2</sub>O, has been identified as a cathode material for NIBs. The combined inductive effects from oxalate and sulfate, and the Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox reactions contribute to reversible Na‐ion insertion/desertion with average discharging voltages of 3.5 V and 3.1 V. The reaction mechanism was detected by in situ synchrotron X‐ray measurements and DFT simulations. After optimization, it was stably cycled for 500 cycles with Coulombic efficiencies as high as around 99 %. Although the performance is not astonishing at the current stage, it may be improved by several strategies, such as replacing iron with multivalent ions, such as Mn or V, to allow multi‐electron migration, or doping with Ti, Al, or other elements to improve the structural stability. The study provides a promising NIB cathode family with the merits of low cost and environmental friendliness for applications of renewable energies.</p><section xml:id="anie201912272-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51822210, 51972329), Shenzhen Science and Technology Planning Project (No. JCYJ20180507182512042), SIAT Innovation Program for Excellent Young Researchers (201811), and Science and Technology Planning Project of Guangdong Province (2018A050506066, 2019B090914003).</p></section><section xml:id="anie201912272-sec-0003" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie201912272-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="anie201912272-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="anie201912272-bib-0001a"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie201912272-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>J.
