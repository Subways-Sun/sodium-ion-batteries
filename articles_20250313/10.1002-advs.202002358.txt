Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Germanium (Ge)‐based materials have been considered as potential anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries owing to their high theoretical specific capacity. However, the poor conductivity and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusivity of Ge‐based materials result in retardant ion/electron transportation and insufficient sodium storage efficiency, leading to sluggish reaction kinetics. To intrinsically maximize the sodium storage capability of Ge, the nitrogen doped carbon‐coated Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure material (Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C) is developed for enhanced sodium storage. The pod‐like structure of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C exposes numerous active surface to shorten ion transportation pathway while the uniform encapsulation of carbon shell improves the electron transportation, leading to enhanced reaction kinetics. Theoretical calculation reveals that Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure can offer decent electron conduction and lower the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier, which further promotes Ge alloying reaction and improves its sodium storage capability close to its theoretical value. In addition, the uniform encapsulation of nitrogen‐doped carbon on Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure material efficiently alleviates its volume expansion and prevents its decomposition, further ensuring its structural integrity upon cycling. Attributed to these unique superiorities, the as‐prepared Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C electrode demonstrates admirable discharge capacity, outstanding rate capability and prolonged cycle lifespan (178 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 4.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 4000 cycles).</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs2051-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Nitrogen doped carbon‐coated Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure is developed as anode material for fast and longevous sodium storage. Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure efficiently promotes the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and electron conduction in the composite, which enhances the sodium storage capability of Ge and accelerates its redox reaction kinetics. Attributed to the structural superiorities, a remarkable discharge capacity, superb rate capability and enhanced cyclic stability can be realized.<blockFixed rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="advs2051-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted great attention as one of the most promising energy storage devices.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0001 #advs2051-bib-0002 #advs2051-bib-0003 #advs2051-bib-0004"><sup>1–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the sluggish kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion results in undesirable electrochemical performance because of the large Na<sup>+</sup> radius.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0005 #advs2051-bib-0006 #advs2051-bib-0007"><sup>5–7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Various anode materials have been investigated to achieve the reversible Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction, such as carbonaceous materials (hard carbons, expanded graphite, and graphene), alloy‐based materials, and metal oxides/sulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0008 #advs2051-bib-0009 #advs2051-bib-0010"><sup>8–10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, graphite, the widely used anode material in commercial lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), only delivers a low specific capacity of 50 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in traditional carbonate‐based electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0011 #advs2051-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The exploration of appropriate anode materials for SIBs with high performance is challenging.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0013 #advs2051-bib-0014 #advs2051-bib-0015"><sup>13–15</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Alloy‐type materials are promising candidates due to their large sodium storage capacity based on alloy/de‐alloy storage mechanism.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0016 #advs2051-bib-0017 #advs2051-bib-0018 #advs2051-bib-0019"><sup>16–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among various alloy‐type materials, Ge has been found to electrochemically react with Na to form Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Ge (369 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) with an atomic ratio of about 1:1.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0020 #advs2051-bib-0021"><sup>20,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, bulk Ge is undesirable for sodium storage because of the sluggish kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, the alloying reactions are usually accompanied by huge volume expansion of the host materials, which leads to anode degradation and poor capacity retention.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0023 #advs2051-bib-0024"><sup>23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> An effective strategy to enhance the reaction kinetics is to design nanostructured heterojunction, which cannot only short the sodium ion diffusion path, but also generate a built‐in electric field at the heterointerfaces, further improving the electrochemical reaction kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0025 #advs2051-bib-0026 #advs2051-bib-0027"><sup>25–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Accompanied surface engineering such as carbon coating of nanosized active materials is commonly employed to prevent their pulverization and aggregation during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0028 #advs2051-bib-0029 #advs2051-bib-0030 #advs2051-bib-0031"><sup>28–31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, the combination of the two above‐mentioned tactics might realize high sodium storage performance of Ge with high rate capability and long‐term cycling stability.</p><p>In this work, a pod‐like Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C is synthesized as anode material to realize the sodium storage of Ge. The design of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure can lower the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier and facilitates electron transportation, rendering enhanced reaction kinetics and improved sodium storage capability of Ge. Meanwhile, the N‐C shell accommodates the volume expansion of Ge during alloying/dealloying process, which endows enhanced architecture integrity. Owing to these structural advantages, the optimal Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C displays outstanding rate capability and long cycling stability with enhanced sodium storage property.</p><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C was prepared by the in situ reduction of CuGeO<sub>3</sub> nanowires with the aid of polydopamine (PDA). The CuGeO<sub>3</sub> nanowire was initially coated by a uniform layer of PDA (CuGeO<sub>3</sub>@PDA). Subsequently, the CuGeO<sub>3</sub>@PDA nanorods undergone carbonization and in situ reduction leads to the formation of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C nanorods with pod‐like nanostructure. The presence of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure can intrinsically enhance the sodium storage efficiency of Ge. As displayed in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1b</link>, the as‐prepared CuGeO<sub>3</sub> nanowires are dispersed regularly and exhibit well‐defined homogeneity. With coating of the PDA layer, the CuGeO<sub>3</sub>@PDA composite inherits the nanorod morphology without particle aggregation (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1c</link>). The TEM results in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1e</link> further demonstrate the nanowire structure of CuGeO<sub>3</sub>. The thickness of PDA coating is ≈9 nm, while the thickness of the inner CuGeO<sub>3</sub> is ≈22 nm (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1f</link>), indicating the homogeneous coating. After reduction via pyrolysis, the CuGeO<sub>3</sub> nanorods transformed into heterostructured Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge, which is wrapped in PDA‐derived nitrogen doped carbon layer, affording the formation of pod‐like Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C composites (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1d</link>). As shown in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1g</link>, the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C exhibits a pod‐like nanostructure with admirable structure integrity, which is able to alleviate the volume variation during cycling. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1h</link>) indicates its good crystallinity with lattice fringes of 0.20, 0.21, and 0.326 nm, which can be ascribed to (020), (<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2051:advs2051-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/advs2051-math-0001.png"><mover accent="true"><mn>1</mn><mo>¯</mo></mover></math>11) plane of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge and (111) plane of Ge, respectively. The TEM image (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1i</link>) and corresponding EDS elemental mapping show homogeneous element distribution of Ge, N, and C. For comparison, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge was prepared by direct annealing of CuGeO<sub>3</sub> nanowires. Without carbon encapsulation, severe aggregation can be observed from the SEM and TEM images of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Furthermore, the Ge@N‐C also displayed irregular bulk particles (Figure S2, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="advs2051-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2051:advs2051-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs2051-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) The schematic synthesis process of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C. b,e) SEM and TEM images of CuGeO<sub>3</sub>; c,f) CuGeO<sub>3</sub>@PDA; d,g) Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C. h) HRTEM image of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C. i) The TEM and the corresponding EDS mapping of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C; j) XRD pattern of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C; k) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption isotherm Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C and Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge; l) The high‐resolution XPS spectrum of Ge 3d of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C.</caption></figure><p>The XRD pattern of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C is shown in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1j</link>, where the diffraction peaks can be assigned to Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge (JCPDS#06‐0693) and Ge (JCPDS#04‐0545). The XRD patterns of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge and Ge@N‐C are shown in Figure S4, Supporting Information, for comparison. The diffraction peaks are strong and sharp, indicating high crystallization.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In contrast, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C shows broad diffraction peaks, confirming that the in situ formed Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge and nitrogen‐doped carbon prevent the aggregation of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge particles. Moreover, the weak peak at ≈26 ° in the XRD pattern of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C carbon is attributed to the carbon coating. As clarified by the TGA, the carbon content in Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C is ≈25.7 wt%, and the ratio of Ge in Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C is ≈26.2% (Figure S4, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1k</link> shows the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption isotherms of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C and Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge, respectively. The Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C delivered a BET specific surface area of 97.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, which is much higher than that of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge (4.5 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>). Simultaneously, the mesoporous structure of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C shows a significantly higher pore volume of 0.18 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S5a, Supporting Information). As shown in Figure S5b, Supporting Information, the Raman spectra of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C exhibits three peaks at 290 cm<sup>−1</sup> (Ge), 1341 cm<sup>−1</sup> (D band, disordered carbon) and at 1580 cm<sup>−1</sup> (G band, graphene) with <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> value of 0.85, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The disordered carbon derived from N‐doping could enhance the electrochemical conductivity of carbon and further improve the sodium storage of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C. As clarified by XPS (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0001">1l</link>), there are two peaks centered at 32.6 and 29.6 eV in Ge 3d spectrum, which are attributed to Cu‐Ge and Ge‐Ge, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> More XPS analysis details are shown in Figure S6, Supporting Information.</p><p>The electrochemical performance of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C is further evaluated. The initial three charge‐discharge cycles of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2051-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>. In the first cycle, the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C shows high discharge capacity of 1170 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a reversible charge capacity of 376 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which is mainly caused by solid electrolyte interface (SEI) formation and some irreversible side reactions, further confirmed by the CV results (Figure S7, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0035 #advs2051-bib-0036"><sup>35,36</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The initial Coulombic efficiency of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C may be enhanced by decreasing the specific surface area to mitigate the side reactions or pre‐sodiation to offer extra sodium resource.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0037 #advs2051-bib-0038 #advs2051-bib-0039"><sup>37–39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the subsequent cycles, the charge‐discharge curves are almost overlapped, indicating the high reaction reversibility. Furthermore, the ex situ (Figure S8, Supporting Information) XRD results reveal the sodium storage mechanism of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C: (i) the crystalline Ge converts into Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Ge with low crystallinity after the initial discharge; (ii) after charge to 3.0 V, the amorphous Na<sub>x</sub>Ge transform to poor crystalline Ge with more active sites, thus ensuring favorable electrochemical performance for subsequent cycles. As depicted in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0002">2b</link> and Figure S9, Supporting Information, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C delivers a high reversible capacity of 347 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles with high capacity retention of 99.3% and high Coulombic efficiency close to 100%, demonstrating high cycling stability. While the capacity of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge electrode is only 65 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles, further revealing the important role of carbon coating to restrain structure degradation. The in situ formed Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure is also the key factor to maximize the sodium storage efficiency of Ge. Without Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge, the Ge@N‐C displayed a specific capacity of only 40 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, due to the sluggish reaction kinetics. The favorable electrochemical performance of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C could be derived from its well‐designed structure, which accommodates volumetric change by carbon coating during cycling, and achieves high Ge utilization efficiency via an in situ formed Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterostructure.</p><figure xml:id="advs2051-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2051:advs2051-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs2051-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Charge‐discharge curves of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C; b) Cycling, c) rate, and e) long‐term cycling performance of the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge, and Ge@N‐C; d) charge‐discharge curves of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C at different current densities; f) long‐term cycling performance of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C at 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>The rate performances are shown in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0002">2c</link>. As the current density increases, the corresponding reversible capacities of the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C electrode decrease. A highly reversible specific capacity of 394 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is obtained when the current density gradually recovers to 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which is close to the initial value. However, without N‐C or Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge and Ge@N‐C show inferior specific capacity. Furthermore, the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C electrode in sodium storage exhibits promising electrochemical performance (Table S1, Supporting Information). The representative charge‐discharge curves of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C at different current densities are presented in Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0002">2d</link>, which exhibits a higher discharge capacity than that of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge (Figure S10a, Supporting Information) and Ge@N‐C (Figure S10b, Supporting Information). Also, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C possessed considerable cycling stability at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> (see Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0002">2e</link>). For the initial 10 cycles, the specific capacity increases from 150 to 205 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating an electrochemical activation process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2051-bib-0040"><sup>40</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After 2000 cycles, the specific capacity of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C remains stable at 195 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Even at high current density of 4 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C keeps a reversible capacity of 178 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 4000 cycles, (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0002">2f</link>). It is undoubtedly that the exterior N‐C shell accommodates volume expansion and alleviates the stress of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge, which prevents particle aggregation and collapse during Na<sup>+</sup> alloying/dealloying, ensuring high cycling stability of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C.</p><p>Hence, to reveal the structural stability, the morphologies of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C before and after 100 GCD cycles are presented. The as‐prepared Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C electrode is flat and smooth with regular pod‐like nanostructure (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2051-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>,<link href="#advs2051-fig-0003">b</link>). After 100 cycles, the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C still keeps the structural integrity, where the active materials tightly contact with the current collector (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0003">3c</link>). Besides, the existence of SEI covers the surface of pod‐like active materials (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0003">3d</link>). As shown in Figure S11, Supporting Information, without the encapsulation of N‐C, severe electrode pulverization and agglomeration of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge can be observed, which induces material pilling off from the current collector during cycling and results in poor cycling stability. Furthermore, the charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C is slightly lower than that of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge (169 versus 223 Ω), indicating that the N‐C layer is beneficial to the charge transportation kinetics initially (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0003">3e</link>). After 100 GCD cycles, the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C increases to ≈355 Ω, which is smaller than that of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge (519 Ω), further demonstrating that the N‐C layer could also contribute to the stable SEI formation and structural integrity (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0003">3f</link>).</p><figure xml:id="advs2051-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2051:advs2051-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs2051-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) SEM images of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C electrode before and c,d) after 100 cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. e) The EIS spectra and corresponding simulation of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C and Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge before and f) after cycling, respectively.</caption></figure><p>The Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C displays remarkably enhanced rate capability and cycling performance, compared to those of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge and Ge@N‐C, which could be attributed to the synergistic effect of the exterior N‐C coating layer, and the intrinsic Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge heterojunction. Most importantly, the intrinsic heterostructure of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge improves fundamentally the sodium storage property of Ge. To clarify this, we have performed the DFT calculations. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2051-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link> shows the optimized state of Na atom on the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge facet. The adsorption energy of Na atom on Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge is −2.53 eV, which is much lower than −0.58 eV of Ge (Figure S12a, Supporting Information), indicating that the Na atom is more easily absorbed on the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge. And the diffusion barrier of Na atom on Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge (0.24 eV, Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0004">4b</link>) is lower than that on Ge (0.35 eV, Figure S12b, Supporting Information), demonstrating that Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion is energetically favorable on the Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge. In the case of heterojunction structure derived from Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge, no bandgap could be found at the Fermi level (Figure <link href="#advs2051-fig-0004">4c</link>,<link href="#advs2051-fig-0004">d</link>), while there is an obvious band gap existing in the bulk Ge (Figure S12c and d, Supporting Information). Next, 5 m<sc>m</sc> Cu(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O and 5 m<sc>m</sc> GeO<sub>2</sub> were added to the above solution, respectively, and the mixed solution was stirred continuously for 1 h. Afterward, the above solution was transferred into a 20 mL Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave and then kept at 180 °C for 24 h. Finally, the blue products were collected by centrifugation and washed with deionized ethanol and water several times and dried via freeze‐drying at −40 °C for 24 h.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2051-sec-0060"><title type="main">Synthesis of Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge, and Ge@N‐C</title><p>125 mg of CuGeO<sub>3</sub> nanowires were dispersed in a 50 mL of Trisbuffer (10 × 10<sup>−3</sup> <sc>m</sc>). Then, 50 mg of dopamine hydrochloride was added to the mixture. After stirring for 10 h, the CuGeO<sub>3</sub>@PDA was collected by centrifugal separation, washed with deionized water, and dried via freeze‐drying at −40 °C for 24 h. Finally, Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge@N‐C was prepared through calcination treatment in 10% Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere at 650 °C for 2 h with a heating rate of 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. Cu<sub>3</sub>Ge/Ge was prepared via a direct annealing CuGeO<sub>3</sub> without PDA coating in 10% Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere at 650 °C for 4 h with a heating rate of 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>. Ge@N‐C was prepared through two main processes. First, Ge nanoparticle was obtained by reducing carboxyethylgermanium sesquioxide (Ge132) in 10% Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere at 700 °C for 2 h. Then, Ge@N‐C was collected by stirring Ge and PDA with a molar 2.5:1 ratio and annealing in 10% Ar/H<sub>2</sub> atmosphere at 650 °C for 2 h.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="advs2051-sec-0070" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>C.S., L.H., and D.L. contributed equally to this work.
