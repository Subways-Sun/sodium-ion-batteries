type="main">Abstract</title><p>Numerous studies have reported that the enhancement of rate capability of carbonaceous anode by heteroatom doping is due to the increased diffusion‐controlled capacity induced by expanding interlayer spacing. However, percentage of diffusion‐controlled capacity is less than 30% as scan rate is larger than 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, suggesting there is inaccuracy in recognizing principle of improving rate capability of carbonaceous anode. In this paper, it is found that the heteroatom doping has little impact on interlayer spacing of carbon in bulk phase, meaning that diffusion‐controlled capacity is hard to be enhanced by doping. After synergizing with tensile stress, however, the interlayer spacing in subsurface region is obviously expanded to 0.40 nm, which will increase the thickness of accessible subsurface region at high current density. So SRNDC‐700 electrodes display a high specific capacity of 160.6 and 69.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 and 50 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Additionally, the high reversibility of carbon structure insures ultralong cycling stability and hence attenuation of SRNDC‐700 is only 0.0025% per cycle even at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 6000 cycles. This report sheds new insight into mechanism of improving electrochemical performance of carbonaceous anode by doping and provides a novel design concept for doping carbon.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202100808-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Heteroatom doping affects the interlayer spacing of the subsurface region much more than the bulk for carbonaceous nanoparticles. The increased accessible subsurface boosts the pseudocapacitive capacity and endows SRNDC‐700 with excellent rate performance up to 50 A g<sup>−1</sup>. This varies from diffusion‐controlled capacity determinism and provides a novel design concept for doping carbonaceous material.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have focused considerable attracts in the last 20 years for the providing of the alternative choice of lithium‐ion batteries in large‐scale energy storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0001 #adma202100808-bib-0002"><sup>1,2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Due to insufficient interlayer space to host larger Na<sup>+</sup> (<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202100808:adma202100808-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma202100808-math-0001.png"><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><msup><mtext>Na</mtext><mo>+</mo></msup></msub><mo>≈</mo><mn>0.102</mn><mtext> </mtext><mtext>nm</mtext></mrow></math> vs <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202100808:adma202100808-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/adma202100808-math-0002.png"><mrow><msub><mi>R</mi><msup><mtext>Li</mtext><mo>+</mo></msup></msub><mo>≈</mo><mn>0.076</mn><mtext> </mtext><mtext>nm</mtext></mrow></math>), graphite has been proven to be unsuitable as anode for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, diversity of porous and defect‐rich carbons, such as hard carbon,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0002 #adma202100808-bib-0004 #adma202100808-bib-0005"><sup>2,4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> graphene,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0006 #adma202100808-bib-0007 #adma202100808-bib-0008"><sup>6–8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0006 #adma202100808-bib-0009"><sup>6,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon quantum dots,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and some biomass derived carbon,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0011 #adma202100808-bib-0012"><sup>11,12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have made great progress in Na<sup>+</sup> storage. For an anode material of SIBs, the specific capacity comes from pseudocapacitive capacity and diffusion‐controlled capacity; to increase the former, more active sites near surface are preferred; to increase the latter, interlayer spacing is expected to be expanded to facilitate the insertion of Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0013 #adma202100808-bib-0014"><sup>13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Plenteous literatures claimed that expanded interlayer spacing can improve the rate capability of anode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0015 #adma202100808-bib-0016"><sup>15,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Actually, the interior of material is seldom utilized at high current densities, which is proven by the fact that percentage of diffusion‐controlled capacity is ≈10% as scan rate is larger than 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Additionally, repeating insertion/deinsertion of Na<sup>+</sup> is a stress‐inducing process and will cause fraction of material, being harmful to stability. Therefore, it is more effective to enhance rate capability and cycling stability by increasing pseudocapacitive capacity.</p><p>Various researches demonstrate that pseudocapacitive capacity depends on surface area and active sites on surface.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0014 #adma202100808-bib-0017 #adma202100808-bib-0018"><sup>14,17,18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As we all know, anode material with high surface area could form much more solid‐electrolyte interphase film in first cycle, resulting in a lower initial columbic efficiency (ICE).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, to procure larger pseudocapacitive contribution, one should create as many active sites as possible without dramatically increasing surface area. Introducing heteroatoms into the skeleton of porous carbon is an effective strategy to provide more active sites.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0019 #adma202100808-bib-0020"><sup>19,20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, a number of methods have been reported for enhancing the doping degree. While high doping degree is bound to destroy the conjugated π bond of carbon and therefore compromises the conductivity of electrode.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Based on above analysis, extending accessible subsurface region is meaningful for boosting pseudocapacitive capacity without trading off ICE and conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0022 #adma202100808-bib-0023"><sup>22,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> At high current density, the relationship of discharge time (<i>t</i>) to thickness of accessible subsurface region (<i>L</i>) can be expressed as <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202100808:adma202100808-math-0003" wiley:location="graphic/adma202100808-math-0003.png"><mrow><mi>L</mi><mtext> </mtext><mo>=</mo><msqrt><mrow><mi>α</mi><mi>D</mi><mi>t</mi></mrow></msqrt></mrow></math>. The constant α depends on geometry and charging model and <i>D</i> represents for the diffusion coefficient of subsurface region.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thereupon, selectively expanding interlayer spacing of subsurface region to obtain larger <i>D</i> is of importance to amplify the pseudocapacitive capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>It has been reported that both large curvature and doping can expand interlayer spacing of subsurface region.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0020 #adma202100808-bib-0025"><sup>20,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, an ideal carbon based anode for SIBs should be composed by interconnected doped carbon nanoparticles, where nanometerization and sharp corners will increase curvature. By examining the properties of various doping atoms, it was found that sulfur is a special one which can form various CSSC (CSSC) bonds with close to 90° bond angle.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Density functional calculation verifies that the bonding between sulfur atoms in carbon framework can extend interlayer spacing of carbonaceous material (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). If such bonds are introduced to carbon skeleton edge, structural distortion will be amplified without increasing doping degree. Thus, the conjugated π bond will be less damaged to ensure higher conductivity. However, it is hard to directly connect CSSC bond to the carbon edge by sulfurizing carbon at high temperature because more energy is needed to adjust 2D structure to match bond angle. Yang et al. sulfurized N‐doped carbon by H<sub>2</sub>S to successfully increase doping degree, but all the doping atoms are isolated with each other.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Carbonization of sulfur‐containing molecules is another method for obtaining S‐doped carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0027 #adma202100808-bib-0028"><sup>27,28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, it is hard to form CSSC bonds by carbonizing precursor with isolated sulfur at high temperature. Therefore, finding a special precursor containing abundant CSSC bonds, part of which are supposed to be kept during carbonization, is conductive to fulfill expectations. By combing the literatures, we found that CSSC bonds have been exploited to stabilize sulfur in metal‐sulfur batteries,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which inspired us to adopt a proper precursor to modify carbon edge with CSSC bonds.</p><p>Herein, sulfur‐rich N‐doped 3D porous carbon framework (SRNDC) is prepared by adding sulfur during carbonization of polyacrylonitrile (PAN) to in situ form CSSC bonds on edge of carbon skeleton (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). By optimizing the synergistic effects of tensile stress and CSSC bonds, the interlayer spacing was extended from 0.34 to 0.40 nm in subsurface region, which dramatically increases the accessible subsurface area and active sites at high current densities (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>). Analysis of electrochemical data suggests that the extended accessible subsurface region significantly enhanced electrochemical performance via expanding pseudocapacitive contribution, outdistancing diffusion‐controlled counterpart. Resultantly, SRNDC‐700 displays high low voltage discharge capacity (295 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> between 0.01 and 1.0 V at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) and outstanding rate performance (205.6 and 88.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 and 40 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively).</p><figure xml:id="adma202100808-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100808:adma202100808-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100808-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the expanded subsurface region and reversible sodiation/desodiation of CSSC bonds of S‐rich N‐doped 3D porous carbon framework (SRNDC).</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0030"><title type="main">Materials Synthesis and Characterization</title><p>To suppress fast evaporation of sulfur at high temperature, porous precursor obtained by solvothermal method is used to prolong reaction time to form CSSC bonds, and meanwhile the 3D multilevel porous carbon can be obtained (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information), which is expected to provide high‐speed pathway for electron/ion transport.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S4</link> in the Supporting Information show that SRNDCs have a honeycomb‐like porous structure composed of crosslinked micro/nanosheets in 3D. Further amplified scanning electron microscope (SEM) images display that the micro/nanosheets are composed of secondary nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002">2b</link>,<link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002">c</link>). By comparing the morphologies before and after carbonization, it is obvious that this hierarchical porous structure of SRNDCs is inherited from precursor (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information), indicating that the modification of carbonization process has little influence on morphology. Unlike SRNDCs, although NDC maintains the similar structure (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S4c1,c2</link>, Supporting Information), the average size of its secondary particles is larger than 200 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S4c3,c4</link>, Supporting Information). The elemental composition of SRNDC‐700 was analyzed by energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) analysis as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002">2d</link>, which gives the evidence of the coexistence and uniform distribution of C, N, and S. Transmission electron microscope (TEM) images of Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002">2e</link>,<link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002">f</link> exhibit that the micro/nanosheets are constructed by particles around 90 nm. Among those particles, plenty of pores are formed, which are expected to facilitate the penetration of electrolyte. According to the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images in Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0002">2g</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S4</link> in the Supporting Information, the interlayer spacing of all samples in bulk phase is ≈0.348–0.350 nm, indicating that doping has little impact on lattice expansion in bulk phase. However, 0.409 nm interlayer spacing of SRNDC‐700 near surface is larger than that in bulk phase and similar phenomena is also observed in other samples but with different expansion degree.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100808-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100808:adma202100808-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100808-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) SEM images and d) element mapping images (C, N, and S) of SRNDC‐700. e,f) TEM images and g) the high‐magnification TEM of SRNDC‐700.</caption></figure><p>The difference of interlayer spacing is attributed to the relaxation of tensile stress on surface <sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and the doping degree, which can be tuned by changing carbonization temperature. This has little effect on interlayer spacing in bulk phase, however, the situation in subsurface region is very different, where heteroatom doping synergize with tensile strain to enlarge interlayer spacing. By investigating the HRTEM images in detail, it is evident that interlayer spacing of SRNDC‐700 in subsurface region is largest, though the tensile strain is smaller than that of SRNDC‐800. It is assumed that the sulfur doping weaken van der Waals force between carbon layers, due to the destroyed conjugated π bonds and distorted 2D layers.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Resultantly, the subsurface region with weakened van der Waals force is vulnerable to tensile strain, large interlayer spacing will be generated. It is worthy of noting that synergic effect between tensile strain and doping has decisive influence on interlayer spacing, although either of the two can expand interlayer spacing alone. That is why SRNDC‐600 has highest content of sulfur, but owns smaller interlayer spacing than SRNDC‐700, because of weaker tensile strain near surface. Besides of expanding interlayer spacing, the sulfur doping can also improve the wettability between electrode and electrolyte, which is beneficial for the high rate and long stable storage of Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>The variation of interlayer spacing is confirmed by X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>. All curves show two broad peaks corresponding to the diffraction of (002) and (100) of amorphous carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> And the 2θ degree of (002) diffractions of NDC and SRNDC‐600, SRNDC‐700, SRNDC‐800 are located at 25.7°, 25.0°, 24.8°, and 25.3°, respectively. According to Bragg diffraction equation, the calculated (002) interlayer spacing is 0.346, 0.356, 0.358, and 0.352 nm, respectively. In spite of the highest content of sulfur, the interlayer spacing of SRNDC‐600 is smaller than that of SRNDC‐700. That is because the evaporation of sulfur atom incurs the tensile stress on surface/subsurface region, accompanied by the shrinkage of secondary nanoparticles. As discussed above, if temperature is increased further, the doping degree will be lowered, which weakens the synergic effect between surface tensile stress and doping. Therefore, the interlayer spacing is narrowed as temperature arrives at 800 °C. The high tensile stress on the surface/subsurface expands interlayer spacing of exterior, but there is little expansion in interior. Thus the interlayer spacing calculated from TEM image is over a range of 0.349–0.409 nm for SRNDC‐700. The interlayer spacing measured by XRD is within the range of values obtained by TEM image because XRD represents the average value of interlayer spacing.</p><figure xml:id="adma202100808-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202100808:adma202100808-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202100808-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) X‐ray diffraction patterns, and b) S 2p spectra and c) Raman scattering spectra of SRNDCs and NDC. d) Schematic diagram of electrons and sodium ions transmission in SRNDC‐700. e) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms of SRNDCs.</caption></figure><p>As mentioned earlier, doping degree has an obvious effect on interlayer expansion in subsurface region. Thus, the presence, valence and bonding of S, C, N, and O were confirmed by XPS (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0003">3b</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). The high resolution‐spectra of S 2p in Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0003">3b</link> are fitted into four distinct peaks. The increased and expanded accessible subsurface facilitate the transport of Na<sup>+</sup> and introduce more reactive sites, ensuring excellent rate performance of SRNDCs and providing additional capacity (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0003">3d</link>).</p><p>The nitrogen adsorption/desorption isotherms were used to analysis the surface area and pore‐size distribution of SRNDCs and NDC (Figure <link href="#adma202100808-fig-0003">3e</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202100808-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information). Being different from doping with isolated heteroatoms, bond angle of CSSC can induce large distortion in structure and more effectively expand interlayer spacing. Ex situ tests indicate that CSSC bonds have a reversible sodiation/desodiation reaction during discharge/charge process, granting the long cycling stability. HRTEM characterization demonstrates that heteroatom doping has little influence on interlayer spacing in bulk phase, but dramatically expands interlayer spacing in subsurface region, by synergizing with tensile stress. The expanded interlayer spacing decreases the diffusion resistance in subsurface region and increases the accessible thickness of subsurface region, which boosts the pseudocapacitive capacity. The SRNDC‐700 electrodes display a high specific capacity of 160.6 and 69.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 20 and 50 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. After 6000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (about 80 s of charge/discharge time), the average capacity fading is only 0.0025% per cycle. At the same time, the electrochemical performance of SRNDC‐700 under different area loading in the half‐cell and Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> || SRNDC‐700 full‐cell are tested. This work is anticipated to provide new insights into the effects of doping on the structure and a new method for analyzing pseudocapacitive capacity.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0070"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0080" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Calculation Methods</title><p>The calculations were performed by density functional theory (DFT) method with the generalized gradient approximation and the Perdew, Burke, and Ernzerhof functional,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> as implemented in the Vienna ab initio simulation package.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During ions and electronic relaxations, the plane‐wave energy cutoff was set to be 560.00 eV, the electronic energy convergence was 10<sup>−7</sup> eV, and the force convergence for ions is 10<sup>−3</sup> eV Å<sup>−1</sup>. The van der Waals correction density functional dispersion correction, version 2 (DFT‐D2) method of Grimme<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202100808-bib-0047"><sup>47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> is employed to correctly describe the interlayer properties of doped graphene.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of Porous Precursor</title><p>Typically, 0.1 g of PAN (<i>M</i><sub>w</sub> = 150 000) was dissolved in 3 mL of <i>N</i>,<i>N</i>‐dimethyl formamide (DMF) within 50 °C water bath. 3 mL of glycerol and 30 mL of isopropanol were stirred at 450 rmp for 1 h in a 50 mL Teflon polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) beaker. The rotation speed was then increased to 700 rmp and the DMF solution was added dropwise into the mixed alcohol system. The resulting suspensions was kept stirring for another 20 mins before it was sealed and transformed into an 180 °C electrical oven. After 6 h of heat preservation, the yellow precipitate was washed thoroughly with absolute alcohol and deionized water. Finally the porous precursor was harvested by lyophilization.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of SRNDCs and NDC</title><p>The above prepared porous precursor was uniformly mixed with 0.5 g of sublimed sulfur and annealed at 600, 700, or 800 °C for 4 h with a heating rate of 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under Ar atmosphere. The obtained black fluffy products were marked as SRNDC‐600, SRNDC‐700, and SRNDC‐800, respectively, which are collectively referred to as SRNDCs. For comparison, the N‐doped carbon framework (denoted as NDC) was prepared by directly annealing porous precursor without adding of sulfur powder at 700 °C.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The XRD were measured by Rigaku D/max 2500 X‐ray diffractometer (Cu <i>k<sub>α</sub></i> radiation, λ = 0.154 nm). SEM (FEI Nova NanoSEM 230) and TEM (JEOL JEM‐2100F) tests were used to reveal the microstructure of the as‐obtained products. Properties and chemical state of the carbon layer were detected with Raman spectrometer (LabRAM HR800), Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR, NICOLET 6700), and X‐ray photoelectron spectrometer (XPS, ThermoFisher VG Escalab‐250xi). The chemical composition of SRNDCs and NDC were confirmed by elemental analyzer (Elementar EL Cube) Micromeritics ASAP 2460 analyzer was used for nitrogen adsorption/desorption test.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>The as‐obtained products served as electrodes both in half‐ and full‐cells for the purpose of study their electrochemical sodium storage performance. The working electrodes were fabricated by spreading the electrode slurry on Cu foil, which was composed of active material (SRNDCs or NDC), conductive carbon (Super P), and sodium carboxymethyl cellulose binder with a mass ratio of 8:1:1, and then dried at 80 °C in vacuum overnight. The mass loading is between 0.71 and 0.84 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> (based on the quality of SRNDCs and NDC). The CR2016 type coin cells were assembled in a glove box (MIKROUNA) filled with ultrahigh purity Argon. For the half‐cell configuration, metallic sodium foil served as counter and reference electrodes, a glass fiber filter paper (Whatman) worked as the separator, and 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate with 5% fluoroethylene carbonate additive was used as the electrolyte. For the full‐cell fabrication, the cathode and anode active material were graphene encapsulated Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> nanoflakes and presodiation SRNDCs, respectively, and the specific capacity ratio of cathode to anode was set at 1:1.2. The presodiation of anode, which was cycled five times in the half‐cell, was to compensate the loss of sodium during the first few cycles in full‐cell. Except for the full batteries, the charge/discharge current density and specific capacity were calculated based on the quality of anode active material (SRNDCs or NDC). CV curves were obtained by using an electrochemical workstation (CHI 660E) at different scan rates in the voltage range of 0.01–3.0 V (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). Galvanostatic discharge/charge tests were carried out on a LAND instrument at different current densities in the same voltage range. EIS measures were performed in the frequency range of 100 KHz to 10 mHz on a MULTI AUTOLAB M204 (Metrohm) electrochemical workstation at room temperature.</p></section></section></section></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0150" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge financial support from the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 51874362 and 51872304) and Ningbo S&amp;T Innovation 2025 Major Special Program (2018B10024, 2019B10<sub>(17)</sub>, and 2020Z101).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0160" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0170" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202100808-sec-0180" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>Research data are not shared.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202100808-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202100808-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202100808-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>N.</givenNames> <familyName>Yabuuchi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Kubota</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Dahbi</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.</givenNames> <familyName>Komaba</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Chem.
