type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) based on conversion‐type metal sulfide (MS) anodes have attracted extraordinary attention due to relatively high capacity and intrinsic safety. The highly reversible conversion of M/Na<sub>2</sub>S to pristine MS in charge plays a vital role with regard to the electrochemical performance. Here, taking conventional MoS<sub>2</sub> as an example, guided by theoretical simulations, a catalyst of iron single atoms on nitrogen‐doped graphene (SAFe@NG) is selected and first used as a substrate to facilitate the reaction kinetics of MoS<sub>2</sub> in the discharging process. In the following charging process, using a combination of spectroscopy and microscopy, it is demonstrated that the SAFe@NG catalyst enables an efficient reversible conversion reaction of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub>. Moreover, theoretical simulations reveal that the reversible conversion mechanism shows favorable formation energy barrier and reaction kinetics, in which SAFe@NG with the Fe–N<sub>4</sub> coordination center facilitates the uniform dispersion of Na<sub>2</sub>S/Mo and the decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S and NaMoS<sub>2</sub>. Therefore, efficient reversible conversion reaction MoS<sub>2</sub>↔NaMoS<sub>2</sub>↔Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S is enabled by the SAFe@NG catalyst. This work contributes new avenues for designing conversion‐type materials with an efficient reversible mechanism.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202007090-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A catalyst of iron single atoms on a nitrogen‐doped graphene (SAFe@NG) substrate is selected to catalyze reversible conversion in MoS<sub>2</sub> anodes. Importantly, the SAFe@NG catalyst with highly active Fe–N<sub>4</sub> sites significantly facilitates the conversion of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S to MoS<sub>2</sub> in the charging processes, resulting in an efficient reversible conversion reaction of MoS<sub>2</sub>↔NaMoS<sub>2</sub>↔Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S in a complete cycle, which is demonstrated by spectroscopy, microscopy, and simulations.<blockFixed xml:id="adma202007090-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202007090-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have achieved great success as energy storage devices for portable electronic products in the past several decades.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the increasing worry of the rarity of lithium resources is stimulating the development of nonlithium metal‐ion batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0002 #adma202007090-bib-0003"><sup>2,3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as next‐generation energy storage alternatives for large‐scale applications due to the high availability of sodium resources, and their high safety and good energy density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0004 #adma202007090-bib-0005 #adma202007090-bib-0006"><sup>4–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, the larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å vs Li<sup>+</sup> 0.76 Å) slows down the ion diffusion kinetics and increases volume change during the insertion/extraction processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As a result, the reversible capacity and cycling stability of SIBs are significantly compromised. For example, graphite anode can hardly storage Na<sup>+</sup> under normal condition, despite it has been successfully used as commercial anode material of LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the larger Na<sup>+</sup> brings even greater challenge for the design and fabrication of high‐performance electrode materials in SIBs.</p><p>Recently, transition metal dichalcogenides have attracted enormous interest due to unique physical and chemical properties and have been widely developed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0009 #adma202007090-bib-0010 #adma202007090-bib-0011 #adma202007090-bib-0012 #adma202007090-bib-0013"><sup>9–13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Especially, 2D metal disulfides (MS<sub>2</sub>, M = Fe, Co, Ni, Mo, W, Re), have attracted increasing attention as promising anode materials for SIBs, because of their low Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion resistance, short Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion length, and high theoretical capacity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0014 #adma202007090-bib-0015 #adma202007090-bib-0016 #adma202007090-bib-0017 #adma202007090-bib-0018"><sup>14–18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Generally, the initial discharging mechanism of MS<sub>2</sub> with Na contains intercalation and conversion reactions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> After discharge, pristine 2D structures are dissociated, while M and Na<sub>2</sub>S particles are formed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the subsequent charging process, owing to the material segregation and poor electrical conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>S, they would irreversibly transform into M and S rather than original MS<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0020 #adma202007090-bib-0021"><sup>20,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, S is served as active material to store Na instead of MS<sub>2</sub> after the first cycle. However, this mechanism suffers from two issues: shuttle effect of polysulfides and poor structural stability, which lead to rapid capacity attenuation and finally electrode failure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0022 #adma202007090-bib-0023"><sup>22,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the irreversible conversion mechanism is one of the major problems to achieve high‐performance MS<sub>2</sub> anode materials for SIBs.</p><p>In order to improve the electrochemical performance of MS<sub>2</sub>, several strategies have been proposed with emphasis on the pristine MS<sub>2</sub> structure. For example, constructing a 3D porous structure to improve structural stability and ion diffusion kinetics;<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0024 #adma202007090-bib-0025"><sup>24,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> increasing surface defects or extending interlayer spacing to decrease ion diffusion resistance;<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0018 #adma202007090-bib-0026 #adma202007090-bib-0027"><sup>18,26,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and introducing phase engineering to increase the electrical conductivity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0018 #adma202007090-bib-0028 #adma202007090-bib-0029"><sup>18,28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The above strategies have been demonstrated to be effective in the first discharging process. However, after the discharging process, the problems of material segregation and poor electrical conductivity of Na<sub>2</sub>S remain, leading to irreversible conversion in the charging process. As a result, the reported electrochemical performance, especially long‐life span cycling performance is unsatisfactory. Therefore, how to improve the dynamic properties of discharged products (M/Na<sub>2</sub>S) in the charging process to accelerate the reversible conversion is thus the key step for achieving high‐performance SIBs.</p><p>Incorporating MS<sub>2</sub> with carbon substrates, especially graphene doped with nonmetals, has been reported to be beneficial for improving the dynamic properties of both pristine MS<sub>2</sub> and discharged products.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0030 #adma202007090-bib-0031 #adma202007090-bib-0032"><sup>30–32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Partially reversible conversion reaction in the charging process has been reported.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0033 #adma202007090-bib-0034 #adma202007090-bib-0035"><sup>33–35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the improvement brought by nonmetal‐doped graphene is limited due to its poor catalytic activity and durability, and the reversible conversion reaction path in the charging process still lacks of understanding. Recently, transition metal single atoms on nitrogen‐doped graphene (SAM'@NG, M’ = Fe, Co, Ni or Cu) with a common M'N<sub>4</sub>C<sub>4</sub> moiety have been widely prepared and demonstrated to exhibit high catalytic activity and distinctive selectivity in electrocatalytic applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0036 #adma202007090-bib-0037 #adma202007090-bib-0038 #adma202007090-bib-0039 #adma202007090-bib-0040 #adma202007090-bib-0041"><sup>36–41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> More importantly, SAM'@NG nanosheets have been investigated as electrocatalysts for Li/Na–S batteries to promote the formation and decomposition of Li<sub>2</sub>S/Na<sub>2</sub>S.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0042 #adma202007090-bib-0043 #adma202007090-bib-0044"><sup>42–44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, it is highly anticipated that SAM'@NG nanosheets show great potential in improving the dynamic properties of M/Na<sub>2</sub>S and thereby, leading to efficient reversible conversion in the charging process. However, despite the merits brought by SAM'@NG, to the best of our knowledge, there is lack of reports on investigating SAM'@NG for the reversible reaction between M/Na<sub>2</sub>S and MS<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>Herein, taking MoS<sub>2</sub> as an example, we propose and demonstrate that SAM'@NG is a promising catalyst to improve the conversion reaction kinetics upon Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction. First of all, theoretical simulation was used to select the most promising transition metal single atom in SAM'@NG. Among them, SAFe@NG was found to exhibit the largest binding energy with MoS<sub>2</sub> and significantly improve the electrical conductivity of MoS<sub>2</sub>, facilitating the reaction kinetics of MoS<sub>2</sub>→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S in the discharging process. The obtained Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S products should show small nanoparticle size and uniformly distribute on the surface of SAFe@NG, suggesting significantly enhanced dynamic properties in the charging process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As expected, the SAFe@NG shows a great effect on the catalytic decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S and NaMoS<sub>2</sub> in the charging process, resulting in an efficient reversible conversion reaction of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub> with a favorable formation energy barrier. Benefiting from the efficient conversion reaction in a complete cycle, the MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAFe@NG displays an impressive high‐rate and long‐life span cycling performance.</p><p>Theoretical simulation was first performed to screen SAM'@NG catalyst for enhancing the electrochemical properties of MoS<sub>2</sub>. As shown in Figure S1 in the Supporting Information, the heterostructure models of MoS<sub>2</sub>/G, MoS<sub>2</sub>/NG, MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAFe@NG, MoS<sub>2</sub>/SACo@NG, MoS<sub>2</sub>/SANi@NG, MoS<sub>2</sub>/SACu@NG were constructed. The adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>ad</sub>) results of the above six heterostructures (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>) indicate that SAFe@NG shows the largest interfacial binding energy to MoS<sub>2</sub> (17.84 meV Å<sup>−2</sup>), which suggests strong stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0031 #adma202007090-bib-0035 #adma202007090-bib-0045"><sup>31,35,45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the total density of states (DOS) for these composites were carried out to study the electrical conductivity (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001">1b</link>). From the density differences at fermi level, all SAM'@NG significantly improve the electrical conductivity of MoS<sub>2</sub> and better than traditional graphene and NG.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As mentioned above, SAFe@NG displays the best potential on improving structural stability and electrical conductivity of MoS<sub>2</sub>, facilitating the conversion reaction of MoS<sub>2</sub>→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S in the discharging process. As a result, the discharged products of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S should uniformly distribute and strongly anchor on the surface of SAFe@NG,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which indicates great potential on improving the dynamic properties in the charging process. According to the theoretical result of MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAM'@NG models, the SAFe@NG catalyst was preliminarily selected and further used to improve the reversible conversion reaction of MoS<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="adma202007090-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007090:adma202007090-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007090-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Adsorption energy and b) DOS of MoS<sub>2</sub> on graphene, NG, SAFe@NG, SACo@NG, SANi@NG, and SACu@NG, respectively. The inset image in (a) is the side view of the MoS<sub>2</sub> adsorption configurations on SAFe@NG. c) TEM image, d) HAADF image and EDS mapping images, and e) AC‐STEM‐ADF image of SAFe@NG, and the red circles stand for Fe single atoms. f) Fe L‐edge of the NEXAFS spectra of SAFe@NG, Fe foil and FePc. g) Fe K‐edge XANES spectra and h) FT‐EXAFS spectra of SAFe@NG, Fe foil, FePc, and Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.</caption></figure><p>A seeding approach was used to prepare SAFe@NG following our previous work.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0047"><sup>47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images of the SAFe@NG demonstrate curved graphene without nanoparticles observed on the surface (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001">1c</link>; Figure S2a, Supporting Information). SAFe@NG was prepared and scaled up as shown in the inset of Figure S2a in the Supporting Information, allowing for significant commercial potential. Furthermore, the energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) elemental mapping images indicate that the C, N, Fe elements are uniformly distributed throughout the structure (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001">1d</link>). The corresponding EDS report (Figure S2b, Supporting Information) clearly shows the obvious Fe peak around 6.4 keV. Isolated single atoms are unambiguously atomically dispersed on the substrate as shown by the aberration‐corrected scanning TEM annular dark‐field (AC‐STEM‐ADF) image, and no Fe particles are found (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001">1e</link>). For comparison, conventional NG was prepared. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of NG and SAFe@NG only present one weakly broad diffraction peak around 26.5°, suggesting ultrathin structure with few layers (Figure S3a, Supporting Information). In addition, no peak associated with Fe‐based species can be detected in SAFe@NG, in consistent with the TEM observation (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001">1c</link>). Raman spectra for NG and SAFe@NG indicate similar ratios of D and G band, which means that the basic sp<sup>2</sup> configuration of graphene is retained in SAFe@NG (Figure S3b, Supporting Information).</p><p>Moreover, the chemical environments of SAFe@NG were investigated by X‐ray absorption spectroscopy (XAS) techniques. The near edge X‐ray absorption fine structure (NEXAFS) spectra of Fe L‐edge feature with two broad peaks corresponds to a splitting of the Fe 2p peaks (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0001">1f</link>). Herein, ex situ XRD (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>), XPS (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4b</link>), HRTEM, and SAED (Figure S18, Supporting Information) characterizations were performed to study the unique conversion reaction. Five states during the first cycle, including pristine (state 1), discharging at 0.5 V (state 2), discharging at 0.01 V (state 3), charging at 1.5 V (state 4), and charging at 3.0 V (state 5) are considered (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4a</link>). From the XRD patterns (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4b</link>), XPS curves (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4c</link>; Table S5, Supporting Information), HRTEM images and SAED patterns (Figure S18, Supporting Information), we demonstrate that the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion process from state 1 to state 3 is MoS<sub>2</sub> → Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>MoS<sub>2</sub> → Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S, agreeing well with a previous report.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> By comparison, the intercalation and conversion reactions also appear in MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure S18, Supporting Information). However, for the charging process (from state 3 to sate 5), MoS<sub>2</sub> and MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAFe@NG deliver different conversion reactions. For MoS<sub>2</sub>, no obvious change occurs during 0.01–1.5 V, and the Na<sub>2</sub>S is oxidized to S at a high potential (1.85 V vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). Irreversible conversion happens in the charging process and the original Na/MoS<sub>2</sub> cell transforms into Na/S(+ Mo) cell after the first cycle (Figure S19, Supporting Information). This irreversible conversion leads to the low performance of pure MoS<sub>2</sub>. With the assistance of SAFe@NG, Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S causes a new conversion reaction at a low voltage zone (0.01–1.5 V). NaMoS<sub>2</sub> is observed at state 4 as demonstrated by XRD (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4b</link>), XPS (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4c</link>) and HRTEM (Figure S17, Supporting Information) characterizations. Therefore, the new reaction mechanism between 0.3 and 1.2 V in the CV curves of MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAFe@NG (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0003">3b</link>) is Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S → NaMoS<sub>2</sub>. In addition, because some discharged products may not be in good contact with the surface of SAFe@NG, a weak signal of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S is detected at state 4. When the voltage increases to 3.0 V, NaMoS<sub>2</sub> disappears and MoS<sub>2</sub> reappears at state 5 (Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">4b</link>,<link href="#adma202007090-fig-0004">c</link>; Figure S17, Supporting Information), suggesting the second reaction pathway of NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub> during high charging voltage. There is a small amount of Mo/S at state 5 owing to the irreversible conversion of contactless Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S. Therefore, for MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAFe@NG, a large amount of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S has two‐step reversible conversion reaction of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub> in the charging process. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>, the energy barriers of the above reaction pathways were computed by the climbing‐image nudged elastic band (CI‐NEB) method.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0066"><sup>66</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>S/Mo in MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode shows pulverization and segregation, only Na<sub>2</sub>S decomposes at a high discharging voltage (1.85 V) with a high formation energy of 8.00 eV (Equation (<link href="#adma202007090-disp-0003">3</link>)), resulting in irreversible conversion. The SAFe@NG enables a new kind of charging reaction in 0.3–1.2 V with a smaller formation energy barrier. The formation energy barriers for Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>, NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub>, and Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→MoS<sub>2</sub> are 4.73, 1.50, and 6.23 eV, respectively. This suggests that the two‐step reversible charging process is more likely to happen due to a smaller formation energy barrier, which agrees well with the experimental results.</p><figure xml:id="adma202007090-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007090:adma202007090-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007090-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Theoretical simulations of the reversible conversion mechanism in the charging process. a) The formation energies for three kinds of conversion reaction pathways. b,c) Side view for bond length and bond angle (b) and charge density difference (c) of Na<sub>2</sub>S adsorption on graphene, NG, and SAFe@NG. The inset image shows decomposition pathway of Na<sub>2</sub>S on SAFe@NG.</caption></figure><p>The catalytic mechanism of SAFe@NG for the reversible conversion of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S was further studied by theoretical simulation. For comparison, conventional graphene and NG substrates were considered. For the first step of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>, the interaction between the substrates and Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S was learned. The <i>E</i><sub>ad</sub> is defined as: the energy of the substrate and the independent Mo or Na<sub>2</sub>S reduces the energy of the substrate with Mo or Na<sub>2</sub>S adsorbed. Thus, the larger the value of <i>E</i><sub>ad</sub>, the stronger is the interaction between Na<sub>2</sub>S and substrates. The atomic models and calculated results are presented in Figure S20a–c and Table S6 in the Supporting Information. Graphene shows a weak bonding interaction with Na<sub>2</sub>S, while NG and SAFe@NG exhibit bonding of N–Na and Fe–S with Na<sub>2</sub>S, respectively. As a result, graphene shows the smallest adsorption energy to Na<sub>2</sub>S (0.52 eV), while SAFe@NG exhibits a suitable value of 1.60 V and NG achieves a large value of 2.25 eV. In addition, NG also shows the largest adsorption energy to Mo (Figure S20d,e and Table S6, Supporting Information). Therefore, both NG and SAFe@NG show significantly positive effect on anchoring Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S. This greatly enables the reaction between Mo and Na<sub>2</sub>S during charging process.</p><p>The geometrical structure, electronic structure, and atomic orbital of Na<sub>2</sub>S/G, Na<sub>2</sub>S/NG, and Na<sub>2</sub>S/SAFe@NG models were analyzed to study the catalytic activity. For the first step of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>, the decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S plays a key role. The bond strength of Na–S in the above three models can be used to evaluate the catalytic activity of the three catalysts. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005">5b</link>, the NaS bond strength can be reflected by the bond length and bond angle (Na1–S–Na2). For the graphene and SAFe@NG substrates, both have the equally weakening effect on the Na1S and Na2S bond of Na<sub>2</sub>S molecules. The Na<sub>2</sub>S on SAFe@NG substrate exhibits a larger NaS bond length of 2.58 Å and a larger Na1SNa2 bond angle of 115.00°, suggesting that the NaS bond in Na<sub>2</sub>S molecules has been greatly weakened by SAFe@NG.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, for the NG substrate, the weakening degrees toward Na1S and Na2S bond are different. The Na<sub>2</sub>S on NG exhibits the largest Na1S bond length of 2.74 Å, small Na2S bond of 2.57 Å and the smallest bond angle of 84.54°. Combination with the charge density difference analysis, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005">5c</link>, the weakening mechanism on Na<sub>2</sub>S in three substrates can be classified into two types (Na affinity mechanism and S affinity mechanism). The interaction between the Na<sub>2</sub>S molecule and NG is mainly through the Na1N bond which can be confirmed by its strong electronic transformation. The Na affinity mechanism in the NG substrate shows the largest Na1S bond and large Na2S bond. The biggest adsorption energy of Na<sub>2</sub>S on NG (2.25 eV) implies that the strong Na1N bond strength makes it hard to break down Na1 atom from NG catalyst, which poisons the activity of the NG catalyst and blocks the subsequent reaction. However, for the Na<sub>2</sub>S/SAFe@NG system, the electron migration accumulates mainly on the FeS bond, which can simultaneously weaken the Na1S and Na2S bond strength.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202007090-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This S affinity mechanism has a moderate adsorption energy (1.60 eV) for Na<sub>2</sub>S on SAFe@NG, which is beneficial to the reversible adsorption and decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S, resulting in durable SAFe@NG catalyst.</p><p>In order to further explore the effect of the single atom Fe on the catalytic activity, the atomic orbitals of Fe and S atoms were investigated. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005">5d</link>, the partial DOS (PDOS) result shows that the Fe‐3d and S‐3p orbitals exhibit significant hybridization in several energy ranges of −2.89 to −2.54, −2.34 to −1.37, and −0.67 to −0.23 eV. Further analysis revealed that the Fe–S hybridization is mainly contributed to the <math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202007090:adma202007090-math-0007" wiley:location="graphic/adma202007090-math-0007.png"><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">d</mi><mi>z</mi></msub><msup><mrow/><msub><mrow/><mn>2</mn></msub></msup></mrow></math> and d<i><sub>xz</sub></i>,<i><sub>yz</sub></i> orbitals of Fe atom and p orbitals of S atom. Partial crystal orbital Hamiltonian populations (COHP) were further studied and shown in Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005">5e</link>,<link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005">f</link>. For p–d<i><sub>xz</sub></i><sub>,</sub><i><sub>yz</sub></i> hybridization, the antibonding part goes through the Fermi level, which weakens the FeS bond strength. For p–<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202007090:adma202007090-math-0008" wiley:location="graphic/adma202007090-math-0008.png"><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">d</mi><mi>z</mi></msub><msup><mrow/><msub><mrow/><mn>2</mn></msub></msup></mrow></math> hybridization, the antibonding part is almost entirely located above the Fermi level and the coupling energy range mainly locates on the deep energy level. Thus, the bonding effect between the Fe–S atoms mainly owe to the bonding behavior of p–<math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="inline" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202007090:adma202007090-math-0009" wiley:location="graphic/adma202007090-math-0009.png"><mrow><msub><mi mathvariant="normal">d</mi><mi>z</mi></msub><msup><mrow/><msub><mrow/><mn>2</mn></msub></msup></mrow></math> hybridization. On the basis of the above analyses, the FeS bond in Na<sub>2</sub>S/SAFe@NG system exhibits an appropriate strength, which is beneficial to both adsorption and decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S, suggesting the excellent durability of SAFe@NG catalyst.</p><p>Moreover, the decomposition barriers of Na<sub>2</sub>S on the three substrates were directly computed, as presented in Figure <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0005">5f</link> and Figure S21 in the Supporting Information. The decomposition process of Na2 atom contains the breaking of Na2S bond and the desorption of Na atoms. Graphene substrate shows the largest decomposition barrier of 1.92 eV, while SAFe@NG exhibits the smallest decomposition barrier of 1.23 eV. NG delivers a large decomposition barrier of 1.47 eV owing to the overlarge adsorption to Na<sub>2</sub>S. As mentioned above, SAFe@NG displays the best catalytic activity for the decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S. Moreover, SAFe@NG possesses suitable adsorption energy to Na<sub>2</sub>S and Mo, which is beneficial to the further reaction between Mo and decomposed Na<sub>2</sub>S to form NaMoS<sub>2</sub>. Therefore, SAFe@NG has the best catalytic effect on reversible conversion reaction of the first step of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>Besides, the effect of NG and SAFe@NG on the second step reaction of NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub> was studied. Graphene shows little effect on the first step reaction and thus, it does not need to be considered in the second step. The bond breaking between Na atom and S atom is the dominate step for the decomposition of NaMoS<sub>2</sub> to form MoS<sub>2</sub>. The thermodynamic configuration of NaMoS<sub>2</sub> on NG and SAFe@NG substrates was performed, as displayed in Figure S22 in the Supporting Information. SAFe@NG shows large adsorption energy to NaMoS<sub>2</sub> with 2.50 eV, whereas NG possesses an overlarge value of 4.61 eV (Table S6, Supporting Information) and thus, promotes the NaS bond. The NaMoS<sub>2</sub> on SAFe@NG exhibits both larger SNaS bond angel (84.34°) and NaS bond length (2.75 Å) than those on NG substrate, demonstrating that the NaS bond in NaMoS<sub>2</sub> molecules is significantly weakened by SAFe@NG. Moreover, as shown in Figure S23 in the Supporting Information, the decomposition barrier of NaMoS<sub>2</sub> on SAFe@NG substrate is 1.45 eV, which is smaller than that for the NG substrate (1.61 eV). Therefore, SAFe@NG shows the best catalytic activity on reversible conversion reaction of the second step of NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub>.</p><p>On the basis of the above discussion, it is demonstrated that SAFe@NG shows great enhancement on catalytic effect for the two‐step reversible conversion in the charging process. As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202007090-fig-0006"><b>6</b></link>, the SAFe@NG substrate shows suitable adsorption energy to pristine Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S and intermediate phase NaMoS<sub>2</sub>, which is not only beneficial to the uniform dispersion. Therefore, Na<sub>2</sub>S is in close contact with Mo and tightly anchored on the Fe–N<sub>4</sub> active sites of SAFe@NG in the first step. Then, the Fe–N<sub>4</sub> active sites are beneficial to the catalytic decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S and the further reaction between Mo and decomposed Na<sub>2</sub>S. The intermediate phase of NaMoS<sub>2</sub> is more likely to form due to the low formation energy barrier. Finally, SAFe@NG can greatly catalyze the decomposition of the intermediate phase of NaMoS<sub>2</sub> to MoS<sub>2</sub> in the second step. Moreover, SAFe@NG serves as a high‐speed channel for electron transfer. Therefore, SAFe@NG greatly improves the dynamic properties of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S, resulting in an efficient reversible conversion reaction of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub> in the charging process and thus, leading to efficient reversion conversion reaction of MoS<sub>2</sub>↔NaMoS<sub>2</sub>↔Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S in a complete cycle.</p><figure xml:id="adma202007090-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202007090:adma202007090-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202007090-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Illustration of the two‐step reversible conversion reaction in the charging process.</caption></figure><p>In summary, SAFe@NG catalyst was chosen to improve the dynamic properties of MoS<sub>2</sub> by the guidance of theoretical simulations. After discharge, the transformed Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S were uniformly dispersed on the surface of SAFe@NG. The SAFe@NG with unique Fe–N<sub>4</sub> active sites has been demonstrated to show great effect on the catalytic decomposition of Na<sub>2</sub>S and intermediate phase of NaMoS<sub>2</sub>, enabling efficient reversible conversion reaction of Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S→NaMoS<sub>2</sub>→MoS<sub>2</sub> in the charging process, confirmed by spectroscopy, microscopy, and simulations. Therefore, the SAFe@NG catalyst significantly improves the reaction kinetics in the discharging and charging processes, leading to efficient reversible conversion reaction of MoS<sub>2</sub>↔NaMoS<sub>2</sub>↔Mo/Na<sub>2</sub>S in a complete cycle. As a result, the designed MoS<sub>2</sub>/SAFe@NG nanosheets deliver a long‐life span cycling performance of 364 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The present work not only demonstrates that the single atom catalysts can boost the reversible conversion reaction in the charging process, but also sheds light on the design of long‐term stability conversion‐type materials in alkali‐metal ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202007090-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>B.C., T.W., and S.Z. contributed equally to this work.
