Here, a novel spontaneous TM layer rippling in the sodium ion battery cathode materials is reported, revealed by in situ X‐ray diffraction, Cs‐corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy, and density functional theory simulation, where the softening and distortion of FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedra collectively drives the flat TM planes into rippled ones with inhomogeneous interlayer distance at high voltage. In such a rippling phase, charge and discharge of Na ions take different evolution pathways, resulting in an unusual hysteresis voltage loop. Importantly, upon discharge beyond a certain Na composition, the rippling TM layer will go back to flat, giving the reversibility of such structural evolution in the following xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0010"><label role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>The layered compound NaTMO<sub>2</sub>, with TM being the 3d transition metal elements, has been an active platform for the development of electrode materials for rechargeable Na ion batteries.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0001 #adfm201803896-bib-0002 #adfm201803896-bib-0003 #adfm201803896-bib-0004 #adfm201803896-bib-0005 #adfm201803896-bib-0006 #adfm201803896-bib-0007 #adfm201803896-bib-0008 #adfm201803896-bib-0009 #adfm201803896-bib-0010 #adfm201803896-bib-0011 #adfm201803896-bib-0012 #adfm201803896-bib-0013 #adfm201803896-bib-0014 #adfm201803896-bib-0015 #adfm201803896-bib-0016 #adfm201803896-bib-0017 #adfm201803896-bib-0018 #adfm201803896-bib-0019 #adfm201803896-bib-0020 #adfm201803896-bib-0021 #adfm201803896-bib-0022"/> In both the intercalation and de‐intercalation processes of Na ions, various Na orderings form and couple with the transition metal charge, spin and orbital orderings to further modulate the fundamental physical and chemical properties of the materials, including the metal‐insulator transition in Na<sub>1/2</sub>VO<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0006"/> superconductivity in water intercalated Na<sub>0.35</sub>CoO<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0023"/> Na patterning and suppressed thermal conductivity in Na<sub>0.8</sub>CoO<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0024 #adfm201803896-bib-0025"/> and spin, charge and orbital stripe orderings in Na<sub>5/8</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0026"/> For the mixed transition metal layered NaTMO<sub>2</sub> compounds,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0011 #adfm201803896-bib-0012 #adfm201803896-bib-0013 #adfm201803896-bib-0014 #adfm201803896-bib-0015 #adfm201803896-bib-0016 #adfm201803896-bib-0017 #adfm201803896-bib-0018 #adfm201803896-bib-0019 #adfm201803896-bib-0020"/> where more than one type of TM sits in the TM layer, less Na orderings are observed due to the perturbation from the randomly mixed TM sites. Meanwhile, the electrochemical capacity is in general improved in the ternary and quaternary mixed TM systems with the absence of Na ordering.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0027"/> Previous study<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028"/> pointed out that the buckling of individual Fe ion in these compounds can help the diffusion of each Na ion. In this article, we further present an unusual collective behavior of such Fe‐containing NaTMO<sub>2</sub> compounds, where the flat TMO<sub>2</sub> layers become rippling beyond a certain voltage in charge and return back to flat toward the end of discharge. The TM layer rippling is directly visualized by Cs‐corrected scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM). The rippling effect also causes the inhomogeneous strain broadening of Bragg peaks observed by our (in situ) X‐ray diffraction (XRD). It is further found by density functional theory (DFT) simulation that rippling is a fundamentally unique property of Fe containing TM oxide layers. Since Na or Li ion diffuses faster at wider interlayer region in the O3 phase,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028 #adfm201803896-bib-0029"/> the wider regions in the rippling phase become more susceptible to both Na ion intercalation and de‐intercalation than the narrower ones, which results in the asymmetric structural evolution between discharge and charge. Specifically, the P3 phase in charge can be missing from discharge at high Fe composition, leading to the novel hysteresis voltage loop with phase‐transition‐free discharge. Notably, the rippled TM layers can go back to the flat phase above the critical Na composition upon battery discharge, as observed by in situ XRD, STEM, and DFT, making it ready to repeat such hysteresis structural evolution in the second charge and discharge, as well as in the following cycles.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0030"><label role="interim">2.1</label><title type="main">Rippling of TMO<sub>2</sub> Layers in DFT Simulation</title><p>We observed in DFT simulation that by applying stress or through coupling to Na clusters at low Na composition, the layered transition metal oxide structure with O3 stacking can relax to a new ground state with rippling TMO<sub>2</sub> layers due to the collective softening and distortion of the contained FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedrons. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a shows an example of stress induced rippling in layered Fe<sub>0.25</sub>TM<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with empty Na layer from DFT simulation (TM = Mn). The Fe and Mn ordering in this particular supercell is shown in Figure S1a (Supporting Information). The interlayer stacking of interest here is O3 type, which was shown previously to be exhibited by Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<i><sub>y</sub></i>TM<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>y</sub></i>]O<sub>2</sub> at high voltage (<i>x</i> &lt; 0.4).<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028"/> Full relaxation of the lattice parameters gives an ordinary flat structure with all TM ions staying in the TMO<sub>2</sub> planes (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>a left) with the in‐plane lattice parameter <i>a</i> of 3.0 Å. However, when the <i>a</i> lattice parameter is fixed to, for example, 2.85 Å, which is very close to the experimental value of O3‐Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Mn<sub>0.25</sub>Fe<sub>0.25</sub>Co<sub>0.25</sub>Ni<sub>0.25</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0017"/> at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.4, O3‐Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0030"/> at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.4 and our in situ XRD measured value of various O3 stacking Fe containing materials at high voltage (shown later), the structure is relaxed to an unusual phase with rippled TM layers (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>a right). Note that fixing <i>a</i> lattice parameter at other smaller values also gives the rippling states. The examination of each TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedron reveals that the FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedrons show much stronger distortions than the MnO<sub>6</sub> ones, as quantified by the distortion index (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>b), which describes the degree of distortion of the TMO<sub>6</sub> octahedrons and the TMO bond angle variance.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0031"/> The comparison in Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>b suggests that the rippling phenomenon is closely related to the higher structural flexibility and softening effect of the FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedrons at high voltage. In comparison, without Fe the rippling of TMO<sub>2</sub> layer (TM = Mn, Co, Ni and their mixtures) is never observed by fixing the lattice parameter at different values, suggesting the unique property of FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedron in making the structural distortion and softening in response to the environmental perturbation.</p><figure xml:id="adfm201803896-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm201803896:adfm201803896-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm201803896-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>DFT calculation of spontaneous rippling in Fe containing TM oxides. a) O3 type Fe<sub>0.25</sub>TM<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with empty Na layer (TM = Mn here), with flat (left) and rippled (right) metal oxide layers, respectively. The unstable flat structure is directly relaxed to the rippled structure by DFT. b) Quantified distortion of FeO<sub>6</sub> and MnO<sub>6</sub> in DFT relaxed rippled structure shown in (a). c) DFT relaxed structures for Na<sub>1/9</sub>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> observed from <i>c</i> (top) and <i>b</i> (bottom) directions with clustered Na (left) and evenly distributed Na (right). d) Energy benefit and TM rippling amplitude for various Fe and non‐Fe containing Na ion cathode compounds. For the <i>x</i>‐axis labels, FM stands for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, FN for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, FC for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, MFCN for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Mn<sub>0.25</sub>Fe<sub>0.25</sub>Co<sub>0.25</sub>Ni<sub>0.25</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, Co for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>CoO<sub>2</sub>, and Ni for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>NiO<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i> = 1/9).</caption></figure><p>We also calculated the structures with different Fe to TM (TM = Mn, Co, Ni) ratios of Fe<sub>0.25</sub>TM<sub>0.75</sub>, Fe<sub>1/3</sub>TM<sub>2/3</sub>, and Fe<sub>0.5</sub>TM<sub>0.5</sub> with various TM orderings and interlayer distances, along with the pure Fe case (Figures S1–S5, Supporting Information). Due to the length scale of rippling, a high throughput DFT calculation<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0032"/> of all TM orderings in such a large supercell is beyond the computational limit. We thus randomly generated several TM orderings at any given Fe composition for further DFT relaxations. In most cases they either show unstable flat structures, from which rippled structures are spontaneously obtained by relaxation as the case of Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>a, or show around 10 meV energy benefit per Fe ion in the rippled structures by manually distorting and DFT relaxing the metastable flat structures. This indicates the rippling phenomenon is not caused by any specific TM ordering, composition or interlayer distance but is rather general when Fe exists. The irrelevance of TM ordering is further supported by our neutron diffraction measurement to be discussed later. However, we note that in some occasional cases with low Fe composition of 0.25 or 1/3, the rippled structures cannot be formed, while all the structures with 50% Fe composition prefer the rippling phase, suggesting the importance of collective contribution from enough FeO<sub>6</sub> to such preference. The hypothetical layered structure without the Na ions thus demonstrates the intrinsic rippling nature of the metal oxide layers mixed with Fe.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0040"><label role="interim">2.2</label><title type="main">Softening and Distortion of FeO<sub>6</sub> for Rippling Phase</title><p>Since we have not put the Na ions to these TMO<sub>2</sub> layers yet, all the TM ions are oxidized to 4+ valence state in our DFT simulation based on the magnetization on each TM ion. In our simulation the Fe<sup>4+</sup> ions are Jahn‐Teller active due to the single occupancy of e<sub>g</sub> orbitals, while Mn<sup>4+</sup>, Co<sup>4+</sup>, Ni<sup>4+</sup> ions are not. The existence of Jahn‐Teller active Fe<sup>4+</sup>O<sub>6</sub> is supported by previous experimental conclusions from Mossbauer and X‐ray absorption spectroscopy measurements of O3‐Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>FeO<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0033 #adfm201803896-bib-0034"/> at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.5, O3‐Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0015"/> at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.4 and P2‐ Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0018"/> at fully charged state, and also consistent with previous computational studies.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028 #adfm201803896-bib-0035"/> However, it was also shown by experiment that for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>(Fe<i><sub>y</sub></i>Ni<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>y</sub></i>)O<sub>2</sub> at high voltage (<i>x</i> &lt; 0.4) a non‐negligible fraction of Fe<sup>3+</sup> is not oxidized to Fe<sup>4+</sup> as expected, showing a discrepancy to the simple ionic model of Fe<sup>3+/4+</sup> redox.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0015"/> This leads to the discussion of other processes such as partially oxidized Fe due to oxygen redox and ligand to metal charge transfer,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0013 #adfm201803896-bib-0015"/> which softens the FeO bonding. The rippling phase of interest here should be contributed by the collective effects of the softened FeO bonding in general and the Jahn‐Teller distortion of oxidized Fe<sup>4+</sup>O<sub>6</sub>.</p><p>The importance of FeO<sub>6</sub> distortion and softening is further supported by comparing the flat structure with the corresponding rippled one, where the distortions of FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedrons are strongly enhanced with rippling (Figure S4e,f, Supporting Information). Note that several Van der Waals correction methods suggested for the calculation of Na deficit structures<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0036 #adfm201803896-bib-0037 #adfm201803896-bib-0038 #adfm201803896-bib-0039 #adfm201803896-bib-0040"/> were also applied in all our calculations with qualitatively similar results (Figure S4, Supporting Information). Interestingly, we find that these Fe<i><sub>x</sub></i>TM<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>2</sub> structures can actually be DFT relaxed to all sorts of variants with different local bending geometries, as illustrated in Figure S5 (Supporting Information), with very small energy variance that is much smaller than the energy difference from the flat structure (Figure S5d, Supporting Information). Thus, the rippling phase can be considered as a type of glass phase with many local energy minima. Note again that although the computation of this ideal Na‐empty model requires external stress by fixing the lattice parameter, stressing non‐Fe containing TMO<sub>2</sub> will not change the TM layers from flat to rippled.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0050"><label role="interim">2.3</label><title type="main">Rippling Phase Coupled with Na Clusters</title><p>In the following calculation of the realistic case with Na ions, we will show that Fe containing rippling structure can be obtained through the special interaction with Na ions, where stress is not needed anymore in the simulation. Consider a realistic high‐voltage state for Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>TMO<sub>2</sub>, where Na ions are present in these rippled structures, our DFT result shows that they prefer to fit into the wider interlayer regions to further enforce the original rippling feature (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>c left), rather than forming the homogeneous Na distribution that forces the relaxation into the flat TM layers (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>c right). The total energy differences between such rippled and flat states are shown in Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0001"/>d for various Na<sub>1/9</sub>TMO<sub>2</sub> layered structures with different Fe compositions. For all the Fe‐based compounds, our DFT simulation shows that the rippling phase is always energetically preferred, while for the non‐Fe cases of NaNiO<sub>2</sub> and NaCoO<sub>2</sub>, there is no such energy preference. Since Na ions experience stronger Na<sup>+</sup>‐Na<sup>+</sup> electrostatic repulsion in the TM rippling phase than the flat phase at a given Na composition due to smaller Na<sup>+</sup>‐Na<sup>+</sup> distance, the rippling of the TM layers dominates the energy benefit. We also calculated five different TM orderings for Na<sub>1/9</sub>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> case (Figure S6, Supporting Information), which all show at least around 10 meV energy benefit per Fe ion in the rippling phase. Note that different correction methods for Van der Waals interaction (D2, D3BJ, without correction) give very close energy benefit (Figure S6b, Supporting Information). Furthermore, different from the previous case with empty Na layer, here we performed full relaxation of lattice parameters for all structures with Na ions. The fact that they did not prefer the flat TM layers under zero external stress indicates that the rippled structure coupled with Na clustering is the global energy minimum above a certain Na composition, where the strain in the rippling phase will not be released due to this energy preference. On the other hand, when the Na layer is fully occupied, the flat structure is energetically preferred than the rippling one, suggesting that there is a rippling to flat phase transition with Na intercalation toward full sodiation. Determining such critical transition Na composition is beyond the computational limit, and we leave this question to be answered by our in situ XRD measurements.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0060"><label role="interim">2.4</label><title type="main">XRD and STEM Evidence of Rippling Phase</title><p>The above prediction from the DFT simulation is consistent with our experimental observations. First, <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a shows the full‐width‐half‐maximum (FWHM) evolution of the (003) peak at different Na compositions obtained from our in situ XRD measurement of layered Na[Mn<sub>0.25</sub>Fe<sub>0.25</sub>Co<sub>0.25</sub>Ni<sub>0.25</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (MFCN).<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0017"/> It clearly shows a reversible evolution of the XRD peaks between sharp and broad shapes during charge and discharge at high voltage, a region with low Na composition between 14% and 25%. Such reversible broadening was also seen in the Fe‐mixed ternary and binary systems (see Figures <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0004"/> and <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0005"/>, and Figure S7 in the Supporting Information) and in previous experiments,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028 #adfm201803896-bib-0030"/> but the underlying mechanism was not understood before. The angle‐dependence of the broadening (change of FWHM) of (003) and (006) peaks of these in situ XRD patterns fits well with the strain broadening model that is consistent with the strain in our rippling phase picture, while it significantly deviates away from the size broadening model<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0041"/> (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0002"/>b). This indicates that the broadening effect is not consistent with any model that relies on the domain separation at high voltage, such as many small domains with flat TM layers but different interlayer distances in a particle. Furthermore, the (011) peak, which corresponds to (hkl) planes almost parallel to <i>c</i> direction (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0002"/>c), is much less broadened than (003) peak, consistent with the much smaller strain in the <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> directions inside TM planes than the interlayer strain in the rippling phase. Thus, the reversible broadening of XRD peaks observed by our in situ XRD is actually a strain broadening of Bragg peaks caused by the rippled TM layers at lower Na compositions, and the relief of such strain in the flat structure at higher Na compositions.</p><figure xml:id="adfm201803896-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm201803896:adfm201803896-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm201803896-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Observation of clustering phase related phenomena by in situ XRD and Cs‐corrected STEM. a) In situ XRD measured FWHM of the (003) peak for MFCN. Although computational evidence suggests that the Fe migration is more possible than other TM (Ni, Co, Mn) at high voltage in these O3 layered materials,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028"/> our results show that Fe migration is not compatible with TM layer rippling, as TM migration to the Na layer actually tends to stabilize the flat layer instead of rippling it (Figure S9, Supporting Information). Also, note again that here the sharp‐broad‐sharp evolution of the (003) peaks of MFCN is totally reversible in one cycle of battery charge and discharge, i.e., the FWHM is the same before the charge and after the complete discharge. Thus, the mechanism of such broadening is intrinsically different from the irreversible broadening caused by stacking faults or TM migration, where the peaks cannot become sharp again in discharge once broadened in charge, as observed in NaCrO<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0042 #adfm201803896-bib-0043"/> NaTiO<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0022"/> and NaFeO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028"/></p><p>It is worth noting that reversible migration has been discussed in some P2 type NaTMO<sub>2</sub> such as P2‐Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Li<i><sub>y</sub></i>Ni<i><sub>z</sub></i>Mn<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>y</sub></i><sub>−</sub><i><sub>z</sub></i>]O<sub>2</sub><link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0044"/> and P2‐Na<sub>2/3−</sub><i><sub>z</sub></i>[Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Fe<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0013"/> However, in addition to the issue of flattening the TMO<sub>2</sub> layer by Fe migration suggested by our DFT above, the incompatibility of such model with the rippling behavior in the O3‐NaTMO<sub>2</sub> is also suggested by our in situ XRD and DFT analysis. The peak broadening in charge starts at high interlayer distance around 5.5 Å for MFCN, Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (discussed later), and other binary and ternary systems (Figure S7, Supporting Information), where the Fe migration barrier is as high as 0.89 eV at single Fe embed in other TM environment, 0.75 eV at 2Fe cluster and 0.58 eV at 3Fe cluster (rare case for less than 50% Fe composition), respectively (Figure S10, Supporting Information), based on our DFT simulation. Thus, with such high migration barriers the Fe migration events will not have enough probability at the interlayer distance of initial peak broadening during charge to cause such abrupt entry into the collective structural rippling, which suggests that the reversible migration model discussed in the P2 compounds may not be applied to the O3 case. However, TM migration may still exist in our systems, as will be discussed in the Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> case at 4.5 V voltage cutoff.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0080"><label role="interim">2.6</label><title type="main">Neutron Diffraction Shows No Transition Metal Ordering</title><p>As already argued by our DFT simulations, the rippling phase observed in these Fe containing layered compounds is not a result of any specific TM ordering. This conclusion is further supported by our neutron diffraction measurements of Na[Mn<sub>1/4</sub>Fe<sub>1/4</sub>Co<sub>1/4</sub>Ni<sub>1/4</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, and Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> at both room temperature and 5 K (Figure S11, Supporting Information), where neither additional Bragg diffraction peaks due to the distinct neutron diffraction cross sections between different TM ions, nor magnetic Bragg diffraction peaks down to 5K were observed, indicating no long range magnetic ordering. We also fit the broad peak in neutron diffraction of Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> at low temperatures (Figure S11c, Supporting Information), which suggests no short range magnetic ordering beyond the first nearest neighbor. Furthermore, no short range magnetic ordering was observed in Na[Mn<sub>1/4</sub>Fe<sub>1/4</sub>Co<sub>1/4</sub>Ni<sub>1/4</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> and Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> at all. The lack of long‐range magnetic ordering is consistent with the lack of TM ordering in the mixed transition metal systems. Combining computation with experiment, we conclude that unlike Li cathode materials where TM orderings often exist<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0045"/> or materials consisting of 3d TM doped with heavy elements such as Bi,<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0046"/> in these sodium cathode compounds containing just 3d TM, neither long range nor short range orderings between TM ions could be the reasons for the general rippling phenomenon in all the Fe containing layered compounds.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0090"><label role="interim">2.7</label><title type="main">Reversible Asymmetric Structural Evolution at High Fe Composition</title><p>Since our DFT calculations predict that the rippling effect strengthens with Fe composition, we further study more details of the rippling phase from a binary system of Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (FC), where a new macroscopic effect of hysteresis voltage loop perfectly reflects the underlying mechanism of asymmetric microstructure evolution related to the rippling phase at high Fe composition. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a shows the evolution of (003) peak FWHM from the in situ XRD measurement (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a) of FC for the first charge and discharge with 4.5 V cutoff (FC4.5). It is known that diffusivity is proportional to the interlayer distance of the layered structure in O3 for both charge‐localized and de‐localized systems.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0028 #adfm201803896-bib-0029"/> Therefore, when charging beyond certain voltage (3.7 V in FC) and entering the rippling phase, Na ions in the region with larger local interlayer distance (black boxed regions in Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0006"/>b) is extracted first due to higher diffusivity, followed by the Na ions in other lower spacing regions (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0006"/>c).</p><figure xml:id="adfm201803896-fig-0006"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:1616301X:media:adfm201803896:adfm201803896-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adfm201803896-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Illustration of the structural evolution of Fe containing Na layered compounds. The hysteresis evolution is hence reversible between cycles.</p><p>It is worth noting that in order for the asymmetric structure evolution to be prominent, the rippling phase has to be percolated. The (003) peak broadening in charge are also found in Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (FM) at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.2, Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (FN) at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.26, Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (FMN) at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.26 and Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> (FCN) at <i>x</i> &lt; 0.3, respectively, together with the asymmetric structural evolution in discharge, as shown in Figure S7 (Supporting Information). Also note that although the Na composition for P3 to high voltage O3 transition in FC (Figure <link href="#adfm201803896-fig-0005"/>b) and some other binary and ternary systems (FM, FN, FMN in Figure S7, Supporting Information) coincides with the Na composition for the flat to rippling phase transition in charge, this is not generally true. This battery test strategy can be explained well by the asymmetric rippling phase evolution, where the P3 phase was bypassed by the coercive rippling phase in discharge to avoid the O3‐P3‐O3 transition that is detrimental to the high rate. In addition, compared with, for example, NaCoO<sub>2</sub>, with a symmetric charge‐discharge evolution (see Figure S13, Supporting Information), FC4.0 with hysteresis voltage loop shows higher average discharge voltage (3.148 V) than NaCoO<sub>2</sub> (2.936 V) and lower over potential, consistent with previous report.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0012"/> Our understanding here thus may lead to the new strategy to engineer the structural evolution with extended rippling phase region for improved battery performance.</p></section></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0110"><label role="interim">3</label><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In conclusion, we have found a novel phenomenon of spontaneous rippling of the TM layers in the Fe containing layered NaTMO<sub>2</sub> compounds. For Fe composition higher than a certain value in a given compound, softening of FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedron accumulates with charging, which drives the whole TM layer to distort and ripple into a novel glass phase with different inhomogeneous interlayer distance distribution among the layers. The underlying microscopic rippling mechanism results in different evolution pathways between charge and discharge in high Fe composition compounds, leading to the macroscopic hysteresis voltage loops. We expect that fully exploration and utilization of the rippling phase may lead to sodium cathode materials with further improved performance.</p></section><section xml:id="adfm201803896-sec-0120"><label role="interim">4</label><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Synthesis and Battery Test</i>: The pristine NaTMO<sub>2</sub> powder samples mentioned in the paper were synthesized by the solid‐state reaction following the previous literatures.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0017 #adfm201803896-bib-0028"/> The cathode film was made by mixing powder of active materials, Super P carbon black (Timcal), and dry PTFE (DuPont) with the weight ratio of 80:15:5. A Swagelok battery was assembled using glass fiber (Whatman GF/F) as a separator, Na metal (99.95% Sigma‐Aldrich) as an anode, and 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF6 (98%, Sigma‐Aldrich) dissolved in EC:DEC (anhydrous, 1:1 volume ratio) as an electrolyte with the moisture level less than 3 ppm. The galvanostatic cycling was tested on Solartron 1470E. The cathode film loading density was around 2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>.</p><p><i>In Situ XRD</i>: The in situ lab XRD was taken on Bruker D8 X‐ray diffractometer equipped with a Mo source from a homemade in situ electrochemical cell with Be window. The in situ cell was charged galvanostatically at C/50 rate on Solartron 1470E with each XRD pattern scanned from 6.5° to 30.5° 2θ angle range (equivalent to 14.1°–69.7° on Cu source) for 1 h, corresponding to 2% Na composition resolution per pattern.</p><p><i>STEM</i>: A Swagelok battery cell was dissembled after reaching designated potentials. Cathode films were sonicated in dimethyl carbonate (DMC). The DMC solutions were then dropped onto the TEM copper grids. The TEM sample was sealed in an airtight bottle before loading into the microscope column. All the sampling processes were done in Ar‐filled glove box. The HAADF‐STEM images were taken on the Hitachi HD 2700C at an accelerating voltage of 200 kV equipped with a probe aberration corrector and cold field‐emission gun at Brookhaven National Laboratory. The collection angle for HAADF‐STEM imaging was 64–341 mrad.</p><p><i>Neutron Diffraction</i>: Neutron diffraction measurements were performed on 5 g each of pristine Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, Na[Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>, and Na[Mn<sub>0.5</sub>Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>Ni<sub>0.5</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> powder using the triple‐axis spectrometer BT‐7 at the NIST Center for Neutron Research.<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0048"/> Measurements were taken in two‐axis mode with a fixed initial neutron energy of 14.7 meV (wavelength 2.359 Å), collimator configuration open‐80'‐sample‐80', and a radial‐position sensitive detector.</p><p><i>DFT</i>: All DFT calculations in this work were performed using the Vienna Ab initio Simulation Package within the projector augmented‐wave approach using the Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof GGA functional and the GGA+U extension to it. A 520 eV plane‐wave energy cutoff was used for all calculations. The Van der Waals interaction was treated by both the Grimme's DFT‐D2<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0038"/> method and cross‐checked with the DFT‐D3 method with Becke‐Johnson damping<link href="#adfm201803896-bib-0039 #adfm201803896-bib-0040"/> on top of GGA+U.
