Realizing their real applications, however, remains a formidable challenge. One is that among the limitations of anode materials, the alloy‐type candidates tolerate fast capacity fading during cycling. Here, a 3D framework superstructure assembled with carbon nanobelt arrays decorated with a metallic bismuth (Bi) nanospheres coated carbon layer by thermolysis of Bi‐based metal–organic framework nanorods is synthesized as an anode material for SIBs. Due to the unique structural superiority, the anode design promotes excellent sodium‐storage performance in terms of high capacity, excellent cycling stability, and ultrahigh rate capability up to 80 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity of 308.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The unprecedented sodium‐storage ability is not only attributed to the unique hybrid architecture, but also to the production of a homogeneous and thin solid electrolyte interface layer and the formation of uniform porous nanostructures during cycling in the ether‐based electrolyte. Importantly, deeper understanding of the underlying cause of the performance improvement is illuminated, which is vital to provide the theoretical basis for application of SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202202673-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A novel 3D carbon framework superstructure assembled with carbon nanobelt arrays decorated with metallic bismuth (Bi) nanospheres wrapped by a carbon layer (Bi@C⊂CFs) is synthesized via thermolysis of Bi‐based metal−organic framework nanorods. xml:id="adma202202673-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the rapid development of portable electronics and smart grids, the demands for large‐scale, eco‐friendly, and safe electrochemical energy storage devices are ever‐growing.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Though lithium‐ion batteries are the predominant devices at present, the limited resource of lithium has restricted their further large‐scale applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the next‐generation candidates, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been attracting extensive attention from both academic and commercial communities in recent years because of low cost and natural abundance of sodium reserves.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Although SIBs have made some perceptible progress, their limited energy density, inferior rate capability, and poor cycling stability are prominent obstacles for practical applications.</p><p>Currently, various cathodes for SIBs have been reported, including sodium layered oxides,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Prussian blue analogue,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and polyanionic compounds.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The major challenge is largely the short of paired anode materials able to offer moderate voltage platform, high capacity, significant rate performance, and long‐term cycling life all together.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The moderate voltage platform and high capacity are connected with the elemental composition while the rate performance and cycling stability mainly depend on electronic conductivity and structural stability of materials. Alloy type materials are promising anode candidates due to their suitable redox reaction potential as well as higher energy density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, bismuth (Bi) has attracted considerable attention as one of the representative promising anodes because of its numerous advantages for sodium storage, including high electronic/ionic conductivity, the appropriate reaction potential, and theoretical specific capacity (386 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Be that as it may, with the volume change (≈244%) caused by the process of Bi to Na<sub>3</sub>Bi alloy, the contact between the active material and the collector is lost, which causes unstable solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) and poor capacity maintenance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Enormous attention has been devoted to relieving the volume expansion of Bi during charge/discharge by using kinds of design strategies, such as coupling with glyme‐based electrolytes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> designing nanostructures<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and incorporating Bi nanospheres into carbonaceous materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0003 #adma202202673-bib-0013"><sup>3b,13</sup></link><sup>−</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Xu and co‐workers reported a direct annealing strategy for synthesizing robust Bi nanoparticles@carbon composites for efficient sodium storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Yu et al. has also optimized Bi@Void@C nanospheres with yolk–shell structure by a template‐based method as advanced electrode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the fundamentals for the selection of the preferred ether‐based electrolyte are not carefully explained, and both the capacity and rate performance of the anode material released can also be further optimized. Therefore, in order to achieve excellent electrochemical properties of Bi‐based anode materials, we should not only design reasonable composite assembly structure, but also understand the effects of matching different electrolytes, which is crucial to realizing the long cycle and high rate capability of SIBs.</p><p>Since metal sites are atomically distributed in metal–organic frameworks (MOFs), well‐dispersed metallic nanoparticles loaded on carbon materials can be produced from MOFs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Herein, we first synthesized a 3D porous hard carbon framework superstructure assembled with 2D ultrathin carbon nanobelt arrays including some metallic Bi nanosphere coated carbon layers (denoted as Bi@C⊂CFs) by a one‐step thermolysis of 1D MOF nanorods. The formation process of superstructure is also investigated detailedly. Criss‐cross carbon frameworks derived from organic ligands can not only have high conductivity and allow for fast electron transport, but also have high porosity and large surface area conducive to full penetration of the electrolyte. In addition, we deeply investigate the structural evolution of Bi@C⊂CFs during the alloy/dealloy reactions by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and density functional theory (DFT) calculations. It thus provides a comprehensive understanding of Bi‐based electrodes in kinetic optimization, electron transfer acceleration, and mechanical stabilization during charge and discharge. The as‐formed nanoporous structures during repeated charge and discharge show the notable stability to accommodate the large volume changes during the cycles. Moreover, the detailed Na storage mechanism is further revealed by operating the in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD). Furthermore, a series of high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM), X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) characterization as well as DFT calculations reveal the chemical composition and structure of ethylene glycol dimethyl ether (DME)‐derived SEI film. Thanks to the strong porous and stable SEI film, Bi@C⊂CFs as an anode material for SIBs demonstrates superior long‐term cycle stability (305 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and ultrahigh rate capability (308.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at 80 A g<sup>−1</sup>). When paired with the organic cathode, the full cell (denoted as Bi@C⊂CFs//PTCDA@450) achieved a high capacity of over 272 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. These studies contribute to advancing the theoretical understanding and practical application of Bi‐based materials in SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>Using bismuth nitrate pentahydrate and trimesic acid (H<sub>3</sub>BTC) as the raw reagents, the rod‐shaped Bi‐MOF‐Rod samples were first synthesized in a dimethylformamide and methanol‐involved solvothermal system and had a representative 3D framework structure, where each Bi<sup>3+</sup> cation is coordinated with six oxygen atoms from H<sub>3</sub>BTC (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting information)<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup>. Based on FESEM and TEM analysis, the product indicates rod‐like structure (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S2a–c</link>, Supporting Information). Powder XRD pattern confirms the well‐crystallized Bi‐MOF‐Rods (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S2d</link>, Supporting Information). After calcination in Ar atmosphere at 600 °C, the 1D Bi‐MOF‐Rods directly cracked into 3D superstructures consisting of 2D ultrathin carbon nanobelt arrays and metallic Bi@C core–shell nanospheres anchored on them (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>). The crystal structure of the Bi@C⊂CFs hybrid was confirmed by XRD pattern (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S3a</link>, Supporting Information) and all of the diffraction peaks can be assigned to rhombohedral phase Bi (JCPDS Card No. 85–1329). Morphology and detailed microstructure of Bi@C⊂CFs were studied by FESEM and TEM. From the low‐magnification FESEM image in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1b</link>, the as‐obtained products are composed of 1D rod‐like superstructures. Higher magnification FESEM images further display such superstructures are assembled by ultrathin nanobelt arrays decorated with Bi nanospheres (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1c,d</link>). Clearly, the thickness of nanobelts is only several nanometers. TEM image further reveals the microstructure of the Bi@C⊂CFs composite, consistent with the FESEM observations (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1e</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S3b–d</link>, Supporting Information). As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S3b–d</link>, Supporting Information, the width of carbon nanobelts and the average diameter of Bi nanospheres are about 20 and 140 nm, respectively. HRTEM analyses clearly indicate Bi nanosphere coated with about 2 nm thick carbon layer and then inserted into the disordered lattice carbon nanobelt array frame, found as hard carbon by analyzing the disorder of graphitic domains (Figures <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1f,g</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In subsequent reactions, the double carbon layers around the Bi nanospheres can not only promote electron/ion transfer and improve the electron conductivity, but also provide an effective buffer space for the Bi nanospheres to alleviate volume expansion during the sodiation/desodiation process. The high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning TEM (HAADF‐STEM) technique was employed to further scrutinize the Bi@C⊂CFs composite (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1h,i</link>), which is consistent with the observation of FESEM/TEM images. In atomic‐resolution HAADF images in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1j</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information, the lattice fringes with an interlayer distance of 0.324 nm correspond to the (012) crystal planes of the metallic bismuth. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0001">1k–m</link>, STEM and the corresponding energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectrum (EDX) elemental mappings unambiguously identify the homogeneous distribution of Bi and C in the Bi@C⊂CFs composite.</p><figure xml:id="adma202202673-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202202673:adma202202673-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202202673-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the synthesis of Bi@C⊂CFs composite. b–d) FESEM, e,f) TEM, g) HRTEM, h–j) HAADF‐STEM images, and k–m) STEM‐EDX mappings of Bi@C⊂CFs composite.</caption></figure><p>XPS was implemented to explore the surface characteristics of the Bi@C⊂CFs composite, where only Bi, C, and O elements are observed (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S6a</link>, Supporting Information). Peaks at 164.2 and 158.9 eV in the Bi 4f spectrum can be assigned to Bi 4f<sub>5/2</sub> and Bi 4f<sub>7/2</sub>, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S6b</link>, Supporting Information). The peaks with the binding energy of 289.5, 285.8, and 284.8 eV found in the high‐resolution C 1s spectrum are assigned to the OC, CO, and CC/CC bonds, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S6c</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Peaks of 533.7, 531.5, and 529.8 eV in the O1s spectrum come from the CO, COBi, and BiO bonds, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S6d</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The interaction between Bi, O, and C can stabilize the Bi nanospheres, ensuring the structural stability of the composites. Due to the exposure to the air during the sample preparation, the oxidation can only occur on the surface of Bi. Raman spectrum of Bi@C⊂CFs shows two typical peaks at ≈1322 and 1586 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which can be attributed to D and G bands of carbon, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S7a</link>, Supporting Information). The intensity ratio of <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> is 1.11, reflecting abundant defects and disorders in carbon frameworks which could afford numerous electrochemical active sites and increase the adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup> and accelerate surface pseudocapacitance behavior, befitting Na storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The content of Bi in the composite is calculated to be 79.8% by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) measurement (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S7b</link>, Supporting Information). To precisely check the porosity textures of Bi@C⊂CFs and pure Bi as the control, nitrogen adsorption–desorption technique was carried out.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Bi@C⊂CFs composite displays a typical mesoporous structure with a with a specific surface area (SSA) of 76.28 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and pore size centered at 10 nm. For the sample of pure Bi, the surface area is 5.20 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> with a pore size mostly less than 3 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S7c,d</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>To understand the formation process of the 3D superstructure, a detailed analysis of the morphological and phase evolution of 1D Bi‐MOF‐Rod during calcination was performed. During pyrolysis, Bi‐MOF‐Rod first deactivated the adsorbed water and object molecules at a relatively low temperature (&lt;200 °C), and then began to decompose with increasing temperature (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S8</link>, supporting Information), with the collapse of the organic ligand structure, which facilitated the formation of a hard carbon framework. The transformation process can be observed by Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) spectra (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information). All peaks gradually flatten with temperature increasing, corresponding to the decomposition of organic ligands and the transformation into belt‐like carbon nanoarrays with small Bi nanospheres immobilized in the superstructures. Combined with the observations of TGA and FTIR, the morphology evolution was monitored at 350 °C at different reaction times. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a,b</link>, after pyrolysis for 5 min at 350 °C, the morphology of the Bi‐MOF‐Rod did not change substantially, but its surface began to become rough and generate the lamella‐like structure (indicated by white arrows in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0002">2a,b</link>) because the coordination bonds between Bi<sup>3+</sup> and organic ligands were broken. After 10 min (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0002">2c</link>), the pore interconnective belt‐like arrays are formed at the end of Bi‐MOF‐Rod but not peel off. Typical FESEM image collected at 20 min indicates that some spherical nanoparticles emerged on the surface of the belt‐like arrays, resulting from the reduction of Bi<sup>3+</sup> into metallic Bi nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0002">2d</link>). When the calcination time increased to 30 or 60 min, 3D nanobelt arrays are produced through the thermal cracking of MOF nanorod (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0002">2e,f</link>). Clearly, through further calcination, more nanospheres produced via Bi atom agglomeration can be observed to distribute on the surface of the carbon nanobelt skeleton and inside the frame (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0002">2g,h</link>). By analyzing the morphology at different durations, it is not difficult to see that with the increase of calcination time, the ligand frameworks in MOF decomposed and then Bi<sup>3+</sup> ions were reduced into metal nanoparticles embedded in carbon nanobelt array frame to obtain Bi@C⊂CFs superstructure. It should be mentioned that carbon nanobelt arrays are directly obtained through the thermal cracking of bulky MOFs, and no additives are employed in the process of transformation, which is different from the synthesis of carbon‐based 1D or 2D micro/nanostructures from MOFs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="adma202202673-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202202673:adma202202673-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202202673-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–h) FESEM images of intermediates by calcining Bi‐MOF‐Rod precursor at 350 °C under an argon flow for 5 min (a,b), 10 min (c), 20 min (d), 30 min (e), 60 min (f), and 2 h (g,h).</caption></figure><p>The successful synthesis of Bi@C⊂CFs as SIBs anode material is confirmed from the superior electrochemical behaviors (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>). All batteries used for cycle testing were pre‐circulated for two cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3a</link> shows the representative cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of Bi@C⊂CFs at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The cathodic peaks in the first cycle at around 1.1 and 0.15 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na originate from the irreversible reactions of the electrolyte decomposition. Two cathodic peaks at 0.38 and 0.58 V are assigned to the stepwise alloy reactions from Bi to NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, respectively. Accordingly, the anodic peaks are associated with the dealloy reactions from Na<sub>3</sub>Bi to NaBi and further to Bi. After the first charge and discharge capacity loss, the redox peaks become sharper and stronger, showing good electrochemical reversibility and cycling stability. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3b</link>, the discharge curves at the current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> exhibit obvious plateaus at 0.68 and 0.49 V corresponding to Bi → NaBi → Na<sub>3</sub>Bi while plateaus at 0.59 and 0.75 V in the charge curves correspond to the reversible reaction of the anode, which are highly consistent with the CV curves.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The initial discharge capacity and charge capacity were 663.3 and 347.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and the initial Coulomb efficiency (CE) was 52.4%. The capacity loss was mainly caused by the irreversible formation of SEI film and partial irreversible sodiation/desodiation process in the first cycle.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The CE in the following cycles remained above 99%, demonstrating a highly reversible and stable cycling performance. The rate capability was further studied, and as expected, the Bi@C⊂CFs electrode provided the best rate property (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3c</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S10</link>, Supporting Information). As the current densities increase from 0.2 to 64 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge capacity hardly fluctuates and remains stable at 323.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Even though the current density continues to increase to 80 A g<sup>−1</sup>, it can still deliver the ultrahigh capacity of 308.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and only takes ≈15 s to complete a full charge or discharge. Moreover, the two notable voltage plateaus still persist at 80 A g<sup>−1</sup>, suggesting the low polarization. It is obvious that Bi@C⊂CFs electrode offers ultrahigh stability and robustness even at extremely high current, which is of significance for the practical application. The rate capability of Bi@C⊂CFs is much better than that of Bi‐based anodes previously reported (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3d</link> and Table <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0009 #adma202202673-bib-0012 #adma202202673-bib-0014 #adma202202673-bib-0015 #adma202202673-bib-0026"><sup>9,12,14,15,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Even after 1000 cycles, a reversible capacity of 325 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be achieved for Bi@C⊂CFs at a high current density of 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3e</link>). The CE of the electrode was stayed at around 100% except for the first few cycles. Such superior stability, excellent reversibility, and high CE all demonstrate that the Bi@C⊂CFs composite is a promising anode material for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to further verify the sustainability of Bi@C⊂CFs, the ultralong cycle test under high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> was carried out. A high reversible capacity of 305 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with 89.4% retention was delivered after 5000 cycles, demonstrating the fast Na<sup>+</sup> reaction kinetics and superior cycling stability (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3g</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S11</link>, Supporting Information). Even under the high load of 4.6 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, the Bi@C⊂CFs composite can still obtain good cycle performance, and the specific capacity of 325 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles under 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S12a,b</link>, Supporting Information). When the high loading is 4.42 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>, and even the current rate reaches 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, it still has a discharge capacity of 194.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S12c,d</link>, Supporting Information). We synthesized the pure Bi sample for the comparison according to the method previously reported.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The phase of pure Bi is the same as that of the Bi@C⊂CFs composite (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S13a</link>, Supporting Information), and the inset is the corresponding FESEM image. The electrochemical performance of pure Bi was tested under the same conditions as the Bi@C⊂CFs. The similar reversible reaction occurred through the observation of CV and charge–discharge curve (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S13b,c</link>, Supporting Information). Clearly, pure Bi nanoparticle has very low initial CE (47.8%) and the low capacity (269.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles, Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S13d</link>, Supporting Information), illustrating the superiority of the Bi@C⊂CFs composite. Moreover, the results of the rate test under the same requirement also demonstrated a lower capacity retention rate (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0003">3f</link>). The galvanostatic charge/discharge curves and rate performance for pure Bi are shown in Figures <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S13e</link> and <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S13f</link>, Supporting Information, respectively. When cycled at the current rate of 80 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the discharge capacity of pure Bi degrades to only 155.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. The improved performance of the Bi@C⊂CFs composite is pertaining to the dual carbon protection layers derived from the MOF structure, which not only guarantee the rapid kinetics, but also well suppress the strain caused by volume expansion.</p><figure xml:id="adma202202673-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202202673:adma202202673-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202202673-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical performance of the Bi@C⊂CFs electrode. a) CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.01 and 1.5 V. b) Charge/discharge profiles at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Rate performance. d) Rate capability comparison with reported Bi‐based anode materials for SIBs. e,g) Cycling performance and the corresponding CE at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (e) and 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (g). f) Rate capability comparison between the pure Bi and Bi@C⊂CFs electrodes.</caption></figure><p>To deeply understand the reasons why Bi@C⊂CFs has excellent performance, the electrodes were tested at different scan rates from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S14a</link>, Supporting Information). The relationship of peak current (<i>i</i>) to scan rate (<i>v</i>) was quantitatively analyzed based on <i>i</i> = <i>av<sup>b</sup></i>, and the values of <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> can be fitted with the slope of the log (<i>i</i>)–log (<i>v</i>) plot. According to previous reports, when <i>b</i> is close to 0.5, it is a diffusion control process and when <i>b</i> is close to 1, it is a surface capacitance control process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S14b</link>, Supporting Information, the fitting shows that the <i>b</i> values are 0.90 and 0.68 for two cathodic peaks and 0.71 and 0.84 for the anode peaks, indicating a mixed mechanism. By fitting the CV curve at a specific scan rate (10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>), the shaded region represents the capacitance contribution, which gradually increases, eventually reaching 90.24% at 10.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S14c,d</link>, Supporting Information). Such a high capacitance contribution ratio strongly favors the rate properties of Bi@C⊂CFs electrodes, especially at high rates.</p><p>The excellent rate performance and cycling stability of the Bi@C⊂CFs anode should also be caused by the unique morphology and structural evolution during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0011 #adma202202673-bib-0014"><sup>11,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> By comparing the original electrode (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S15</link>, Supporting Information) with that after one cycle (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>), it is not difficult to find a layer of SEI on the surface of the material after the first cycle. As the cycle proceeds, the SEI on the electrode surface becomes more homogeneous and dispersed (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4b,c</link>). After 100 cycles, the surface of the electrode formed a uniformly porous nanostructure (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4d</link>). This morphological evolution of the Bi@C⊂CFs electrode is schematically shown in the Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4e</link>. In Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S17</link>, Supporting Information, the surface of the electrode is observed to be covered by a dense SEI layer after repeated charge and discharge, which not only consumes much electrolyte, but also hinders the diffusion of ions throughout the active material, resulting in a rapid decrease in capacity. In the DME‐based electrolyte, the formed nanosize porous structure is crucial for the excellent capacity retention, providing not only a shorter diffusion distance for Na ions, but also a larger electrode–electrolyte contact interface. Second, the formation of nanosize porous structures reduces the structural strain and volume deformation induced by repeated Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction processes, thereby improving cycle stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Next, the effect of DME‐based electrolyte on the formation of porous structure was investigated by DFT.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0011 #adma202202673-bib-0029"><sup>11,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The adsorption energy of the DME molecule on the bismuth substrate (012) crystal surface was calculated to be 0.56 eV (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4f</link>). As the cycle proceeds, the Bi nanospheres gradually burst under the strong adsorption energy between the DME molecules and the Bi substrate (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information), and the ruptured particles gradually form a stable porous structure, consistent with the previously observed results of the FESEM images. In order to harvest more information about the kinetic process, Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption on pure Bi and Bi@C⊂CFs is simulated, revealing the advantages of the designed structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Given the complexity of the actual double carbon layer coated Bi nanospheres, graphene monolayer is applied to represent carbon layer for a simplified model. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4g,h</link>, the addition of graphene leads to the improvement of conductivity and the redistribution of the electron density around the Na atoms. The diagrams of sodium migration path on the Bi/graphene interface and Bi surface are exhibited in the side and top views (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4i</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S20</link>, Supporting Information).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Finally, based on the calculation results in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0004">4j</link>, it can be found that the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> on Bi/graphene surface is more inclined to occur than on the pure Bi surface, showing the promotive role of graphene.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> By designing a reasonable structure, it can prevent the aggregation of sodium ions during the cycle and accelerate the reaction process, thus having the ability of long cycle and high rate.</p><p>In addition, the chemical composition of SEI formed in different electrolytes was analyzed. In the DME‐based electrolyte, SEI was uniform and thin (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>); however, in EC/DEC‐based electrolyte, SEI was uneven and thicker in the 20th cycle (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005">5e</link>). In contrast, the uniform and thin SEI films exhibited stable cycling performance, while the uneven and thick SEI gradually consumed electrolyte during the cycling process, thus affecting battery performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The components were analyzed for the above two SEI layers by ex situ XPS. In the DME‐based electrolyte, analyses of the C 1s and O 1s spectra in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005">5b,c</link> reveal that the main components of SEI layer are sodium alkoxides (RCH<sub>2</sub>ONa) and polyethers and that two distinct peaks in the F 1s spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005">5d</link>) are assigned to NaF and PF. In contrast, in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte, the main components of the SEI layer are sodium alkylcarbonates (ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na) and polyester (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005">5f–h</link>). The ROCO<sub>2</sub>Na has been reported to be destabilized and further decomposed into RCH<sub>2</sub>ONa and Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, which causes the formation of less stable SEI layer in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0011 #adma202202673-bib-0029 #adma202202673-bib-0030"><sup>11,29,30a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This follows that the uniform and thin SEI layer formed in the DME‐based electrolyte is more stable and also can guarantee the fast transmission of Na<sup>+</sup> across the interface and the structural stability of the Bi electrodes, thus providing good rate performance and cycle stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Considering that the reduction reaction during electrolyte decomposition is strongly associated with the lowest unoccupied molecular orbit (LUMO) levels, to obtain intrinsic differences in the two electrolyte, we calculated the highest occupied molecular orbit (HOMO) and LUMO levels in the electrolyte, based on the DFT and using an SMD implicit solvation model.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is calculated that the LUMO level (−0.79 eV) of sodium hexafluorophosphate (NaPF<sub>6</sub>) is much lower than DME (2.31 eV) in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005">5i</link>, indicating that NaPF<sub>6</sub> has a higher reductibility than DME so that the NaPF<sub>6</sub> decomposes earlier than the DME throughout the discharge process, forming an inorganic‐rich interphase layer before forming organic layer. In contrast, NaPF<sub>6</sub> (−0.66 eV), EC (0.94 eV), and DEC (1.27 eV) have close LUMO levels in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-fig-0005">5j</link>, leading to the decomposition of the three molecules almost simultaneously to produce mixed SEI layers of organic and inorganic fractions. Inorganic‐rich SEI layers are known to be more dense and sustainable than hybrid layers. The former can effectively protect the further breakdown of the electrolyte in the system, while the latter is easy to break and the liquid electrolyte can penetrate, leading to the continuous growth of the SEI layer, slow kinetics, and a rapid decline in capacity during cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> At the same time, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was applied to test the resistance of electrochemistry. On the basis of the equivalent circuit diagram in Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S21</link>, Supporting Information, after the first cycle, the value of charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte was 37.6 Ω and only 14.16 Ω in the DME‐based electrolyte. With the cycle number increase, the value of <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> in the DME‐based electrolyte gradually decreased; instead the <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> value in the EC/DEC electrolyte increased (Figure <link href="#adma202202673-supitem-0001">S22</link>, Supporting Information). This suggests that the excellent electrochemical reaction kinetics of Bi@C⊂CFs is due to the rapid ion diffusion channels and shorter transmission paths brought about by the formation of stable and porous SEI films.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202202673-bib-0030 #adma202202673-bib-0034"><sup>30a,34</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="adma202202673-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202202673:adma202202673-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202202673-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) HRTEM images of the Bi@C⊂CFs electrodes after 20 cycles in DME‐based (a) and EC/DEC‐based (b) electrolytes. b–d) High‐resolution XPS spectra of O 1s (b), C 1s (c), and F 1s (d) for Bi@C⊂CFs in the DME‐based electrolyte after cycles. f–h) High‐resolution XPS spectra of O 1s (f), C 1s (g), and F 1s (h) for Bi@C⊂CFs in the EC/DEC‐based electrolyte after cycles. i,j) HOMO and LUMO energy levels of solvent and salt: i) NaPF<sub>6</sub> and DME in DME electrolyte, j) NaPF<sub>6</sub>, EC, and DEC in EC/DEC electrolyte. Clearly, this stage is related to the desodiation of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi to NaBi. The excellent performance of the full battery undoubtedly proves that the Bi@C⊂CFs composite can be used as a reliable anode sodium‐storage material.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>We have demonstrated a well‐designed MOF‐engaged strategy for the synthesis of a 3D framework superstructure assembled with carbon nanobelt arrays decorated with carbon layer coated metallic Bi nanospheres (Bi@C⊂CFs) for efficient sodium storage. The sodium‐storage behaviors were scrutinized systematically for more understanding. We found that, during the charge/discharge process, the surface of the active material produces a uniform and thin SEI, and the surface of the electrode gradually forms a nanoporous structure, enhancing the utilization of active materials and shortening the ion/electrons diffusion length. In situ XRD analyses confirmed the sodium‐storage mechanism for Bi@C⊂CFs composite. A series of characterization detection and theoretical calculation unveil the fundamentals underlying the DME‐based electrolyte improving the sodium storage of Bi@C⊂CFs over EC/DEC‐based electrolyte. Moreover, ex situ HRTEM and XPS help analyze the thickness and chemical composition of SEI layer. The Bi@C⊂CFs composite manifested excellent sodium‐storage performance, including high reversible capacities of 325 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 1000 cycles, 305 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 5000 cycles, and an ultrahigh rate capability up to 80 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity of 308.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, the full cell by pairing Bi@C⊂CFs with an organic cathode delivered stable cycling performance, demonstrating its potential in practical large‐scale applications. This work provides an easy way to develop anode materials and provides useful insights into the application of Bi‐based composites in high‐performance SIB systems.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors gratefully acknowledge the financial supports provided by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (U21A2077, 21871164), the Taishan Scholar Project Foundation of Shandong Province (ts20190908), the Natural Science Foundation of Shandong Province (ZR2021ZD05, ZR2019MB024), and Anhui Kemi Machinery Technology Co., Ltd. for providing a Teflon‐lined stainless steel autoclave.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0050" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0060" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202202673-sec-0070" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>Research data are not shared.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202202673-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202202673-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202202673-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>J.
