xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Carbonaceous materials are considered strong candidates as anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), which are expected to play an indispensable role in the carbon‐neutral era. Herein, novel braided porous carbon fibres (BPCFs) are prepared using the chemical vapour deposition (CVD) method. The BPCFs possess interwoven porous structures and abundant vacancies. The growth mechanism of the BPCFs can be attributed to the polycrystalline transformation of the nanoporous copper catalyst in the early stage of CVD process. Density functional theory calculations suggest that the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energies of the mono‐vacancy edges of the BPCFs (−1.22 and −1.09 eV) are lower than that of an ideal graphene layer (−0.68 eV), clarifying in detail the adsorption‐dominated sodium storage mechanism. Hence, the BPCFs as an anode material present an outstanding discharge capacity of 401 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g−1 after 500 cycles. Remarkably, this BPCFs anode, under high‐mass‐loading of 5 mg cm−2, shows excellent long‐term cycling ability with a reversible capacity of 201 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles. This study provided a novel strategy for the development of high‐performance carbonaceous materials for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs3907-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Braided porous carbon fibres (BPCFs) present interwoven structures and abundant vacancies, originating from high‐density carbon “fiber seeds” induced by high‐density catalytic activity (111) planes. The BPCFs anode shows excellent cycling ability with a reversible capacity of 201 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3907:advs3907-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3907-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), one of the alternatives to lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), have promising commercial application prospects owing to abundant sodium resources and the similarity of their working mechanism to that of LIBs. However, the large sodium ion radius limits further improvement in the barrier performance, posing a challenge in designing novel electrode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0002"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Currently, carbonaceous materials<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0003"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> such as hard carbon,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0004"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> soft carbon, carbon nanofibers (CNFs),<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0005"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and graphite<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0006"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> are promising anode materials for SIBs because of their excellent electrical conductivity, low cost, and environmental friendliness.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0007"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Although these carbonaceous anode materials have achieved remarkable results in recent years,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0008"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> there still exist difficulties in their practical applications, such as layer spacing modulation,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0009"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> micro‐nanostructure design,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0010"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and a high commercialization cost. The sodium storage mechanism of carbonaceous materials has been generally recognized to involve the following: 1) a high‐potential sloping region capacity corresponding to Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption and intercalation<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0011"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and 2) a low‐voltage plateau region related to pore filling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0012"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Thus, construction of appropriate defects and interlayer distances can remarkably improve the sodium storage capacity of carbonaceous materials.</p><p>Among carbonaceous materials, one dimensional CNFs (1D CNFs) with a large specific surface area and porosity present excellent sodium storage performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0013"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Chemical vapor deposition (CVD) is a simple, low‐cost, controllable, and adjustable method for fabricating 1D carbon nanomaterials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0014"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The catalytic growth mechanism of CNFs mainly includes a “tip‐based model” and a “base growth model.”<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0015"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Particularly, the types and shapes of catalyst particles significantly affect the morphologies and structures of CNFs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0016"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Yang et al. reported that the preparation of CNFs can be controlled by modulating the morphology of copper nanoparticles as well as the adsorption of C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub> on Cu (111).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0017"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Additionally, there are many studies on methods to improve the sodium storage capacity of CNFs. Wen et al. reported an excellent high capacity of 148 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> for nitrogen‐doped CNFs with interwoven nanochannels.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0010"><sup>9a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Pint group reported that defect‐containing helical CNFs on 3D foams can present a sodium storage capacity exceeding 280 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a moderate rate of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> with a stable cycling performance over 200 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0018"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the mechanism of high‐efficiency sodium storage based on the catalytic growth model of CNFs is still inconclusive.</p><p>In this study, braided porous carbon fibers (BPCFs) with dense vacancies and interwoven structures were synthesized by a one‐step in situ catalytic CVD method. The high‐density catalytically active (111) planes on the surface of a nanoporous multicrystal copper were found to preferentially adsorb carbon (C) atoms, forming “fiber seeds.” As the fibers grow, adjacent “fiber seeds” intertwine and form the framework for BPCFs. Concurrently, high‐density vacancies are formed during the process of carbon “fiber seeds” winding. The deposited carbon yield was evaluated as 140% after growth for 60 min. Consequently, the BPCFs anode presents a high specific capacity of 401 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 500 cycles. Meanwhile, the BPCFs anode shows remarkable cycling stability and the capacity remained 201 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles with a high‐mass‐loading of 5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> at a high current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> used in SIBs. Based on the developed understanding of the growth mechanism of the CNFs, this novel strategy for regulating sodium storage offers a route for designing high‐performance carbonaceous sodium storage materials.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3907-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link> shows a schematic of the fabrication process of the nanoporous multicrystalline copper catalyst used in this study. A 3D nanoporous copper (3D NPC) with a 40 nm uniform ligament was obtained by chemical corrosion of Cu<sub>30</sub>Mn<sub>70</sub> (at%) alloy in HCl solution (0.025 mol L<sup>−1</sup>). This process has many advantages such as easy preparation, low cost, and good feasibility (see Figures <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S1–S3</link>, Supporting Information). The composition of the NPC was measured to be ≈92.41 at% Cu, ≈5.24 at% O, and ≈2.35 at% Mn, by energy dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (Figures <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S1</link> and <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S2</link>, and Table <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). The presence of a small amount of Mn suggests that the nanoporous structure is mainly formed by the selective leaching of Mn from the Cu<sub>30</sub>Mn<sub>70</sub> alloy, and the appearance of O reflects a slight oxidation of the 3D NPC. Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0001">1a</link> shows the presence of nanocopper ligaments in single‐crystal grains. The corresponding selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern further shown in the upper right inset in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0001">1a</link> verifies this characteristic. However, the high growth temperature (600 ℃) causes coarsening of the copper ligaments. The coarsened copper ligaments exhibit a multicrystal structure, which is confirmed by scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and SAED (Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0001">1b</link>). This structure is expected to expose additional high‐catalytic activity (111) planes on each ligament during the growth process of the CNFs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0019"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="advs3907-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3907:advs3907-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3907-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SEM images and schematic of a) 3D NPC and b) nanoporous multicrystal copper after sintering.</caption></figure><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> illustrates the growth process of the BPCFs. As a catalyst, the synthesized nanoporous multicrystal copper with ligament sizes of 250–300 nm exposes high‐density catalytically active crystal planes. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002">2b</link>, after 10 min of growth, carbon “fiber seeds” are uniformly dispersed on the surfaces of the catalyst particles, indicating that the “fiber seeds” prefer to grow on the high‐catalytic activity (111) planes. Concurrently, Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S4a</link> (Supporting Information) shows the crystalline structure of the initial growth state of carbon “fiber seeds” located on the surfaces of the nanoporous multicrystal copper catalyst particles. The difference in the contrast suggests that the carbon source gas presents selectivity for different catalytically active crystal planes of the nanoporous multicrystal copper catalyst particles during the adsorption process, among which, the (111) plane shows high catalytic activity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0017"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, the high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (TEM) (HRTEM) image of the nanoporous multicrystal copper particles in Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S4</link> (Supporting Information) further verifies that dense crystal planes are uniformly distributed on their surfaces. The carbon “fiber seeds” have longitudinal and intertwined structures, which become the framework of the BPCFs with the increase in the growth time (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). This process creates a porous structure and introduces abundant vacancy defects during the growth of the CNFs. In addition, we find that the basic frame of the BPCFs is formed after 30 min of growth, and BPCFs with an average diameter of 300 nm are obtained after 60 min, as shown in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002">2c,d</link>, respectively. A magnified SEM image of the BPCFs in Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S6</link> (Supporting Information) shows that the above‐mentioned porous structure is formed on the surfaces and in the interior of the CNFs, further confirming the characteristics of an interwoven growth (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). This 3D porous network structure can increase the specific surface area, providing active sites for Na<sup>+</sup>. Simultaneously, the 3D porous network can efficiently reduce the transport distance of ions/electrons and promote the buffering capacity during the sodiation/desodiation process. Uniform BPCFs with lengths of several micrometres are interconnected, which can be beneficial for electron conductivity (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S6b</link>, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002">2e</link> shows that a thin layer of amorphous carbon is formed on the surfaces of the BPCFs, which can produce many defects, which provide numerous adsorption sites for Na<sup>+</sup>. The HRTEM images of BPCFs shown in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002">2f</link> with different contrasts further demonstrate the presence of abundant nanoscale porous structures on the surface and in the interior of the BPCFs. These 3D interconnected porous networks can be used as reservoirs for the electrolyte, contributing to efficient Na<sup>+</sup> transport. The above‐mentioned structural characteristics are consistent with the SEM characterization results. Furthermore, the formed porous structures can induce disorder in the CNFs. The HRTEM images also suggest that the BPCFs mainly contain randomly amorphous carbon and partially crystalline carbon, as also shown in the SAED pattern (upper right inset in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002">2g</link>). The spacing between the interlayers is ≈0.387 nm, which can provide large intercalation spaces to accommodate Na<sup>+</sup> and reduce the kinetic energy consumption. Moreover, the diameter of the BPCFs having a homogeneous texture is consistent with that of the NPC, suggesting that the catalyst particles affect not only the microstructure but also the macroscopic characteristics of the carbon product (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information). Concurrently, the color of the surface of the catalytic substrate changes from metallic lustre to carbon black, suggesting the formation of a carbon product after addition of the carbon source gas for 60 min at 600 ℃ (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). The yield of the deposited carbon reaches a maximum of 140% with a growth time of 60 min (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S9</link>, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="advs3907-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3907:advs3907-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3907-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic of growth process of BPCFs. SEM image of carbon fibres after b) 10 min, c) 30 min, and d) 60 min. e,f) TEM images of BPCFs at different magnifications, g) HRTEM image of BPCFs (insets: SAED pattern).</caption></figure><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3907-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link> shows the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern of the as‐prepared BPCFs. Two remarkable broad diffraction peaks are observable, which correspond to the (002) and (100) planes of graphite, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0020"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Their broadness suggests the amorphous properties of the BPCFs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0021"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Raman spectrum of the BPCFs is shown in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0003">3b</link>. Two distinct characteristic peaks are observed at ≈1340 and ≈1580 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which are associated with the D and G bands of graphite, respectively. The D band indicates the defects and disorder degree of carbon, and the G band is the result of <i>sp</i><sup>2</sup> stretching. The quality of carbon can be determined by comparing the <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> ratio. For the BPCFs, the <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> ratio is ≈0.95, suggesting poor crystallinity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0022"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This characteristic can provide abundant vacancy defects for the adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0023"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="advs3907-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3907:advs3907-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3907-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD pattern and b) Raman spectrum of BPCFs. c) Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms of BPCFs (inset shows pore size distribution). d) High‐resolution XPS C1s spectrum of BPCFs.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0003">3c</link> shows the typical N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms and pore size distribution of the BPCFs. The isotherms from the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) analysis are typical type‐IV curves with a hysteresis loop in the readsorption branch, suggesting that the BPCFs contain numerous mesopores.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0024"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The BPCFs offer a large specific surface area of ≈372 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating that the sample can provide sufficient number of active sites for Na<sup>+</sup>. Furthermore, the pore size distribution of the BPCFs is principally in the range of ≈2–5 nm, and a large part of the mesopores can offer rapid transmission channels for the electrolyte and ions/electrons. To further explain the characteristics of the BPCFs, we obtained the data of X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). As shown in Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S10</link> (Supporting Information), there are two distinct peaks at ≈285 and 532 eV corresponding to the C1s and O1s peaks, respectively, suggesting that the BPCFs contain both C and O elements. Fitting of the C1s peak yields four energy peaks located at 284.76, 285.1, 286.6, and 287.3 eV, corresponding to C‐sp<sup>2</sup>, C‐sp<sup>3</sup>, CO, and C═O, as shown in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0003">3d</link>. The appearance of the C‐sp<sup>3</sup> peak indicates the presence of defects in the sample.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0025"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high percentage of C‐sp<sup>3</sup> supports the formation of numerous vacancy defects (Table <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). The entire preparation of BPCFs does not involve oxygen or oxygen‐containing compounds. Therefore, the small number of oxygen‐containing groups probably originates from oxygen adsorption during the transfer of the sample, which could react with sodium to form some irreversible compounds (a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI)) during the initial discharge process.</p><p>The electrochemical performance of the BPCFs was evaluated by cyclic voltammetry (CV), galvanostatic charging–discharging, and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3907-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link> shows the CV curves of the BPCFs obtained with a scan rate of 0.1 mv s<sup>−1</sup>. In the first discharge cycle, the distinct irreversible peaks at 1.17 V can be assigned to the formation of an SEI film, which can cause partial sodium ion consumption and electrolyte degradation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0026"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The reduction peak at 0.4 V is attributed to the insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> into the electrode material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0027"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the following cycles, the curves almost overlap, suggesting the formation of a stable SEI film. Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0004">4b</link> presents the first three discharge/charge voltage profiles of the BPCFs at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In the first discharge curve, discharge voltage plateaus from 1.3 to 0.9 V and from 0.7 to 0.3 V are observed, which correspond to the irreversible peaks in the CV curve. Furthermore, in the following cycles, the degrees of overlap in the discharge/charge voltage profiles and the CV curves are similar, which shows that the BPCF electrode has excellent reversibility. The reversible discharge capacity of the BPCFs as an electrode material at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> maintains excellent cyclic stability during the charging and discharging cycles (Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0004">4c</link>). The Coulombic efficiency approaches 99% after 100 cycles. The specific capacitance of the BPCFs retains its high discharge capacity of 400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles, which does not undergo significant capacitance attenuation. Correspondingly, the microstructure of the BPCFs does not remarkably change after 500 cycles (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information), which further verifies the excellence of the braided porous structure. The BPCFs show remarkable sodium storage ability and stable cycling performance compared with other advantageous carbon material electrodes (Table <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). Concurrently, control samples of straight‐walled carbon fibers (SWCFs) were prepared under similar experimental conditions as the BPCFs. To prevent the recrystallization and coarsening of the NPC during heating, the copper catalyst and the carbon source gas, i.e., acetylene, were directly sent to a heating zone preheated to 600 ℃. From Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S13</link> (Supporting Information), the diameter of the SWCFs is ≈50 nm, which is smaller than that of the BPCFs (200 nm, Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0002">2</link>). Moreover, the SWCFs do not possess a porous structure owing to the lack of the catalytically active (111) planes. The SWCFs present electrochemical properties similar to those of the BPCFs. However, the capacity of the former is approximately half of that of the latter (430 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). Specifically, their capacity is 192 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles at a current density of 0.1 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, as presented in Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S14</link> (Supporting Information). The high specific capacitance may be attributed to the vacancies providing sufficient adsorption sites for Na<sup>+</sup>, and the outstanding cyclic stability can be attributed to the stable framework of the 3D interconnected networks.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3907-bib-0028"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The rate performance of the active electrode material was further analyzed by changing the current density. The results in Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0004">4d</link> show that the BPCFs anode achieves reversible discharge capacities of 449, 397, 335, 298, 260, 223, and 195 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The rate performance of the BPCFs shows a high discharge capacity among recently reported carbon materials (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S15</link>, Supporting Information). The initial Coulombic efficiency (68.4%) at a high current density (10 A g<sup>−1</sup>) is significantly higher than that measured at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (40.35%). This is because the electrochemical reaction rate cannot match with the voltage drop; therefore, more sodium ions are mainly inserted in the nanopores and the crystalline carbon layer with a large layer spacing in the BPCFs. The initial Coulombic efficiency of carbon‐based materials can be effectively improved using an appropriate electrolyte. We configured 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>/dimethoxyethane (DME) and 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub>/tetraethylene glycol dimethyl ether (TEGDME) (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S16</link>, Supporting Information) to improve the initial Coulombic efficiency. The discharge capacity of the BPCFs shows a gradual increase in the first 500 cycles, indicating that a new irreversible SEI film gradually forms on their surfaces as the cycle progresses at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs3907-fig-0004">4e</link>). The BPCFs electrode with mass loading of 1 mg cm<sup>–2</sup> show a similar cycling performance at a current density of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S17</link>, Supporting Information). The Coulombic efficiency of the BPCFs||BPCFs symmetric cells was maintained at 97.6% after ten cycles (Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S19</link>, Supporting Information). The BPCFs||Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> full cells also showed excellent Coulombic efficiency. Moreover, for an in‐depth understanding of the cycling performance of the BPCFs, we prepared electrodes with different active material mass loadings: 2, 3, and 5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. As presented in Figure <link href="#advs3907-supitem-0001">S21</link> (Supporting Information), with the increase in the mass loading, the specific capacity and cycle performance of the BPCFs electrode show good stability. The excellent fast charge and discharge capability of the BPCFs electrode may be ascribed to the interconnected porous structure, which shortens the diffusion distance of Na<sup>+</sup> and electrons, and the large interlayer distance (0.387 nm), which provides sufficient transmission space. This result shows that the mono‐vacancy increases the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption ability. Interestingly, the calculated results show that the defect edges also have high Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption ability, and the adsorption energy decreases with the increase in the distance from the vacancy (−1.28, −1.13, and −1.09 eV, respectively). Carbon “fiber seeds” were initially grown on high‐catalytic activity crystal planes of the catalyst particles. The adjacent carbon “fiber seeds” were interwoven along the direction of the fiber length, becoming the framework of the BPCFs and introducing abundant vacancies. The BPCFs, having a stable frame and porous network structures, present remarkable electrochemical performance with a high charge/discharge capacity of 401 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles. The calculated results confirm that the considered vacancy and its near edges could effectively enhance the Na + adsorption capacity. Therefore, these results lead to broadening of the applications of SIBs in large‐scale energy storage systems.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of 3D NPC</title><p>Cu<sub>30</sub>Mn<sub>70</sub> alloy strips with a thickness of 30 µm were prepared using smelting belt swing technology. Mn in Cu<sub>30</sub>Mn<sub>70</sub> was corroded out using the dealloying method, in aqueous HCl solution (0.025 mol L<sup>−1</sup>) at 40 ℃ in a vacuum chamber, and the interconnected 3D NPC was obtained.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of BPCFs Structure</title><p>The as‐prepared 3D NPC was first placed in a furnace and immersed in a mixed gas of acetylene (C<sub>2</sub>H<sub>2</sub>) (10 sccm) and Ar (250 sccm) at 600 ℃ for 1 h and cooled to 20 ℃ in an Ar‐protecting atmosphere. BPCFs were uniformly grown on the surface of the 3D NPC. Subsequently, the 3D NPC with BPCFs was corroded by immersion in FeCl<sub>3</sub> (5 g) + HCl (10 mL) + H<sub>2</sub>O (100 mL) solution for 12 h. Subsequently, it was dipped in concentrated nitric acid solution (68% purity) for 3 h at room temperature. Finally, the BPCFs were rinsed with deionized water and vacuum‐dried.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3907-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The microstructures and morphologies of an as‐prepared sample were investigated by field emission SEM (FE‐SEM, S‐8100, Hitachi) and TEM (FEI Tecnai G2 S‐Twin instrument with a field emission gun operating at 200 kV). The structure of the carbon fibres was characterized by powder XRD (Rigaku D/Max‐2400 with Cu K<i>α</i> radiation).
