type="main">Abstract</title><p>Regulating redox kinetics is able to spur the great‐leap‐forward development of room‐temperature sodium–sulfur (RT Na–S) batteries, especially on propelling their Na‐ion storage capability. Here, an innovative metal oxide kinetics accelerator, orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> Na‐ion conductor, is proposed to functionalize porous carbon nanoreactors (CNR) for S cathodes. The Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is shown to chemically immobilize sodium polysulfides via strong affinity. Theoretical and experimental evidence reveals that the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> can bidirectionally regulate redox behaviors of S cathodes, which accelerates reduction conversions from polysulfides to sulfides as well as promotes oxidation reactions from sulfides to S. In situ and ex situ characterization techniques further verify its electrochemical lasting endurance in catalyzing S conversions. The well‐designed S cathode demonstrates a high specific capacity of 1377 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, outstanding rate capability of 405 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and stable cyclability with a capacity retention of 617 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over 600 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. An ultralow capacity decay rate of 0.0193% per cycle is successfully realized, superior to those of current state‐of‐the‐art RT Na–S batteries. This design also suits emerging Na–Se batteries, which contribute to outstanding electrochemical performance as well.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs4882-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>In this work, an intrinsic metal oxide electrocatalyst, orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>, is revealed to bidirectionally regulate the redox behaviors of room‐temperature Na–S batteries, which are able to capture polysulfides via strong interactions, catalyze electrochemical reactions of S speciation, and improve redox kinetics. alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Room‐temperature sodium–sulfur (RT Na–S) batteries potentially become one of large‐scale integrative electrochemical energy storage techniques due to some unique intrinsic advantages: abundant resources of Na and S, inexpensive price of key materials, high theoretical capacities of electrodes, and high energy density of devices.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0001 #advs4882-bib-0002 #advs4882-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In nearly a decade of a rapid development road since 2010, a series of significant challenges are retarding the accomplishment of high‐performance RT Na–S batteries, mainly encompassing poor electronic conductivity of S and its solid‐phase discharge products, high solubility of polysulfide intermediates in liquid electrolytes, sluggish electrochemical kinetics of S species with Na ions, and dramatic volume variation of S cathodes during (de)sodiation process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0004 #advs4882-bib-0005"><sup>4,5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In recent years, delicate design of carbons with various fine nanostructures has significantly improved the reactivity of S species and alleviated the mechanical stress induced by volume expansion to a large extent.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0006 #advs4882-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Accordingly, it becomes the key to further developing Na–S batteries to both inhibit shuttle effect of sodium polysulfides (NaPSs) and boost thier electrochemical kinetics.</p><p>Metal oxides with rich surface sites have much promise to immobilize soluble NaPSs via polar–polar interactions so as to overcome the shuttle effect; whereas, most of the polar metal oxides exhibit too low intrinsic charge transfer capability to promote redox conversions of the surface‐bonded polysulfides, leading to generation of “dead S” and low capacities.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0008 #advs4882-bib-0009"><sup>8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to improve the electron transfer ability of these polar metal oxides and catalyze phase transformations of surface‐bonded S species, some typical strategies have been put forward for enhanced RT Na–S chemistry. A typical approach is to elevate the charge transfer capability of metal oxides confined within carbon skeletons through tailoring their geometrical sizes, according to the well‐known nanosized effect.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0010 #advs4882-bib-0011"><sup>10,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Creating favoring defects via vacancies and/or elemental doping is also demonstrated to trigger the electrocatalytic effect of metal oxides toward S conversions through tuning their Fermi level.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0012 #advs4882-bib-0013"><sup>12,13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Another useful method is to constitute heterostructures through deepening atomic‐scale interfacial coupling between metal oxides and substrate materials or epitaxially growing electrocatalysts on metal oxide surfaces.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0014 #advs4882-bib-0015"><sup>14,15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Most often, these strategies necessitate cumbersome preparing and processing procedures and thus, require peculiar synthetic routes.</p><p>As a matter of fact, some metal oxides with decent charge transfer efficiency are recently verified to electrochemically catalyze polysulfide conversions or act as redox mediators, such as MoO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> MnO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> TiO,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Their polar and catalytic properties are able to help not only capture soluble polysulfide intermediates via strong chemical bonding effect but also accelerate redox reactions of S species anchored on polar surfaces; thus, stabilizing S cathodes and propelling electrochemical performance. Moreover, for these catalytic metal oxides, less efforts on their structural design and chemical synthesis need to be made, leading to lower fabrication cost in contrast to those polar but nonconductive metal oxides that need extra structural/component modification for activating the electrocatalytic activity.</p><p>Among a great variety of metal oxide catalysts reported in metal–sulfur batteries, so far, orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> exhibits multiple unique merits for electrochemical energy storage, attributable to its open framework, layered structure, and chemical stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is an abundant material in nature and has a decent bulk electronic conductivity (3.4 × 10<sup>−6</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>) compared with almost insulating metal oxides (e.g., TiO<sub>2</sub>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nb–O crystalline structure leads to its strong sulfiophilicity for chemisorbing soluble polysulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recent studies regarding Na‐ion batteries demonstrate that the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is a superior Na ion conductor, enabling ultrafast Na ion transport not only at their surfaces but also in the bulk.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0023 #advs4882-bib-0024"><sup>23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is expected to serve as an electrochemically stable redox promotor in a long‐term cycling process due to its high corrosion resistance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To our best knowledge, such an ideal redox accelerator has still not been investigated in RT Na–S batteries, hitherto. The underlying viability of orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> in RT Na–S batteries can be inferred, given its high performance in similar lithium–sulfur (Li–S) batteries.</p><p>In this work, we elaborate a rod‐like carbon nanoreactor (CNR) as the substrate material for both Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> and S. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> sufficiently embodies the design concept and synthetic process of the elaborated S electrode material. The CNR featuring a hierarchically porous structure functions as a container for S guest, a buffer of volume change, and more significantly, as a carrier platform of electrochemical reactions. In previous works on RT Na–S batteries, metal oxides such as TiO<sub>2</sub> could not catalyze redox conversions of S due to poor electronic conductivity. Differently, the orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> with a superior Na ion conductivity is evidenced as both a polar NaPS adsorbent and an excellent redox regulator, enabling to both inhibit polysulfide shuttling and accelerate conversion kinetics in working RT Na–S batteries. A series of electrocatalysts such as metal sulfides/carbides is shown to facilitate reduction reactions of S/polysulfides; however, catalytic oxidation reactions from Na<sub>2</sub>S/polysulfides to S are still less reported and proved, to the best of our knowledge. Impressively, theoretical and experimental analysis indicates that the orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> can bidirectionally regulate redox behaviors of S cathodes, that is, both catalyzing reduction reactions from polysulfides to sulfides and promoting oxidation reactions from sodium sulfides to S. Moreover, uniformly dispersed orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> renders the whole CNR an ultrafast Na ion conduction network, in favor of solid–solid conversions among insoluble solid‐phase products via fast transport of Na ions. Electrochemical endurance of the orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is disclosed via in‐situ XRD and ex‐situ XPS results, which contributes to lasting catalytic conversions of S cathodes with improved kinetics. As a result of the functional synergy of micro/nanostructures and chemical compositions, the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR cathode harvests a high discharge specific capacity of 1377 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, excellent rate capability of 405 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and outstanding cyclability with a capacity retention of 617 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 600 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. In addition, it may be generalized to other similar rechargeable metal–chalcogen batteries, exemplifying Na–Se batteries in this work. This work reveals the stable kinetics promotion effect of orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> for S conversion chemistry and highlights the importance of functionalized design of cathode materials for achieving high‐performance RT Na–S batteries.</p><figure xml:id="advs4882-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs4882:advs4882-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs4882-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Synthesis and morphologies of samples. a) Schematic illustration of the synthesis of the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR. b–e) TEM, HRTEM, STEM images, and corresponding EDS elemental mappings of the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR. f,g) TEM images of the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR composite. h) STEM image and EDS elemental mappings of the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR composite.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0030"><title type="main">Materials Design and Characterization</title><p>A lot of research literature demonstrates that hierarchical nanopores hold outstanding confinement ability and encapsulation effect for S guest.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0025 #advs4882-bib-0026 #advs4882-bib-0027"><sup>25–27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to achieve uniform dispersion of S molecules and ideal immobilization toward them, an easily synthesized Zn‐based metal–organic framework (MOF‐74) was directly harnessed as a template to prepare a hierarchically porous carbon (Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001">1a</link>). XRD pattern in Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information; SEM image in Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information; and TEM images in Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information; jointly demonstrate that the MOF‐74 is in good accordance with previous reports,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0028 #advs4882-bib-0029"><sup>28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> confirming its successful synthesis. In the subsequent carbonization process, Zn ions in the precursor were reduced into gas‐state metallic Zn and taken away by the Argon flow, while the rest of the organic components were pyrolyzed into porous carbon. Eventually, the precursor was successfully converted to structure‐stable hierarchically porous carbon that functions as a carbon nanoreactor (CNR). The CNR integrally inherits the nanorod‐like microscopic morphology of the precursor and more importantly, possesses abundant hollow nanovoids, as evidenced by Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information. The nanorod‐like architecture provides electrically conductive pathways for rapid electron transport, favoring fast kinetics in multielectron‐involved electrochemical reactions.</p><p>Next, Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanoparticles were implanted onto the whole CNR skeleton to endow it with multifunctional feature. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001">1b</link>, the resultant ultrafine Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanoparticles are uniformly dispersed onto the CNR substrate, which is favorable for multiple‐site adsorption and interface conversions of intermediates. Despite the incorporation of plentiful Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanoparticles, the CNR still retains its original morphologies of porous nanorods without any structural change (Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001">1c</link>), suggesting its structural stability. Furthermore, an interplanar distance of 0.403 nm can be clearly observed from Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001">1d</link>, which exactly corresponds to the (001) plane of orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>. The scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) accompanied with the corresponding energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) was utilized to detect the elemental distributions of the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR composite (Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001">1e</link>). It proves the homogeneous dispersion of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> into the CNR, evidently indicating effective implantation of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> into carbon matrix.</p><p>X‐ray diffraction (XRD) was operated so as to analyze the phase components of the as‐prepared materials (Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0001">1a,b</link>). There appear two obvious characteristic peaks located 26° and 44° in the pattern of CNR, where the former is related to graphitic carbon diffraction (002) while the latter corresponds to (101) facet of carbons.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It suggests the partially graphitized amorphous carbon nature of the CNR. Any peaks associated to metallic Zn are not observed, indicating complete removal of Zn and high purity of the CNR. The XRD pattern of CNR shows that the highly crystalline precursor MOF‐74 is fully converted to amorphous carbon through a facile high‐temperature carbonization treatment. The implantation of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanoparticles leads to generation of many new characteristic peaks with high intensity that are very coincident with those of PDF# 30–0873, which fully identifies its orthorhombic phase and high crystallinity. The thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) result (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information) indicates that the content of orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is 16.8% in the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR. As sublimed S is composited with the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR, the peak intensity of orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> is slightly reduced compared with the Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR substrate. At the same time, there is lack of obvious characteristic peaks ascribed to S in the XRD pattern of the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR composite. XRD pattern of pristine sublimed S is displayed in Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information. Such a case discloses that S is evenly infused into hollow nanovoids of the CNR. Good encapsulation of S into carbon nanopores is beneficial to suppressing dissolution and shuttling of polysulfide intermediates.</p><p>Raman spectroscopy with an examined wavelength range from 100 to 2000 nm was implemented to reveal micro/nanostructure nature of the CNR, Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR, and S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs4882-fig-0002"><b>2</b>b</link>). Two clear peaks positioning at 1350 and 1599 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be witnessed from the Raman spectra of all samples, which actually represent D band and G band of carbons, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The D band acts as an indicator of disorder structure of amorphous carbon while the G band suggests the graphitization structure of carbon. The intensity ratio of D band and G band (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) can usually quantificationally reflect the graphitization level of carbon and thus, unveils its defective nature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0032 #advs4882-bib-0033"><sup>32,33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The calculated <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> value of CNR is about 1.08, fully demonstrating its partially graphitized defective carbon structure and further validating the aforementioned XRD analysis result. Despite post‐treatments via impregnation of both Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> and S, the <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> value of sample materials remains unchanged, consequently proving structural/chemical stability of the CNR. Notably, no characteristic peaks from pure S (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information) are detected in the Raman spectra of the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR, indicating successful and sufficient infiltration of S into porous carbon once again. The separator from the cell with the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR presents a white color in contrast to the obvious yellow color of that with the S/CNR (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S22</link>, Supporting Information). This suggests the well‐inhibited polysulfide shuttling phenomenon with the help of Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>. Furthermore, we took an observation of microscopic structures of materials after cycling via TEM and HRTEM. The results in Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S23</link> (Supporting Information) indicate that the overall morphology of nanorods and porous structure are maintained very well despite a long‐term (de)sodiation process. The well‐maintained pore structure, as displayed in Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S24</link> (Supporting Information), demonstrates that the as‐designed substrate material can adapt the volume change and continuous redox reactions very well. The elemental distributions after cycling (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S25</link>, Supporting Information) also suggest the stable existence and uniform distribution of S into the substrate material during electrochemical process. Note that the observed elemental F should be ascribed to decomposition of electrolyte to form an interface layer, suggesting the generation of side reactions.</p><p>The higher capacity, better cycling stability, and outperformed rate capabilities of the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR should be generally attributed to the joint action of unique micro/nanostructures of CNR and Na‐ion conductive orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> with excellent kinetical promotion. The Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>‐functionalized nanoreactors with ultrafine porosity accommodates the active S and its volume change during cycling as well as functions as a chemical container for redox conversions of the active S. The synergy of physical confinement of CNR and chemical anchoring of polar Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> nanoparticles realizes both robust adsorption of NaPSs via Nb–S and Na–O bonds and their desorption, thereby inhibiting the shuttle effect and ensuring the following redox conversions. More importantly, orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> as a good Na‐ion conductor plays a crucial role in kinetically propelling electrochemical conversions from Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i> to Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Na<sub>2</sub>S (Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0005">5f</link>).</p><p>The S loading content is a key parameter to determine the practical applications of S cathodes, so the loading content of S in the composite is further evaluated to 60% (denoted as S<sub>60</sub>/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR) to test its cycling performance. The as‐obtained S<sub>60</sub>/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR maintains a specific capacity of 520.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S26</link>, Supporting Information). Compared with the S cathode with a loading content of 42%, the cycling capacity is evidently lower, suggesting the profound influence of S content in Na‐ion storage properties. In order to realize high‐loading and high‐performance S cathodes, further structural design and compositional regulation needs to be adopted in future research.</p><p>In consideration of superiorities of the as‐designed multifunctional nanoreactors in structures and components, we speculate that it may also allow much promise in universality for other battery systems with a multielectron redox electrochemistry. Thus, we select the emerging Na–Se batteries as an example to illustrate this point. The Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR is composited with pristine Se to produce a Se/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR composite with a Se content of 56% (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S27a</link>, Supporting Information), that is higher than most of current literature.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0052 #advs4882-bib-0053 #advs4882-bib-0054 #advs4882-bib-0055 #advs4882-bib-0056 #advs4882-bib-0057 #advs4882-bib-0058"><sup>52–58</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The multielectron/phase‐involved stepwise redox mechanism can be revealed by the CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S27b</link>, Supporting Information). The Se/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR composite cathode can exhibit a high initial specific capacity of 724 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and maintains 403 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S27c</link>, Supporting Information). When evaluating its rate performance (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S27d</link>, Supporting Information), reversible capacities of 609, 432, 415, 370, and 363 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> are obtained at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Other than those, the Na–Se batteries harvest outstanding cyclability with a capacity retention of 273 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S27e</link>, Supporting Information).</p></section><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0060"><title type="main">Electrochemical Evolution Mechanism of S Cathodes</title><p>The aforementioned discussion involves the structures and compositions, chemical affinity, kinetics properties, and electrochemical performance of the as‐designed materials. Here, in‐situ and ex‐situ analysis was also carried out in an attempt to getting deep insights into the sodium storage mechanism of the S cathode.</p><p>It was recognized by Liu et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0059 #advs4882-bib-0060"><sup>59,60</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Yan et al.,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0048 #advs4882-bib-0061"><sup>48,61</sup></link><sup>]</sup> utilizing detailed characterization, comparison, and analysis, that the generation of polysulfides and parasitic nucleophilic side reactions would be involved at the early cycling process, but after building a stable electrolyte–electrode interface layer, the electrochemical evolution of the S cathode was dominant by quasi‐solid–solid reactions. Based on such a quasi‐solid redox mechanism, they further elucidated the distinguished definition of electrocatalysts for RT Na–S batteries with Li–S batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0062"><sup>62</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These advanced recognitions toward RT Na–S chemistries are very helpful for our following characterization and analysis.</p><p>The redox behaviors of the as‐assembled Na–S batteries is first investigated via CV tests (Figure <link href="#advs4882-supitem-0001">S28</link>, Supporting Information). A sharp peak around 2.05 V is usually attributed to electrochemical transformations from the pristine S to long‐chain polysulfides at the first cathodic scan process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0047 #advs4882-bib-0063"><sup>47,63</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These polysulfides can be further converted into less soluble short‐chain polysulfides in the following reactions. Another evident small peak between 0.8 and 0.9 V is often regarded as the electrochemical signal corresponding to formation of Na<sub>2</sub>S.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0048 #advs4882-bib-0064"><sup>48,64</sup></link><sup>]</sup> There appear a series of irregular and irreversible impurity peaks between 1.0 and 1.6 V, mainly due to the side reactions from electrolyte solvent and polysulfides to form the solid–electrolyte interface (SEI) and cathode–electrolyte interface (CEI) layer. At the second cathodic scan, a highly repeatable peak centered at 1.6 V is believed to correspond to conversions from dissolved Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i> (4 &lt; <i>x</i> ≤ 8) to Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0048 #advs4882-bib-0065"><sup>48,65</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A shifted peak between 1.2 and 1.4 V actually reflects the conversion from Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> to short‐chain insoluble products compared with the first cycle, the shift of which may be caused by the unstable side reactions and electrochemical polarization. The broad shoulder peak at 1.0 V corresponds to the terminal reaction to generate Na<sub>2</sub>S. In all the scans, a substantial broad anodic peak positioning at about 1.9 V represents the oxidation process from Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>/Na<sub>2</sub>S to S, slight current attenuation of which suggests the same‐extent loss of S species during this process. These CV curves are also in good accordance with discharge–charge profiles. The difference in redox behaviors at the 1st–2nd cycle might be posed by decomposition of electrolyte and electrochemical activation of S species.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0038 #advs4882-bib-0039"><sup>38,39</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>To get further insight to electrochemical evolution of the S guest, in‐situ XRD was utilized to detect the phase changes of S during discharge–charge process at the first cycle. The S cathode with an elevated S content of 66% was alternatively employed for harvesting stronger phase‐change signals of cathode materials (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs4882-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link>). The characteristic peaks were highly matched with that of S<sub>8</sub> (PDF# 73–5065) at the OV state. As the main peaks of S vanished along with a lowered voltage (2.0 V), two obvious peaks located at ≈10.47° and 22° emerged, which is ascribed to formation of long‐chain polysulfides (Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0066"><sup>66</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Once the voltage was discharged to 1.5 V, two characteristic peaks attributed to Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> (PDF# 71–0516), which positioned at 13.8° and 29°, respectively, could be clearly detected.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0060 #advs4882-bib-0067"><sup>60,67</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This indicates the transformations from long‐chain polysulfides to less soluble Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>. Afterward, three peaks at 16.7°, 17.1°, and 28.5° appeared when discharging to 1.2 V, which arose from the signals of solid‐phase Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub> (PDF# 81–1771). Following that, Na<sub>2</sub>S was continuously generated at the voltage range from 1.2 to 0.8 V, and finally, only three peaks of Na<sub>2</sub>S (PDF# 03–6920) could be observed, which located at 14.1°, 16.3°, and 23.4°, respectively. This clear phase change process fully confirms the electrochemical analysis from the abovementioned CV curves. As such, a multielectron/multiphase electrochemical evolution mechanism could be built in the designed RT Na–S batteries: S<sub>8</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i> → Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub> → Na<sub>2</sub>S. At the same time, during the charging process, featuring peaks from Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>, Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i>, and S<sub>8</sub> reappeared in turn, demonstrating the highly reversible redox reactions of the designed S cathode.</p><figure xml:id="advs4882-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs4882:advs4882-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs4882-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical evolution mechanism. a) In‐situ XRD patterns accompanied with a discharge–charge profile at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Cathodic CV curve and XPS spectra of S 2p at different discharge states.</caption></figure><p>As is well‐known, XPS is sensitive to value state change of surface elements of materials. It was, therefore, employed to examine the S cathode at different discharging states (Figure <link href="#advs4882-fig-0006">6b</link>). At the OV state, XPS spectra of S 1s from the fabricated electrode exhibited three peaks corresponding to S 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, S 2p<sub>3/2</sub>, and SO<i><sub>x</sub></i><sup>2−</sup>, respectively. With discharging to 1.8 V, two new peaks emerged at binding energies of 163.10 and 163.75 eV, respectively. According to the previous reports,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0042 #advs4882-bib-0068"><sup>42,68</sup></link><sup>]</sup> it was caused by the formation of high‐order Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i> (<i>x</i> = 4–8). When the voltage reached down to 1.5 V, the two peaks evidently shifted to 162.78 and 163.35 eV, respectively. This demonstrates that S species were further reduced to lower chemical values, which actually corresponded to conversions from high‐order polysulfides to low‐order sulfides (Na<sub>2</sub>S<i><sub>x</sub></i>, <i>x</i> = 2–4).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs4882-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As the sodiation proceeded, these complex polysulfides/sulfides were gradually reduced to Na<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub> and to the terminal Na<sub>2</sub>S.</p><p>These in‐situ and ex‐situ characterization results are in very good accordance with the previous reports about RT Na–S batteries and it is of greater importance that they sufficiently verify the multielectron/phase electrochemical evolution mechanism of S cathodes.</p><p>In summary, orthorhombic Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> incorporated into porous carbon nanoreactors (Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR) was developed as a bidirectional redox regulator for RT Na–S batteries, which could accelerate reduction and oxidation conversions of S cathodes as well as promote solid‐phase transformations of low‐order sulfides via fast Na‐ion diffusion. Uniformly dispersed Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub> onto carbon nanorods endowed the S cathode with abundant polar sites to prevent the irreversible loss of the active materials. The rod‐like porous carbon nanoreactors offered favorable physical confinement to the S guest due to their fine porous structure and adapted its dramatic volume change during (de)sodiation process. Benefitting by these multifunctional features of the designed material, the S/Nb<sub>2</sub>O<sub>5</sub>–CNR accomplished superior rate capability of 405 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 2 A g<sup>−1</sup> and excellent cycling stability (617 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 600 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>). It is worth pointing out that such a multifunctional host material is potentially extended to other similar guest materials (e.g., Se and Te). This work exploits a novel intrinsic metal oxide electrocatalyst and reveals its distinguished electrocatalytic mechanism with previous electrocatalysts (i.e., bidirectional catalysis). In addition to those, this work considerably sheds light on synergistic effect of micro/nanostructures and chemical compositions on reinforcing overall properties of S cathodes and we expect it can trigger the domino effect of developing metal oxide electrocatalysts for RT Na–S batteries.</p></section></section><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0070" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors are grateful for support from the National Key Research and Development Program of China (2019YFB2203400), the “111 Project” (B20030), and the Australian Research Council (DP200102215).</p></section><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0080" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0090" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs4882-sec-0100" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="advs4882-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="advs4882-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="advs4882-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>R.</givenNames> <familyName>Usiskin</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Lu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Popovic</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>M.</givenNames> <familyName>Law</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>P.</givenNames> <familyName>Balaya</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.‐S.</givenNames> <familyName>Hu</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.</givenNames> <familyName>Maier</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Nat.
