The efficient deposition of Li and Na over 1000 cycles was achieved due to the porous architecture and deposition of conducting few‐layer graphene. Post‐cycling FESEM, XPS, and depth profiles provide Li/Na deposition insights.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202300068:asia202300068-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202300068-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Metallic Li and Na are considered ideal anode materials for high‐energy rechargeable Li or Na metal batteries (MBs), including Li−S, Li‐O<sub>2</sub>, Li‐CO<sub>2</sub>, Na−S, Na‐O<sub>2</sub>, and Na‐CO<sub>2</sub> batteries.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> The reason being their high theoretical specific capacity (Li=3860 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and Na=1165 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) and low electrochemical potential (Li= ‐ 3 V and Na= ‐ 2.71 V vs. standard hydrogen electrode (S.H.E.)).<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0007"><sup>[2]</sup></link> However, the stumbling block towards the practical implementation of the metal anode is the hyperreactivity of Li/Na metal. The single electron in their valence orbital makes them susceptible to quick reaction. The rapid formation of solid electrolyte interface (SEI) upon the reaction of metallic Li/Na with the organic electrolytes is an example of their reactive nature. Although SEI plays a crucial role by selectively allowing metal ions to pass through it, its uncontrolled growth can cause the loss of the active Li/Na and ultimately lowered coulombic efficiency (C.E.).<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0007"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Also, the formation of unstable SEI during plating and stripping leads to volume expansion and unwanted dendrite growth. Dendrites can pierce the separator and induce internal short circuits leading to thermal runaway of the battery.</p><p>A stable and robust SEI layer is a key parameter for stable metal deposition as plating/stripping occurs beneath the SEI layer. Good amount of research work has been carried out in the past decade to understand Li deposition mechanism and deal with Li dendrite to realize Li metal anode (LMA) application.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0008"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Most of this research is focused on developing a stable SEI layer in the metal anode.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0012"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Prominent strategies for realizing stable metal anode are categorized as, artificial SEI layer,<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0016"><sup>[5]</sup></link> patterned current collectors,<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0019"><sup>[6]</sup></link> electrolyte engineering,<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0022"><sup>[7]</sup></link> porous conducting scaffolds for efficient metal deposition,<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0025"><sup>[8]</sup></link> solid‐state electrolyte, etc.</p><p>The uncontrolled volume change during plating/stripping results in a huge internal stress with a floating interface which damage cells leading to potential safety hazard and questions for safe mounting of the battery into a confined space in a practical application. The internal stress on the electrodes with a planar host is tricky to control. However, designing a porous host to accommodate volume fluctuation with cycling and release the stress from plating can effectively address the abovementioned issues.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0028"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Porous metals as current collectors with structural modification especially dedicated to tackling the volume changes and stress regulation have been effectively employed as hosts for metal anodes. The key idea is to exploit the pores to diminish the volume fluctuation with cycling and regulate the ionic flux.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0029"><sup>[10]</sup></link> An ideal host for Li/Na metal anode should possess good flexibility, lightweight, conducting, high surface area to reduce local current density, good chemical stability so that any side reaction with electrolyte can be avoided, and good wettability for Li/Na with the electrolyte.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0007"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Graphene is an attractive, lightweight Li/Na host scaffold owing to its excellent electrical conductivity, mechanical stability, and electrochemical activity.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0030"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Graphene shows chemical stability in organic electrolyte solvents, and a 3D porous network aids fast ion transfer in electrodes during cycling. Wang et al. showed the wrinkled graphene cage with improved mechanical stability to host Li.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0030"><sup>[11]</sup></link> The soft 3D Au nanoparticles @graphene hybrid aerogel proposed by Yang et al. efficiently accommodated the volume expansion during cycling for the LMA. They have showed the efficient performance even for high areal capacity of 8 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0030"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Whereas, Yu et al. utilized the 3D printing technique to fabricate superelastic graphene with pore structures for the Na storage application. They have precisely tailored multiscale graphene bulk structure which ultimately enhanced the Na deposition efficiency to 99.84%.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0030"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Hence the graphene in its various forms have efficiently being utilized to host alkali ions.</p><p>Considering the excellent chemical and mechanical properties of graphene, in this work, a few‐layer, graphene‐deposited on porous stainless steel (pSS_Gr) is used as an efficient lightweight Li and Na host scaffold. This 3D free‐standing binder‐free host is prepared by a chemical vapor deposition (CVD) technique which allows a thin layer deposition of graphene over porous stainless steel. The pyrolyzed product of perfluorohexane to fluorine and carbon leads to etching of steel upto few micomolar level and deposition of carbon at lattice site respectively.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> The introduction of C into lattice of stainless steel can be understood as the changing in the lattice structure from BCC to FCC at temperature above critical temperature of steel. The generation of more interstitial sites due to this phase transformation helps to alloy other elements.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> The prepared host demonstrated stable Li plating stripping at 6 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> current and 2.54 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity over 1000 cycles with 98% C.E. The Na metal anode (NMA) has shown stable performance at 4 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> and 1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> over 1000 cycles with ∼100% C.E. and 8 mV overpotential. Such porous conducting scaffold help suppress dendrite growth and control the volume variation that occurs during metal plating/stripping cycles. Post‐cycling FESEM and XPS analysis helps in understanding the metal deposition in graphene. Further, a full cell composed of Li‐plated pSS_Gr as an anode and LiFePO<sub>4</sub> (LFP) as the cathode is electrochemically tested at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> current density with stable 100 cycles.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">3D porous pSS_Gr anode design</title><p>The structural characterization of prepared 3D pSS_Gr has been performed for phase confirmation and phase purity. Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0001">1</link>(a) shows the Raman spectra of 3D pSS_Gr representing a synthesis of high‐quality graphene. The very small D band positioned at 1335 cm<sup>−1</sup> probably arises because of the lattice strain at the curvature of pores in the stainless steel on which graphene is deposited. A narrow and sharp G band positioned at 1582 cm<sup>−1</sup> with higher intensity gave I<sub>d</sub>/I<sub>g</sub> ratio of 0.12 indicating purity of deposited graphene. An equally intense and sharp 2D band positioned at 2661 cm<sup>−1</sup> indicates that the deposited graphene comprises just few layers. The nitrogen adsorption‐desorption curve shown in Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0001">1</link>(b) represents Type‐III isotherm with small hysteresis indicating the broad coverage of micron size pores created by etching of stainless steel. The specific surface area of prepared 3D pSS_Gr was found to be 205 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> with 0.153 ccg<sup>−1</sup> total pore volume. The morphological studies were performed using FESEM and TEM, shown in Figures <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0001">1</link>(c–e) and 1(f–h), respectively, showing the interconnected web of micron‐sized pores decorated with graphene. The interplanar spacing of the order of ∼0.36 nm is observed from TEM fringes analysis and is shown in Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0001">1</link>(h). Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0002">2</link>(a) shows a V vs. time plot, where initial fluctuations in the graph are associated with forming a SEI layer that stabilizes after some cycles. 3D pSS_Gr has shown stable performance for 1000 cycles with C.E. of 98%, as shown in Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0002">2</link>(b). Further, to understand the plating behaviour, Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0002">2</link>(c) representing V vs capacity plot, demonstrates the nucleation overpotential and growth overpotential for Li deposition in 3D pSS_Gr. For the first cycle, the potential during plating drops drastically to −253.64 mV value which is the highest for these current and capacity value experiments. This potential is called nucleation overpotential, indicating Li nucleation happening beneath the SEI layer. Further, this potential shifted upwards and showed a stable flat voltage plateau termed mass‐controlled growth potential. The overpotential in the case of metal anode application is the difference between the flat voltage plateaus of plating and stripping conditions. Initially, the overpotential values appeared higher, and further, after some cycles, they showed stable values. The graphene‐deposited stainless steel has shown minor fluctuations in voltage, a slight increase in C.E. after initial cycles, and a drop in the overpotential. This behavior is observed because of the polarization that occurred from decreased resistance due to SEI stabilization.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0038"><sup>[13]</sup></link> This indicates that some cycles are required to form a stable SEI layer as plating occurs beneath the SEI layer. The interlayer spacing between graphene sheets and the ordered porous structure of stainless steel act as a reservoir at the nanoscale for Na deposition. The C.E. achieved in this particular experiment is ∼100%, shown in Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0005">5</link>(c). The non‐fluorinated peaks (Figure <link href="#asia202300068-fig-0009">9</link>(e) and (f))of carbonyls (CO, COOR, CO<sub>3</sub>) and aliphatic chains (C−C−C−H) arise due to the degradation of electrolyte solvent.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0042"><sup>[14]</sup></link> These carbonyls, along with the fluorinated and sulfonated carbons, are the main organic component of the SEI. Starting from the surface, the spectra were recorded for etching depths of 10, 20, 50 and 100 nm. Few‐layer graphene deposition with simultaneous etching of stainless steel was achieved from the CVD method. The interlayer gaps between graphene sheets and porous structure act as a reservoir for Li and Na deposition. 3D pSS_Gr has shown stable performance for LMA at 6 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> current and 2.54 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity values for 1000 cycles with 98% C.E. When tested for NMA, 3D pSS_Gr outperform at 4 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> current and 1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> capacity value for 1000 cycles with ∼100% C.E. A proof‐of‐concept model Li metal battery full cell was fabricated using Li plated 3D pSS_Gr anode with LFP cathode and tested at 1 C rate which delivers a specific capacity of 67 mAhg<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0012" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0013" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials</title><p>Stainless steel (SS) foil (0.1 mm thickness) was procured from Alfa Aesar. perfluorohexane was procured from Sigma and used for the synthesis. bis(trifluoromethane)sulfonimide (LITFSI), dioxolane (DOL), dimethoxyethane (DME), lithium nitrate (LiNO<sub>3</sub>), lithium hexafluorophosphate (LiPF<sub>6</sub>), ethylene carbonate (EC), dimethyl carbonate (DMC), fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) and Diglyme were purchased from Sigma Aldrich and used for the preparation of electrolytes. Lithium discs, NaPF<sub>6</sub> salt, separator quartz fiber paper, and Celgard 2500 were procured from Global Nanotech. All materials were used as received.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0014" numbered="no"><title type="main">Synthesis of 3D porous network (3D pSS_Gr)</title><p>The few‐layer graphene deposited stainless steel (3D pSS_Gr) current collector was synthesized by the CVD technique.<link href="#asia202300068-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> Initially, the stainless steel was cut into circular discs of 0.785 cm<sup>2</sup> area. These discs were washed with deionized water, acetone, and isopropyl alcohol to remove impurities from the surface by sonicating for 10 min each. Later, it was dried at 60 °C. Further, the dried discs were kept in a CVD quartz tube furnace. The CVD furnace is connected to a vacuum from one side and a gas flow assembly with the precursor liquid on the other side. Here, perfluorohexane (PFH, C<sub>6</sub>F<sub>14</sub>) was used as a precursor liquid. PFH was filled and sealed in a flask in the glove box. After sealing, the flask was carefully connected to the CVD furnace with no dissolved oxygen in PFH. The furnace was heated to 950 °C under Ar/H<sub>2</sub> gas. Upon reaching this temperature, the tube was flushed with Ar gas for 1 min. Then, PFH was passed in the tube furnace for 12 min under vacuum. Stainless steel etching and deposition of graphene occurred simultaneously at 950 °C. Further, the furnace was cooled down to room temperature under Ar gas flow, and 3D pSS_Gr samples were taken out from the furnace.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0015" numbered="no"><title type="main">Structural Characterization</title><p>Raman analysis was performed using LabRam HR800 from JY Horiba micro Raman spectrometer instrument with 632.8 nm diode laser. NOVA NANO FESEM 450 instrument was used for the morphological study of prepared samples and post‐cycling electrodes with 18 kV working potential and WD=5.2–5.7 mm. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed using IFEI, Tecnai F30, FEG microscope operating with 300 kV accelerating potential. X‐ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS) measurements were carried out using VG Micro Tech ESCA 3000 instrument with monochromatic Al K<sub>α</sub> (1486.6 eV) as the x‐ray source, and pressure for the analyzer chamber was maintained at 1×10<sup>−8</sup> mbar during measurements. The surface area study was performed using Brunauer−Emmett−Teller (BET) adsorption method using Quantachrome BET surface analyzer with N<sub>2</sub> adsorption up to 1 bar on the sample‘s surface.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0016" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical testing</title><p>The as‐prepared pSS_Gr current collector was used as active electrode material for Li/Na deposition. The Cells were fabricated in Ar filled glove box (oxygen level &lt;0.1 ppm and H<sub>2</sub>O level &lt;0.1 ppm) in CR2032 cell assembly with Li/Na as counter and reference electrode. For LMA application, the electrolyte used was 1 M (LITFSI) lithium salt dissolved in a 1 : 1 by volume mixture of dioxolane and dimethoxyethane with 0.3 M LiNO<sub>3</sub> as an additive. In the case of NMA application, the electrolyte sued was 1 M NaPF<sub>6</sub> salt dissolved in Diglyme. Quartz fiber paper was used as a separator to separate negative and positive electrodes. The full cell of Li deposited pSS_Gr as anode and LFP as cathode was fabricated in CR2032 cell assembly with 1 M LiPF<sub>6</sub> in EC:DMC:EMC (1 : 1 : 1 by volume) with 5 % FEC as an electrolyte and celgard 2500 as a separator. The plating‐stripping measurements on prepared material were performed using MTI corporation battery analyzer with constant current charge‐discharge. The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy analysis (EIS) was carried out in a Biologic VMP3 multichannel electrochemical workstation equipped with EC Lab software in the frequency range of 5 mHz – 1000 kHz with an AC amplitude of 10 mV.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia202300068-sec-0017" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>A.P. is thankful to CSIR for a SRF fellowship.
