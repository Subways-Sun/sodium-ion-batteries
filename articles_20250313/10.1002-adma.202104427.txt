However, the low conductivity and poor cycling stability at high current densities hamper their applications. Herein, a versatile dual‐template method is reported to elaborate ordered mesoporous single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon composite with high specific area, uniform pore size, and large pore volume. The single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is confined in the carbon matrix. The mesopores between the composite nanorods provide fast electrolyte diffusion. The obtained nanocomposite shows a high sodium‐storage capability, excellent rate capacity, and very good cycling performance. A capacity of 310 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can remain at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 2500 cycles. Furthermore, a sodium‐ion battery (SIB) full cell composed of the MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon composite anode and a Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) cathode maintains a specific capacity of 330 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> during 100 cycles. The mechanism is investigated by in situ and ex situ characterizations as well as density functional theory (DFT) calculations.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202104427-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A mesoporous single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon composite is successfully synthesized, which displays remarkable electrochemical performance for both sodium‐ion batteries and sodium‐ion full cells. The reaction mechanism is systematically investigated by in situ and ex situ characterizations. This work may be expected to guide the future design protocol for various mesoporous single‐layered transition‐metal sulfide/carbon composite materials.<blockFixed xml:id="adma202104427-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202104427-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as auspicious alternatives for the state‐of‐the‐art lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) because of the rich natural abundance (Na: 2.3 wt% vs Li: 0.0017 wt%).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0001 #adma202104427-bib-0002 #adma202104427-bib-0003 #adma202104427-bib-0004 #adma202104427-bib-0005 #adma202104427-bib-0006"><sup>1–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, owing to the bigger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) compared to Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å), the reaction kinetics of SIBs is sluggish with severe volume changes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0003 #adma202104427-bib-0004 #adma202104427-bib-0007 #adma202104427-bib-0008 #adma202104427-bib-0009 #adma202104427-bib-0010"><sup>3,4,7–10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> 2D transition‐metal sulfides with high theoretical specific capacity (670 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for MoS<sub>2</sub>) and tunable interlayer spacing have widely been investigated as desirable anode materials for SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0011 #adma202104427-bib-0012 #adma202104427-bib-0013 #adma202104427-bib-0014 #adma202104427-bib-0015 #adma202104427-bib-0016"><sup>11–16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the inadequate charge/mass transfer<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and large volume variation during the charge/discharge cycling tend to deteriorate cycling stability and high rate performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0017 #adma202104427-bib-0018"><sup>17,18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To solve these issues, strategies on modulation of microstructure, expansion of interlayer distance, as well as incorporation of various conductive matrices have been employed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0003 #adma202104427-bib-0017 #adma202104427-bib-0019 #adma202104427-bib-0020 #adma202104427-bib-0021"><sup>3,17,19–21</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Amphiphilic molecules and polymers such as cetyltrimethylammonium bromide (CTAB), oleylamine, oleic acid, and polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP) have been used as expanders and/or carbon precursors to synthesize large interlayer space or single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon composites to improve their electrochemical kinetics and structural stability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0002 #adma202104427-bib-0022 #adma202104427-bib-0023 #adma202104427-bib-0024 #adma202104427-bib-0025 #adma202104427-bib-0026"><sup>2,22–26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Wang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared a 3D MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoflower with an expanded interlayer spacing on double‐layer carbon tubes with the assistance of PVP. Zhu et al. prepared a single‐layered ultrasmall nanoplate of MoS<sub>2</sub> embedded in carbon nanofibers by an electrospinning process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Mesoporous materials with a large specific surface area, narrow pore size distribution, and large pore volume offer abundant active sites and efficient mass/charge transfer for sodium storage. Traditionally, ordered mesoporous metal sulfide and selenide materials are synthesized by using ordered mesoporous silica as a hard template (e.g., Pluronic P123 for SBA‐15).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0027 #adma202104427-bib-0028 #adma202104427-bib-0029 #adma202104427-bib-0030 #adma202104427-bib-0031"><sup>27–31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Normally, the organic template (such as Pluronic P123 for SBA‐15) is removed by calcination or other methods before the mesoporous silica is used as a hard template. However, the as‐prepared mesoporous silica with the organic template is still inside the pore channels and is seldom be used directly as templates because their pore channels are blocked. Unexpectedly, the nitrogen sorption results (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information) of the untreated SBA‐15 (with P123 still inside, named as SBA‐15‐P123) show that it already has an open pore framework with a considerable specific surface and pore volume. These features make it a potential hard template.</p><p>We herein report a versatile dual‐template strategy to fabricate single‐layered mesoporous transition‐metal sulfide/carbon composites by using SBA‐15‐P123 as the template. The mesostructured silica framework provides a confined space to prevent the metal sulfides from growth outside the channels, while Pluronic P123 polymer serves as a matrix to facilitate the highly dispersed metal sulfide precursor and acts as the carbon source. The dual‐template strategy avoids the use of an additional carbon source, and a poisonous sulfur source (such as S and H<sub>2</sub>S), resulting in an eco‐friendly and controllable process. Specifically, single‐layered mesoporous MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon materials (denoted as SMSC‐<i>x</i>‐Mo‐<i>y</i>, where <i>x</i> represents the template (1 represent SBA‐15‐P123, 2 for MCF‐P123, 3 for KIT‐6‐P123, 4 for MCM‐41‐CTA<sup>+</sup>, and 5 for pure P123) and <i>y</i> represents different metal sulfide loading amounts) are constructed with the MoS<sub>2</sub> loading up to 80 wt% and the surface area up to 237 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>. The detailed physicochemical properties of all the composites are summarized in Table <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information). When evaluated as an anode material for SIBs, the SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 shows excellent cycling stability and outstanding high rate performance. Furthermore, this method is also suitable for synthesizing other 2D metal sulfide/carbon composites such as WS<sub>2</sub> and ReS<sub>2</sub> with potential applications such as energy storage,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> catalysis,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> biomedicine,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and sensor.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p></section><section xml:id="adma202104427-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, thiourea is mixed with SBA‐15‐P123. Then, H<sub>3</sub>PMo<sub>12</sub>O<sub>40</sub> (PMA) is added to the mixed solution during magnetic stirring. After the solvent is evaporated, thiourea and PMo<sub>12</sub><sup>−</sup> are impregnated into the pore of silica template. Owing to the existence of thiourea, there is no need to add extra harmful sulfur vapor and/or H<sub>2</sub>S gas at the following vulcanization process in Ar atmosphere. As the temperature increased, P123 is pyrolyzed and simultaneously intercalated into the layer space of MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets. Finally, after the template removal, a series of single‐layered mesoporous MoS<sub>2</sub>/C composites (e.g., SMSC‐1‐Mo‐1, SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2, and SMSC‐1‐Mo‐3) with the carbon contents of 30%, 26%, and 20% are formed. The carbon contents are determined by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). Pure P123 and SBA‐15‐OH (silanol‐rich SBA‐15)<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> are also used as templates for the synthesis. Single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is also observed in the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image of SMSC‐5‐Mo by only using P123 as the soft template and a carbon source (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). Single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> is absent by using SBA‐15‐OH as the template (named as meso‐MoS<sub>2</sub>; Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information). The TEM image of meso‐MoS<sub>2</sub> shows an interlayer spacing of 0.65 nm (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S4b</link>, Supporting Information), indicating that P123 plays an important role in formation of single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> structure. The carbon content of annealed thiourea@SBA‐15‐P123 is 7.7% (Figures <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S5</link> and <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). When annealing SBA‐15‐P123 without impregnating thiourea in Ar, P123 is completely decomposed and there is almost no residual carbon. The thiourea may facilitate the carbonization process of P123 to a certain degree. Thus, the P123 is pyrolyzed and simultaneously intercalated into the interlayer space of MoS<sub>2</sub> sheets during the formation of MoS<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="adma202104427-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202104427:adma202104427-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202104427-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the formation process of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2. b) SAXS patterns of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐1, SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2, SMSC‐1‐Mo‐3 and SBA‐15‐P123. c) XRD patterns of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐1, SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 and SMSC‐1‐Mo‐3. d) N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms and pore size distribution of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2. e) SEM image, f,g) TEM images, h) HRTEM image, i) STEM image, and j) corresponding elemental mappings of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2.</caption></figure><p>Small‐angle X‐ray scattering (SAXS) patterns of all the SMSC‐1‐Mo samples in Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1b</link> clearly show four well‐resolved scattering peaks that can be ascribed to (100), (110), (200), and (210) planes of 2D hexagonal symmetry with the space group of <i>p</i>6<i>mm</i>, indicating good replication of the ordered mesoporous silica template. Powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1c</link>) of meso‐MoS<sub>2</sub> shows distinct reflections of 2H phase MoS<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 37–1492), while the (002) reflection peak of all the SMSC‐1‐Mo composites disappears, suggesting no stacking of MoS<sub>2</sub> layers in the <i>c</i>‐direction. Besides the two reflection peaks at 33.5° and 59.1° of (100) and (110) planes of MoS<sub>2</sub>, the SMSC‐1‐Mo composites also display two weak reflections at ≈7.5° and 17.6°, which are attributed to the reflection from adjacent single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> sheets.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0018 #adma202104427-bib-0020 #adma202104427-bib-0023 #adma202104427-bib-0026"><sup>18,20,23,26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These two peaks shift to lower degree positions with the decreasing MoS<sub>2</sub> content, indicating that higher single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> content formed.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0035 #adma202104427-bib-0036 #adma202104427-bib-0037"><sup>35–37</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1d</link> and in Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S7a</link> (Supporting Information), the nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 and the other two composites have two distinguishable capillary condensation steps, coinciding with a type IV isotherm. The Brunauer–Emmet–Teller (BET) surface areas of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐1, SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2, and SMSC‐1‐Mo‐3 are calculated to be 237, 190, and 182 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>. The pore volumes are 0.39, 0.29, and 0.22 cm<sup>3</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (shown in Table <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S1</link> in the Supporting Information). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1e</link>) image of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 shows a rod‐like morphology analogous to the SBA‐15 template. TEM (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1f,g</link>) images confirm that the 2D hexagonal structure and single‐layered MoS<sub>2</sub> sheets occur. High‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1h</link>) clearly shows the lattice of MoS<sub>2</sub> sheet. The interplane spacing is ≈0.26 nm, consistent well with the (101) crystal plane of 2H phase of MoS<sub>2</sub>. The fast Fourier transform (FFT) image displays a typical hexagonal lattice structure. Similar structures appear in SMSC‐1‐Mo‐1 and SMSC‐1‐Mo‐3 (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). Surprisingly, the element of N is detected besides Mo, S, and C in SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1j</link>) based on scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0001">1i</link>). Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S9</link> (Supporting Information) shows that the content of N is about 3.0 wt%, consistent well with the result of elemental analysis (Table <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information).</p><p>Furthermore, Raman spectroscopy and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) were carried out to analyze the chemical compositions of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 and meso‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>, the bands at 383–385 and 407–411 cm<sup>−1</sup> are assigned to in‐plane E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> and out‐of‐plane A<sub>1g</sub> vibrational modes of MoS<sub>2</sub>. The frequency separation between E<sup>1</sup><sub>2g</sub> and A<sub>1g</sub> modes of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 (23.4 cm<sup>−1</sup>) is much lower than that of meso‐MoS<sub>2</sub> (26.8 cm<sup>−1</sup>), suggesting an enlarged layer spacing of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0018 #adma202104427-bib-0020"><sup>18,20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The relative intensity ratio (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub>) values of D and G peaks is 0.78 for SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2, demonstrating the existence of amorphous carbon.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202104427-bib-0005 #adma202104427-bib-0038"><sup>5,38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The full‐scan XPS spectra (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-supitem-0001">S10a</link>, Supporting Information) also indicate the existence of Mo, S, C, N, and O elements both in SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 and meso‐MoS<sub>2</sub>. The above residual carbon and N‐doping in the samples originate from the pyrolysis of P123 and decomposition of thiourea. This means that the lost 0.80<i>e</i><sup>−</sup> of the Na is completely transferred to MoS<sub>2</sub> on the MoS<sub>2</sub>Na configuration, while the depleted 0.77<i>e</i><sup>−</sup> of Na is distributed on both MoS<sub>2</sub> (0.12<i>e</i><sup>−</sup>) and graphene (0.65<i>e</i><sup>−</sup>) on the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐G (002) Na configuration. Therefore, the existence of graphene on MoS<sub>2</sub> tunes the electrons transfer between Na and the composites, contributing to more easily anchoring of Na for SIBs. Another merit of adding graphene to the MoS<sub>2</sub> materials is the enhanced conductivity (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0005">5c</link>), as shown by the change from the semiconductor‐characteristic total density of states (TDOS) of MoS<sub>2</sub> to conductor‐characteristic TDOS of MoS<sub>2</sub>‐G (002). The increased conductivity accelerates the charge transfer. Moreover, the existence of graphene promotes the migration of Na atoms (Figure <link href="#adma202104427-fig-0005">5d</link>), as shown by the lower migration barriers of Na on MoS<sub>2</sub>‐G (002) than that on MoS<sub>2</sub>. This promotion results in fast reaction kinetics and excellent electrochemical performance. The DFT calculations and the experiment results all demonstrate that the superior electrochemical performance of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 is mainly attributed to its highly predominated charge‐storage mechanism of the capacitive process. The obtained MoS<sub>2</sub>/carbon nanocomposites have a high specific surface area of up to 237 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> and a high MoS<sub>2</sub> content of up to 80%. The unique single‐layered structure significantly promotes the electrical conductivity and structural stability. As expected, the SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 displays a remarkable electrochemical performance with prolonged cycle life, superior reversible capacity, and excellent rate capability. Besides, the reaction mechanism of SMSC‐1‐Mo‐2 has been systematically investigated by in situ and ex situ characterizations. This work could be expected to guide the future designing protocol for various mesoporous single‐layered transition metal sulfide/carbon composite materials.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202104427-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge the funding support from the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (Program Nos. 2018YFE0201703 and 2018YFE0201701), the “1000‐Youth Talents Plan,” and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (Grant Nos. 2042021kf0213 and 2042019kf0230).
