However, the controlled synthesis of transition metal selenides, especially those derived from metal‐organic‐frameworks with well‐controlled structure and morphology is still challenging. In this paper, highly porous NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC composite microspheres were synthesized by simultaneous carbonization and selenization of a Ni−Co‐based metal‐organic framework (NiCo‐MOF) and characterized by scanning electron microscopy, transition electron microscopy, X‐Ray diffraction, X‐Ray photoelectron spectroscopy and electrochemical techniques. The rationally engineered NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC composite exhibits a capacity of 325 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and 277.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Most importantly, the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC retains a capacity of 293 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles, with a capacity decay rate of 0.025 % per cycle.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>NiCoSe<sub>4</sub> microspheres</b> derived from NiCo‐MOFs display excellent electrochemical performance as anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000132:asia202000132-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000132-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIB) are the main energy storage devices for the application in electric vehicles, energy storage grids and mobile electronics, etc., due to the considerable energy density, high operating voltage, and long cycle life.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0001"/> However, due to the limited resources of lithium on earth, the cost of lithium is surging upon the expanding market needs. Relative to lithium, sodium resource is undoubtedly more abundant on earth and thus cost‐favorable than lithium in practical sense. Besides, the redox potentials of sodium is only 0.3 V higher than lithium,<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0002"/> making it ideal alternative to lithium.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0003"/> However, due to the larger ionic radius and slow diffusion kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> than Li<sup>+</sup>, the excellent anode materials developed for LIBs are unsuitable for SIBs.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0004"/> Thus, it is highly desirable to explore suitable electrode materials for SIBs.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0005"/></p><p>Transition metal selenides, such as SnS,<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0006"/> MoS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0007"/> CoSe<sub>x</sub>,<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0008"/> NiSe<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0009"/> have drawn tremendous attention due to their environment‐friendliness and high theoretical capacity for Na<sup>+</sup> storage. For example, CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles wrapped by N‐doped and CNTs‐intertwined carbonaceous materials (CoSe<sub>2</sub>@N‐CF/CNTs) developed by our group exhibit a reversible capacity of 606 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycle.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0010"/> Lately, binary transition metal selenides (BTMSs) have been explored as anode material, owing to their superior conductivity to monometallic selenides and more redox active sites. For instance, iron‐cobalt and iron‐nickel selenides have been reported by Hou and coworkers, which display electrochemical performances outperforming monometallic selenides.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0011"/></p><p>Metal organic frameworks (MOF) have been widely used as precursors for transition metal chalcogenides, which might be a promising protocol to obtain binary transition metal selenides. Recently, Wu et al. develops aggregated nanocubes of metal selenides embedded in a three‐dimensional (3D) N‐doped carbon network arising from a MOF. The material exhibits an improved cycle stability and a moderate reversible capacity of 360.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0012"/> However, the exploration of the promising metal selenides, especially BTMSs, as anode materials for SIBs is still at its infancy, and further efforts are needed to optimize the performance from the morphology and composition points of view.</p><p>In this work, a binary Ni−Co‐based MOF (NiCo‐MOFs) is synthesized and used as a precursor material to produce porous NiCoSe<sub>4</sub> microspheres, wrapped by reduced graphene oxide (rGO) and used as anode materials for SIBs (scheme <link href="#asia202000132-fig-5001"/>), which show excellent electrochemical performances and notably improved stability, as compared with reported transition metal selenides.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000132-fig-5001"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000132:asia202000132-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000132-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration for the preparation of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussions</title><p>The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images of NiCo‐MOF are shown in Figure S1, where porous microspheres with size of 10∼12 μm are observed. When further zoomed in, it is observed that the surfaces of such microspheres are comprised of bundles of nanorods. After selenization, the microsphere morphology of NiCo‐MOF is well preserved for NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC, as can be seen in SEM images in Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0001"/>a–b. But, the size of microspheres are apparently reduced to 4–7 μm, and the nanorods on the surface of NiCo‐MOF are converted into interwoven network. In Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0001"/>c for the low magnification SEM image of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC, the voids highlighted by the red circle suggests the hollow structures of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC possesses. This unique hollow structure possibly offers the advantages of (1) providing high surface area and thus sufficient electrochemical active sites for sodium intercalation; (2) the feature size in the nanodomain shortens the ion diffusion path, accelerating the charge transfer rate and improving long‐term cycling stability; (3) hollow cavities are beneficial to alleviating severe volume expansion and mechanical stress in the charging/discharging processes. Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0001"/>e displays the SEM image of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO, clearly showing that NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO microspheres are wrapped by rGO nanosheets. The elemental mapping of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC microspheres and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO are shown in Figure S2a‐b and Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0001"/>g, where Ni, Co and Se distribute evenly over the samples and overlap very well with each other, suggesting the formation of target materials. The content percentages of Co, Ni and Se in NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO are shown in Figure S2b and the elemental ratio of them are approximately 1 : 1 : 4. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0001"/>d and f, revealing an interplanar spacing of 0.26 nm and 0.21 nm, corresponding to (210) and (220) plane of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>, respectively. In addition, the fast Fourier transform performed by NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO is also shown in Figure S1d. The lattice plane distance of (220) derived from Fourier transform is in agreement with that from HRTEM.</p><figure xml:id="asia202000132-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000132:asia202000132-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000132-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a–b) Low and (c) high magnification SEM images of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC microspheres; and (d) high TEM images of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC; (e) SEM, (f) HR‐TEM and (g) elemental mapping images of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0002"/>a shows the XRD diffraction patterns of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC, displaying seven peaks at 2θ=30.4°, 33.8°, 37.3°, 43.4°, 51.4°, 56.2° and 58.6° respectively, which are right between (200), (210), (211), (220), (311), (230), and (321) planes of both NiSe<sub>2</sub> ( PDF no. 65‐5016) and CoSe<sub>2</sub> ( PDF no. 65‐3327),<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0013"/> confirming the formation of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC due to the selenization of NiCo‐MOF. The Raman spectra of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO are displayed in Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0002"/>b. The peaks at 1358 and 1578 cm<sup>−1</sup> are corresponding to the D band and G bands of carbon materials, respectively.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0014"/> The I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub> value is 0.94 for the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC and 1.01 for the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO, respectively, suggesting that the carbon layers formed on NiCoSe<sub>4</sub> after selenization process is smaller than that of the carbon layer encapsulating the rGO layer.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0015"/></p><figure xml:id="asia202000132-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000132:asia202000132-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000132-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) XRD, (b) Raman and (c) XPS spectra of N 1s (d) Ni 2p (e) Co 2p and (f) Se 3d XPS spectra of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO.</p></caption></figure><p>In Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0002"/>c, nitrogen is clearly observed at 399.83 eV (pyrrolic N) for NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0016"/> Figures <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0002"/>d–f is the high resolution XPS spectra of Ni 2p, Co 2p and Se 3d respectively. The Ni 2p and Co 2p spectra can be fitted into two spin‐orbit doublets and two shake‐up satellites (marked with “Sat.”).<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0012b"/> In Ni 2p spectra, the spin‐orbit doublet of Ni 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ni 2p<sub>1/2</sub> for Ni<sup>2+</sup> are observed at 853.11 and 870.66 eV while that for Ni<sup>3+</sup> are observed at 855.15 and 874.89 eV. The peaks at 860.22 and 881.04 eV attribute to satellite peaks of Ni.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0017"/> The high resolution XPS spectra for Co 2p is shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0002"/>d. The peaks at 778.5 and 793.3 eV are corresponding to Co<sup>2+</sup>, while the signals at 780.9 and 796.9 eV can be attributed to Co<sup>3+</sup>. The satellite peaks located at 786.3 and 802.8 eV also evidence the existence of metallic Co.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0018"/> The presence of Ni<sup>3+</sup> and Co<sup>3+</sup> might originate from the oxidation of Ni<sup>2+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup> upon exposure to air.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0017"/> The XPS spectra of Se 3d are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0002"/>f. There are two peaks at binding energy of 54.69 and 56.01 eV, corresponding to Se 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Se 3d<sub>3/2</sub>, respectively, and which ascribed to the Se<sup>2−</sup> ions and consistent with the previous reports.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0017 #asia202000132-bib-0019"/> According to the previous reports,<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0012b #asia202000132-bib-0017"/> the peak at 58.93 eV might be the SeO<sub>x</sub>.</p><p>The carbon and rGO content in NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO is determined by TGA measurement.(Figure S4) After complete thermal decomposition, the resulting residue are found to be the mixture of CoO (PDF no. 71‐1178) and NiO (PDF no. 71‐1179)<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0010"/> (XRD pattern in Figure S5). The weight percentage of rGO in the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO is calculated to be 17 wt%. BET measurements are conducted to evaluate the porous nature. The pore size distribution of the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO is investigated by N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption measurement (Figure S6). It is found that the N<sub>2</sub> adsorption/desorption isotherms have a type IV isotherm with a hysteresis loop at a relative high pressure, demonstrating their mesoporous characteristics. The pore size calculated employing the Barrett‐Joyner‐Halenda (BJH) method is ca. 35 nm. The specific surface areas of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC obtained from the isotherms are 49.0 and 10.6 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The higher specific surface area of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO than NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC is likely due to the layered structure of rGO.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0020"/></p><p>The electrochemical performances of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO as anode materials of SIBs are evaluated on half cells. Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0003"/>a and Figure S7a show the CV curves of the first three cycles of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC electrodes at a scan rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.3‐3 V. During the 1st cathodic process, NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC exhibit dominant cathodic peaks at 0.93 and 0.94 V. These signals are originated from the conversion of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub> into Ni, Co, and Na<sub>2</sub>Se and the formation of a solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0013 #asia202000132-bib-0021"/> In the second discharge process, four reduction peaks (1.44, 1.31, 1.13, 0.78/0.76 V) for NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO is seen as a result of generating Ni<sub>x</sub>Se, Co<sub>x</sub>Se, and Na<sub>2</sub>Se. In the first charging process, three oxidation peaks are observed at 1.54, 1.84, 1.93 V, possibly due to the generation of Ni<sub>x</sub>Se, Co<sub>x</sub>Se, NiSe<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub> and subsequent decomposition of the SEI layer.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0022"/> The formation of ultrafine NiCoSe<sub>4</sub> nanocrystals during the first desodiation process induces the shift of reduction peak to higher potential, which attributed to the gradual activation of the anode materials.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0013 #asia202000132-bib-0017 #asia202000132-bib-0022a"/> The CV curves of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrode exhibit a much enhanced overlapping than NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC in the subsequent 2–3 cycles, indicating improved reversibility by rGO wrapping.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0013 #asia202000132-bib-0017 #asia202000132-bib-0023"/> In Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0003"/>b and Figure S7b, three discharge plateaus are clearly observed at 1.43, 1.1 and 0.78 V, in agreement with the CV measurements.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0013 #asia202000132-bib-0024"/></p><figure xml:id="asia202000132-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia202000132:asia202000132-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia202000132-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>(a) CV curves at a scan rate at 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, (b) charge‐discharge curves at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> of the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO for the first three cycles; (c) cycling performance and (d) rate performance of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrodes (1 A g<sup>−1</sup>) at a voltage range of 0.3–3 V. (e) EIS spectra of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrodes before and after cycling at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0003"/>c further exhibits the cycling performance of the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC electrodes at a current of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC electrode delivers an initial discharge capacity of 444.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which decreases gradually to 280 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 220 cycles, and finally retains 238.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles. However, the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrode delivers an initial discharge capacity of 559.4 and a reversible capacity of 293 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles. We have also performed the test for high‐loading (1.57 and 2.0 mg ⋅ cm<sup>−2</sup> NiCoSe<sub>4</sub> loadings) on NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrode. In Figure S8, it can be observed that the electrode with loading of 1.57 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> and 2.0 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> displays a capacity of 268.1 and 240.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 220 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, quite promising for practical application. The outstanding Na‐storage properties of the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrode might be due to its unique conductive networks structure, which consists of rGO layers and interconnected voids.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0022a #asia202000132-bib-0025"/> This unique structure provides following advantages: The conductive carbon network constructed by rGO nanosheets not only transmit sodium ions and electrons quickly and efficiently, but also alleviate the corresponding volume expansion and contraction during the sodiation/desodiation process, mainly due to its high flexibility and conductivity.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0026"/></p><p>Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0003"/>d shows the rate capabilities of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC electrodes at current rates from 0.1 to 10 A	 g<sup>−1</sup>. The initial discharge and charge reversible specific capacity of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO are 503 and 439.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, which show a low initial Coulombic efficiency (Ec) of 87.3 % due to the electrolyte decomposition and generation of SEI film. In the subsequent cycles, the Coulombic efficiency remains approximate 100 %. The NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO exhibits a reversible specific capacity of 425, 400, 345, 327, 310, 295, and 277.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current rates of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, and 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Furthermore, when the current rate returns back to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 70 cycles, the reversible specific capacity is recovered to 355 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, higher than that of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC, demonstrating the excellent rate capability.</p><p>The EIS of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC are shown in Figure <link href="#asia202000132-fig-0003"/>e and Figure S7c, which were composed of a semicircle region and a sloping line, corresponding to the electrode/electrolyte interfacial charge‐transfer resistance (R<sub>ct</sub>) and Warburg impedance (Z<sub>w</sub>) due to the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the electrolyte, respectively. As the cycling proceeds, the formation of SEI film and activation of the electrode results in dramatically reduced R<sub>ct</sub>. Besides, the rGO wrapping can significantly reduce the charge transfer resistance of the electrode (23.4, 13.7 and 6.8 Ω vs 40.5, 23.2 and 8.5 Ω), possibly due to: (1) the increased electrode/electrolyte interface, as demonstrated by the BET measurement; and (2) the increased conductivity of rGO than the carbon layer on NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC formed during selenization, resulting in faster interfacial kinetics. The graphene coating of the NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO electrode results in an outstanding storage performance, exhibiting a specific capacity of 293.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 1500 cycles at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The long‐life cycling stability might be attributed to the hierarchical structure of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO, which prevents the structural variation during charge/discharge processes. It also displays a superior rate performance of 277.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>. This study provides a versatile method for the preparation of multi‐metal selenides and can be extended to new electrode structures for other high‐performance energy storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental section</title><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material synthesis</title><p><i>Synthesis of NiCo‐MOFs</i>: All chemicals in this experiment are analytical grade and used directly without further purification. 1.49 mmol of Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> ⋅ 6H<sub>2</sub>O, 1.49 mmol of Ni(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub> ⋅ 6H<sub>2</sub>O, 1.43 mmol of trimesic acid, and 3 g of polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP) were dissolved in a mixed solution containing 20 mL absolute ethanol, 20 mL N,N‐dimethylformamide and 20 mL water. After stirring for 30 min, the solution was transferred into an autoclave and heated at 150 °C for 10 h. The resulting precipitates was collected by centrifugation at 10000 rpm for 3 min. Finally, the product is washed three times with water and ethanol in sequence, and dried at 60 °C to yield NiCo‐MOFs.</p><p><i>Synthesis of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC</i>: 0.5 g NiCo‐MOFs and 1 g selenium powder were mixed and then heated to 550 °C with a ramp rate of 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup> under argon atmosphere. After reacting at this temperature for 12 h, the 0.5 g NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC is produced with a color in black.</p><p><i>Synthesis of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO</i>: 100 mg NiCoSe<sub>4</sub><i>@NC</i> was added into 20 mL DI water under violently stirring. To this solution, 4 mL suspension of graphene oxide (5 mg/mL) and 1 g ascorbic acid were then added sequentially. After being uniformly dispersed, the solution was heated at 95 °C for 2 h. Last, the product was washed three times with distilled water and collected by freeze‐drying for further use.</p><p>The graphene oxide in aqueous solution was prepared by a modified Hummers method.<link href="#asia202000132-bib-0029"/> Briefly, graphite powder ( 3 g, Aladdin) and NaNO<sub>3</sub> (3 g) were separately added to concentrated H<sub>2</sub>SO<sub>4</sub> (138 mL, 98 % in weight) and agitated at ice‐water bath for 30 min, into which KMnO<sub>4</sub> (18 g) was added slowly and stirred for another 60 min. After diluted with DI water (240 mL), the solution was heated at 90 °C for 30 min. Next, 30 % H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (18 mL) and DI water (600 mL) were added into the mixed solution. At the moment, the color of solution turned into bright yellow, accompanied with generation of a large number of bubbles. The product was centrifuged at 10000 rpm for 3 min and washed with HCl aqueous solution to remove remaining metal ions, followed by washed with DI water until the pH of solution reaches ca. 7. Finally, the slurry was made into an aqueous GO suspension (5 mg/mL).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material Characterization</title><p>Thermal gravimetric analysis (TGA) was performed on Mettler Toledo TGA/SDTA851 at a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup> in O<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. XRD surveys were employed (in the 2θ of 10°–70°) on a Bruker D8 diffractometer using Cu−Kα radiation (λ=1.5406 Å). The BET N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption isotherms test were analyazer at 77 K with an Autosorb apparatus. X‐ray photoelectron spectra (XPS) were tested with a Phi X‐tool instrument. Raman spectra were obtained with a high resolution dispersive Roman spectrometer at an excitation wavelength of 514 nm. The field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM, Hitachi S‐4800) and HRTEM (JEOL, JEM‐2010) were used to investigate the morphology and composition of the materials.</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>Electrochemical measurements were performed using a pure sodium foil as the counter electrode in a 2032 coin cell. The working electrodes were prepared by following materials and methods: 80 % active material, 10 % acetylene black conductive additive, and 10 % CMC‐SBR (CMC:SBR=3 : 7 by weight ratio) binder were mixed with appropriate deionized water to acquire a homogeneous slurry. Next, the slurry was uniformly applied to the copper current collector using a film blade, and dried in a vacuum oven at 60 °C for 24 h to remove the water. The mass loading of each electrode was estimated 1–1.2 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The cells were assembled in an argon‐filled glove box, in which the O<sub>2</sub> and H<sub>2</sub>O contents were no more than 0.1 ppm. The Na foil was used as both counter electrode and reference and Whatman GF/D as a separator. The electrolyte solution is containing of 1.0 mol L<sup>−1</sup> NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> in diglyme. Cyclic voltammetry (CV) analyaze were required from CHI760 electrochemical workstation and electrochemical impedance (EIS) measurements were investigated by the same at envioronment temperature. Galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD) tests were carried out with battery testing system (CT2001 A, LAND) in the voltage range of 0.3–3 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Supporting Information</title><p>SEM images and XRD patterns of NiCo–MOFs; EDX elemental mapping of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC composite; TGA and XRD of the TGA products of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC in O<sub>2</sub> atmosphere; N<sub>2</sub> adsorption‐desorption profiles of NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC@rGO and NiCoSe<sub>4</sub>@NC;</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51562034), Gansu Province Science Foundation for Distinguished Young Scientist (No. 1606RJDA315).</p></section><section xml:id="asia202000132-sec-0010" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="asia202000132-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="asia202000132-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="asia202000132-bib-0001a"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="asia202000132-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>C.
