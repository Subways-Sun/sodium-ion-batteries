Metal sulfides have demonstrated encouraging results, but still suffer from sluggish kinetics and severe capacity decay associated with the phase change. Herein we show that rational electrode design, that is, building efficient electron/ion mixed‐conducting networks, can overcome the problems resulting from conversion reactions. A general strategy for the preparation of hierarchical carbon‐coated metal sulfide (MS⊂C) spheres through thermal sulfurization of metal glycerate has been developed. We demonstrate the concept by synthesizing highly uniform hierarchical carbon coated vanadium sulfide (V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C) spheres, which exhibit a highly reversibly sodium storage capacity of 777 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, excellent rate capability (410 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), and impressive cycling ability.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Sphere of spheres</b>: An efficient electron and ion conducting network is formed by the thermal sulfurization of metal glycerate spheres. The hierarchical V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres exhibit a high sodium‐storage capacity, excellent rate capability, and very impressive cycling ability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201901840:anie201901840-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201901840-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201901840-sec-0001"><p>Sodium‐ion (Na<sup>+</sup>) batteries (SIBs) have recently attracted growing attention for large‐scale applications, because sodium resources are virtually inexhaustible with a rather uniform distribution around the world.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0001 #anie201901840-bib-0002 #anie201901840-bib-0003"/> The larger ionic radius of the Na<sup>+</sup> ion when compared to the Li<sup>+</sup> ion suggests sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> transport kinetics, lower theoretical capacity, and larger volume variation during Na<sup>+</sup> ion insertion and extraction.</p><p>Searching for suitable host materials for reversible and rapid Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and extraction remains still a serious challenge. Significant progress has been made for SIB cathodes that exhibit acceptable electrochemical performances comparable to their Li<sup>+</sup> batteries (LIBs) counterparts.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0004 #anie201901840-bib-0005"/> In regard to anode materials, however, the situation remains challenging. Graphite, the commercial anode for LIBs, does not function in SIBs owing to its extremely low capacity.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0006"/> Several different types of metal oxides, such as TiO<sub>2</sub>, have been exploited as insertion anode materials for SIBs, but suffer from limited specific capacities (below 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0007 #anie201901840-bib-0008 #anie201901840-bib-0009"/> Owing to multiple electron‐transfers per metal center, conversion‐reaction materials have the potential to provide a high theoretical capacity, at least twice as high as that achieved in insertion type materials. Of course, utilizing the conversion reaction of metal sulfides poses a series of challenging thermodynamic and especially kinetic difficulties associated with the phase change combined with poor cycling stability and sluggish kinetics.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0010 #anie201901840-bib-0011"/></p><p>Metal sulfides have garnered more attention than oxides as anodic material for SIBs owing to several inherent advantages: Kinetics of sodiation/desodiation processes might be easier than in the case of oxides because of the reduced bond strength.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0012"/> In addition, metal sulfides possess a more suitable interlayer spacing for an easier Na<sup>+</sup> ion uptake, thus enabling high capacity and a better reversibility. Many different types of metal sulfides, including MoS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0013"/> Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0014"/> FeS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0015"/> SnS,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0012"/> have been extensively investigated for SIBs. Although the electrochemical performance of previously reported metal sulfides was better than metal oxides, it was still unsatisfactory in terms of reversible capacity, cycle stability, and rate capability. Tailoring the particle size of the electroactive materials to reduce both the ionic and electronic transport distance is a common strategy to improve the electrochemical performance. However, in this case it will be quite difficult to efficiently access each particle by ionic and electronic current collectors, and the electrode's internal circuitry. To fully exploit the benefits of nanostructuring, it is a must to construct highly efficient and stable electrochemical circuits involving a confined volume active mass,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0016 #anie201901840-bib-0017"/> as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0001"/> a. The spherical shape of the particles provides for a low surface‐to‐volume ratio, potentially a high packing density, and isotropic physical properties.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0018 #anie201901840-bib-0019 #anie201901840-bib-0020"/> Such a hierarchical morphology has the advantages of shorter charging time as the optimizes the network resistances.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0021"/> In the case that the contact circuitry causes resistance problems, the construction of hierarchical electrode is a perfect way to solve the aforementioned problems (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0001"/> b).<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0022"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201901840-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201901840:anie201901840-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201901840-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic diagrams of a) electrochemically integrated circuit employing ionic and electronic conductors and the active mass and b) spherical hierarchical electrode architecture. c) Schematic illustration of the formation process of hierarchical MS<sub><i>x</i></sub>⊂C spheres.</p></caption></figure><p>Here, we developed a new and generally applicable strategy to synthesize carbon coated metal sulfide (MS⊂C) composed of nanoscale carbon‐coating primary particles with a core–shell structure. The method involves solution synthesis of uniform metal glycerate solid spheres and subsequent thermal annealing in H<sub>2</sub>S. Today, the low cost of vanadium (ca. US $5.5 kg<sup>−1</sup>)<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0023"/> and lightweight elements has sparked renewed interest and research in many energy storage technologies. Reports of vanadium sulfide as anode materials for SIBs are still very scarce.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0024 #anie201901840-bib-0025"/> Thus, taking V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> as a model system, highly uniform carbon‐coated V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C) spheres are directly obtained by annealing vanadium glycerate in H<sub>2</sub>S without addition of external carbon sources. Thereby a highly efficient electron/ion conducting system with a very robust architecture is realized. Profiting from these advantages, the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres display a high reversible capacity of 777 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, an excellent rate capability (410 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) and impressive cycling stability.</p><p>Our strategy for the formation of hierarchically MS⊂C spheres is shown schematically in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0001"/> c. Firstly, V‐glycerate spheres as the precursor are prepared by a facile solvothermal method. The V‐glycerate spheres are highly uniform with a diameter of about 400 nm (Supporting Information, Figure S1). The V‐glycerate solid spheres are of amorphous structure (Figure S2), which can be easily converted into V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres by a simple thermal sulfurization in H<sub>2</sub>S/Ar atmosphere. At the beginning of the reaction, the V‐glycerate first decomposes to produce V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> nanocrystals embedded in a carbon matrix that is created in the reducing environment. With increasing calcination temperature, the sulfidation reaction starts with an exchange of terminal O<sup>2−</sup> ligands of the oxide for S<sup>2−</sup> by reaction with H<sub>2</sub>S to form the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>. Because of the limited reaction space inside the carbon matrix, undesired particle growth is effectively suppressed during sulfurization, resulting in small V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanocrystals embedded in a carbon matrix.</p><p>Scanning electron microscopy (SEM), transmission electron microscopy (TEM), and X‐ray diffraction (XRD) were employed to investigate the morphology and structure of the different materials obtained. From the XRD pattern (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> a), all Bragg peaks can be indexed to the hexagonal phase V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS card No. 37‐1115),<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0026"/> demonstrating that the V‐glycerate precursor is completely converted into phase‐pure V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> via thermal sulfidation. No significant diffraction peaks of carbon are observed, demonstrating its amorphous state. The low‐magnification SEM examination reveals that the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres are still quite uniform, with diameters of about 400 nm (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> b, Figure S3). The enlarged SEM image in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> c shows that the surface of the spheres is very rough and additional coated by small nanoplates. TEM was used to further investigate the microstructure and to obtain chemical information of the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres. The TEM images (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> d,e) and energy‐filtered TEM (EFTEM) (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> f) demonstrated spherical morphology with an average diameter of about 400 nm which is in good agreement with SEM results. Many primary nanocrystals embedded in the carbon matrix are observed from the TEM image in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> g. A thin layer of carbon derived from the carbonization of organic residues coated on the surface of primary nanocrystals works as electronic contact to all intimately connected nanocrystals. The thickness of the nanoplate was determined to be about 5 atomic layers from the projection of its wrinkle in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> h. Moreover, the interlayer spacing in the edge area is found to be 0.57 nm, corresponding to the (100) plane of V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (JCPDS: 37‐1115, <i>d</i>=0.569 nm). An atomic‐resolution image of the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> is shown in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> i. The periodic lattice fringe spaces were found to be 0.255 nm, corresponding to the interlayer spacing of (204) plane for hexagonal V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>. Elemental mapping analysis based on energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) demonstrates uniform distribution of carbon‐coating layers on/in V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> spheres (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0002"/> j), which in addition to suppressing of the particles’ growth also improves electric interconnectivity of the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> nanocrystals. In addition, a hollow structured V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> can be confirmed, which might be caused by Ostwald ripening effect.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0027 #anie201901840-bib-0028"/> The hierarchical V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres provide a very effective mixed‐conducting network together with the greatly reduced sodium diffusion length within the particles, which is expected to alleviate the problems associated with conversion reactions.</p><figure xml:id="anie201901840-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201901840:anie201901840-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201901840-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) XRD pattern, b),c) SEM images, d),e) TEM images, and f) EFTEM thickness mapping of V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres. g),h),i) HRTEM images, inset in (h) is the intensity profile along the arrow indicate interlayer spacings. j) EFTEM elemental mapping: bright‐field TEM image and corresponding carbon‐k, vanadium‐L, and sulfur‐L maps.</p></caption></figure><p>Importantly, the present approach is simple and potentially general, which can be used to synthesize other hierarchical MS⊂C spheres. For example, uniform Mn‐glycerate spheres with a diameter of around 1–1.5 μm can be synthesized by a similar solvothermal method (Figure S4). After a simple thermal sulfurization, the Mn‐glycerate spheres were successfully converted into hierarchical MnS⊂C spheres. All characteristic peaks are in good agreement with the standard card of cubic MnS (JCPDS card No. 88‐2223) without impurity (Figure S5a).<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0029"/> Note the perfectly preserved spherical morphology of Mn‐glycerate precursor (Figure S5b). The surface of the MnS⊂C spheres, however, is very rough, suggesting that the spheres are composed of primary nanoparticle subunits with an average size of around 20–40 nm, as confirmed by an enlarged SEM image (Figure S5c). The TEM image further reveals that these MnS⊂C spheres are highly porous, with mesopores being uniformly distributed across the particles (Figure S5e). The HRTEM image (Figure S5f) clearly shows the carbon layer coating on the surface of the MnS particles and hence forms the core–shell structure. In addition, the uniform carbon layers on/in hierarchical MnS spheres can be demonstrated by the C, Mn and S elemental mapping of MnS⊂C spheres (Figure S5d). Our strategy was further extended to synthesize hierarchical cobalt sulfide spheres. Highly uniform Co‐glycerate spheres with a diameter of around 600 nm were successfully synthesized (Figure S6). Then, Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>⊂C spheres were formed in situ by thermal sulfurization of Co‐glycerate. Figure S5h shows the XRD pattern of Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub>⊂C spheres, in which all of the diffraction peaks can be assigned to cubic Co<sub>3</sub>S<sub>4</sub> phase (JCPDS Card No. 19‐0367). SEM image (Figure S5i,j) show that the spherical morphology is well retained but the surface is very rough. It is found that the spheres were composed of large amounts of small crystallites retained by a thin carbon coating shell (Figure S5g).</p><p>To identify the targeted design of hierarchical V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres, a coin‐type cell configuration was used to evaluate its electrochemical properties. Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0003"/> a show the charge–discharge profiles of the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres electrode for the first five cycles at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres electrode delivers discharge and charge capacities of 850 and 777 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> during the first cycle, respectively, with a high Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 91.4 %. A large voltage plateau at about 1 V associated with the irreversible reaction during the initial sodiation is usually observed for traditional metal sulfides.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0012 #anie201901840-bib-0024 #anie201901840-bib-0030 #anie201901840-bib-0031 #anie201901840-bib-0032"/> Noticeably, the discharge–charge profiles of V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C are sloping. This CE value is obviously higher than other metal sulfide electrodes that usually suffer from low CE of &lt;80 % (Table S1). The high CE of V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C might be attributed to synergistic effects associated with the small nano active materials, a robust architecture with an efficient electron/ion mixed‐conducting network. The differential capacity (d<i>Q</i>) versus potential curve of the potentiodynamic cycling with galvanostatic acceleration (PCGA) analysis of the first five cycles was present in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0003"/> b. A sharp peak around 0.1 V is observed during the first discharge and disappearing from the second cycle, which can be ascribed to some side reactions during the initial sodiation process. Because of its unique architecture, the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres exhibit an excellent rate performance (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0003"/> c). The V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres can be reversibly cycled at 200, 500, 1000, and 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> with specific capacities of 700, 623, 561, and 484 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> respectively. Note that the capacities still exceed 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The pristine V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> was prepared (Figure S7) and compared with V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres. The pristine V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> electrode can only maintain discharge capacities of 332, 189, and 104 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1000, 2000, and 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0003"/> d). The V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C electrode exhibited a much higher storage capacity and much better rate capability than pristine V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub> (Figure S8). The rate capability of V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C is apparently superior to many other vanadium based sulfides, such as VS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0025"/> VS<sub>4</sub>,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0033"/> CuV<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>,<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0034"/> and FeV<sub>2</sub>S<sub>4</sub>.<link href="#anie201901840-bib-0035"/> To further verify the stable cycle performance of the V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C electrodes, we cycle the cell for 600 cycles at 1500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, as depicted in Figure <link href="#anie201901840-fig-0003"/> e. The capacity loss with low CE was observed during the initial 15 cycles, which may result from sodiation‐induced slight mechanical degradation and the formation of unstable solid‐electrolyte interphase (SEI). After formation of well‐connected electron/ion mixed‐conducting network and stable SEI, continuous discharge–charge sodiation reactivates the electrode materials, increasing the Na storage capacity during subsequent cycles (15–50 cycles). Then the cells show a stable cycling stability without signs of capacity degradation up to 600 cycles. The cycling stability of hierarchical V<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>⊂C spheres exceeds most other metal sulfide‐based materials which typically suffer from limited cycle life (Table S1). It is demonstrated that this method is very effective in yielding hierarchical nano/microspherical materials with a conductive matrix that formed in situ without the addition of external carbon sources.
