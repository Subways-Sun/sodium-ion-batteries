type="main">Abstract</title><p>Bismuth is a promising anode material for state‐of‐the‐art rechargeable batteries due to its high theoretical volumetric capacity and relatively low working potential. However, its charge storage mechanism is unclear, hindering further improvement of the cell performance. Here, using in situ transmission electron microscopy and X‐ray diffraction techniques as well as theoretical analysis, it is found that a large anisotropic volume expansion of 142% occurs along the <i>z</i>‐axis largely due to the alloy reaction during sodiation, significantly reducing the electrochemical performance of bismuth electrodes. To address this problem, ultrathin few‐layer bismuthene with a large aspect ratio is rationally synthesized, and can relieve the expansion strain along the <i>z</i>‐axis. A free‐standing bismuthene/graphene composite electrode with tunable thickness achieves a strikingly stable and high areal sodium storage capacity of 12.1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup>, which greatly exceeds that of most reported electrode materials. The clarification of the charge storage mechanism and the superior areal capacity achieved should facilitate the development of bismuth‐based high‐performance anodes for practical electrochemical energy‐storage applications.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201807874-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>The charge storage mechanism of a bismuth electrode</b> with a large anisotropic volume expansion along the <i>z</i>‐axis is investigated by in situ techniques. A bismuthene/graphene composite electrode, which can relieve expansion strain along the <i>z</i>‐axis, is rationally designed and shows a strikingly stable and high areal sodium‐storage capacity with promise for practical applications.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201807874:adma201807874-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource xml:id="adma201807874-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201807874-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are drawing intensive attention as a potential and attractive alternative to lithium‐ion batteries due to the virtually inexhaustible and widely available sodium resources.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0001 #adma201807874-bib-0002 #adma201807874-bib-0003 #adma201807874-bib-0004 #adma201807874-bib-0005"/> Considering future practical commercial applications of SIBs, their areal capacities with high mass loading are crucial for whole cells.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0006 #adma201807874-bib-0007 #adma201807874-bib-0008 #adma201807874-bib-0009 #adma201807874-bib-0010"/> Despite intensive efforts, most electrode materials to date exhibit lower areal capacities than graphite anodes.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0011 #adma201807874-bib-0012 #adma201807874-bib-0013 #adma201807874-bib-0014"/> One of the major scientific challenges for SIBs resides in developing new electrode materials with high areal capacities.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0015 #adma201807874-bib-0016 #adma201807874-bib-0017 #adma201807874-bib-0018 #adma201807874-bib-0019"/> Alloying‐based materials can work as high‐capacity electrodes to replace graphitic carbon.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0018 #adma201807874-bib-0019"/> In particular, bismuth (Bi) is an appealing anode candidate for SIBs because of its high theoretical volumetric capacity and favorable sodium alloy potential.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0020 #adma201807874-bib-0021 #adma201807874-bib-0022 #adma201807874-bib-0023"/></p><p>Bismuth, the last element of the pnictogens (i.e., group VA), is a well‐known low‐cost, nontoxic, and environment‐friendly metal with a high electronic conductivity and chemical properties similar to those of its lighter homologs phosphorus and antimony.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0024 #adma201807874-bib-0025 #adma201807874-bib-0026"/> In terms of appearance and structure, bismuth closely resembles graphite: it is gray and flaky with a puckered layered crystal structure composed of six‐membered rings<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0026"/> (Figure S1, Supporting Information). Its interlayer spacing along the <i>c</i>‐axis is 3.979 Å, which remarkably facilitates the diffusion of lithium (1.52 Å) and sodium (2.04 Å) ions,<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0026 #adma201807874-bib-0027"/> rendering it a very promising anode material for rechargeable batteries. Regrettably, bismuth generally shows a large capacity decay<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0028"/> due to severe pulverization caused by a large volume change of 352% during sodiation and desodiation processes<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0029"/> (Table S1, Supporting Information). More importantly, the specific expansion mechanism of bismuth during battery operation has not yet been clarified. Thus, unraveling the charge storage mechanism and proposing a rational design for sophisticated nanostructures to accommodate volume expansion and optimize the performance of bismuth‐based SIBs is relevant and urgent.</p><p>Here, we discover that sodium storage in bismuth proceeds through sodium‐ion insertion followed by reversible crystalline‐phase evolution, which results in a large anisotropic volume change along the <i>z</i>‐axis. Hence, the 2D structure of few‐layer bismuthene (FLB) was specifically designed, and its expansion direction perpendicular to the flake surface is not constrained.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0030 #adma201807874-bib-0031"/> Moreover, such ultrathin FLB with a large aspect ratio can be used as building blocks to construct uniform, compact, and stable electrodes with graphene, giving rise to a high reversible capacity, excellent rate performance, long cycle stability, and superior areal capacity as an anode material for SIBs.</p><p>First, to gain atomic‐level insights into the structural stability and electrochemical properties of electrode materials, we first conducted a series of first‐principles calculations on Bi and its two alloy compounds, NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, using the Vienna ab initio simulation package (VASP) (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a). Structurally, bismuth is composed of puckered sheets with an average layer‐to‐layer distance of 3.979 Å, and the calculated lattice parameters are <i>a</i> = <i>b</i> = 4.546 Å and <i>c</i> = 11.802 Å.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0029 #adma201807874-bib-0032"/> Sodium ions are first inserted between the bismuth layers along the <i>x</i>‐axis‐oriented channels because only the channels along the <i>x</i>‐axis are wide enough (5.62 Å) to allow sodium ion diffusion; those along the <i>y</i>‐axis are too narrow (0.71 Å). To simplify the structural construction of NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, we focus on the arrangement of Bi atoms, which interestingly retains the form of the 2D layer structure (Figure S2, Supporting Information). Based on the simplified framework, the distance between adjacent Bi atoms in NaBi expands by 141% along the <i>z</i>‐axis and contracts by 24% along the <i>x</i>‐axis or <i>y</i>‐axis during alloying. In terms of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, an expansion of 142% occurs along the <i>z</i>‐axis, while only 16% expansion occurs along the <i>x</i>‐axis or <i>y</i>‐axis direction (Figure S3, Supporting Information). Thus, most of the expansion during battery operation should originate from the <i>z</i>‐axis.</p><figure xml:id="adma201807874-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201807874:adma201807874-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201807874-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>In situ TEM experiments. a) Schematics of structural evolution of bismuth to Na<sub>3</sub>Bi via NaBi during electrochemical sodiation. Purple: Bi; Yellow: Na. b) High‐resolution TEM image of a pristine bismuth flake. c–e) Time‐lapse TEM image of sodiation in bismuth.</caption></figure><p>To directly observe and quantitatively assess the anisotropic expansion of the bismuth anode upon electrochemical operation, we further conducted in situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) observations<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0033 #adma201807874-bib-0034 #adma201807874-bib-0035"/> (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0001"/>b–e and Figure S4, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0001"/>b) of a pristine bismuth flake reveals its single crystalline nature with a well‐resolved fringe spacing of 0.231 nm. Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0001"/>c–e presents sequential TEM images of the bismuth flake prior to and after in situ sodiation, where an ≈13% expansion along the <i>x</i>/<i>y</i>‐axis is visible after potentiostatic sodiation. In contrast, a large volume expansion (≈349% and ≈337%) is observed for the nanoparticles (marked by red arrows) during the reaction, which is consistent with the theoretical volume expansion (≈352%) for full sodiation of bismuth to form Na<sub>3</sub>Bi. A significant expansion of ≈170% is inferred to take place along the <i>z</i>‐axis of the bismuth flake, which is in line with the theoretical analysis of the crystal structure. Therefore, minimizing the thickness of the bismuth layers would help lessen the stress that accumulates along the <i>z</i>‐axis.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0036"/></p><p>To further shed light on the impact of the resultant expansion on battery performance, we conducted in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) tests and electrochemical measurements with different voltage windows.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0036"/> The XRD pattern of the pristine electrode can be indexed to that of pure bismuth (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). The peaks representing the (003) and (110) planes shift to lower angles and are attenuated during the initial sodiation process (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0002"/>b), indicating that the distance between the bismuthene layers along the <i>z</i>‐axis increases. This increase can be attributed to the intercalation of sodium ions. The XRD peaks of bismuth are clearly visible until the potential reaches 0.75 V. These peaks indicate that the layered structure is preserved and that the host structure is not significantly altered during the intercalation process of sodium ions, thereby guaranteeing high reversibility upon cycling in the 0.75–2.1 V region (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0002"/>c). Nonetheless, the specific capacity is limited to a negligible value of 18 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> due to the intercalation of 0.16 sodium ions. Upon further sodiation to 0.59 V, the characteristic bismuth peaks for the (003), (012), (104), and (110) planes vanish completely, and peaks corresponding to the (001), (100), (101), (110), and (002) planes of NaBi exclusively appear due to the alloy reaction, resulting in increased capacity while poor stability (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0002"/>d).</p><figure xml:id="adma201807874-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201807874:adma201807874-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201807874-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Sodiation storage mechanism in bismuth. a) In situ XRD patterns of a bismuth anode operated at different states and charge/discharge capacities against the voltage during the initial cycle. b) XRD patterns within a selected 2θ range during the first cycle against the voltage. c–e) The charge–discharge profiles of bismuth electrodes at a current density of 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup> between 0.75–2.1, 0.59–2.1, and 0.1–2.1 V. f) Discharge capacity retention of bismuth electrodes between different voltage ranges at a current density of 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>Next, another phase transition during the second discharge plateau is clearly observed with the NaBi peaks weakening and the Na<sub>3</sub>Bi peaks strengthening. When the battery is fully discharged to 0.1 V, the XRD pattern can be well indexed to pure Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, indicating that the alloy reaction is close to completion. As the voltage window enlarges, alloying proceeds, and capacity increases; therefore, the alloying process is primarily responsible for the high specific capacity of bismuth (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0002"/>e). Nevertheless, the two alloying processes both induce a large volume expansion, which causes mechanical and structural fracture, leading to quick capacity decay during cycling (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0002"/>f). The XRD patterns can return to the original state after the subsequent charging process, demonstrating that the transformations among the three compounds are completely reversible. In conclusion, although the initial sodium‐ion intercalation does not significantly alter the host structure, a large volume change occurs mainly along the <i>z</i>‐axis during the alloying reaction, resulting in the large capacity loss upon cycling in SIBs, which implies the 2D atomic‐scale bismuthene an ideal electrode material.</p><p>2D FLB was synthesized on a large scale using a simple wet chemical method via a two‐step process of liquid‐phase exfoliation followed by room‐temperature reduction. The XRD patterns of FLB and bulk bismuth are very similar (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a) and can be indexed to a hexagonal structure with the space group of R‐3m (JCPDS No. 44‐1246),<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0021 #adma201807874-bib-0026"/> while the (003) peak of FLB shifts to a lower angle with an increasing full‐width at half‐maximum value, suggesting that FLB has an expanded <i>c</i>‐axis interlayer distance compared to that of bismuth powder (Figure S5, Supporting Information). The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image clearly reveals that the as‐prepared product consists of hierarchical microflowers assembled by ultrathin 2D nanosheets (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>b and Figure S6, Supporting Information). A typical TEM image in Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>c shows a free‐standing bismuth nanosheet, and the corresponding HRTEM image (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>d) clearly shows a lattice spacing of 0.229 nm, which can be ascribed to the (110) plane of rhombohedral bismuth and indicates a good crystalline nature. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) pattern of the circled area (inserted in Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>d) reveals that the zone axis is along the [001] direction. Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>e,f shows the low‐magnification high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) image and the corresponding energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping image of a polygonal bismuthene layer, and these images indicate that the crystal is composed of elemental bismuth. To further confirm the chemical composition of the as‐prepared products, we performed X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), and the results show the presence of Bi, C, and O without any impurities (Figure S7, Supporting Information). The corresponding high‐resolution spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>g) clearly reveals that the as‐prepared FLB sample has 4f<sub>7/2</sub> (Bi1) and 4f<sub>5/2</sub> (Bi2) doublets at 156.9 and 162.3 eV, respectively, which are characteristic of Bi bonding. In addition, a pair of low subbands are also observed at 158.7 and 164.1 eV, suggesting the presence of oxidized bismuth due to unavoidable oxidation during sample processing.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0037"/> The atomic force microscopy (AFM) image shows that the average topographic height is ≈4 nm (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0003"/>h), which is equivalent to ten atomic layers, and the lateral dimension is found to vary from 300 to 1500 nm. Such ultrathin 2D FLB may alleviate the large volume expansion that occurs during the alloying reaction and lessen the diffusion of both sodium ions and electrons, which are beneficial for sodium storage performance.</p><figure xml:id="adma201807874-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201807874:adma201807874-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201807874-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural characterization of FLB. a) XRD spectra of FLB and bulk bismuth. b) SEM image of FLB. c) TEM image of a typical bismuth flake.d) HRTEM image of a selected area in panel (b) with the measured lattice spacing. Inset: corresponding FFT pattern. e) Low‐magnification STEM image of a well‐shaped FLB. f) Bi element mapping. g) High‐resolution Bi 4f spectrum of FLB. h) AFM image and corresponding height profile.</caption></figure><p>As expected, the FLB‐based electrode displays a higher reversible capacity and slower capacity decay than bulk bismuth (Figure S8, Supporting Information). To further improve the cycle stability of the electrode and adjust the areal loading in a facile way, we fabricated FLB/graphene (FLB‐G) films with tunable thicknesses by vacuum filtration.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0038"/> The as‐prepared FLB‐G film is flexible and can be bent without fracture (see inset of <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a). Bi, C, and O are homogeneously distributed on the whole surface without any impurities, as shown in the EDS maps, XPS spectra and XRD patterns (Figures S9–S11, Supporting Information). Additionally, a typical cross‐section SEM image of the FLB‐G film (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0004"/>a) shows a continuous cross‐linked honeycomb foam structure, suggesting that the FLB and graphene strongly integrate with each other. This structure holds substantial promise for high‐areal‐capacity energy‐storage applications.</p><figure xml:id="adma201807874-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201807874:adma201807874-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201807874-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical characterization of FLB‐G. All specific capacities of the composite electrodes are based on the mass of the bismuthene. a) Cross‐sectional SEM image and a typical digital photograph of a FLB‐G film. b) CV curves of the FLB‐G electrode at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.1 and 2.1 V. c) GITT curves at the fifth cycle for FLB‐G. d) Rate performance of FLB‐G and graphene films at various current densities from 0.2 to 2.0 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>. e) Cycling performance of FLB‐G at a current density of 1.2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> after electrochemical pretreatment of 20 cycles at 0.05 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>.</caption></figure><p>As a proof‐of‐concept, we directly applied an as‐prepared free‐standing FLB‐G film with an areal loading of 1.7 mg cm<sup>−2</sup> as a SIB anode without any additional current collector or binder. A typical cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurement for the first ten cycles was carried out to investigate the redox behavior of the FLB‐G electrode (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0004"/>b). The first cathodic process shows a broad reduction peak followed by two oxidation peaks in the anodic scan, representing an activation process caused by bismuth sodiation and solid–electrolyte interface film formation.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0021"/> During the subsequent cycles, the overlapping curves with two distinct couples of reversible redox peaks located at 0.64/0.75 and 0.43/0.62 V indicate the highly reversible formation of NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0021"/></p><p>To probe the sodiation and desodiation processes on the FLB‐G film electrode, we further conducted galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) experiments, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0004"/>c. In these experiments, the voltage variation in the plateau was very small, and the Coulombic efficiency was nearly 100%, indicating that the sodiation–desodiation processes are kinetically fast and close to equilibrium. The charge–discharge curves of the FLB‐G electrode exhibit a similar shape at different current densities, and the FLB‐G film possesses a stable capacity of 381 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 0.2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup>, while it still retains a high reversible capacity of 263.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> even at the high current density of 2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0004"/>d and Figure S12, Supporting Information), demonstrating excellent rate performance. In contrast, at such a high current density, pure graphene only possesses a low specific capacity of 3.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. Notably, the capacity can be fully recovered when the current density returns to 0.2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> after 60 cycles, indicating good stability after the high rate discharging and charging test. Furthermore, the long‐term cycle performance measurement (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0004"/>e) demonstrates that the FLB‐G electrode delivers superior capacity retention after 1000 cycles with a reversible capacity of 317 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a current density of 1.2 mA cm<sup>−2</sup> (Figure S13, Supporting Information). In addition, the nanosheets still remain in the composited film after long cycles (Figures S14 and S15, Supporting Information), suggesting the good stability of the few‐layer bismuthene during the charge–discharge process. Such a superior cycling stability is attributed to the ultrathin nature of the bismuthene which relieves the expansion strain along the <i>z</i>‐axis and its rational hybridization with graphene which acts as an elastic buffer to accommodate the large volume change and enhances the conductivity of the composite film.</p><p>To achieve a high areal capacity for potential practical applications, the performances of batteries with different mass loadings were investigated. A series of hybridized electrodes based on FLB‐G films with different thicknesses ranging from 10 to 143 µm (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a) were obtained by adjusting the volume of the mixed dispersion, and the corresponding mass loadings increased from 1.7 to ≈42 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. These electrodes display excellent stability with virtually no capacity loss after 50 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0005"/>b–d), and the capacity linearly scales with the areal loading, reaching an exceptional value of 12.1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> for the thickest electrode film and further increasing the mass loadings will make the composite films fragile. In addition, their potential profiles (Figure <link href="#adma201807874-fig-0005"/>e) are similar, which indicates that the increased areal loading may not create ion diffusion obstacles due to the highly interconnected network structure and provides superb electron transfer properties due to the high conductivity of graphene.</p><figure In particular, we identified a large anisotropic volume expansion of ≈142%, which mainly results from alloy reactions occurring along the <i>z</i>‐axis, leading to rapid capacity decay upon cycling. We rationally designed and successfully synthesized a 2D FLB that can alleviate the stress accumulated along the critical <i>z</i>‐axis and allow sodium ions to rapidly diffuse due to a shorter diffusion distance. We further directly employed free‐standing FLB‐G films with tunable thicknesses as the working electrode and obtained a striking stable and high areal capacity of 12.1 mAh cm<sup>−2</sup> with a thickness of 143 µm due to the conductive network architecture, and this capacity remarkably exceeds that of most previously reported electrode materials. Afterward, the resultant raw dispersion was centrifuged for 30 min at 500 rpm to remove the unexfoliated bismuth. Subsequently, 35 mL of an as‐prepared sodium borohydride solution (1.0 mg mL<sup>−1</sup>) was added dropwise and stirred for 30 min. The black flocculant was collected by centrifugation and washed several times with deionized water and ethanol. After freeze‐drying under vacuum, the target product was finally obtained.</p><p><i>Synthesis of Flexible FLB‐G Films</i>: The FLB dispersion was then mixed uniformly with a graphene oxide dispersion in a mass ratio of 8:2. Free‐standing FLB‐G films with different mass loadings (1.7, 2.3, 5.1, 9.6, 14.6, 20.1, 25.0, 29.7, 34.8, and 42.1 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>) were prepared by direct vacuum filtration of the resultant mixed dispersion through a polyester membrane with a pore size of 0.15 µm. Subsequently, the free‐standing films were placed in an autoclave with 200 µL hydrazine monohydrate (98%) and heated at 80 °C for 10 h. After further drying in a vacuum oven at 60 °C overnight, homogeneously flexible FLB‐G films were obtained.</p><p><i>Characterization</i>: The morphology, structure, and composition of the samples were systematically investigated by field‐emission SEM (JEOL‐7500), TEM (Tecnai G2 F20 UYWIN), XPS (ESCALAB 250), and XRD (Rigaku Dmax 7000). XRD analysis was carried out from 20° to 80° using Cu Kα radiation. The thickness of FLB was determined by atomic force microscopy (Bruker Multimodel‐8).</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurements</i>: Battery performance was evaluated by galvanostatic cycling of CR 2032 coin‐type cells with FLB‐G films as the working electrode without any binders or conductive additives and sodium foil as the counter electrode. The electrolyte consisted of a solution of 1.0 mol L<sup>−1</sup> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme, and the glass fiber film (Whatman) served as the separator. The cells were assembled in an argon‐filled glove box with water, and the oxygen content was maintained below 0.1 ppm. Galvanostatic discharge–charge measurements were conducted on a Land CT2001A battery tester at various current densities with the potential ranging from 0.1 to 2.1 V versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup>. CV and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were carried out using a Solartron 1470E electrochemical workstation. The EIS measurements were performed at the open circuit potential with a sinusoidal signal in a frequency range from 100 kHz to 0.01 Hz at an amplitude of 5 mV.</p><p><i>In Situ TEM Experiments</i>: The in situ measurements were conducted using a JEOL 2100 plus TEM operated at 200 kV. The in situ TEM electrochemical cell was incorporated into a Nanofactory TEM‐STM specimen holder in which the FLB was dispersed onto amorphous carbon films supported on copper half‐grids. The carbon film played a similar role to that of the carbon black used in the general bismuth‐carbon composite electrode, and Na metal was coated onto a piezo‐driven W probe as the counter electrode. A thin layer of Na<sub>2</sub>O formed on Na metal as the solid electrolyte. All of the components were assembled in an argon glove box and transferred into the TEM column using a sealed Ar‐filled bag to avoid air exposure. During the in situ electrochemical tests, a constant negative direct current potential was applied to the bismuth electrode against the Na source during the sodiation process. The sodiation processes were captured by real‐time imaging in TEM mode.</p><p><i>In Situ XRD</i>: The in situ XRD data were acquired using a homemade SIB cell with an inner diameter of 18 mm. The FLB‐G free‐standing film was directly used as the electrode. A sodium plate was used as the counter electrode, 1.0 mol L<sup>−1</sup> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme was used as the electrolyte, and a Walkman glass fiber was used as the separator. The XRD chamber was equipped with a Be window to allow X‐ray passage. In situ XRD patterns of the electrodes were recorded by a Rigaku Dmax 7000 X‐ray diffractometer in a scanning range of 15°–43° (2θ) with a step size of 0.02° s<sup>−1</sup> in the time‐step mode. The corresponding galvanostatic charge–discharge cycling was carried out at a current density of 30 mA g<sup>−1</sup> between 0.1 and 2.1 V versus Na/Na<sup>+</sup>.</p><p><i>Computational Details and Models</i>: The density functional theory calculations were carried out by the plane‐wave technique with exchange‐correlation interactions modeled using the functional Perdew–Burke–Ernzerhof form of the generalized gradient approximation as implemented in the VASP.<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0057"/> The ion‐electron interactions were described by the projector augmented plane‐wave approach,<link href="#adma201807874-bib-0058"/> and the cutoff energy was set to 500 eV. Structural optimizations were performed by minimizing the forces on all atoms to below 0.02 eV·Å<sup>−1</sup> and the energy to below 10<sup>−5</sup>eV. A 3 × 3 × 1 k‐point grid was used to sample the k‐space for the 3 × 3 supercell of the bismuthene monolayer. The vacuum layer was set to 15 Å to avoid the interaction between neighboring images. All the atoms in the slab were allowed to relax during the geometrical optimization process. The transition state structure of Na diffusion on the bismuthene monolayer was determined by the climbing image‐nudged elastic band method and was further confirmed by imaginative frequency. The adsorption energy of the Na atoms at different sites was calculated by the equation <i>E</i><sub>ad</sub> = <i>E</i><sub>total</sub> − <i>E</i><sub>Na</sub> − <i>E</i><sub>Bi‐monolayer,</sub> where <i>E</i><sub>total</sub>, <i>E</i><sub>Na</sub>, and <i>E</i><sub>Bi‐monolayer</sub> are the total energy of the bismuth monolayer with Na adsorption, a Na atom in a vacuum, and the pure Bi monolayer, respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201807874-sec-0030" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>J.Z. and J.C. contributed equally to this work.
