Therefore, development of high‐voltage O3‐type cathodes remains challenging because it is difficult to raise the phase‐transition voltage by reasonable structure modulation. A new example of O3‐type sodium insertion materials is presented for use in NIBs. The designed O3‐type Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> material displays a highest redox potential of 3.7 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) among the reported O3‐type materials based on the Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> couple, by virtue of its increased Ni−O bond ionicity through reduced orbital overlap between transition metals and oxygen within the MO<sub>2</sub> slabs. This study provides an orbital‐level understanding of the operating potentials of the nominal redox couples for O3‐NaMO<sub>2</sub> cathodes. The strategy described could be used to tailor electrodes for improved performance.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Batteries included</b>: An O3‐type sodium insertion material, Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, displays a high redox potential of 3.7 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na (based on the Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> couple). Reduced orbital hybridization between the oxygen 2p and nickel 3d orbitals within the metallic layers increases the Ni−O bond ionicity. Good structural reversibility is obtained because the O3‐type phase transforms reversibly into a P3‐type phase.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201804130:anie201804130-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201804130-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="anie201804130-ntgp-0001"><note xml:id="anie201804130-note-1001"><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201804130-sec-0001"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (NIBs) have recently attracted much interest as low‐cost and sustainable alternatives to lithium‐ion batteries—especially because the abundance of Na resources render the technology suitable for future large‐scale energy storage initiatives.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0001"/> In realizing key‐enabling materials for NIBs, various Na‐intercalation compounds, such as oxides,<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0002"/> phosphates,<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0003"/> hexacyanoferrates,<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0004"/> and organics<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0005"/> have been explored as possible insertion cathodes. Layered transition‐metal oxides Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MO<sub>2</sub> (M: transition metal), one of the most well‐studied candidates, have been classified into P2 and O3 phases by Delmas et al. according to the Na occupation sites and repeated unit cell.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0006"/> In a P2 layered structure (Na/M&lt;0.8), Na<sup>+</sup> ions occupy prismatic sites where the close‐packed oxide‐ion layers stack in an ABBA manner while Na<sup>+</sup> ions in the O3 phase (Na/M&gt;0.8) occupy octahedral sites and the oxide‐ion framework is packed closely in an ABCABC pattern.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0007"/></p><p>Sodium‐based O3‐type layered oxides have to bear complex structural evolution and electrochemical behaviors during deep (de)intercalation of Na ions because of the larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) compared to Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å).<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0008"/> Na ions in the O3‐type layered structure are usually stabilized at edge‐shared octahedral sites with minimized O–O interlayer repulsion. Na<sup>+</sup> ions migrate through the edge‐sharing intermediate tetrahedral sites because a high activation energy barrier prevents diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions from one octahedral site to another. This phenomenon is analogous to the Li<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion path in layered Li<sub><i>x</i></sub>MO<sub>2</sub>, which is believed to impose a high energy barrier on Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion because occupation of tetrahedral sites by comparatively large Na<sup>+</sup> ions is unfavorable. Na vacancies are thus created at face‐shared tetrahedral sites with MO<sub>6</sub> octahedra to reduce Na–Na repulsion. The transition from O3 to P3 is thus easily achieved by the gliding of MO<sub>2</sub> slabs without breaking M−O bonds.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0009"/> This reaction mechanism has two major effects on the electrochemical behavior of the O3 phase: 1) easy access between hexagonal O3 and P3 phases is expected to give rise to low voltage plateaus (average 3 V vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) in most of the Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MO<sub>2</sub> materials, with the result that nearly half of the capacity is distributed in this region; 2) substituting inactive metal ions with a compatible electronic state into the crystal lattice fails to suppress the phase transition from O3 to P3.</p><p>It is critical to understand the factors that determine the redox potential of O3‐type oxides, and to develop a rational strategy to control the operating voltage. Herein, we report a new cathode material, Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, with the stoichiometric composition of a P2 phase, which is abnormally related to an O3‐type layered structure. Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Sn<sup>4+</sup> cationic disordering occurs because the cations possess the same ionic radii (Ni<sup>2+</sup> (0.69 Å) and Sn<sup>4+</sup> (0.69 Å)). The electrochemistry and structural evolution of the Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compound were comprehensively analyzed using experimental and computational techniques. Sn<sup>4+</sup> ([Kr] 4d<sup>10</sup>) does not possess single d electrons and is thus unable to interact with O through its d orbitals. Orbital hybridization only occurs between O and Ni, and charge delocalization in the metallic layer is thus prevented, and the iconicity of Ni−O bond is increased to a large extent. In this case, O3‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> displays a highly suitable Ni redox potential of about 3.7 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) owing to the Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> redox. A combined analysis of macroscale in situ X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and atomic‐scale scanning transmission electron microscopy (STEM) reveals that the O3‐type phase transforms reversibly into a P3‐type phase until 0.35 Na is removed from the structure. Importantly, this 3.7 V voltage is by far the highest Ni redox potential reported to date for O3‐type Na‐based layered materials, and it is competitive among NIB cathode hosts.</p><p>The O3‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0.7, 0.8, 0.9, and 1.0) compounds were synthesized by a classical solid‐state reaction. All the diffraction lines can be indexed to a rhombohedral symmetry with a space group of <i>R</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie201804130-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201804130:anie201804130-math-0001"/><i>m</i> (Supporting Information, Figure S1), which is isostructural with <i>α</i>‐NaFeO<sub>2</sub>. Excess Sn ultimately results in phase separation for <i>x</i>=0.6 and 0.5 (Figure S2). Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> a presents XRD patterns and Rietveld refinement of the typical O3‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sample, giving the lattice parameters <i>a</i>=<i>b</i>=3.1044(9) Å, <i>c</i>=16.638(2) Å, and <i>V</i>=138.87(3) Å<sup>3</sup> with a reliability index (<i>R</i><sub>wp</sub>) of 6.58 % (Supporting Information, Table S1). The structural illustration of O3‐type Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> a inset) indicates that Ni/Sn ions occupy the octahedral 3b Wyckoff sites of the Ni(Sn)O<sub>2</sub> slabs, while Na ions are accommodated in the octahedral 3a sites of the NaO<sub>2</sub> layer. In theory, when a Na off‐stoichiometry condition (typically 0.6&lt;<i>x</i>&lt;0.8 in Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MO<sub>2</sub>) is applied to materials synthesis, the P2‐type phase with Na in prismatic sites is empirically known to be structurally stabilized.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0010"/> However, in the Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.7≤<i>x</i>≤1.0) phase‐stability diagram, the O3 structure with Na in octahedral sites is abnormally preferred (especially for <i>x</i>=0.7) because it minimizes both O–O interlayer repulsions and total thermodynamic energy determined by density functional theory (DFT; Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> b). The lattice parameters of other O3‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=0.8, 0.9, and 1.0) samples are also refined in Figure S3 and the corresponding crystallographic parameters are listed in Tables S2–S4. Notably, the changes in the Na layer distance (d‐spacing) and interslab distance increase monotonously with increasing Sn<sup>4+</sup> in the structure (Figure S4, Table S5). According to the refined structures, the Na layer spacing is increased from 3.220 Å (NaNi<sub>0.5</sub>Sn<sub>0.5</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) to 3.379 Å (Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) thus further reducing the O–O interlayer repulsions, which accounts for the improved phase stability over a wide range of Na stoichiometries.</p><figure xml:id="anie201804130-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201804130:anie201804130-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201804130-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The XRD and Rietveld plots of Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> materials. b) The calculated total energy of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at different Na concentrations. c) The SEM image, d,f) ABF images and e,g) HAADF‐STEM images at the [010] ((e); scale bar=0.5 nm) and [210] ((g); scale bar=0.5 nm) zone axes. h) EDS maps of Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples.</p></caption></figure><p>The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image displayed in Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> c reveals that layered Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples are composed of interconnected 100–500 nm primary particles. The morphologies of other O3‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples (<i>x</i>=0.8, 0.9 and 1.0) are shown in Figure S5. Detailed atomic‐scale structural information on layered Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples was confirmed by annular bright‐field (ABF) and high‐angle annular dark‐field (HAADF)‐STEM techniques. The dark dots in the ABF‐STEM images, and the white dots in the HAADF‐STEM images represent the Ni and Sn atom positions. The positions of the light elements Na and O contained within columns in the layered structure are revealed by faint points of contrast on the interlayer positions in the ABF‐STEM images.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0011"/> The ABF‐STEM images of octahedral MO<sub>2</sub> at the [010] zone axis are consistent with the O3 atomic model (see Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> d inset for convenient visualization), where Na atoms are clamped by layered MO<sub>2</sub>, and O columns stack in an ABCABC mode. The distance of the adjacent layer <i>d</i><sub>c</sub> in the HAADF‐STEM images (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> e) is approximately 0.55 nm, corresponding to the interlayer distance determined from refined crystallographic data. The slightly misplaced Ni/Sn atoms (viewed along the [201] zone axis in Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> f,g) indicates the frequent occurrence of stacking faults in the layered materials. Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) was performed to determine the crystal structure of Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> in detail. A distance of 2.41 Å between neighboring lattice fringes was revealed by high‐resolution TEM image and corresponds to the (012) planes in O3‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figures S6a,b). Bright spots in the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns (Figure S6c) are indexed to the corresponding reflections arising from the O3‐type layered structure. Energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping of the crystallites (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0001"/> h) reveals homogeneous distribution of Na, Ni, Sn, and O elements in the particles.</p><p>The electrochemical properties of various O3‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples were evaluated in Na half cells. Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0002"/> a shows the first three cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of the Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with one pair of reversible cathodic/anodic peaks located at 3.88 and 3.69 V, respectively. These potentials are very “abnormal” for Ni‐based O3‐type Na oxides, although a similar potential was reported for P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Te<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0012"/> The two redox peaks correspond to the de/intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions from two distinct lattice sites (octahedral in the O3 structure and prismatic in the P3 phase, as discussed in the proceeding text) in the crystal structure and involve the Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> couple, as confirmed by XAS studies (see proceeding discussion). Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0002"/> b compares the typical charge/discharge voltage profiles of the Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode materials between 2.0 and 4.0 V at a C/10 rate (1 C=128 mA g<sup>−1</sup>). The reversible discharge specific capacity of Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was measured to be 64 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding 0.35 Na extraction from the crystal structure. The discharge profile consists of a 3.73 V plateau accompanied by the P3–O3 phase transition, and followed by a sloping region above 3.5 V. During the subsequent cycles, the reversible capacity stabilizes at about 60 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 80 % after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0002"/> c). As the evaluated rate capability in a Na cell (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0002"/> d), the reversible capacities are 64, 59, 54, and 50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, based on fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion (10<sup>−12</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>; as shown in Figure S7 at 0.1C, 0.2C, 0.5C, and 1C, respectively). Increasing Sn incorporation in O3‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> increases the redox potential (3.73, 3.56, 3.36, and 3.27 V for <i>x=</i>0.7, 0.8, 0.9, and 1.0; Figure S8). Furthermore, independently of the Ni/Sn ratio, all Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrodes show a reversible (de)intercalation of almost 50 % Na per unit formula based on the Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> redox (Figure S9). The charge compensation mechanism of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion was elucidated by X‐ray absorption near‐edge structure (XANES) spectra. The Ni K‐edge absorption spectrum (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0003"/> a) shows apparent shifts of the edge position toward the higher energy region (about 2 eV), demonstrating that Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> redox couple is responsible for the charge compensation mechanism (1 e<sup>−</sup> transfer) as charge increases from 2.0 to 4.0 V.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0013"/> In contrast, the Sn K‐edges (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0003"/> b) show no apparent change, indicating that the tetravalent tin ions are electrochemically inactive. The interatomic distances of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are also revealed by extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectra at the corresponding Ni and Sn K‐edges (Figures <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0003"/> c,d). In the first Ni−O coordination shell of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and consistent with the XANES results, the Ni K‐edge presents a lowered Fourier transform (FT) magnitude and shortened interatomic distance, while the Sn K‐edge shows nearly no change upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction, which is a result of the Ni<sup>2+</sup> oxidation‐induced Jahn–Teller effect (Figure S13, Tables S6 and S7). In the Ni−M (M=Sn/Ni) and Sn−M second coordination shells, both Ni and Sn K‐edges confirm a slight shift of the decreased in‐plane interatomic distances after electrochemical oxidation, which matches well with the minor change of lattice parameter <i>a</i> upon Na removal, as revealed by in situ XRD measurements described in the proceeding text.</p><figure xml:id="anie201804130-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201804130:anie201804130-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201804130-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a,b) Ex situ XANES spectra at the Ni K‐edge (a) and Sn K‐edge (b). c,d) The corresponding ex situ EXAFS spectra at the Ni K‐edge (c) and Sn K‐edge (d) of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrodes collected at different charge/discharge states.</p></caption></figure><p>The structural evolution obtained by an operando XRD study during the first charge/discharge cycle is displayed in Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0004"/> a. Upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction beyond <i>x</i>≈0.35 in Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, the intensity of the (104)<sub>O3</sub> peak clearly disappeared while new (012) and (015) diffraction lines located at 2<i>θ</i>=37.35° and 46.78° appeared, demonstrating the formation of a new P3 phase by a biphasic process occurring over a very narrow Na composition domain (&lt;0.02). Subsequent desodiation results in a complete P3 solid solution region without any appearance of new peaks in the XRD pattern up until <i>x</i>≈0.27, which is further supported by TEM characterization (Figure S14). The (00l) peaks shifted to a lower angle during Na<sup>+</sup> extraction, thereby indicating a slightly enlarged interslab distance for the new P3 phase based on the Bragg equation (2<i>d</i>sin<i>θ</i>=<i>n</i>λ). Furthermore, following a complete cycle, and even 100 cycles, the XRD pattern and morphology are completely recovered (Figures S15 and S16), suggesting good structural reversibility. Further analysis on in situ XRD data affords us a good 2D contour map (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0004"/> b) and 3D visual map (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0004"/> c) of the O3–P3 structural changes in Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> materials. In total, Sn introduction into the O3‐type structure elevates the redox voltage, delays the O3–P3 phase transition, and extends the solid solution zone over a wider compositional range.</p><figure xml:id="anie201804130-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201804130:anie201804130-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201804130-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Operando XRD data for the Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode during the first cycle. The inset structures illustrate a complete O3–P3 phase transition according to the gliding of the MO<sub>2</sub> layers. Part of the MO<sub>2</sub> slabs in the O3 phase (originally stabilized at edge‐shared octahedral sites) glides toward (1/3, 2/3, 0) and is associated with the formation of vacancies; thereby resulting in a P3 phase with connected edge‐ and face‐sharing prismatic Na sites. The evolution of the lattice parameters of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> phases during Na<sup>+</sup> deintercalation is also plotted in Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0005"/> a as a function of the Na content. Note that the <i>a</i> lattice parameters show a monotonous decrease with decreasing Na content, which was attributed to the contraction of the NiO<sub>6</sub> octahedron upon Ni<sup>2+</sup> oxidation to Ni<sup>3+</sup>. The slight increase of <i>c</i> parameters upon charging is indicative of a slightly elongated interslab distance between adjacent O<sup>2−</sup> layers during Na removal.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0014"/> Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0.27≤<i>x</i>≤0.70) undergoes a relatively small volume change of approximately 0.6 % before and after Na extraction, which is beneficial to the cycling stability.</p><figure xml:id="anie201804130-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201804130:anie201804130-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201804130-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) GITT curves (top) and lattice parameters evolution (bottom) of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> as a function of Na<sup>+</sup> composition. b) The calculated DOS for Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. c) Molecular orbital and electronic structures of Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. d) Summary of average operating voltage for Ni‐based O3‐type compounds.</p></caption></figure><p>It is well‐established that holes are generated on the unoccupied O 2p and transition metal 3d orbitals at the Fermi level in desodiated NaMO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0015"/> On the basis of the calculated density of states (DOS) for Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> using ab initio calculations (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0005"/> b), the partial DOS (pDOS) of the Ni e<sub>g</sub> orbital is overlapped with that of the O 2p orbital, indicating the O 2p orbital at the Fermi level exhibits significant orbital hybridization with the Ni e<sub>g</sub> orbital. The electronic structure and molecular orbitals of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> are depicted in Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0005"/> c. Sn<sup>4+</sup> with filled d<sup>10</sup> electrons does not participate in hybridization within the MO<sub>2</sub> layers; the O 2p orbitals only hybridize with the Ni e<sub>g</sub> orbitals to delocalize the hole/electrons over both Ni and O. Thus a higher electronic density on O and an increased energy difference between Ni 3d and O 2p orbitals are achieved, leading to an increase in the Ni−O bond ionicity and Ni redox reaction, with superior high potential observed for Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201804130-bib-0016"/> For traditional NaNi<sub><i>y</i></sub>M′<sub>1−<i>y</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (M′=transition metals such as Fe, Co, Mn, and so on), where the unoccupied O 2p orbital hybridizes with both Ni e<sub>g</sub> and M′ e<sub>g</sub> at the Fermi level, the contribution from the Ni e<sub>g</sub> orbital to the frontier orbital is considerably smaller than that of NaNi<sub><i>y</i></sub>M′′<sub>1−<i>y</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (M′′=transition metals with no d electrons or heavier chalcogenides such as Ti, Sn, Sb, Bi, Te, and so on). Rather, NaNi<sub><i>y</i></sub>M′<sub>1−<i>y</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> generates a relatively lower voltage; such redox flexibility and versatility have been verified with experimental results (Figure <link href="#anie201804130-fig-0005"/> d). In comparison to the average operating voltage of the most common Ni‐based O3‐type cathodes studied in the literature, the new material, O3‐Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, offers a very competitive 3.7 V high voltage among transition metal layered oxides.</p><p>In summary, we have successfully synthesized a whole new family of O3‐type cathode materials with the general formula Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>/2</sub>Sn<sub>1−<i>x</i>/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The first such candidate, Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, delivers a reversible capacity of 64 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with a working Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>3+</sup> potential located at 3.7 V (versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na), the highest known value among all Ni‐based O3‐type insertion compounds. This abnormally high voltage observed for Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is explained by the reduced hybridization between the O 2p and M 3d orbitals in the MO<sub>2</sub> layers; hence the resulting increased Ni−O bond ionicity. Furthermore, the phase transition from O3 to P3 phases is largely delayed until the extraction of approximately 0.35 Na from Na<sub>0.7</sub>Ni<sub>0.35</sub>Sn<sub>0.65</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, manifesting a short biphasic process that occurs over a narrow Na composition domain (&lt;0.02) and a good structural reversibility. This strategy for engineering the chemical composition of O3‐type cathodes at a molecular orbital level provides new insight into the redox potential of NaMO<sub>2</sub> and will guide rational design of high‐voltage layered cathode materials in the future.</p><section xml:id="anie201804130-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Basic Science Center Project of National Natural Science Foundation of China (grant no. 51788104), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (grant nos. 51772301 and 21773264), the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (grant no. 2016YFA0202500), and the “Transformational Technologies for Clean Energy and Demonstration”, Strategic Priority Research Program of the Chinese Academy of Sciences (Grant No. XDA21070300).
