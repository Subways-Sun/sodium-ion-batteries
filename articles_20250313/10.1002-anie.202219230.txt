This exciting finding opens a new avenue towards high‐performance Mn‐based oxide cathodes for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A general approach of annealing in argon xml:id="anie202219230-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been regarded as a highly cost‐effective choice for grid‐scale energy storage systems by virtue of the abundant and worldwide available Na reserves.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Nevertheless, the practical energy density of SIBs (generally &lt;160 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>) is still inadequate to satisfy various application scenarios, which calls for cathodes (a bottleneck of battery performance) that can host more Na ions per unit mass.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> Layered transition metal (TM) oxides are one of the most potential candidates owing to their high specific capacities and rich chemical compositions.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0005"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Among them, the Mn‐dominant (Mn‐based) oxides hold the promise of fulfilling the low Cost‐Per‐kWh and high sustainability industrialization demands, since Mn element is inexpensive, earth‐abundant, and environmentally benign, in particular, Mn acts as a redox center with low atomic weight (54.94).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0009"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, some intrinsic issues concerning Mn redox, including the Jahn–Teller (J–T) effect and low redox potential (≈2.0 V), tend to favor Mn as a stabilizer (Mn<sup>4+</sup>) rather than a redox‐active center (Mn<sup>3+</sup>), thus compromising the advantages of Mn‐based layered oxides.</p><p>Generally, the J–T effect originates from the high‐spin Mn<sup>3+</sup> (t<sub>2g</sub><sup>3</sup>e<sub>g</sub><sup>1</sup>) with the degenerated 3<i>d</i> orbits, which is manifested as the elongation of two axial Mn−O bonds and the contraction of the other four equatorial bonds.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0013"><sup>[5]</sup></link> When the directions of distorted Mn<sup>3+</sup>O<sub>6</sub> octahedra behave collectively and collinearly, the cooperative J–T distortion (CJTD) emerges, resulting in a large volumetric strain and undesirable intragranular cracks.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0014"><sup>[6]</sup></link> This is widely recognized as the lead cause of the poor structural stability and rapid capacity fading of Mn‐based oxide cathodes. To address this drawback, several strategies have been developed. Doping low‐valance cations (such as Li<sup>+</sup>, Mg<sup>2+</sup>, Zn<sup>2+</sup>, Al<sup>3+</sup>, et.al) in TM layers can control the valence of Mn at a level close to +4, thereby reducing J–T regions.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0017"><sup>[7]</sup></link> The introduction of Na‐site dopants or the design of heterophase (such as layered/spinel structures) can modulate the Mn−O bonds, thus mitigating Mn−O octahedra distortion.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0015 #anie202219230-bib-0023"><sup>[6a, 8]</sup></link> However, those approaches generally apply at the sacrifice of specific capacity. The second critical issue facing Mn‐based oxide cathodes is the low Mn redox potential, which is extremely unfavorable for high energy density. To the best of our knowledge, almost no study has been conducted to inherently settle this challenge. Therefore, it is of particular interest to explore an effective route to simultaneously alleviate the J–T distortion, increase the capacity, and elevate the operating potential of Mn redox towards fully utilizing its high energy/cost value.</p><p>Herein, we find, for the first time, that annealing in argon rather than the commonly used air atmosphere is a universal synthesis strategy for Mn‐based oxide cathodes to comprehensively upgrade their Na‐storage performance. This innovative method can in situ create bulk oxygen vacancies that are selectively located adjacent to Mn ions. As a result, Mn domains of new conception appear and effectively disturb the CJTD, significantly enhancing the cycling stability. Moreover, the generation of bulk oxygen vacancies reduces the average Mn valence and meanwhile lowers Mn 3<i>d</i>‐orbital energy level due to the reduction of ligand symmetry, thereby increasing the specific capacity and operating potential of Mn redox reaction (energy density improves by about 50 % for the model P2‐Na<sub>0.75</sub>Mg<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode). The detailed local/electronic structures, dynamic structural evolutions, and charge compensation mechanisms of the Ar‐ and Air‐treated samples are systematically decoded and compared by combined experimental and theoretical evidence. The proposed simple and general approach is highlighted to pave the avenue for the worldwide applications of Mn‐based oxide cathodes for high‐performance SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202219230-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The Na<sub>0.75</sub>Mg<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2</sub> materials were pre‐sintered into P3 phase (Figure S1) and then heat‐treated in argon or air atmosphere to obtain the P2 phase (designated as Ar‐NMM or Air‐NMM, respectively). The morphologies and compositions of the two samples are analyzed in Figure S2 and Table S1, both are flake‐like particles with sizes in the range of 1.0–5.5 um and thicknesses in the range of 200–800 nm, and the Na:Mg:Mn molar ratios coincide well with the expected values. Compared with the black red color of the Air‐NMM powder, the Ar‐NMM powder is deep black (Figure S3), implying the successful reduction of Mn upon annealing in Ar. Rietveld refinements of powder X‐ray diffraction (PXRD) patterns (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1a</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">b</link>, Tables S2 and S3) show that both samples crystallize in the hexagonal P2‐type layered structure (space group: <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mcm</i>), despite a small amount of MgO impurity in Ar‐NMM. The (100) superlattice characteristic peak and additional faint spots in the selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (labeled with red arrows in the inset of Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1a</link>) of Air‐NMM corroborate the honeycomb ordering within TM layers (Figure S4).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0027"><sup>[9]</sup></link> In contrast, this long‐range structural ordering is greatly disrupted in Ar‐NMM, indicating the structural rearrangement during the Ar treatment (as detailed in the mechanism section). Due to the reduction expansion effect,<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0031"><sup>[10]</sup></link> Ar‐NMM possesses enlarged lattice parameters (<i>a</i>=<i>b</i>=5.012 Å, <i>c</i>=11.278 Å) compared to those of Air‐NMM (<i>a</i>=<i>b</i>=5.000 Å, <i>c</i>=11.242 Å), as evidenced by PXRD (Figure S5), transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Figure S6), and Raman spectroscopy (Figure S7) results.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219230-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219230:anie202219230-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219230-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electronic and local structures of Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM. PXRD Rietveld refinements of a) Air‐NMM and b) Ar‐NMM. Insets show the SAED patterns along the [001] projection. EDS line scans of the O/Mn count signals of c) Air‐NMM and d) Ar‐NMM. Insets show the corresponding TEM images. e) Mn K‐edge XANES spectra and f) corresponding Fourier‐transformed EXAFS spectra. g) X‐ray PDF data, the representative peaks of Mn−O and Mn−TM bonds marked with different colors are visually identified in (h). h) Schematic crystal structure diagram.</p></caption></figure><p>Electron paramagnetic resonance (EPR) is a highly sensitive technology to detect unpaired electrons, thus enabling the characterization of oxygen vacancies (denoted as Vo).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0034"><sup>[11]</sup></link> As shown in Figure S8, the sharp EPR signals centered at g=2.003 indicate the presence of Vo in both materials, and Ar‐NMM features more Vo because of the higher signal intensity. The weak signal of Air‐NMM is rooted in the high‐temperature process that leads to inevitable oxygen defects.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> To identify the difference in oxygen deficiency between Ar‐NMM and Air‐NMM, energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) line scans along the dashed arrows (400 nm surface depth, insets of Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1c</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">d</link>) were performed, and the count signals of the O/Mn ratio are plotted in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1c</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">d</link>.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0036"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The signals of Ar‐NMM maintain almost constant at low ratios throughout the depth, while the signals of Air‐NMM rise slightly within the 30 nm surface depth and then remain unchanged in the interior. This suggests that Vo is uniformly distributed in the bulk Ar‐NMM material but intensively distributed on the surface of Air‐NMM, as also evidenced by the etching O 1s X‐ray photoelectron spectra (XPS, Figure S9). In addition, the O 1s XPS further affirms the low content of oxygen defects in Air‐NMM, thereby their influence is not considered in the following.</p><p>The bulk Vo can alter the electronic and local structures of Ar‐NMM. As confirmed by the Mn K‐edge X‐ray absorption near‐edge spectra (XANES, Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1e</link>), the valence of Mn in Ar‐NMM is lower than that in Air‐NMM because the system tends to be electrically neutral after the introduction of Vo. The local structural rearrangement is revealed by the Fourier‐transformed extended X‐ray absorption fine structure (EXAFS) spectra (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1f</link>).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0037"><sup>[14]</sup></link> Compared with Air‐NMM, Ar‐NMM exhibits a decreased first Mn−O shell peak, signifying the reduced average O coordination numbers; while, the stronger second Mn−TM shell peak symbolizes more Mn ions aggregation around Mn (since the neighboring atoms with large atomic numbers have strong backscattering ability),<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0038"><sup>[15]</sup></link> that is, Mn domains. These assessments are further demonstrated by the X‐ray pair distribution function (PDF, Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1g</link>), a technique providing real‐space local structural determination.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0041"><sup>[16]</sup></link> Moreover, all G(r) peaks of Ar‐NMM show longer pair distances than those of Air‐NMM, manifesting the increased Mn<sup>3+</sup> content. As can be concluded, benefitting from the innovative synthesis method, bulk oxygen vacancies are successfully introduced into Ar‐NMM, reducing the Mn valence and tuning the TM intralayer configuration. The crystal structures of Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM are schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1h</link>.</p><p>Given the apparent electronic/local structural differences brought by bulk Vo between Ar‐NMM and Air‐NMM, it is of particular interest to explore the differences in their electrochemical performance. To avoid complicating the Na‐storage mechanism such as triggering oxygen redox chemistry, the voltage range was set to 1.5–4.0 V so that we could focus on the effects of Vo on Mn redox. Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2a</link> shows the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of both cathodes, where no redox peak is observed at around 4.0 V, precluding the oxygen redox reaction.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0044"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The main oxidation/reduction peaks arising from Mn redox locate at 2.44 V/2.03 V for Ar‐NMM and 2.20 V/1.80 V for Air‐NMM, which unambiguously ascertain the elevated equilibrium potential of Mn redox in Ar‐NMM. This is an original and eye‐catching finding for Mn‐based cathodes. Additionally, the pair of redox peaks at around 2.36 V in Air‐NMM associated with Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy ordering obviously weaken in Ar‐NMM, as a result of the TM ions disorder.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0047"><sup>[18]</sup></link> Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2b</link> and Figure S10 compare the galvanostatic charge/discharge profiles of the two cathodes, the initial discharge capacities of Ar‐NMM and Air‐NMM at 0.5 C (1 C=140 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) are 166 and 123 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The significantly increased capacity of Ar‐NMM is ascribed, on one hand, to more Na ions (de)intercalation due to the lower average Mn valence in the pristine state, on the other hand, to the strengthened pseudocapacitance behaviors enabled by the formation of more oxygen vacancies (more facile surface Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption with lower binding energies, Figures S11 and S12).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0048"><sup>[19]</sup></link> Moreover, the Ar‐NMM cathode exhibits a much higher output voltage than that of Air‐NMM (2.39 vs. 2.16 V), benefiting from the elevated Mn redox potential. As a result, the energy density of Ar‐NMM improves by about 50 % compared to that of Air‐NMM (from 265.8 to 396.9 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>), which is a remarkable amelioration for Mn‐based layered oxide cathodes. Notably, the air stability of Ar‐NMM is also greatly improved because the higher Mn redox potential leads to a more difficult chemical exchange between Na<sup>+</sup> and H<sup>+</sup> in ambient air (Figure S13).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0049"><sup>[20]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202219230-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219230:anie202219230-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219230-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemistry of the Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM cathodes. a) CV curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Charge‐discharge profiles in the first two cycles at 0.5 C within 1.5–4.0 V vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na. c) Rate capability. d) Cycling performance at 5 C after the preactivation at 0.5 C for 5 cycles. e) Average discharge voltage decay at 5 C. Full battery performance of Ar‐NMM: f) charge–discharge curves in the first three cycles at 0.5 C within 1.4–3.9 V; g) cycling performance at 2 C after the preactivation at 0.5 C for 5 cycles, inset shows the working light powered by the pouch cell; h) rate capability.</p></caption></figure><p>The introduction of bulk Vo also leads to desirable improvements in rate capability and cycling stability (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2c</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">d</link>). Specifically, Ar‐NMM affords a better rate performance of 63 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C as well as a higher cycling durability of 83.1 % capacity retention at 5 C over 500 cycles, in contrast to 21 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C and 60.3 % capacity retention after 500 cycles for Air‐NMM. The enhanced rate capability of Ar‐NMM is attributed to the improved electrical conductivity resulting from oxygen vacancies, which act as shallow donors to increase the electron concentration and thus facilitate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusivity (Figures S14 and S15, Table S4).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0048"><sup>[19]</sup></link> Moreover, the voltage decay of Ar‐NMM is effectively mitigated with an average fading of 0.30 mV per cycle, less than that of Air‐NMM (0.33 mV per cycle, Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2e</link>). Therefore, the Na‐storage performance of Ar‐NMM has been comprehensively upgraded owing to the manufacture of bulk oxygen vacancies, which outperforms those of the previously reported Mn‐rich oxide cathodes for SIBs (Table S5).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0045 #anie202219230-bib-0050"><sup>[17a, 21]</sup></link> Furthermore, a hard carbon//Ar‐NMM full battery was constructed, delivering a promising energy density of approximately 233 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> (based on cathode and anode active materials), a satisfactory capacity retention of 85.1 % at 2 C after 100 cycles, and a respectable rate capability of 70 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2f</link>–<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">h</link>). The homemade light was successfully launched by the pouch cell (inset of Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2g</link>), verifying the great application prospects of Ar‐NMM.</p><p>To unravel the charge compensation mechanism of the improved Ar‐NMM electrochemistry, ex situ XANES was employed to track the electronic structure changes during the first cycle. Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0003">3a</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0003">b</link> shows the Mn K‐edge spectra of Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM, the corresponding Mn oxidation states are quantificationally analyzed by the integration method (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0003">3c</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0003">d</link> and Note S1), as has been applied in the previous report.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0055"><sup>[22]</sup></link> The pristine Mn valences are calculated to be +3.65 and +3.51 for Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM, respectively, thereby their compositions could be successively determined as Na<sub>0.75</sub>Mg<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>1.994</sub> and Na<sub>0.75</sub>Mg<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>1.941</sub>. During the desodiation process, the Mn K‐edge spectrum shows an apparent shift towards high energy for Ar‐NMM while the shift is negligible for Air‐NMM, in agreement with the fact that the initial charge capacity of Ar‐NMM (50 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) is much higher than that of Air‐NMM (21 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2b</link>). After full discharge, Mn is reduced from +3.74 to +3.01 for Ar‐NMM, in contrast to the reduction from +3.75 to +3.20 for Air‐NMM, validating the improved Mn redox activity after the introduction of oxygen vacancies. Similar Mn valence changes are also validated by ex situ XPS (Figure S16). Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0003">3e</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0003">f</link> displays the variation of Na contents in Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM determined by the Mn valance changes upon charge/discharge. In the fully discharged state, the Na contents of both cathodes exceed the threshold of 1 due to the pseudocapacitance behaviors occurring in the near‐surface region.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0056"><sup>[23]</sup></link> Besides, Ar‐NMM possesses a higher Na content compared with Air‐NMM owing to the more violent pseudocapacitance. Consequently, a higher energy density could be achieved in the Ar‐NMM cathode.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219230-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219230:anie202219230-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219230-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Charge compensation mechanisms of the Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM cathodes. Mn K‐edge XANES spectra in selected voltage states for a) Air‐NMM and b) Ar‐NMM; insets show the magnified spectra. c), d) Mn valence evolution determined by the integration method with the MnO, Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and LiMn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> reference data from ref. <link href="#anie202219230-bib-0055">[22]</link>. Na contents calculated by the Mn oxidation state changes in e) Air‐NMM and f) Ar‐NMM.</p></caption></figure><p>The structural evolutions of the two cathodes during the first cycle and the second charge process were monitored by in situ XRD (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4a</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">b</link>), which behaved quite differently. The Air‐NMM electrode undergoes a conventional structural evolution, with <i>c</i>‐parameter expansion and <i>a</i>‐parameter shrinkage upon charge, and a following reverse change upon discharge. In sharp contrast, an unprecedented and intriguing structural evolution was observed in Ar‐NMM. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4b</link>, upon first charge, the (00<i>l</i>) peaks initially move leftward and reach the local maximum at 3.44 V, originating from the increased interlayer electrostatic repulsion induced by Na extraction. Afterwards, the (00<i>l</i>) peaks moderately shift rightward until the end of charge with no evidence of phase transition, indicative of the TM layer's slight gliding (denoted as P2′ phase).<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0059"><sup>[24]</sup></link> This is consistent with the additional peaks occurring at around 3.51 V in the CV profile (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0002">2a</link>). Meanwhile, the (110) peak continuously shifts to high angles, suggesting the decreasing <i>a</i>‐parameter. Upon first discharge, the <i>c</i>‐parameter initially increases above 3.0 V and then decreases monotonously. However, the (110) peak becomes too featureless to identify the <i>a</i>‐parameter due to the enhanced structural stress in the fully sodiated state,<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0045"><sup>[17a]</sup></link> so the (112) peak is chosen as a better indicator based on the known <i>c</i>‐parameter evolution. The (112) peak moves leftward between 4.0 and 2.2 V because of the reduction of Mn ions, followed by a zigzag shift between 2.2 and 1.5 V (as highlighted in the red rectangular of Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4b</link>). Such a zigzag shift of the (112) peak reappears within 1.5–2.5 V on the second charge. This signifies that the <i>a</i>‐parameter abnormally contracts (expands) accompanied by the reduction (oxidation) of Mn ions within this voltage interval, which has never been observed before for layered oxide cathodes. The lattice parameter evolutions are further quantified through Rietveld refinements (right panels of Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4a</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">b</link>), where Ar‐NMM exhibits a larger <i>c</i>‐parameter variation but a smaller <i>a</i>‐parameter change (also indicated from the smaller offsets of the (110) peak) during the first cycle. More impressively, for Ar‐NMM, the <i>a</i>‐ and <i>c</i>‐parameters after the second charge can almost completely recover to the state after the first charge process, suggesting the higher structural reversibility and thus the better cycling stability of Ar‐NMM than those of Air‐NMM.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219230-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219230:anie202219230-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219230-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Cooperative J–T distortion disruption by Mn domains in Ar‐NMM. In situ XRD patterns of a) Air‐NMM and b) Ar‐NMM during the first cycle and the second charge process at 0.25 C, as well as corresponding lattice parameter variations (right panels). HADDF‐STEM images along the [001] projection and corresponding EELS spectra of c), e) Air‐NMM and d), f) Ar‐NMM; scale bar, 1 nm. g) Schematic diagram illustrating the function of Mn domains in disrupting the CJTD upon cycling.</p></caption></figure><p>To understand the abnormal <i>a</i>‐parameter evolution of Ar‐NMM, we focus on the difference in TM layers between these two cathodes considering the confirmed local cation rearrangement (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0001">1</link>) and the fact that TM layer determines the <i>a</i>‐parameter change. High‐angle annular dark‐filed scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) images along the [001] projection of Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM were recorded (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4c</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">d</link>). The relatively bright dots reflect the higher Mn concentration in each atomic column due to the heavier atomic weight of Mn than that of Mg. For Air‐NMM, many successive Mg@Mn<sub>6</sub> honeycomb superstructure units are visually identified (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4c</link> and Figure S17). In contrast, some separated bright‐dot regions in the TM layers of Ar‐NMM are observed (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4d</link> and Figure S18), indicating the formation of Mn domains. The line profiles of electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) along the arrows in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4c</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">d</link> were collected to further analyze the electronic structure changes (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4e</link>,<link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">f</link>). The Mn L<sub>2,3</sub>‐edges have no obvious change along the line scans for both materials, while the higher peak intensity ratio of L<sub>3</sub>/L<sub>2</sub> in Ar‐NMM than that in Air‐NMM manifests the lower Mn valence,<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0060"><sup>[25]</sup></link> coinciding well with the XANES results. As for the O K‐edge pre‐peak, distinct differences between the two samples are detected: (1) the peak intensity of Ar‐NMM is always lower than that of Air‐NMM, indicative of the existence of bulk oxygen vacancies in Ar‐NMM;<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0035"><sup>[12]</sup></link> (2) compared with the constant pre‐peak intensity of Air‐NMM, the peak intensity of Ar‐NMM gradually weakens along the line profile, implying the increasing oxygen vacancy concentration in the Mn domains.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0060"><sup>[25]</sup></link> These results demonstrate that the introduction of bulk oxygen vacancies is accompanied by the formation of Mn domains, which is a brand‐new concept for Mn‐based layered oxide cathodes. In this scenario, different Mn‐domain regions separated by Mg ions would undergo individual redox reactions with anisotropic J–T distortions upon cycling, thus disrupting the CJTD (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0004">4g</link>) and leading to the smaller <i>a</i>‐parameter variation and higher cyclic stability of Ar‐NMM.</p><p>Further investigation of the underlying correlation between oxygen vacancies and Mn domains was conducted by density functional theory (DFT) computations. The formation energies (<i>E</i><sub>form</sub>) of oxygen vacancy in the four coordination environments (i.e., Mn<sub>3</sub>Na<sub>3</sub>, (Mn<sub>2</sub>Mg<sub>1</sub>)Na<sub>3</sub>, (Mn<sub>1</sub>Mg<sub>2</sub>)Na<sub>3</sub>, and Mg<sub>3</sub>Na<sub>3</sub>) were calculated (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0005">5a</link>), revealing the lowest <i>E</i><sub>form</sub> (9.03 eV) for Mn<sub>3</sub>Na<sub>3</sub> coordination and highest <i>E</i><sub>form</sub> (10.62 eV) for Mg<sub>3</sub>Na<sub>3</sub> coordination. This is interpreted as Mn<sup>4+</sup> ions near Vo can absorb the residual electrons left by Vo and are reduced to Mn<sup>3+</sup> in the Mn<sub>3</sub>Na<sub>3</sub> coordination, while Mg<sup>2+</sup> ions cannot absorb extra electrons in the Mg<sub>3</sub>Na<sub>3</sub> coordination, thus favoring the accumulation of Mn ions near oxygen vacancies. The most stable atomic arrangements of the 2×2×2 supercells without and with bulk Vo (Na<sub>18</sub>Mg<sub>4</sub>Mn<sub>14</sub>O<sub>36</sub> and Na<sub>18</sub>Mg<sub>4</sub>Mn<sub>14</sub>O<sub>34</sub>, respectively) are simulated and presented in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0005">5b</link>. As expected, each TM layer consists of the [Mg<sub>2</sub>Mn<sub>7</sub>] configuration for the structure without Vo, but the TM layers are composed of the [Mg<sub>1</sub>Mn<sub>8</sub>] (with Vo) and [Mg<sub>3</sub>Mn<sub>6</sub>] (without Vo) configurations for the structure featuring bulk Vo. These results further illustrate that the bulk oxygen vacancies are preferentially generated adjacent to Mn ions and trigger the formation of Mn domains for a higher thermodynamic stability, in good agreement with the EELS results.</p><figure xml:id="anie202219230-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219230:anie202219230-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219230-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>DFT computations. a) Formation energies of oxygen vacancy in different coordination environments. b) The theoretically most stable supercell structure without/with the bulk Vo. c) Projected DOS of the Mn 3<i>d</i> orbitals in Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM. d) Schematic band diagrams derived from (c) and 3<i>d</i> orbital electronic configurations of Mn<sup>3+</sup> and Mn<sup>4+</sup>. e) Illustration of the reduced symmetry of Mn−O ligands after the introduction of bulk Vo.</p></caption></figure><p>The elevated Mn redox potential of Ar‐NMM is also closely related to bulk Vo. We compared the electronic structures of the Mn 3<i>d</i> orbitals in Air‐NMM and Ar‐NMM by density of states (DOS, Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0005">5c</link>), and the schematic band diagrams are plotted in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0005">5d</link>. The Mn 3<i>d e</i><sub>g</sub> states with spin‐up electrons are divided into the electron‐occupied states in valence band and the electron‐unoccupied states in conduction band. After introducing the bulk Vo via annealing in Ar, the energy level of the occupied Mn 3<i>d e</i><sub>g</sub> states moves away from the Fermi level (<i>E</i><sub>F</sub>), indicative of an enlarged energy gap between Mn redox and Na<sup>+</sup>/Na (or Na metal anode), leading to the increased Mn redox potential. This is because oxygen vacancy would reduce the local symmetry of Mn−O ligands from cubic or tetragonal mode to C<sub>4<i>v</i></sub> (Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0005">5e</link>), resulting in the local orbital coupling and thus lowering the Mn 3<i>d</i>‐orbital energy level.<link href="#anie202219230-bib-0061"><sup>[26]</sup></link></p><p>In light of the above experimental and theoretical evidence, it is found that the upgraded overall Na‐storage performance of Ar‐NMM is tightly correlated with the changes in Mn ions (including the valence state, Mn domains, and local symmetry of Mn−O ligands) arising from bulk oxygen vacancies. Therefore, we believe that the Ar‐annealing strategy has the potential to be extended to other Mn‐based layered sodium oxides. Impelled by this idea, we also successfully prepared the classical Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.67</sub>Cu<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub>0.67</sub>Fe<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode materials. The electrochemistry comparisons of these cathodes between annealing in Ar and Air are shown in detail in Figures S19–S22 and summarized in Figure <link href="#anie202219230-fig-0006">6</link>. As can be concluded, the present Ar‐atmosphere heat treatment is a widely applicable approach to simultaneously improve the specific capacity, cycling stability, and Mn redox voltage of the Mn‐based oxide cathodes for SIBs. In addition, annealing in nitrogen atmosphere can also introduce oxygen vacancies to improve the Na‐storage performance of Mn‐based oxide cathodes (Figure S23).</p><figure xml:id="anie202219230-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202219230:anie202219230-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202219230-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Universal rule for improving Na‐storage performance. Electrochemistry comparisons of various Mn‐based layered oxide cathodes between annealing in argon and air atmospheres.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202219230-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, we originally decipher the universal role of annealing in Ar in upgrading the energy density and cycling durability of Mn‐based sodium oxide cathodes, by comparing the model Air‐ and Ar‐Na<sub>0.75</sub>Mg<sub>0.25</sub>Mn<sub>0.75</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples. Bulk oxygen vacancies are generated during the sintering process in Ar, leading to the reduction of Mn ions and formation of the new‐concept Mn domains. The reduced Mn valence enables more Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation upon charge/discharge and thus the increased capacity, the Mn domains effectively disrupt the cooperative Jahn–Teller distortion and thereby enhance the structural stability. More fascinatingly, the Mn redox potential in layered oxide cathodes, to the best of our knowledge, is firstly elevated because oxygen vacancies can lower the Mn 3<i>d‐</i>orbital energy level. As a result, the energy density of Ar‐NMM significantly increases by about 50 % compared with that of Air‐NMM, and the cycling life is also greatly prolonged (83.1 % capacity retention after 500 cycles at 5 C). This work sheds brand‐new light on the advancement of Mn‐based oxide cathode materials for high‐performance Na‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202219230-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgments</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (21825102, 22075016, 21731001, and 22103057), National Key Research and Development Program of China (2018YFA0703702), and Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (FRF‐TP‐20‐020A3 and QNXM20220060).
