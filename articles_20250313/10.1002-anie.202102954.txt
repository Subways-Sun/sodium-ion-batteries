Nanostructure design that elaborately resolves the above issues is highly desired but still remains a big challenge. Herein, unique hollow nanoreactors assembled with nitrogen‐doped carbon networks for in situ synthesis of the GeP electrodes are proposed for the first time. Such nanoreactors form a self‐supported conductive network, ensuring sufficient electrolyte infiltration and fast electron transport. They restrain crystal growth and accommodate the volume expansion of GeP simultaneously. Reaction kinetics and confinement effect are optimized through nanoreactor size regulation. The optimized GeP electrode has high reversible capacities and outstanding cyclability and rate performance for sodium storage, outperforming most previously reported phosphides.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A unique structure of hollow nanoreactors is designed for in situ synthesis of GeP electrodes. numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Metal phosphides (SnP, FeP, GeP<sub>3</sub>/C, and GeP<sub>5</sub>/C) have been widely studied as promising sodium ion battery (SIB) anodes by virtue of its high reversible capacities and low operating voltage.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Among various transition metal phosphides, GeP‐based materials possess a layered structure with an interlayer spacing of 0.64 nm,<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> which could accommodate reversible Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0005"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Furthermore, it holds a high electronic conductivity of 5×10<sup>3</sup> S m<sup>−1</sup>,<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0006"><sup>[4]</sup></link> making it promising for high energy density anode for SIBs.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0007"><sup>[5]</sup></link> However, the bulk GeP materials show inferior sodium storage performance because of the sluggish ion diffusion kinetics and huge volume change during sodiation/desodiation process, which cause material pulverization and destroy the integrity of electrodes.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0008"><sup>[6]</sup></link></p><p>Currently, the synthesis approaches of GeP nanomaterials have been limited to ball‐milling and GeP bulk exfoliation.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0004 #anie202102954-bib-0007 #anie202102954-bib-0009"><sup>[2, 5, 7]</sup></link> GeP obtained through ball‐milling generally showed a morphology of irregular particles with sizes ranging from tens of nanometers to a few microns, which was not favorable for solid‐state ion diffusion and vulnerable to pulverization.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0010"><sup>[8]</sup></link> Meanwhile, the GeP derived from the exfoliation exhibited nanosheet morphology, which was of low yield and hard to further regulate the architecture.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0013"><sup>[9]</sup></link> Therefore, most reported GeP anodes showed poor rate capability and inferior cycling life limited to less than 100 times.</p><p>Nanostructure design is a powerful strategy to improve the electrochemical performance,<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0014"><sup>[10]</sup></link> which could efficiently facilitate Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics,<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0015"><sup>[11]</sup></link> relieve volume change and decrease structure distortion during cycling.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0018"><sup>[12]</sup></link> However, nanostructure design of GeP still encounters a bottleneck because GeP experiences crystal growth, aggregation and phase transition during the phosphating process.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0009 #anie202102954-bib-0013 #anie202102954-bib-0021"><sup>[7, 9, 13]</sup></link> Limiting crystal growth and aggregation of GeP is the key to realize nanostructure design.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0022"><sup>[14]</sup></link> On this basis, constructing free‐standing electrodes to eliminate the negative effect of the inactive polymeric binder is of equal importance.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0025"><sup>[15]</sup></link> From the above consideration, realizing specific architecture design for GeP electrode is the key to improve its electrochemical performance but still remains a big challenge.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0028"><sup>[16]</sup></link></p><p>Herein, a unique structure of hollow nanoreactors assembled with conductive nitrogen‐doped carbon networks for in situ synthesis of GeP electrode was designed. These nanoreactors well confined Ge‐based precursor in the cavities, which not only restrained free crystal growth of GeP particles during the phosphating synthesis but also effectively buffered volume change, preventing aggregation and restacking of GeP during sodiation/desodiation process. Furthermore, the confining effect of the nanoreactors and Na<sup>+</sup> reaction kinetics of the electrodes could be further optimized through regulating the fiber width, hollow size and tube thickness of the nitrogen‐doped carbon fibers. Attributing to synergistic effects of high conductivity, inhibited volume change and accelerated Na<sup>+</sup> reaction kinetics, the optimized 3D networked free‐standing GeP electrode delivered high reversible capacity (830 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), outstanding cyclability (keep stable over 350 cycles) and rate capability (330 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) when used as an anode in SIBs. This work taps into a thrilling potential that stimulates the advance of current GeP based batteries one step closer towards real‐world applications.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202102954-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0001">1</link>, the general fabrication procedure for the hollow nitrogen‐doped carbon fiber encapsulating GeP nanoreactors (GeP/CN‐<i>x</i>) involves three major steps, the electrospinning, thermal treatment and phosphating process. To create the nanoreactors, the key is to choose two different polymer compositions during electrospinning and accurately control the subsequent heating condition. In our electrospinning process, GE123/PMMA/DMF was used as the inner spinning solution, and PAN/DMF was chosen as the outer spinning solution. TG analysis on the inner and outer composites verifies that during the calcination process, the PAN/DMF was carbonized, forming the outsider nitrogen‐doped carbon fiber (Supporting Information, Figure S1). At the same time, the inner side GE123/PMMA/DMF transformed to Ge particles along with the complete decomposition of PMMA (Supporting Information, Figure S2). During the subsequent phosphating process, in each nanoreactor, the Ge particles were well confined in the cavity, and the crystal growth of GeP particles was herein restricted inside, producing a final hollow structured 3D networked free‐standing GeP/CN electrode. To optimize the confining effect of the nanoreactor, the fiber width, hollow size and tube thickness of the nitrogen‐doped carbon fibers were regulated via changing the needles varying nozzle sizes. For comparison, a solid structured 3D networked free‐standing GeP/CN electrode (GeP/CN‐0) was prepared following the same procedure but using uniaxial spinning with GE123/PAN/DMF as the spinning solution.</p><figure xml:id="anie202102954-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202102954:anie202102954-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202102954-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Illustration of the preparation process of hollow nanoreactors assembled with nitrogen‐doped carbon fibers for in situ synthesis of GeP electrodes.</p></caption></figure><p>Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) was conducted to investigate the morphology evolution during the synthesis process of GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> electrodes. As can be seen in the Supporting Information, Figure S3, the GE123/PAN and GE123/PAN/PMMA films show 3D networking structure consisting of smooth nanofibers with fiber widths of about 230 nm and 540 nm, respectively. After thermal treatment, the nanofibers shrank due to the carbonization of the polymer components (Supporting Information, Figure S4). It should be noted that many nodules appear on the surface of GeP/CN‐0 nanofibers after calcinating and phosphating processes. This is because traditional carbon fiber coating can hardly restrict crystal growth and aggregation of GeP particles occurs upon phosphating (Supporting Information, Figure S4c). Surprisingly, the fibers in hollow structured electrode exhibit a smooth surface, indicating that the hollow nitrogen‐doped carbon nanoreactors could successfully buffer the aggregation and control crystal growth of GeP (Supporting Information, Figure S4d).</p><p>To optimize the confining effect of the nanoreactors, the fiber width, hollow size and tube thickness of the GeP/CN electrodes (GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=1, 2, 3)) were adjusted precisely by varying the nozzle size of the spinning needles. The inner needle sizes are regulated from 0.4 to 0.6 mm, and the outer needle sizes are tuned from 0.85 to 1.3 mm for the purpose of continuous and stable spinning. Cross‐section SEM images for all electrodes indicate a closed thickness of about 21 um for all the free‐standing electrodes (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0002">2 a</link>–c). Clearly, the 3D networking structure is well maintained, and the obtained electrodes possess high flexibility and mechanical stability (the inset in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0002">2 c</link>). The fiber width is adjusted from 800 nm for GeP/CN‐1, 600 nm for GeP/CN‐2 to 400 nm for GeP/CN‐3 (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0002">2 d</link>–f). Moreover, the hollow size is tuned from 380 nm for GeP/CN‐1, 320 nm for GeP/CN‐2 to 230 nm for GeP/CN‐3 (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0002">2 g</link>–i), and the tube thickness ranges from 210 nm for GeP/CN‐1, 140 nm for GeP/CN‐2 to 85 nm for GeP/CN‐3 (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0002">2 g</link>–i). The precise regulation of the nanoreactor size facilitates to buffer the volume change of GeP and greatly shortens the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion distance, resulting in improved cycling stability and reaction kinetics.</p><figure xml:id="anie202102954-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202102954:anie202102954-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202102954-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a)–c) Cross‐section SEM images, d)–f) SEM images, g)–i) Magnified SEM images for GeP/CN‐1, GeP/CN‐2, and GeP/CN‐3, respectively.</p></caption></figure><p>Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) of GeP/CN‐3 was further characterized to elucidate the detailed morphology and structure properties of the electrode. Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0003">3 a</link> confirms the hollow structure of GeP/CN‐3 with a cavity size of about 200 nm and tube thickness of about 100 nm. A magnified TEM image indicates that the GeP nanoparticles are well embedded inside the conductive carbon tubes (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0003">3 b</link>). The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) view further demonstrates that the GeP nanoparticles are well confined by the carbon matrix (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0003">3 c</link>). Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0003">3 d</link> exhibits lattice fringe spacings of 0.64 nm, indexing to the (001) plane of GeP phase, further confirming the layered structure properties.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0004"><sup>[2]</sup></link> The large interlay spacing could greatly facilitate Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the solid.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0029"><sup>[17]</sup></link> The selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern of individual GeP shows continuous circles, which can be indexed to the (−3 1 1), (−8 0 2), (0 2 1), and (−16 0 5) planes of GeP, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0003">3 e</link>). The element mapping images elucidate that GeP nanoparticles are well embedded in the hollow of nitrogen doped carbon fiber (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0003">3 f</link>–j). Currently, GeP was generally synthesized through the crystallization of Ge and P under extreme experimental conditions, with particle size at micron meters.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0009 #anie202102954-bib-0032"><sup>[7, 18]</sup></link> Precise nanostructure design for GeP‐based materials has never been reported to the best of our knowledge. In this work, we realized the morphology tuning together with electrode architecture design for GeP for the first time. In this unique structure, the hollow nitrogen‐doped carbon nanoreactors could not only increase the electronic conductivity of the electrode but also alleviate the clustering and volume change of GeP nanoparticles during sodiation/desodiation process.</p><figure xml:id="anie202102954-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202102954:anie202102954-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202102954-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a),b) TEM images, c),d) HR‐TEM images, e) SAED pattern, f) HAADF image, g)–j) element mapping image of GeP/CN‐3 electrode.</p></caption></figure><p>Powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD) was tested to clarify the crystal structure evolution during the preparation process. The XRD patterns for all electrospinning precursors can be assigned to the GE123 phase (Supporting Information, Figure S5), which are transferred to Ge/CN after the thermal treatment (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 a</link>). With further phosphating, pure GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) electrodes with all diffraction peaks well indexed to the monoclinic GeP (JCPDS#44‐1125) are obtained (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 b</link>). These results indicate that pure GeP was successful synthesized via the sealed phosphating process.</p><figure xml:id="anie202102954-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202102954:anie202102954-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202102954-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>XRD patterns of a) Ge/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) and b) GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) electrodes. c) XPS survey d) Ge 3d, e) P 2p and f) C 1s spectrum of GeP/CN‐3. g) Raman spectrum for Ge/CN‐3 and GeP/CN‐3 electrodes. h) Brunauer‐Emmett‐Teller spectrum for GeP/CN‐3 electrode. i) TG results for GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> electrodes.</p></caption></figure><p>The compositions and surface chemical states for GeP/CN‐3 were analyzed through X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). The XPS survey spectrum shows the peaks of Ge, P, C, N, and O elements without any impurity (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 c</link>). In the Ge 3d XPS spectrum, three peaks located at 30.1, 31.4 and 31.8 eV are observed, which can be assigned to Ge‐Ge, Ge 3d<sub>5/2</sub>(Ge‐P) and Ge 3d<sub>3/2</sub>(Ge‐P), respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 d</link>).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0033"><sup>[19]</sup></link> The P 2p spectrum exhibits four peaks, the peaks at 129.3 and 130.6 eV can be ascribed to Ge‐P,<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0007 #anie202102954-bib-0034"><sup>[5, 20]</sup></link> and the other two peaks at 129.9 and 130.2 eV correspond to P‐P (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 e</link>).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0004 #anie202102954-bib-0035"><sup>[2, 21]</sup></link> The C 1s spectrum can be divided into three peaks. The strong peak at 284.6 eV can be indexed to C−C/C=C, while the other two peaks at 280.6 and 288.5 eV are the signals of C‐N and C‐O, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 f</link>).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0036"><sup>[22]</sup></link> N 1s spectrum can be deconvoluted into pyridinic N (21.1 %), pyrrolic N (34.7 %), and graphitic N (44.2 %), respectively. The ratio of N element in the nitrogen doped carbon is calculated to be 17.6 %, which can improve the electronic conductivity of the carbon matrix (Supporting Information, Figure S6).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0037"><sup>[23]</sup></link></p><p>Raman measurements for Ge/CN‐3 and GeP/CN‐3 were further carried out. As exhibited in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 g</link>, the Ge/CN‐3 shows a main peak at 291.4 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which is consistent well with the Raman spectrum of standard Ge powder (Supporting Information, Figure S7).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0004 #anie202102954-bib-0038"><sup>[2, 24]</sup></link> In the spectrum of GeP/CN‐3, two peaks corresponding to Ge‐P and Ge‐Ge stretching modes are observed at 361.0 and 200.4 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0034 #anie202102954-bib-0039"><sup>[20, 25]</sup></link> The other three peaks located at 135.7, 246.3 and 308.1 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be assigned to the plane deformation, rocking and skeleton stretching for Ge‐P‐Ge modes, respectively.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0040"><sup>[26]</sup></link> Furthermore, two peaks appear at 1329.5 and 1581.8 cm<sup>−1</sup> for both samples, which correspond to D and G bands of nitrogen‐doped carbon, respectively.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0041"><sup>[27]</sup></link> The XRD, XPS, and Raman results together confirm the successful synthesis of the pure GeP/CN phase. Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area and pore size distribution (inset) of all electrodes were tested and are shown in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 h</link> and the Supporting Information, Figure S8. As seen, the surface area for GeP/CN‐0, GeP/CN‐1, GeP/CN‐2, GeP/CN‐3 are 9.8, 12.2, 41.9, and 60.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Notably, the surface area could be increased through the introduction of the inner hollow, and it would be further increased by decreasing the fiber width, tube thickness and hollow size. Furthermore, micropores with pore sizes of 5–15 nm are formed throughout the nanofiber owing to the decomposition of the polymer during the thermal treatment (the inset in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 h</link> and the Supporting Information, the inset in Figure S8). The hierarchical porous structure with the adjustable surface area could ensure sufficient electrolyte infiltration, shorten the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion distance and provide more active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0042"><sup>[28]</sup></link> To determine the GeP content in the GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> electrodes, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) was carried out (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0004">4 i</link>). Considering that GeP is easily oxidized in air and would decompose above 600 °C, we conducted the TGA test at the N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere. The TGA curves show a weight loss of about 14.1 % below 600 °C, which corresponds to the decomposing of GeP and the weight loss of P. According to the P weight loss, the content of GeP for all samples is calculated to be about 49 %.</p><p>To evaluate the confining effect regulation of the hollow nitrogen‐doped carbon nanoreactors on the electrochemical properties of GeP, sodium storage performance for all GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> electrodes was investigated. Considering that the mass of nitrogen doped carbon is 51 % of the total electrode, we evaluated the sodium storage performance of nitrogen doped carbon networks. As shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S9, all nitrogen doped carbon electrodes deliver a negligible reversible capacity at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Therefore, we calculate the reversible capacities of GeP/NC‐<i>x</i> electrodes based on the mass of GeP. The initial discharge‐charge curves for all electrodes at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> show similar tendency with a discharge plateau at about 0.85 V in the initial discharge process, which is originated from the formation of SEI film and the side reactions of the electrolyte (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0005">5 a</link>).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0045"><sup>[29]</sup></link> Notably, the charge/discharge plateaus of GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> are entirely below 1 V, indicating that GeP is favorable for achieving high energy density in SIBs.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0044"><sup>[28b]</sup></link> As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0005">5 a</link>, initial discharge/charge capacities of 893/793 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, with an initial Columbic efficiency (ICE) of 88.8 % are observed for GeP/CN‐3. In the case of GeP/CN‐0, GeP/CN‐1 and GeP/CN‐2 electrodes, the ICEs are calculated to be 74.1 %, 81.9 %, and 85.3 %, respectively. The CE for all electrodes increases to 100 % in the initial three cycles and keeps steady over cycling (Supporting Information, Figure S10). As it is well known, ICE is a critical parameter in determining the practical application of active materials and the GeP/CN‐3 electrode achieves a higher ICE compared with the others. Moreover, GeP/CN‐3 shows excellent cycling stability, maintaining a high reversible capacity of 762 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. For comparison, GeP/CN‐0 exhibits continuous capacity decay and GeP/CN‐1 and GeP/CN‐2 only show 594 and 691 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0005">5 b</link>). These hollow structured nanoreactors could better restrain the volume change compared to that of the solid structure, resulting in improved cycling stability. Therefore, the ICE and reversible capacity could be further promoted by decreasing the fiber width, hollow size and tube thickness. This is because the optimized structure with a higher surface area could facilitate Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/extraction kinetics and provide more sodium storage sites.</p><figure xml:id="anie202102954-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202102954:anie202102954-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202102954-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Discharge–charge profiles of GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) electrodes at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. b) Cycling performance for all GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. c) Rate capability for all GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) electrodes. d) The rate performance of GeP/CN‐3 electrode in comparison with previous reported transition metal phosphates.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0006 #anie202102954-bib-0009 #anie202102954-bib-0049 #anie202102954-bib-0050 #anie202102954-bib-0051 #anie202102954-bib-0052 #anie202102954-bib-0053 #anie202102954-bib-0054 #anie202102954-bib-0055 #anie202102954-bib-0056 #anie202102954-bib-0057"><sup>[4, 7, 30a–i]</sup></link> e) Long‐term cycling performance for all GeP/CN−<i>x</i> electrodes at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 350 cycles.</p></caption></figure><p>The impact of structural regulation of the nanoreactors on the electrochemical performances was further highlighted by the rate performance. As for GeP/CN‐3, high reversible capacities of 831, 665, 561, 470, 428, and 382 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> are obtained at 100, 500, 1000, 1500, 2000 and 3000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Even at a high rate of 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, a high reversible capacity of 330 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> could still be achieved, which is about 10 times higher than that of the GeP/CN‐0 electrode (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0005">5 c</link>). When the current density is restored to 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, 790 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained, which is very close to the original value. Charge–discharge curves for all electrodes at different current densities further elucidate smaller polarization of GeP/CN‐3 (Supporting Information, Figure S11). Notably, the rate performance of the flexible free‐standing GeP/CN‐3 electrode in this work is much better than that of the other three electrodes, as well as the previously reported GeP‐based materials and other kinds of phosphides for sodium storage (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0005">5 d</link>; Supporting Information, Table S1).<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0006 #anie202102954-bib-0009 #anie202102954-bib-0048"><sup>[4, 7, 30]</sup></link> The superior rate capability could be originated from the facilitated Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics rooting in the hollow structure tuning as well as the fast electron transport resulting from the special 3D conductive networking free‐standing electrode design.</p><p>The long‐term cyclability of all the electrodes was investigated at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup> over 350 cycles, as shown in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0005">5 e</link>. As for the solid GeP/CN‐0 control electrode, capacity decays fast over the first 20 cycles and only 177 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> is maintained after 350 cycles. On the other hand, the in situ synthesized GeP electrodes within the confined nanoreactors show significantly improved cycling stability and increased reversible capacity. After slight initial capacity decay, a reversible capacity of 553 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> has stabilized over 350 cycles for GeP/CN‐3, indicating superior cycling stability, which excels most previously reported metal phosphides (Supporting Information, Table S2). Moreover, the CE for GeP/CN‐3 keeps stable at about 100 % after the initial several cycles (Supporting Information, Figure S12). The digital images (Supporting Information, Figure S13) and SEM images (Supporting Information, Figure S14) for GeP/CN‐3 electrode after cycling elucidate high structural stability and excellent confining effect of the unique structure.</p><p>Cyclic voltammetry (CV) for GeP/CN‐3 was conducted at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 0–3.0 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0006">6 a</link>) to investigate the reaction mechanism. During the first discharge process, an irreversible peak shows up at 1.1 V, which can be ascribed to the formation of SEI film and the side reactions from the electrolyte.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0045"><sup>[29]</sup></link> As the electrode further discharging to below 1.0 V, two reduction peaks arise, which can be assigned to the multistep sodiation reactions: GeP→Ge→Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ge.<link href="#anie202102954-bib-0059"><sup>[31]</sup></link> Accordingly, three oxidation peaks can be indexed to the dealloying (0.14 V), deconversion (0.53 V), and deintercalation (0.75 V) reactions, respectively. These results demonstrate a conversion‐alloying combined reaction mechanism for GeP/CN‐3 electrode, which is consistent well with the previously reported results.</p><figure xml:id="anie202102954-fig-0006"><label>6</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202102954:anie202102954-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202102954-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Cyclic voltammetry (CV) for GeP/CN‐3 at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 0–3.0 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na). b) Cyclic voltammetry (CV) for GeP/CN‐3 at different scan rates. c) Capacitive contribution for GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) at different scan rates. d) Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) for GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) electrodes. e) Calvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) profiles for GeP/CN‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i>=0, 1, 2, 3) electrodes at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. d) Diffusion coefficient calculated from the GITT results.</p></caption></figure><p>With the aim of determining the nanoreactor design in regulating the reaction kinetics, CV curves from 0.2 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> for all the electrodes were also collected. As displayed in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0006">6 b</link> and the Supporting Information, Figure S15, GeP/CN‐3 electrode shows better‐preserved CV curves with much smaller polarization as the scan rate increases, indicating that the reaction kinetics is greatly facilitated companying with the hollow structure regulation. Moreover, the GeP/CN‐3 electrode delivers the highest ratio of capacitive contribution among all electrodes, which could realize faster Na<sup>+</sup> storage (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0006">6 c</link>; Supporting Information, Figure S16).</p><p>Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) results demonstrate increased slopes of the inclined line and decreased semicircle diameters for GeP/CN‐3, indicating that both the charge transfer among the electrode and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the solid materials are improving (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0006">6 d</link>). The Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics for all the electrodes are evaluated by the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT). As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0006">6 e</link>, all the electrodes exhibit similar GITT potential profiles, demonstrating the same sodium storage mechanism. The Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient results calculated according to Fick's second law also show a greatly promoted Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient for GeP/CN‐3 (Figure <link href="#anie202102954-fig-0006">6 f</link>). The above kinetic analysis together illustrates accelerated charge transfer and promoted Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics, which would account for the improved reversible capacity and rate capability for GeP/CN‐3 electrode.</p><p>Furthermore, the excellent sodium storage performance for GeP/CN‐3 electrode could be unraveled for the following reasons: 1) The free‐standing electrode with high surface area and sufficient mesopores ensure efficient electrolyte infiltration, which would effectively shorten the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathways and accelerate the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics; 2) the hollow nitrogen‐doped carbon nanoreactors effectively restrain free crystal growth of GeP during the phosphating synthesis, resulting in small particle size of GeP with shortened Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion distance; 3) the hollow nitrogen‐doped carbon nanoreactors could not only facilitate electron transport but also buffer volume change, preventing aggregation and restacking of GeP during sodiation/desodiation process. The good electronic conductivity and self‐integrity of the so‐prepared electrodes could avoid the negative factors caused by polymeric binders and other additives.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202102954-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>Unique structure of hollow nanoreactors assembled with conductive nitrogen‐doped carbon networks for in situ synthesis of GeP electrodes were fabricated via a facile electrospinning approach followed by a sealed phosphating process. Such self‐supported electrodes possess abundant porosity, high conductivity and excellent mechanical stability, which not only act as a container to control the crystal growth of GeP during phosphating but also effectively restrain the volume change of GeP during sodiation/desodiation process. The size of the nanoreactors, thus the confinement effect and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics were ideally tunable by varying the fiber width, hollow size and tube thickness. We proved that the electrochemical performance of the obtained GeP anode highly depends on its architecture and the nanoreactor design shows great potential in solving the volume change problem for those 3D networked free‐standing GeP/CN electrodes. The optimized GeP/CN‐3 electrode exhibited high reversible capacities of 830 and 330 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 100 and 4000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and excellent cycling stability was achieved at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup> over 350 cycles. This work highlights the promising practical application of GeP in SIBs and offers guidance for rational structure design of advanced nanomaterials for various energy storage devices.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202102954-sec-0004" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 51933007, No. 51673123, and No. 22005346), and the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (No. 2017YFE0111500).</p></section><section xml:id="anie202102954-sec-0005" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography style="numbered"><bib xml:id="anie202102954-bib-0001"><citation type="other" xml:id="anie202102954-cit-0001"> </citation></bib><bib xml:id="anie202102954-bib-0002"><label>1a</label><citation type="journal" xml:id="anie202102954-cit-0002"><author><givenNames>J.
