Herein, this work demonstrates a simple, gel‐derived method to synthesize uniform 1D elongated sub‐nanotubes with an anatase/bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystal wall (TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs). The transformation mechanism of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs is studied by various ex situ characterization techniques. The resulting 1D nanostructures exhibit, synchronously, a high aspect ratio, open tubular interior, and anatase/bronze nanocrystal TiO<sub>2</sub> wall. This results in excellent properties of electron/ion transport and reaction kinetics. Consequently, as an anode material for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs display an ultrastable long‐life cycling stability with a capacity of 107 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 16 C after 4000 cycles and a high‐rate capacity of 94 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 32 C. This a high‐rate and long‐life performance is superior to any report on pure TiO<sub>2</sub> for SIBs. This work provides new fundamental information for the design and fabrication of inorganic structures for energy and environmental applications.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201804116-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>1D elongated sub‐nanotubes with an anatase/bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystal wall</b> are synthesized through a gel‐derived method. This new structure exhibits a high aspect ratio, open interior, and an anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall, that results in fast electron/Na<sup>+</sup> transport and large storage sites. This work enables new paths to the design and fabrication of inorganical structures for energy and environmental applications.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804116-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201804116-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201804116-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Inorganic structures with excellent materials properties have received a great deal of research interest in high‐rate and long‐life energy storage devices because of their great significance. As one of the classical and most investigated oxides, nanostructured TiO<sub>2</sub> has been extensively studied as an anode material in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) due to its structural stability, safe working‐voltage, and low‐cost.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0001 #adma201804116-bib-0002 #adma201804116-bib-0003"/> However, several challenges remain for the design of an ideal TiO<sub>2</sub> anode for SIBs. Generally, the morphology, particle size, pore size, and phase of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanostructures play important roles in materials properties and electrochemical performance. A range of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanostructures from 0D to 3D have therefore been synthesized and widely reported.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0004"/> Among these, 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes have attracted significant attention owing to the potential properties of large surface area, fast electron/ion transport, and short ion diffusion path.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0005 #adma201804116-bib-0006 #adma201804116-bib-0007 #adma201804116-bib-0008 #adma201804116-bib-0009 #adma201804116-bib-0010"/> Generally, 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes have been prepared by three (3) methods: 1) template‐assistance; 2) anodic oxidation; and 3) hydrothermal/solvothermal synthesis.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0005 #adma201804116-bib-0006 #adma201804116-bib-0007 #adma201804116-bib-0008 #adma201804116-bib-0009 #adma201804116-bib-0010 #adma201804116-bib-0011 #adma201804116-bib-0012 #adma201804116-bib-0013 #adma201804116-bib-0014 #adma201804116-bib-0015 #adma201804116-bib-0016 #adma201804116-bib-0017"/> However these are limited, respectively, by a complex fabrication, low production rate, and a significant cost.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0005 #adma201804116-bib-0006 #adma201804116-bib-0007 #adma201804116-bib-0008"/> A significant challenge therefore is the synthesis of a less complex, low‐cost, and greater production of large‐scale 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes.</p><p>The material properties of 1D nanotubes can be significantly improved by increasing aspect‐ratio.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0005 #adma201804116-bib-0011 #adma201804116-bib-0012 #adma201804116-bib-0013"/> The diameter of TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube can be readily adjusted to nanosize. However, a defined length of nanotube is difficult to synthesize in tens of micrometers.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0005 #adma201804116-bib-0006 #adma201804116-bib-0007 #adma201804116-bib-0008"/> Although a modified mechanical force‐driven hydrothermal method was recently reported to synthesize high‐aspect‐ratio TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes,<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0011 #adma201804116-bib-0012"/> a drawback is that it is limited by a low yield, and requires harsh reaction conditions.</p><p>For practical application of high‐aspect‐ratio nanotubes in SIBs, the contact of materials and electrolyte associated with the active area needs to be enhanced.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0018"/> High‐aspect‐ratio nanotubes with a small diameter could impede contact between the electrolyte and enclosed tubular interior.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0019"/> Although 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes with a high aspect ratio possess potential for enhanced materials properties, their reaction kinetics and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion are significantly restrained. Therefore, 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes with the high aspect ratio and open interior are considered promising for practical structure. However, these are reported rarely.</p><p>Moreover, the introduction of phase engineering into TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes can effectively modulate the electron–hole pairs and internal interface properties.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0007 #adma201804116-bib-0020"/> Generally, bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> is more favorable for Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation compared with anatase, rutile, and amorphous TiO<sub>2</sub> due to its unique, open crystal structure.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0021 #adma201804116-bib-0022"/> However, all reported experimental results of bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> for SIBs are not satisfactory. This is because of sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> kinetics associated with poor crystallization degree.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0023 #adma201804116-bib-0024 #adma201804116-bib-0025"/> Nevertheless, because of a 2D diffusion path and high crystallization degree of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub>, they can show better SIBs performance than bronze TiO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0026"/> However, performance can be improved with increasing amount of theoritical storage sites.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0021 #adma201804116-bib-0022"/> A combination of bronze phase and anatase phase in a TiO<sub>2</sub> nanostructure is believed to be an effective strategy to overcome these drawbacks and, in addition, achieve a positive synergistic effect. This has been demonstrated with application in lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) and SIBs.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0027 #adma201804116-bib-0028 #adma201804116-bib-0029 #adma201804116-bib-0030"/> However, the 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotube with bronze/anatase nanocrystal wall has not been reported in SIBs.</p><p>The development of a facile method for large‐scale fabrication of 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes with a high aspect ratio, open interior, and anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall is highly desired for superior high‐rate and long‐life SIBs, but remains a major research challenge. Here, we demonstrate a convenient and scalable method to prepare this material that results in a high‐aspect‐ratio, open interior with 74% circle, and anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall. TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs have large surface area (152.8 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>), adequate material‐electrolyte contact area, abundant anatase/bronze interfaces, fast and direct electron/ion transport, and short ion diffusion distance. Consequently, the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs show great promise for application in SIBs, with a long‐life performance with a capacity of 107 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 16 C after 4000 cycles and a high‐rate capacity of 94 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 32 C. These results verify the superiority of the unique structure.</p><p>A white CH<sub>3</sub>COO–[Ti–O–Ti]–OBu gel (Figure S1, Supporting Information) was prepared through a novel nonhydrolytic sol–gel route as described in Equations (S1) and (S2) (Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0031 #adma201804116-bib-0032"/> Typically, as shown in scanning electron microscopy (SEM, <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, Figure S2a, Supporting Information and Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0001"/>b) images, it can be observed that the CH<sub>3</sub>COO–[Ti–O–Ti]–OBu gel exhibits an elongated nanoribbon‐structure with the length of tens of micrometers and a width of about 80 nm. The side‐view high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM, Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0001"/>c) image shows that the nanoribbon is a layered structure with small thickness of about 13 nm. The typical interlayer spacing of 1.42 nm of gel nanoribbon can also be detected in X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern (Figure S2b, Supporting Information). The Fourier transform‐infrared (FTIR) spectrum (Figure S3, Supporting Information) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Figure S4, Supporting Information) reveals that the gel nanoribbons are composed of a [Ti–O–Ti] bridge and CH<sub>3</sub>COO and OBu ligands that accords with previous work.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0033 #adma201804116-bib-0034 #adma201804116-bib-0035 #adma201804116-bib-0036 #adma201804116-bib-0037 #adma201804116-bib-0038"/> The obtained gel act as a template to prepare TiO<sub>2</sub> after a simple calcination process. Moreover, thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) and differential scanning calorimeter (DSC) were carried out and disclosed the gel‐derived TiO<sub>2</sub> formation mechanism (Figure S5, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0039"/> The gels calcined at different temperatures (100, 200, 300, 400, 500, and 600 °C) were conducted to ex situ study the changes through XRD, FTIR, SEM, and TEM. As shown in Figures S6–S9 (Supporting Information), temperature‐dependent studies (Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0001"/>d) disclose that the elongated nanoribbons (I) initially roll into elongated sub‐nanotubes (II) in the low‐temperature region (&lt;250 °C), owing to volatilization of gases.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0017 #adma201804116-bib-0040"/> The organic groups in the obtained gel sub‐nanotubes decompose in the middle‐temperature region (250–350 °C), forming TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with amorphous wall (Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs) at 300 °C. The desirable 1D elongated TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes, with anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall and a large number of interfaces (IV), are then achieved in high temperature (400 °C). However, TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with predominantly anatase nanocrystal wall (Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs), and broken sub‐nanotubes, are formed at elevated temperature of 500 and 600 °C, respectively.</p><figure xml:id="adma201804116-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804116-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image, b) TEM image, and c) HRTEM image of CH<sub>3</sub>COO–[Ti–O–Ti]–OBu gel. d) Schematic illustration of the preparation of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs converted from gel.</caption></figure><p>Following calcination of the gel at 400 °C in air, a significant amount of 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with an ultralong length up to tens of micrometers is produced (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a). Typically, the obtained TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs exhibit 25 nm in outer radius (<i>R</i>, Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0002"/>b) and 7.8 nm in thickness (<i>h</i>, Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0002"/>c). This strongly suggests that the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs have high aspect ratio with small thickness. The TEM image of sub‐nanotubes demonstrates the open interior with unsealed arc (Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0002"/>b). The radian (α) of formed subtubes can be defined by the relationship, α = <i>L</i>/(<i>R</i> – <i>h</i>). The arc length (<i>L</i>) of the obtained sub‐nanotube is equal to the width (80 nm) of the gel template. The percentage of the arc to the total circle can be derived from, α/2π = 80/2π(<i>R</i> – <i>h</i>). Consequently, the TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes have around 74% circle. The scanning‐TEM (STEM) mappings of sub‐nanotubes (Figure S10, Supporting Information) show that the sub‐nanotubes are composted of Ti and O elements with a uniform distribution. From the selected area electron diffraction pattern (SAED; Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0002"/>b, inset) and XRD pattern (Figure S6b, Supporting Information), it can be readily discerned that the diffraction rings and peaks are attributed to bronze (JCPDS 35‐0088) and anatase (JCPDS 21‐1272) TiO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0027 #adma201804116-bib-0028 #adma201804116-bib-0029 #adma201804116-bib-0030"/> This confirms that the CH<sub>3</sub>COO–[Ti–O–Ti]–OBu gel was transferred into anatase/bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> during calcination. The HRTEM image (Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0002"/>d) shows that the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs consist of a significantly large number of small nanocrystals with abundant interfaces, in which the interplanar distances of 0.35 and 0.362 nm are assigned to, respectively, (101) planes of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> and (001) planes of bronze TiO<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="adma201804116-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804116-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image, b) TEM image and corresponding SAED pattern, c,d) HRTEM images of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. The inset in (b) is a schematic illustration of the sub‐nanotube cross‐section, and the letters <i>h</i>, <i>R</i>, and α in (b) represent, respectively, the SNT wall thickness, outer SNT radius, and rolled radian. A, B, and lines in (d), represent, respectively, anatase, bronze, and crystallite interfaces.</caption></figure><p>Raman spectrum and cyclic voltammetry (CV) curve were used to confirm the phase composition of the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a, both bronze and anatase phases can be readily detected in the Raman spectrum TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0027 #adma201804116-bib-0028 #adma201804116-bib-0029"/> Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0003"/>b shows a typical CV curve of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs with a potential window of 1.0–3.0 V at a scan rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The Li half cells, using Li metal as a reference electrode, were fabricated based on previously reported details.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0041"/> Moreover, the weight content of anatase and bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> can be estimated by an electrochemical quantification method based on the peak areas in CV curve.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0030 #adma201804116-bib-0042"/> Consequently, the weight content of anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> is about 71%. The reduction peaks can be readily observed in TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. According to previous work,<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0027 #adma201804116-bib-0028 #adma201804116-bib-0029"/> the large A‐peak is located at 1.71 V, and arises from Li<sup>+</sup> diffusion in the anatase phase, while the two (2) middle S1‐/S2‐peaks are attributed to surface redox charging in the bronze phase at 1.46 and 1.53 V. XPS analysis was further carried out to investigate the chemical state of the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. The Ti 2P fine spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0003"/>c) displays two major peaks, belonging to Ti 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Ti 2p<sub>1/2</sub> with binding energy separation of 5.75 eV. This suggests the existence of Ti<sup>4+</sup> in TiO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0035 #adma201804116-bib-0036 #adma201804116-bib-0037"/> The N<sub>2</sub> sorption isotherm was employed to determine the surface area and pore distribution of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0003"/>d, the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs exhibit a high Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface area of 152.8 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> with adequate mesoporous texture. The Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs also show high specific surface area. This is attributed to the identical 1D sub‐nanotube structure with high aspect ratio and open interior (Figure S11, Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201804116-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804116-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Raman spectrum, b) CV curve, c) Ti 2p fine XPS spectrum, and d) N<sub>2</sub> sorption curve of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. The inset in (d) shows the corresponding pore size distribution. A and B in (a) represent, respectively, anatase and bronze.</caption></figure><p>The TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with a high aspect ratio, open interior, and anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall are favorable for applications in energy and environmental fields, especially SIBs. The elongated TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall (TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs) and two control materials (Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs) were applied as anode materials in SIBs to evaluate performance of electrochemical storage of Na<sup>+</sup>. <b>Figures</b> <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a and Figure S12 (Supporting Information) present the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves with a potential window of 0.05–3.00 V at a scan rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. There is one irreversible peak ranging over 0.7–1.3 V in the first cathodic process that results from the formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) and the decomposition of electrolyte.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0043 #adma201804116-bib-0044 #adma201804116-bib-0045 #adma201804116-bib-0046 #adma201804116-bib-0047"/> In the following cycles, the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs show one pair of broad reductive/oxidative peaks located at ≈0.59/0.85 V and ≈0.52/0.81 V, respectively, whilst the Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs display no peaks owing to amorphous structure.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0043 #adma201804116-bib-0044 #adma201804116-bib-0045"/> Because of sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> kinetics, no divided peaks of anatase and bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> are observed and the intensity of these peaks becomes stronger.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0045"/> The lower voltage deviation between the oxidative and reductive peaks in TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs suggests that the anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall with abundant interfaces have high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0030"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201804116-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201804116-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemical properties of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs of Na half‐cells: a) CV curves. b) Rate performance of Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs, TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs, and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. c) Long‐life cycle performance at 16 C. d) CV curves at different rates and corresponding e) capacitive contribution. 1 C = 335 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0004"/>b presents the rate capacities. Because the 1D sub‐nanotubes with a high aspect ratio and open interior show enhanced electron conductivity and Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion, they demonstrate high‐rate capabilities (Table S1, Supporting Information). The TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs show an initial Coulombic efficiency (CE) of 43.5% due to the adverse reactions.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0043 #adma201804116-bib-0044 #adma201804116-bib-0045"/> In the following cycles at 1C, the CE increased to around 95% during which the storage capacity is slowly increased due to electrochemical activation.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0045"/> Moreover, it is worth pointing out that, the solid anatase/bronze interfaces in TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs could provide additional storage sites via a pseudocapacitive‐like mechanism.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0030 #adma201804116-bib-0048"/> Therefore, the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs with anatase/bronze wall afford the best rate performance with 191, 170, 148, 129, 110, and 94 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, and 32 C, respectively (Figure S13a, Supporting Information). Importantly, this is the best rate capability ever reported in previous works on pure TiO<sub>2</sub> for SIBs (Figure S13b, Supporting Information). An interesting finding also is that the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs deliver smaller polarization than Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> at higher rates as can be seen in the discharge–charge profiles (Figure S14, Supporting Information). This further demonstrates that the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs with bronze/anatase nanocrystals wall demonstrate enhanced electron and ion transport.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0049"/> The enhanced kinetics of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs is also confirmed by electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS, Figure S15a, Supporting Information) and fitted equivalent circuit (Figure S15b, Supporting Information). As shown in Table S3 (Supporting Information), the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs show the smallest SEI‐film resistance (<i>R</i><sub>f</sub>) and charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) due to their bronze/anatase interfaces.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0030"/> Moreover, the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients of the three anodes can also be studied by the Warburg coefficient (σ) (Figure S15c, Supporting Information). The σ value of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs (19.2) is lower than those of Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs (110.8) and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs (23.7). According to previous work,<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0041 #adma201804116-bib-0050 #adma201804116-bib-0051"/> the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficient of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs is, respectively, 33.3 and 1.5 times higher than those of Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. Therefore, the bronze/anatase nanocrystals wall is significantly beneficial to the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction, resulting in high Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics. Figure S16 (Supporting Information) presents the cycling performance at 2C. It can be seen that the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs remain showing a high capacity of 177.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles. This is, respectively, 1.54 and 1.28 times greater than those of Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. More impressively, as shown in Figures <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0004"/>c and Figure S17 (Supporting Information), the three electrodes all exhibit excellent long‐life cycling stability at a high rate (16 C). This result manifests that all 1D subtubes with a high aspect ratio and open interior have strong stability during Na<sup>+</sup> discharge–charge processes. Figure S18 (Supporting Information) demonstrates that the sub‐nanotube structure could be well maintained in the TiO<sub>2</sub>‐SNTs anode even after 4000 cycles at 16 C. It is noteworthy that the TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs also show highest reversible capacity of 107.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 4000 cycles with an ultralow capacity fading of 4.43% per 1000 cycles. To the best of our knowledge, this excellent long‐life performance under high rate is superior to the most of reported on pure TiO<sub>2</sub> for SIBs (Table S2, Supporting Information). It is concluded that the 1D elongated sub‐nanotubes with a high aspect ratio and open interior are favorable to Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and electron transport. Moreover, the anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall with abundant interfaces enhance charge‐transfer kinetics and Na<sup>+</sup> storage sites.</p><p>The CV curves (Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0004"/>d) from 0.2 to 8 mV s<sup>−1</sup> were carried out to determine the origin of the high rate performance of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. The large surface area and small anatase/bronze nanocrystal of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs are favorable to capacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage. The degree of capacitive effects can be characterized according to the Equation <link href="#adma201804116-disp-0001"/><link href="#adma201804116-bib-0052 #adma201804116-bib-0053"/><displayedItem xml:id="adma201804116-disp-0001" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma201804116-math-0001.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mo>=</mo><mi>a</mi><msup><mi>v</mi><mi>b</mi></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><p>The peak current (<i>i</i>) obeys a relationship with the scan rate (<i>v</i>), while <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are constants. The <i>b</i>‐value can be determined from the log(<i>v</i>)–log(<i>i</i>) slope. Generally, the <i>b</i>‐value is in the range of 0.5–1. The greater the <i>b</i>‐value is, the greater the contribution of the capacitive process. The <i>b</i>‐values of reduction and oxidation peaks are 0.84 and 0.82, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0004"/>d), demonstrating that TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs have favorable capacitive kinetics. Moreover, the capacitive contribution at different sweep rates can be quantitatively analyzed based on the Equation <link href="#adma201804116-disp-0002"/><link href="#adma201804116-bib-0052 #adma201804116-bib-0053"/><displayedItem xml:id="adma201804116-disp-0002" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201804116:adma201804116-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/adma201804116-math-0002.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mrow><mfenced><mi>V</mi></mfenced></mrow><mo>=</mo><msub><mi>k</mi><mn>1</mn></msub><mi>v</mi><mo>+</mo><msub><mi>k</mi><mn>2</mn></msub><msup><mi>v</mi><mrow><mn>0.5</mn></mrow></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem>where <i>i</i> is the measured current associated with a scan rate (<i>v</i>), and <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>0.5</sup> represent capacitive and diffusion‐controlled contributions, respectively. As seen in Figure <link href="#adma201804116-fig-0004"/>e, it can be readily observed that the capacitive contribution rises with increasing scan rate and reaches a value of 85.8% at 8 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs exhibit a similar trend and result in high values in capacitive contributions. This is due to their same material properties of high aspect ratio and open interior (Figure S19, Supporting Information). It needs to be pointed out that the capacitive contributions of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs are slightly more than those of Am‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs and Pa‐TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs. This discloses that the surface/interface controlled pseudocapacitive reactions, derived‐from their unique structures of high aspect ratio, open interior, and anatase/bronze phases, are responsible for the excellent rate performance of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs.</p><p>From these electrochemical findings, it is clear that it is the unique structure of 1D TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with a high aspect‐ratio, open interior, and anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall, which is responsible for the enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance. These structural advantages are illustrated in Figure S20 (Supporting Information). a) The high aspect ratio and small thickness of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs can effectively enhance electron transport and shorten Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion pathways. b) The high surface area of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs facilitates Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion. c) The open interior of TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs assures full contact between active material and electrolytes, thereby making best use of the active material during electrochemical cycling. d) The anatase/bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> nanocrystal wall with abundant interfaces contributes additional Na<sup>+</sup> storage sites, but also enhances the charge‐transfer kinetics.<link href="#adma201804116-bib-0030"/></p><p>In summary, we have demonstrated that 1D elongated TiO<sub>2</sub> sub‐nanotubes with about 74% circle can be prepared in a simple gel‐derived synthesis. A formation mechanism has been studied by ex situ characterization techniques that reveal that elongated gel nanoribbons are rolled into a sub‐nanotubes architecture in a primary volatilization and these gel sub‐nanotubes decompose and crystallize to form anatase/bronze TiO<sub>2</sub> at a temperature of 400 °C. These as‐prepared TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs exhibit high surface area, fast electron/ion transport, short ion diffusion path, and large storage sites due to high aspect ratio, open interior, and anatase/bronze nanocrystal wall. This results in TiO<sub>2</sub> SNTs with superior high‐rate performance and long‐life performance for promising anode material for SIBs. These findings underscore that high aspect ratio and open interior are essential materials properties for 1D nanotubes for ultrafast SIBs. More importantly, findings hold significant promise to provide new fundamental information for the design and fabrication of inorganic structures for energy and environmental applications.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201804116-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors acknowledge the financial support by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 51472177, 11474216, 51272173, and 21576202), and China‐EU Science and Technology Cooperation Project (Grant No. SQ2013ZOA100006).
