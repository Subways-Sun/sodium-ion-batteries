However, anodes with long cycle life and large reversible capacities are still lacking and therefore limiting the development of SIBs. Here, a bulk Bi anode with surprisingly high Na storage performance in combination with glyme‐based electrolytes is reported. This study shows that the bulk Bi electrode is gradually developed into a porous integrity during initial cycling, which is totally different from that in carbonate‐based electrolytes and ensures facile Na<sup>+</sup> transport and structural stability. The achievable capacity of bulk Bi in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐diglyme electrolyte is high up to 400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and the capacity retention is 94.4% after 2000 cycles, corresponding to a capacity loss of 0.0028% per cycle. It exhibits two flat discharge/charge plateaus at 0.67/0.77 and 0.46/0.64 V, ascribed to the typical two‐phase reactions of Bi ↔ NaBi and NaBi ↔ Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, respectively. The excellent performance is attributed to the unique porous integrity, stable solid electrolyte interface, and good electrode wettability of glymes. This interplay between electrolyte and electrode to boost Na storage performance will pave a new pathway for high‐performance SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en" xml:id="adma201702212-abs-0001"><p><b>A bulk Bi anode coupled with glyme‐based electrolytes</b> presents a high reversible capacity of 400 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and capacity retention of 94.4% after 2000 cycles, corresponding to a capacity loss of 0.0028% per cycle. This is attributed to the unique porous integrity evolved during initial cycles, stable solid electrolyte interface, and good electrode wettability of glymes.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201702212-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201702212-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted significant academic and technological research interests in recent years.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0001"/> The battery performance strongly relies on the cathodes, anodes, electrolytes, and their compatibility. Cathodes with various structures, like NASICON, P2, or O3, have been attempted and exhibited stability in carbonate‐based electrolytes.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0002"/> However, although lots of anode materials have been reported, the anode is still faced with great challenges for high performance SIBs.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0003"/> The essential merits of anodes should have sodiation potentials between 0.3 and 1.0 V to compromise the Na dendrite formation and voltage penalty of SIBs, large capacity, high Coulombic efficiency, and long cycle life.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0004"/> Such desirable anodes have seldom been reported, and are becoming the determining factor for the development of SIBs. Therefore, exploring high‐performance anodes will be crucial for the development of rechargeable SIBs.</p><p>Transition‐metal oxides and sulfides based on conversion‐reaction mechanism can lead to large capacities but poor cycle life with voltages of ≈1.5 V.[<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0003 #adma201702212-bib-0005" style="qv">3c,d,5</link>] Metals in III‐A, IV‐A, and V‐A of the periodic table, such as Sb, Bi, and Sn, are attractive for high theoretical capacities of &gt;300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> by forming alloys with Na.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0006"/> However, severe pulverization during alloying and dealloying resulted in rapid capacity decay in carbonate‐based electrolytes.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0007"/> Large amounts of carbon were applied to reduce the adverse effect, but the electrochemical performance of electrodes is far from satisfactory. The promising type of anodes is titanium‐based metal oxides, which showed an average voltage of 0.75 V and small capacities of ≈70 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.[<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0003 #adma201702212-bib-0008" style="qv">3a,8</link>] They presented good cycle stability due to the zero‐strain effect upon Na (de)intercalation. Graphite, based on the co‐intercalation of solvates of Na<sup>+</sup> and glyme molecules in electrolytes, showed long cycle life and capacities of ≈110 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with voltage plateaus between 0.5 and 1.1 V.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0009"/> In carbonate‐based electrolytes, hard carbon exhibited large discharge/charge capacities of ≈300 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0010"/> Nevertheless, its low operating voltage close to 0 V favors Na‐dendrite formation, especially at high current loads, and can incur safety issues.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0011"/> It is clear that the electrochemical behavior of anodes is strongly related to the applied electrolytes, like carbonates and glymes. Also, rational selection of electrolytes has recently been demonstrated to promote battery performance.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0012"/> Studying the interplay of electrodes and electrolytes to produce high Na storage performance will pave a new path for the development of SIBs.</p><p>In an empirical view, the best way to improve anode performance for Li/Na storage is to apply zero‐strain materials or carbon modification, aiming to reduce the loose electric contact during cycles, especially for the conversion‐reaction and alloying–dealloying mechanisms. Here, we reported that, by coupling with glyme‐based electrolytes, the microsized Bi is gradually developed into a porous integrity with ultralong cycling stability. This excellent performance is attributed to the unique porous integrity evolved in the glyme‐based electrolytes, stable solid electrolyte interface (SEI), and good electrode wettability of glymes. To the best of our knowledge, such superior anode performance of SIBs has never been reported before. This synergy to boost anode performance will shed light on the understanding and designing of high‐performance batteries.</p><p>The commercial bulk Bi is used as received without any treatment. It is 15–18 µm in size, as revealed by scanning electron microscope (SEM) and shown in the inset of <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a; Figure S1 (Supporting Information). The sharp diffraction peaks of the bulk Bi in the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern can be well indexed to a rhombohedral arrangement with a space group of <i>R</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/adma201702212-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-math-0001"/><i>m</i> in both of Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"/>b and its inset. The Bi electrode is composed of 80 wt% of bulk Bi, 10 wt% of Super P, and 10 wt% of sodium carboxylmethylcellulose (CMC). Its electrochemical performance is evaluated in coin cells with a Bi electrode, Na anode, and 1.0 <sc>m</sc> of NaPF<sub>6</sub> in diglyme (NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2). Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"/>c shows the typical cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves of the Bi electrode over the initial four cycles between 0.1 and 2.1 V at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. One broad reduction peak in the cathodic scan can be observed to be followed by two oxidation peaks in the anodic scan of the first cycle, which is due to activation of the Bi electrode and formation of SEI. In the following cycles, two distinct couples of redox peaks are located at 0.67/0.77 and 0.46/0.64 V, respectively, and are overlapped. The area ratio of the two peaks in each cathodic/anodic scan is close to 1:2, suggesting reversible formation of NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0013"/></p><figure xml:id="adma201702212-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image of the bulk Bi. b) XRD pattern with the inset refined structure model of the bulk Bi. c) CV curves of the Bi electrode at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.1 and 2.1 V. d) Discharge/charge curves of the fifth cycle at different current densities. e) Cycling stability at 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte.</caption></figure><p>The typical discharge/charge curves of the Bi electrode in the fifth cycle at varied current densities are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"/>d. The reversible capacities are achieved for 394.8, 385.1, 382.9, 371.9, 369.8, 362.9, and 356.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at different current densities from 50 to 100, 400, 800, 1200, 1600, and 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Small polarization with current density increase indicates fast kinetics, and thus good rate capability on the Bi electrode. In the initial cycle, the Bi electrode presents a very high Coulombic efficiency of 94.8% and discharge capacity of 414.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure S2 (Supporting Information). This is contributed by the theoretical specific capacity of Bi (384.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) based on formation of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, Super P (150 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) in Figure S3 (Supporting Information), and formation of SEI on the Bi electrode. This is the highest reversible capacity of Na anodes as reported. Of note, there are two typical flat plateaus in both the discharging and charging processes beyond the initial cycle, which is consistent with the two couples of redox peaks in the CV curves in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"/>c. It means that in each separate process there involve two typical two‐phase reactions. The capacity stability responding to the varied current densities is depicted in Figure S4 (Supporting Information). Obviously, the capacity is decreased by as small as 9.8% with the current density dramatically increased by 40 times from 50 to 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. At each current density, the Coulombic efficiency is approaching 100%, indicative of good reversibility and rechargeability. The cycling performance of the Bi electrode is evaluated at a low current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for 70 cycles in Figure S5 (Supporting Information). Furthermore, the cycling stability of the Bi electrode is examined at 400 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for 2000 cycles in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"/>e, which runs for more than five months. The discharge/charge capacity is almost constant at 389 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, and the capacity retention is high up to 94.4%, corresponding to a capacity loss of 0.0028% per cycle. These indicate excellent rate capability and ultralong cycle life of the Bi electrode by coupling with the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte. The bulk Bi also shows better electrochemical performance than the reported materials in terms of discharge/charge plateaus, capacity retention, rate capability, and Coulombic efficiency, as listed in Table S1 (Supporting Information).</p><p>The electrochemical performance of the Bi electrode is also investigated with various compositions of the glyme‐based electrolytes and binders. 1,2‐Dimethoxyethane, diglyme (G2), triglyme, and tetraglyme in combination with NaPF<sub>6</sub> and NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> are employed as electrolytes. Consistent discharge/charge profiles in all the electrolytes are obtained in Figures S6–S10 (Supporting Information) and good cycling stability of the Bi electrodes in the various electrolytes are comparatively presented in Figure S11 (Supporting Information). The Bi electrode with the CMC binder is comparable to those with polyvinylidene fluoride, polytetrafluoroethylene, and sodium alginate in Figures S12–S14 (Supporting Information), respectively. These confirm that the excellent electrochemical performance of the Bi electrode is enabled by its synergy with the glyme‐based electrolytes, showing little dependence on either the selected Na salts or binders. This is totally different from that in carbon‐based electrolytes,<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0007"/> and implies the essential role of the glyme molecules on the demonstrated battery performance.</p><p>The gradual sodiation/desodiation of Bi in the second discharge/charge cycle was monitored by ex situ XRD, as displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a,b. After discharged to 0.7 V in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>a, the rhombohedral Bi is converted to the tetragonal NaBi, as evidenced by its characteristic diffraction peaks at 25.8°, 31.8°, 36.5°, and 41.2°, respectively, in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>b. They completely disappear with the presence of diffraction peaks of 18.3°, 21.0°, 32.9°, 33.7°, and 47.8° after fully discharged to 0.1 V, which are attributed to the new phase of hexagonal Na<sub>3</sub>Bi. The symmetric charging process in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>a is also confirmed by the reversible formation of Bi and the gradual disappearance of the phases of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi and NaBi in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>b. The selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns of the fully discharged and charged Bi electrodes present the characteristic diffraction points of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi ((110), (112), (203), and (211)) and Bi ((012), (110), (202), and (204)) in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>c,d, respectively. They are in good accordance with the XRD patterns in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/> and high resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) images in Figure S15 (Supporting Information). The two flat plateaus in each discharging/charging process of Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>a,c suggest the presence of two typical two‐phase reactions, which are corresponding to Bi ↔ NaBi and NaBi ↔ Na<sub>3</sub>Bi, respectively. Moreover, the capacity of the plateau at 0.67/0.77 V is half of that at 0.46/0.64 V in the discharging/charging process. It further confirms the gradual formation of NaBi and Na<sub>3</sub>Bi in the two distinct steps. These suggest an alloying/dealloying reaction mechanism in the Bi electrode in couple with the glyme‐based electrolyte.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702212-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Discharge and charge profile of the second cycle. b) XRD patterns of the Bi electrode at the states as labeled in (a). SAED patterns of the Bi electrodes after c) discharged to 0.1 V and d) charged to 2.1 V, respectively.</caption></figure><p>The surprisingly high electrochemical performance of the bulk Bi electrode in the glyme‐based electrolytes is in sharp contrast to the rapidly decaying performance in 1 <sc>m</sc> NaPF<sub>6</sub> in propylene carbonate (NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC) in Figure S16 (Supporting Information) under the same conditions, which is in agreement with previous reports.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0014"/> The morphological evolution of the Bi electrodes in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte is captured to comparatively depict with that in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte during cycles in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>; Figure S17 (Supporting Information). It is obvious that the bulk Bi electrode in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"/>a is ruptured after only ten cycles of sodiation/desodiation in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"/>b; Figure S17b (Supporting Information). Remarkably, in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte, the Bi electrode is developed into a porous integrity after 100 cycles in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"/>c, and it is sustained for even 2000 cycles, as depicted in Figure S18 (Supporting Information). The porous feature is shown in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"/>d; Figures S18 and S19 (Supporting Information), respectively. These guarantee superior kinetics of Na<sup>+</sup> in the bulk Bi electrode. The impedance of the Bi electrode in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte is stable with cycles as presented in Figure S20 (Supporting Information), while in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte it is exponentially increased in Figure S21 (Supporting Information). This is linked to the developed morphological features of the Bi electrode during cycles and the good wettability of the glyme‐based electrolytes. They are revealed by the small contact angles by contrasting with the large ones of the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte in the insets of Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"/>b,c, and Figure S22 (Supporting Information). This unique morphological evolution has never been observed in the sodiation/desodiation processes before, and guarantees the superior rate capability and ultralong cycle life of the Bi electrode by coupling with the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte. In addition, the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte is demonstrated to show considerable electrochemical stability of up to 4.3 V in Figure S23 (Supporting Information).</p><figure xml:id="adma201702212-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM image of the pristine Bi electrode. b) SEM image of the Bi electrode after ten cycles in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte and its contact angle (inset). SEM images of the Bi electrodes after c) 100 and d) 2000 cycles in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte, respectively.</caption></figure><p>In order to clearly demonstrate the evolution of the porous integrity of Bi electrode in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte during cycles, the Bi electrode was prepared by pasting a mixture of bulk Bi particles and CMC binder onto a Cu foil without any carbon additives. The discharge/charge profiles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> are presented with a very small irreversible capacity in the first cycle in Figure S24 (Supporting Information). The SEM images of the discharged/charged Bi electrodes in the 1st, 10th, 50th, and 100th cycles are displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a,b, respectively. Pinholes are generated in the bulk Bi in the initial cycles, which can be facilely filled with the hydrophilic glyme‐based electrolytes. They are then developed into larger pores to reduce the strain and minimize surface energy but still sustain the integrity of the bulk Bi during the 100 cycles, as demonstrated in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0004"/>a,b.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0015"/> No pulverization or cracks in the bulk Bi can be observed, which is quite different from the Bi electrode in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte (Figure S17, Supporting Information). This is consistent with its ultralong cycle life and stable impedance. The proposed schematic morphological evolution is depicted in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0004"/>c. The Na<sup>+</sup>‐G2 solvates could facilely penetrate into the void spaces of the bulk Bi to minimize surface energy, which are generated by swelling and contraction of Bi upon Na insertion and extraction. The porous integrity of Bi is gradually formed and sustained during cycles in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0004"/>a,b, and enables facile electron and Na<sup>+</sup> transport through the whole electrode and realizes fast kinetics and long cycling stability.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702212-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SEM images of the a) discharged and b) charged Bi electrodes without carbon additives in the 1st, 10th, 50th, and 100th cycle, respectively. c) Schematic illustration of morphological evolution of the Bi electrode during cycles.</caption></figure><p>HRTEM images of the discharged and charged Bi electrodes are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a,b. Well‐developed facets of Na<sub>3</sub>Bi and Bi after discharged to 0.1 V and charged back to 2.1 V, respectively, can be clearly observed. They agree well with the XRD and SAED patterns in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0002"/>. Thin layer of SEI film of several nanometers can be seen on both the discharged and charged Bi electrodes, which are in good accordance with the very small irreversible capacity in the first cycle in Figures S2 and S25 (Supporting Information) and high Coulombic efficiencies during cycles in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0001"/>. The elemental compositions of the surfaces of the discharged and charged Bi electrodes are depicted in the energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) mappings in Figure <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0005"/>c,d. The elements of Na, Bi, F, and their combinations are uniformly distributed in surface layers of both the discharged and charged Bi electrodes. They are believed to derive from NaPF<sub>6</sub>, electrolyte solvent of G2, binder of CMC containing Na, and Bi electrode. In line with the SEM images in Figures <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0003"/> and <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0004"/>, no elemental aggregations or cracks can be observed. The stable SEI film with uniformly distributed elements on the Bi particle is responsible for facile Na<sup>+</sup> transport and structural stability of the Bi electrode during sodiation/desodiation.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702212-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>HRTEM images of the Bi electrodes a) discharged to 0.1 V and b) charged to 2.1 V, respectively. EDS mappings of the elements of Na, Bi, and F on the c) discharged and d) charged Bi electrodes, respectively.</caption></figure><p>The compositions of the SEI films on the first discharged and charged Bi electrodes, as well as the pristine electrode, are further characterized by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201702212-fig-0006"><b>6</b></link>, the XPS spectra of C 1 s, O 1 s, and F 1 s of these Bi electrodes are comparatively displayed. The binder of CMC can be identified from the CC, CH<sub>2</sub>, COC, and RCOONa species with the characteristic peaks at 283.6, 284.9, 286.8, and 288.2 eV in the C 1 s spectra and CO and RCOONa at 533.1 and 531.6 eV in the O 1 s spectra, respectively. The peak at 283.5 eV in the C 1 s spectra is assigned to Super P. An obvious increase of the CH<sub>2</sub> peak at 284.9 eV in the C 1 s spectra after discharge/charge suggests formation of sodium alkoxides (RCH<sub>2</sub>ONa), which is derived from the glyme molecules of G2 and is essential for the interfacial stability of the Bi electrode.<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0016"/> The new peak at 289.8 of the discharged/charged Bi electrode in the C 1 s spectra indicates formation of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, and it is also confirmed by the new peaks at 530.2 eV in the O 1 s spectra. Another new peak at 285.4 eV in the C 1 s spectra is assigned to polyethers<link href="#adma201702212-bib-0017"/> resulted from decomposition of G2, while in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐PC electrolyte polyesters is found at 285.9 eV, as shown in Figure S26 (Supporting Information). The two evident peaks at 684.2 and 688.1 eV in the F 1 s spectra are ascribed to NaF and the P–F species, respectively. The main different compositions of the SEI layers in glyme‐based electrolytes and carbonate‐based electrolytes are sodium alkoxides and polyethers versus sodium alkycarbonates and polyesters (Figure S26, Supporting Information). These lead to the huge difference in the electrochemical performance of the Bi electrodes. The SEI formed in the glyme‐based electrolyte ensures fast Na<sup>+</sup> transport in the interface and structural stability of the Bi electrode, which is mainly related to the glyme solvents, as demonstrated by the little dependence on Na salts and binders in Figure S6–S14 (Supporting Information). In addition, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was carried out on the Bi electrode with a constant current pulse duration of 30 min and a relaxation time of 120 min at 40 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure S27 (Supporting Information). The Coulombic efficiency in each GITT cycle is near 99%, and the discharge capacities are around 390 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with small polarization beyond the first discharge. This suggests good stability of the SEI films as directly observed in the HRTEM images in Figure S25 (Supporting Information) and fast kinetics of the Bi electrode.</p><figure xml:id="adma201702212-fig-0006"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201702212:adma201702212-fig-0006" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201702212-fig-0006-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>XPS spectra of the Bi electrodes: a) C 1 s, b) O 1 s, and c) F 1 s spectra of the pristine, discharged, and charged Bi electrodes in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte, respectively.</caption></figure><p>In summary, the Bi electrode in the NaPF<sub>6</sub>‐G2 electrolyte exhibits two typical two‐phase reactions of Bi ↔ NaBi and NaBi ↔ Na<sub>3</sub>Bi with flat discharge/charge plateaus at 0.67/0.77 V and 0.46/0.64 V, respectively, via the highly reversible alloying/dealloying mechanism. In couple with the glyme‐based electrolytes, the bulk Bi is gradually developed into a porous integrity. The unique features guarantee facile Na<sup>+</sup> transport and structural stability, and thus enable the superior rate capability and extremely high capacity retention of 94.4% even after 2000 cycles. The large capacity of ≈400 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> and ultralong cycle life of the microsized Bi electrode in combination with the glyme‐based electrolyte are rewarded by the unique porous integrity, stable SEI, and good electrode wettability of glymes. The promising results will encourage more investigations into the interplay of electrolyte and electrode for performance enhancement in all electrochemical systems.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201702212-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>C.W. and L.W. contributed equally to this work.
