type="main">Abstract</title><p>P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is a dominant cathode material for sodium‐ion batteries due to its high theoretical capacity and energy density. However, charging P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> to voltages higher than 4.2 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) can induce detrimental structural transformation and severe capacity fading. Herein, stable cycling and moisture resistancy of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> at 4.35 V (vs. Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) are achieved through dual‐site doping with Cu ion at transition metal site (2a) and unusual Zn ion at Na site (2d) for the first time. The Cu ion doping in 2a site stabilizes the metal layer, while more importantly, the unusual alkali‐metal site doping by Zn ion serves as O<sup>2‐</sup>Zn<sup>2+</sup>O<sup>2‐</sup> “pillar” for enhancing electrostatic cohesion between two adjacent transition metal layers, preventing the crack of active material along the <i>a</i>–<i>b</i>‐plane and restraining the generation of O2 phase upon deep desodiation. This unique dual‐site‐doped [Na<sub>0.67</sub>Zn<sub>0.05</sub>]Ni<sub>0.18</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode exhibits a prominent cyclability with 80.6% capacity retention over 2000 cycles at an ultrahigh rate of 10C, demonstrating its great potential for practical applications. Impressively, the full cell devices with [Na<sub>0.67</sub>Zn<sub>0.05</sub>]Ni<sub>0.18</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and commercial hard carbon as cathode and anode, respectively, can deliver a high energy density of 217.9 Wh kg<sup>‐1</sup> and excellent cycle life over 1000 cycles.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202103210-abs-0001" type="graphical" effectively restrain P2–O2 phase transition and prevent material crack due to the enhanced electrostatic cohesions between two transition metal layers, leading to ultralong life span sodium‐ion full xml:id="adma202103210-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202103210-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been widely expected as promising candidates for large‐scale electric energy storage system to realize smart grid because of its virtues of low cost and physicochemical property similar to lithium‐ion batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0001 #adma202103210-bib-0002 #adma202103210-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the electrochemical properties of SIBs have not met the requirements of practical applications due to the lack of suitable cathode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recently, inspired by successful lithium‐based layered transition metal oxides (LTMOs), sodium‐based LTMOs (Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>TMO<sub>2</sub>: TM is transition metal like Co, Fe, Mn, Cu, Ti, Ni, etc.) have been extensively studied as SIB cathodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0005 #adma202103210-bib-0006 #adma202103210-bib-0007 #adma202103210-bib-0008 #adma202103210-bib-0009 #adma202103210-bib-0010 #adma202103210-bib-0011"><sup>5–11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among these layered oxides, high ionic conducting P2‐type<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0012 #adma202103210-bib-0013 #adma202103210-bib-0014"><sup>12–14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> demonstrates much potential for practical applications owing to the merits of high specific capacity and energy density derived from two electron transfer of Ni<sup>2+</sup>/Ni<sup>4+</sup> redox pair.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0015 #adma202103210-bib-0016"><sup>15,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, it suffers from severely irreversible P2–O2 phase transformation due to the gliding of metal layers driven by the strong O<sup>2−</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> electrostatic repulsion between two transition metal layers (TMO<sub>2</sub>) upon deep desodiated state, resulting in inferior cycling performance and rate capability due to the structure degradation and reduced Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics in O2 structure originated from the increased activation energy.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0017 #adma202103210-bib-0018"><sup>17,18</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Recently, some pioneering strategies, including cation doping, nanoengineering, and coating tactics,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0015 #adma202103210-bib-0019 #adma202103210-bib-0020 #adma202103210-bib-0021 #adma202103210-bib-0022"><sup>15,19–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have been investigated to improve the electrochemical properties of P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, among which cation doping has been comprehensively studied to effectively suppress the phase transition, leading to better performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0005 #adma202103210-bib-0006 #adma202103210-bib-0011 #adma202103210-bib-0023 #adma202103210-bib-0024 #adma202103210-bib-0025"><sup>5,6,11,23–25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Wang et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> reported that Mg‐doped P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Mg<i><sub>x</sub></i>O<sub>2</sub> can improve the cycling stability. However, most of those doping strategies only substitute the transition metal site in TMO<sub>2</sub> layers, which cannot effectively reduce the O<sup>2−</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> electrostatic repulsion when charged to low Na content state, leading to serious cracking of materials along the <i>a</i>–<i>b</i>‐plane and further resulting in the exfoliation of active material. Therefore, further reducing the interlayer electrostatic repulsion could better alleviate the material cracking along the <i>a</i>–<i>b</i>‐plane, which could contribute to the stable cycling at high voltage aiming to higher energy/power density. Excitingly, it could be expected that the phase transition and the crack formation can be alleviated through alkali‐metal site substitution with transition metals due to the strong electrostatic cohesion of O<sup>2−</sup>M<sup>n</sup><sup>+</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> “pillar.”<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Unfortunately, realizing this unusual alkali‐metal site doping with high cycling stability at a high cutoff voltage of 4.35 V remains a big challenge in P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, which greatly hinders its practicability.</p><p>Herein, we realize the unusual site‐selective doping in alkali‐metal sites with Zn ions to generate a promising layered cathode, i.e., [Na<sub>0.67</sub>Zn<sub>0.05</sub>]Ni<sub>0.18</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (denoted as NZNCMO), which can not only restrain the P2–O2 phase transition by reduced O<sup>2−</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> electrostatic repulsion between TMO<sub>2</sub> layers thanks to the stable O<sup>2−</sup>Zn<sup>2+</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> “pillar,” but also promote the interfacial diffusion kinetic through lowering the activation energy. The site‐selective doping feature is firmly proved by the combined characterizations including X‐ray diffraction (XRD) Rietveld refinement and spherical aberration corrected transmission electron microscope (ACTEM). This unusual‐doped cathode could deliver high capacity retentions of 97.2%, 90.9%, 80.6% at 0.1C, 1C, and 10C (1C = 170 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) over 100, 200, and 2000 cycles, respectively, when evaluated in half‐cell configuration. More importantly, the full cell device assembled by NZNCMO cathode and commercial hard carbon anode can provide a prominent energy density of 217.9 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at a power density of 60.3 W kg<sup>−1</sup> (based on the whole mass of cathode and anode) and show excellent cycle life over 1000 cycles, demonstrating its promising practicability.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202103210-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The P2‐type NZNCMO is synthesized by a facile and scalable sol–gel method. Briefly, the stoichiometric ratios of metal acetates are dissolved in deionized water with certain amounts of citric acid as chelating agent. After evaporation of the solvent, the final product can be obtained via thermal annealing treatment under air atmosphere (see details in the Experimental Section) according to thermogravimetric analysis (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). Besides, the control samples of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.23</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (denoted as NNCMO) and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (denoted as NNMO) are also prepared by the similar method as NZNCMO. The crystal structure of these three samples are characterized by XRD, as displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S2</link> (Supporting Information), where all the three samples can be readily indexed to hexagonal structure (JCPDS‐27‐0751) with little impurities of NiO phase in NNMO and CuO in NNCMO and NZNCMO, which have also been broadly observed in previous literatures possibly due to the limiting solubility of Ni and Cu.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0026 #adma202103210-bib-0027"><sup>26,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a,b</link>, indicate that NZNCMO possesses typical plate‐like morphology. High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) analysis suggests clear lattice fringes with interplanar spacings of 5.6 and 2.46 Å, as illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0001">1c,d</link>, which can be ascribed to the (002) and (100) planes of the typical P2‐type layered structure. In addition, X‐ray energy dispersive spectrum (EDS), as displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S3</link> (Supporting Information), exhibits the existence of Na, Zn, Ni, Cu, Mn, O elements and the elemental mapping results (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0001">1e</link>) indicate that all the included elements are uniformly distributed throughout the plate‐like particle. Meanwhile, the control samples of NNMO (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information) and NNCMO (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information) also display similar plate‐like morphology with uniform distribution of all elements. The X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurement is investigated to identify the chemical states and further confirms the doping of Cu/Zn ions (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information). The high‐resolution spectrum of Zn 2p (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S6a</link>, Supporting Information) in NZNCMO possesses two peaks at 1020.9 and 1043.9 eV, respectively, which indicates the divalent state of Zn. The valence of Cu in NNCMO and NZNCMO can be indexed to +2,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> while those of Ni and Mn are divalent and tetravalent, respectively, which indicates that inducing Cu and Zn ions does not affect the valence state of Ni and Mn ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202103210-bib-0011 #adma202103210-bib-0029"><sup>11,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In order to get more insights into the structural information of individual Cu doped and Cu, Zn dual‐site‐doped samples, the Rietveld refinement was performed, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0001">1f,g</link> and Tables <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S1</link> and <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S2</link> (Supporting Information). The results indicate that the lattice parameters of NZNCMO (<i>a</i> = 2.8907 Å and <i>c</i> = 11.194 Å) show an expanded <i>a</i>–<i>b</i>‐plane and a contracted <i>c</i>‐axis compared to that of NNCMO (<i>a</i> = 2.8813 Å, <i>c</i> = 11.205 Å), originating from the increased Na<sup>+</sup>Na<sup>+</sup> electrostatic repulsions in the Na layer (NaO<sub>2</sub>) in‐plane direction and the increased O<sup>2−</sup>Na<sup>+</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> electrostatic cohesions between two TMO<sub>2</sub> layers, respectively. This phenomenon ensures a confidence that some other metal ions could be introduced into Na layers. In order to figure out which metal ion is introduced into the Na layer, we synthesized the sample of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Cu<sub>0.15</sub>Ni<sub>0.18</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (denoted as Cu<sub>0.15</sub>) and conducted the Le Bail refinement on both NNMO and Cu0.15, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S7</link> (Supporting Information). The refined results show that the lattice parameter <i>c</i> of NNMO and Cu<sub>0.15</sub> is 11.187 and 11.207 Å, respectively. Compared with the lattice parameters of NZNCMO and NNCMO, the Cu<sub>0.15</sub> sample exhibits larger <i>c</i>‐axis, which suggests that the Cu ions prefer to occupy the 2a sites rather than 2d sites (Na‐site). The refined atomic occupied information (Table <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information) confirms that Zn ions should be doped into 2d sites. Besides, after putting Cu ions into Na sites and making the Zn ions at 2a sites, the agreement factors of Rietveld refinement are obviously increased (Table <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information), which further validate the 2d site doping of Zn ion. In addition, the contraction of d<sub>(ONaO)</sub> with a smaller value of 3.2435 Å in NZNCMO also confirms the introduction of Zn ion into Na layer when compared to the value of 3.3194 Å in NNCMO and indicates the enhanced electrostatic cohesion of alkali‐metal layer after the unusual Zn doping (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S8</link>, Supporting Information). The diagrams of crystal structure of NNMO, NNCMO, and NZNCMO with a 4 × 5 supercell are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S9a–c</link> (Supporting Information), respectively. The density functional theory (DFT) calculation results also suggest that Zn ion occupies the alkali‐metal site rather than Cu ion from the thermodynamic values, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S9d</link> (Supporting Information). The calculated formation energies (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S9e</link>, Supporting Information) for NNMO, NNCMO, and NZNCMO are all negative and show no obvious variation, indicating that the crystal structure can be stably preserved and maintained after doping.</p><figure xml:id="adma202103210-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202103210:adma202103210-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202103210-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Morphological and structural characterizations of NZNCMO: a) FESEM and b) TEM images; c,d) HRTEM analysis; e) EDS mapping results; powder XRD profiles and Rietveld refinement patterns of f) NNCMO and g) NZNCMO.</caption></figure><p>In order to further confirm the unusual alkali‐metal site Zn‐ion doping fact, the spherical ACTEM was conducted on the edge of an individual particle of NZNCMO, as indicated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>. First, the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0002">2b</link>) indicates the typical hexagonal structure, which is consistent with the simulative pattern (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0002">2c</link>) viewed from [−220] axis. Meanwhile, high‐angle‐annular‐dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF–STEM) image (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0002">2d</link>) exhibits the same feature as simulative specific crystal structure viewed from [−220] axis, as schematically illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0002">2e</link>. In addition, the enlarged view of HAADF–STEM image (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-supitem-0001">S10</link>, Supporting Information) shows that the distance between two TMO<sub>2</sub> layers is 5.59 Å, which is in accordance with the refined lattice parameter. Importantly, it can be observed from the two enlarged views of annular‐bright‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (ABF–STEM) image of NZNCMO (Figure <link href="#adma202103210-fig-0002">2f</link>) that there are darker spots existing between two adjacent TMO<sub>2</sub>, which provides a strong and direct evidence regarding the selective site doping of Zn in the Na layers. Prominently, the as‐synthesized [Na<sub>0.67</sub>Zn<sub>0.05</sub>]Ni<sub>0.18</sub>Cu<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode can maintain a decent capacity retention rate of 80.6% after 2000 cycles at a high rate of 10C. Importantly, the in situ XRD characterization and ion diffusion and charge transfer kinetics study together with microstructure analysis after deep cycling reveal that the unique dual‐site doping strategy can effectively decrease the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion activation energy, increase the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion rate in bulk material, and inhibit the formation of O2 phase at the end of the charging state. More importantly, the Zn ion doping in Na‐site can serve as a “pillar” that efficiently decreases the d<sub>(ONaO)</sub> and enhances the O<sup>2−</sup>Na<sup>+</sup>O<sup>2−</sup> electrostatic cohesion, which can strengthen the structure of layered cathode and suppress crack generation, leading to superior cycling stability, excellent rate performance, and moisture resistancy. Inspiringly, sodium‐ion full cell is preliminarily demonstrated by using NZNCMO and commercial hard carbon as cathode and anode, respectively, which shows a high energy density of 217.9 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> at 60.3 W kg<sup>−1</sup> and an ultralong life span over 1000 cycles. Our work represents an important step toward developing high performance P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>O<sub>2</sub> layered oxide for practical applications and benefits the advance of cost‐effective SIBs for next‐generation grid‐scale energy storage.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202103210-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p>Experimental details are provided in the Supporting Information.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202103210-sec-0050" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>G.Q.Z. acknowledges the financial support from the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 51772284), the Recruitment Program of Global Experts and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (Grant No. WK2060000016).
