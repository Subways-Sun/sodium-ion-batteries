However, the construction of hybrid structures with decent physical/electrochemical properties is still challenging. Now, the elaborate design and synthesis of hierarchical nanoboxes composed of three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> as anode materials for sodium‐ion batteries is reported. Through a facile multistep template‐engaged strategy, ultrathin MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are grown on nitrogen‐doped carbon‐coated Cu<sub>2</sub>S nanoboxes to realize the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> configuration. The design shortens the diffusion path of electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions, accommodates the volume change of electrodes during cycling, enhances the electric conductivity of the hybrids, and offers abundant active sites for sodium uptake. By virtue of these advantages, these three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hierarchical nanoboxes show excellent electrochemical properties in terms of decent rate capability and stable cycle life.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Three‐layered hierarchical</b> Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes are synthesized through a multistep template‐engaged strategy. With the unique shell architecture and complex composition, these Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes exhibit enhanced electrochemical properties as an anode material for sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915917:anie201915917-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915917-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201915917-sec-0001"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted widespread attention from both academic and industrial battery communities in recent years, in view of the fascinating merits of natural abundance and low cost of sodium resources.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0001 #anie201915917-bib-0002 #anie201915917-bib-0003 #anie201915917-bib-0004"/> Tremendous efforts have been put in exploring suitable anode materials with desirable electrochemical properties. Owing to the large compositional diversity and decent electrochemical reversibility, metal sulfides have been shown as attractive anode materials for SIBs.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0005 #anie201915917-bib-0006"/> For example, Co<sub><i>x</i></sub>S,<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0007 #anie201915917-bib-0008"/> Cu<sub><i>x</i></sub>S,<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0009 #anie201915917-bib-0010"/> Sn<sub><i>x</i></sub>S,<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0011 #anie201915917-bib-0012"/> MoS<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0013 #anie201915917-bib-0014"/> and Fe<sub><i>x</i></sub>S<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0015 #anie201915917-bib-0016"/> have been reported to deliver high reversible capacity. However, metal sulfides still face some challenges for practical applications, including poor electric conductivity, huge volume changes, and sluggish ionic transport kinetics.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0017 #anie201915917-bib-0018"/> Rational designs in composition and structure have been proven as effective strategies to address these issues. In particular, compared to single metal sulfides, the incorporation of different metal sulfides into one smart hybrid nanostructure has been proposed to facilitate the electrochemical reactions owing to the preserved advantages of individual components and the possible synergetic effects (that is, enhanced electric/ionic conductivity, electrochemical activity, and mechanical stability).<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0019 #anie201915917-bib-0020 #anie201915917-bib-0021 #anie201915917-bib-0022"/> For example, we have designed hierarchical CuS@CoS<sub>2</sub> double‐shelled nanoboxes with decent electrochemical performance.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0023"/></p><p>Owing to the good electric conductivity and low cost, carbonaceous materials, especially the nitrogen‐doped carbon, have been widely adopted to decorate the electrode materials thus facilitating the electrochemical properties.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0024 #anie201915917-bib-0025"/> On the other hand, hierarchical hollow nanostructures with high surface area and low‐dimensional nanoscale building units have shown prominent advantages for sodium storage.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0026 #anie201915917-bib-0027 #anie201915917-bib-0028"/> The hollow configurations can help to buffer the strain during sodium insertion/extraction, and the permeable nanoshells can promote the transport of electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions and offer a large contact area for full infiltration of electrolyte.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0029 #anie201915917-bib-0030"/></p><p>On account of the aforementioned considerations, the integration of these strategies into one alliance may greatly contribute to the creation of new nanostructures with boosted sodium storage capability. Herein, we report the delicate design and fabrication of three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hierarchical nanoboxes through a facile multistep template‐engaged strategy. Starting from Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocube templates, CuS nanoboxes are formed via the sequential sulfidation and etching processes (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0001"/>). Afterwards, the CuS nanoboxes are coated with polydopamine (PDA) and annealed to form the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes. Lastly, the hierarchical Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes are obtained by growing a layer of MoS<sub>2</sub> on the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes. The as‐synthesized three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hollow architectures with hybrid shells and nanosized building blocks fully integrate the advantages of each component. As expected, the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> hierarchical nanoboxes manifest superior electrochemical properties with high specific capacity and stable cycle life.</p><figure xml:id="anie201915917-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915917:anie201915917-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915917-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Illustration of the synthetic process of the three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes.</p></caption></figure><p>Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes synthesized through a reduction method are adopted as the starting templates. The Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes are highly uniform with an average size of about 600 nm (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0002"/> a,e; Supporting Information, Figure S1a–c). Powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD; Supporting Information, Figure S1d) and energy‐dispersive X‐ray (EDX; Supporting Information, Figure S2) analyses indicate the phase purity of the Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes. When the Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes are immersed in a proper amount of Na<sub>2</sub>S solution, the Cu<sub>2</sub>O@CuS core–shelled nanocubes are formed.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0031 #anie201915917-bib-0032"/> Field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0002"/> b,f; Supporting Information, Figure S3) show that the as‐generated particles maintain the cubic shapes, while EDX spectrum confirms the presence of S element in the nanocubes (Supporting Information, Figure S4). Afterwards, the Cu<sub>2</sub>O core can be selectively dissolved by sodium thiosulfate solution to generate the CuS nanoboxes. Most of the diffraction peaks in the XRD pattern of the obtained product correspond to hexagonal CuS (Supporting Information, Figure S5a), and the elemental composition of Cu and S is further confirmed by the EDX spectrum (Supporting Information, Figure S5b). The cubic nanostructure is well preserved after the etching of the Cu<sub>2</sub>O core (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0002"/> c; Supporting Information, Figure S6a,b). The hollow interior is further elucidated by TEM and FESEM observations (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0002"/> g; Supporting Information, Figure S6c,d). A uniform PDA layer is then coated on the CuS nanoboxes to achieve the CuS@PDA nanoboxes. FESEM and TEM images reveal that the CuS@PDA product well inherits the nanobox structure but the surface becomes slightly rough (Supporting Information, Figure S7). EDX spectrum also suggests the successful coating of PDA layer on the CuS nanoboxes (Supporting Information, Figure S8). During a subsequent high‐temperature treatment, the PDA layer is carbonized to form nitrogen‐doped carbon, which can simultaneously reduce CuS into Cu<sub>2</sub>S.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0033 #anie201915917-bib-0034"/> The nanobox structure is well preserved (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0002"/> d,h; Supporting Information, Figure S9a–c), and the elemental mapping images clearly confirm the coating of nitrogen‐doped carbon on the structure (Supporting Information, Figure S9d). The XRD pattern and EDX results (Supporting Information, Figure S10) validate the transformation of CuS@PDA into Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon.</p><figure xml:id="anie201915917-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915917:anie201915917-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915917-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a)–d) FESEM images and e)–h) TEM images of the a),e) Cu<sub>2</sub>O nanocubes, b),f) Cu<sub>2</sub>O@CuS nanocubes, c),g) CuS nanoboxes, and d),h) Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes.</p></caption></figure><p>Through a facile solvothermal method, ultrathin MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are uniformly grown on the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes. The XRD pattern of as‐prepared product matches well with those of tetragonal Cu<sub>2</sub>S and hexagonal MoS<sub>2</sub> (Supporting Information, Figure S11a), and EDX spectrum reveals a Mo/Cu molar ratio of 0.41:1 in the obtained product (Supporting Information, Figure S11b). The D and G bands in the Raman spectrum confirm the existence of carbon (Supporting Information, Figure S12).<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0035"/> The contents of carbon and nitrogen are determined to be around 22.9 and 2.1 wt %, respectively, via EDX analysis. The panoramic FESEM images show that the obtained Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> particles inherit the cubic structure with a rough surface (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0003"/> a,b; Supporting Information, Figure S13a). The closer observations clearly reveal that the hierarchical shells are composed of randomly assembled ultrathin nanosheets (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0003"/> c; Supporting Information, Figure S13b,c). The well‐preserved hollow configuration of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes is confirmed by TEM examinations (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0003"/> d,e; Supporting Information, Figure S13d). No noticeable interlayer gaps can be discerned between the different layers (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0003"/> f), indicating that the three layers are highly allied. Consistent with the XRD result, a set of visible lattice fringes with an interplanar distance of 0.62 nm can be assigned to the (002) plane of MoS<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0003"/> g). Elemental mappings (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0003"/> h) of a single Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanobox demonstrate that the Cu, S, Mo, C, and N elements are uniformly distributed throughout the entire nanoparticle. For comparison, bare MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets are synthesized through a similar method (Supporting Information, Figures S14, S15).</p><figure xml:id="anie201915917-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915917:anie201915917-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915917-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a)–c) FESEM images, d)–f) TEM images, and g) HRTEM image of Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes; h) elemental mapping images of a single Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanobox.</p></caption></figure><p>The Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes are used as anode materials for SIBs. The sodium storage behavior is first evaluated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) analyses (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0004"/> a). During the initial cathodic scan, a broad peak at 1.85 V corresponds to the insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into MoS<sub>2</sub> and Cu<sub>2</sub>S.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0036 #anie201915917-bib-0037"/> The following small peak at 0.92 V is related to the further insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> into Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MoS<sub>2</sub> accompanied by the formation of a solid electrolyte interphase layer.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0036 #anie201915917-bib-0038"/> A series of peaks at about 0.75, 0.66, and 0.52 V is attributed to the sequential phase transitions with the further intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into the Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Cu<sub>2</sub>S structure and the conversion reaction to generate Cu and Na<sub>2</sub>S.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0033 #anie201915917-bib-0039"/> The pronounced peak at 0.10 V is related to the conversion reaction (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MoS<sub>2</sub>+Na<sup>+</sup>→Mo+Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>S).<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0036 #anie201915917-bib-0040"/> In the following cycles, two cathodic peaks at around 1.49 and 0.77 V emerge, corresponding to the insertion reaction and conversion reaction of Cu<sub>2</sub>S and MoS<sub>2</sub>, respectively.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0009 #anie201915917-bib-0036 #anie201915917-bib-0041"/> During the anodic scans, the oxidation peaks at 1.60 and 2.19 V are assigned to the desodiation of the Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Cu<sub>2</sub>S,<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0009 #anie201915917-bib-0033 #anie201915917-bib-0041"/> while the peak at 1.94 V corresponds to the conversion from Na<sub>2</sub>S and Mo to the MoS<sub><i>x</i></sub>.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0036 #anie201915917-bib-0040"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201915917-fig-0004"><title electrode at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Typical discharge–charge voltage profiles of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanobox electrode at a current density of href="#anie201915917-fig-0004"/> b displays the representative galvanostatic discharge‐charge curves of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes at a current density of 0.05 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The electrode delivers an initial charge capacity of 442 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with nearly overlapped discharge–charge voltage profiles of the second and third cycles. The rate capability of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanobox electrode is explored with gradually increasing current densities (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0004"/> c). When tested at 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, average capacities of 430, 410, 386, 368, 359, 337, 316 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be delivered, respectively. Impressively, a decent capacity of 297 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained with further increasing the current density to 3.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The superb rate capability is also validated by the discharge–charge curves at different current densities (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0004"/> d). On the contrary, the capacities of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes and MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets show rapid decay with the elevation of current density (Supporting Information, Figure S16). The rate performance of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes is also superior to some of the MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrodes.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0013 #anie201915917-bib-0042 #anie201915917-bib-0043 #anie201915917-bib-0044 #anie201915917-bib-0045"/></p><p>Moreover, the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanobox electrode also demonstrates superb cycling stability. Specifically, the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes manifest stable cyclability over 200 cycles at 0.3 A g<sup>−1</sup> with an average capacity fading of only 0.098 % per cycle (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0004"/> e), which is superior to some of reported MoS<sub>2</sub>‐based electrodes (Supporting Information, Table S1).<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0013 #anie201915917-bib-0042 #anie201915917-bib-0043 #anie201915917-bib-0044 #anie201915917-bib-0046"/> In comparison, the capacities of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon nanoboxes and MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets fade rapidly with cycling. Moreover, the nanobox structure of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode can still be well preserved after prolonged cycling (Supporting Information, Figure S17). The above results highlight the greatly enhanced electrochemical performance of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes, which should benefit from the unique hierarchical hollow configurations accompanied by the synergetic effect of the three well‐designed shells of the sandwich‐like Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes. Specifically, the inner layer of Cu<sub>2</sub>S nanobox backbone can accommodate the large volumetric change during repeated cycling to achieve stable cycling performance. The middle nitrogen‐doped carbon could maintain the nanobox structures. Both the Cu<sub>2</sub>S backbone and sandwiched nitrogen‐doped carbon layer could enhance the electric conductivity of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes so as to effectively facilitate the charge transfer process as revealed by electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) spectra (Supporting Information, Figure S18). The outer layer of ultrathin MoS<sub>2</sub> nanosheets with high surface area enables the full infiltration of electrolyte and offers decreased diffusion path for electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions to achieve high capacity. Besides, the three‐layered alignment could enhance the structural stability of the hybrids, rendering greatly enhanced cycling performance.</p><p>To gain some insights into the high performance of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes, the kinetic study based on CV tests at different scan rates is performed (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0005"/> a). According to the equation between measured current (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>v</i>): <i>i</i>=<i>a</i> <i>v</i><sup><i>b</i></sup>, the <i>b</i> value can be derived from the relationship between the log values of <i>i</i> and <i>v</i>.<link href="#anie201915917-bib-0023"/> The <i>b</i> value indicates the sodium storage behavior. More specifically, a <i>b</i> value of 0.5 signifies a feature of the diffusion‐controlled process, whereas a <i>b</i> value of 1.0 indicates that the system is mainly controlled by the capacitive process. The results show that the <i>b</i> value is higher than 0.65 for all the redox peaks (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0005"/> b), indicating the dominant fast kinetics of capacitance‐controlled behavior. The capacitive contribution in the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes can be further determined by separating the capacitive capacity and diffusion‐controlled capacity. For example, the capacitive contribution is prominent with a high value of around 73.1 % at a scan rate of 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0005"/> c). The proportion of capacitive capacity steadily rises with the elevating scan rate and finally attains 84.4 % at 4 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201915917-fig-0005"/> d). The high‐rate capability and capacitive behavior of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes might be ascribed to the structural features with a hierarchically hollow structure composed of ultrathin nanoparticles and nanosheets.</p><figure xml:id="anie201915917-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201915917:anie201915917-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201915917-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) The CVs of the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode at various scan rates. b) Determination of the <i>b</i> value using the relationship between peak current and scan rate. c) Separation of the capacitive and diffusion currents in the Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> electrode at a scan rate of 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> with the capacitive fraction shown by the shaded region. d) Contribution ratio of the capacitive and diffusion‐controlled charge versus scan rate.</p></caption></figure><p>In summary, we have demonstrated a multistep templating strategy for the fabrication of hierarchical three‐layered Cu<sub>2</sub>S@carbon@MoS<sub>2</sub> nanoboxes.
