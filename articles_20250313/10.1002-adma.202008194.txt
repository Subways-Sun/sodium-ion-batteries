type="main">Abstract</title><p>Oxygen‐redox of layer‐structured metal‐oxide cathodes has drawn great attention as an effective approach to break through the bottleneck of their capacity limit. However, reversible oxygen‐redox can only be obtained in the high‐voltage region (usually over 3.5 V) in current metal‐oxide cathodes. Here, we realize reversible oxygen‐redox in a wide voltage range of 1.5–4.5 V in a P2‐layered Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>[Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> cathode material, where intrinsic vacancies are located in transition‐metal (TM) sites and Mg‐ions are located in Na sites. Mg‐ions in the Na layer serve as “pillars” to stabilize the layered structure during electrochemical cycling, especially in the high‐voltage region. Intrinsic vacancies in the TM layer create the local configurations of “□–O–□”, “Na–O–□” and “Mg–O–□” to trigger oxygen‐redox in the whole voltage range of charge–discharge. Time‐resolved techniques demonstrate that the P2 phase is well maintained in a wide potential window range of 1.5–4.5 V even at 10 C. It is revealed that charge compensation from Mn‐ and O‐ions contributes to the whole voltage range of 1.5–4.5 V, while the redox of Fe‐ions only contributes to the high‐voltage region of 3.0–4.5 V. The orphaned electrons in the nonbonding 2<i>p</i> orbitals of O that point toward TM‐vacancy sites are responsible for reversible oxygen‐redox, and Mg‐ions in Na sites suppress oxygen release effectively.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202008194-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>[Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> with native transitional metal (TM) vacancies is designed as a novel cathode material for sodium‐ion batteries. The TM vacancies lead to nonbonding O 2p orbitals in this material, pointing toward these vacancies triggering reversible whole‐voltage‐range oxygen redox during charge and discharge processes. This work provides new ideals for design of cathode materials in anionic redox chemistry.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202008194:adma202008194-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202008194-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202008194-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Development of cathode materials with high capacity and reliable stability is crucial for the advancement and commercialization of rechargeable Na‐ion battery (NIB). Among a large number of candidates, P2‐type layered cathode material stands out due to its high structure stability and wide Na‐ion diffusion channels in trigonal prismatic coordination.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Yet, its implementation in practical NIB has been largely plagued by the intrinsically low theoretical capacity solely based on the transitional metal (TM)‐based redox reactions. To enhance the practical capacity, various strategies have been reported, among which arising the involvement of oxygen (O) redox reaction. O redox reaction provides a much higher degree of charge compensation than that solely based on TM redox, allowing the electrode to host a larger amount of charge carriers, which has been well documented in the study of Li‐ion battery (LIB) cathodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0002 #adma202008194-bib-0003 #adma202008194-bib-0004 #adma202008194-bib-0005 #adma202008194-bib-0006 #adma202008194-bib-0007 #adma202008194-bib-0008 #adma202008194-bib-0009 #adma202008194-bib-0010"><sup>2–10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Inspired by the method of excessive Li doping into the TM layer with subsequent change of O orbitals in LIBs, researchers have been applying similar method in NIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0011 #adma202008194-bib-0012 #adma202008194-bib-0013 #adma202008194-bib-0014"><sup>11–14</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>So far, the main strategy to trigger the O redox reaction in P2‐type cathode is by the realization of Na–O–X configuration, where X stands for doping of a non‐TM element in the TM layer to replace TM ions. Fundamentally, the triggering of O redox using this method has been ascribed to the unique Na–O–X configuration, where unhybridized oxygen 2p orbitals are relocated to a level above those of Na–O–TM configuration. Following this paradigm, previous studies have successfully and readily triggered O redox activity upon cycling of P2‐type Na<sub>0.72</sub>Li<sub>0.24</sub>Mn<sub>0.76</sub>O<sub>2</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Na<sub>2/3</sub>[Mg<sub>0.28</sub>Mn<sub>0.72</sub>]O<sub>2</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> cathode materials, whose TM ions are partially replaced by Li and Mg, respectively. Charged to a relatively high voltage, the Na<sup>+</sup> and even the Li<sup>+</sup> and Mg<sup>2+</sup> in the TM layers are substantially removed from the lattice and thus leave plenty of vacancies (terms as “□”) in the host lattice. It is such cycling‐generated “Na–O–□” and “□–O–□” configurations that ultimately trigger the O redox reaction. In addition to the replacement of TM ions with alkaline/alkaline‐earth metal ions, the direct introduction of vacancies in TM sites during material synthesis is also promising to trigger O‐redox reaction as a result of the generation of nonbonding oxygen 2p orbitals along “Na–O–□” and “□–O–□” configurations, such as Na<sub>4/7</sub>Mn<sub>6/7</sub>□<sub>1/7</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reported by Boisse et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0017 #adma202008194-bib-0018 #adma202008194-bib-0019"><sup>17–19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and Li et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0020 #adma202008194-bib-0021"><sup>20,21</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Although oxygen redox reactions have been recognized as an effective way to break through the bottleneck of capacity limit for both LIB cathode and NIB cathode, the aforementioned strategies applied to boost O redox activity inevitably cause irreversible structural changes to the cathode host, such as loss of lattice O and interlayer migration of TM ions during charge–discharge. As a result, P2‐type cathode utilizing O redox reaction for high‐capacity Na<sup>+</sup> storage typically suffers from severe voltage drops and capacity degradation upon cycling,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0011 #adma202008194-bib-0016"><sup>11,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which hinders its further development and commercialization. In addition, the triggering of O redox reaction typically occurs at a high charge voltage range,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0012 #adma202008194-bib-0015 #adma202008194-bib-0016 #adma202008194-bib-0017"><sup>12,15–17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which means that only a limited portion of the whole voltage range for NIB has been successfully utilized for O redox while the major portion is still based on TM redox reaction with limited capacity contribution.</p><p>Herein, we report the successful doping of Mg<sup>2+</sup> into the Na layers, instead of into TM layers of a traditional P2‐type Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Fe<sub>0.4</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode, which gives a composition of Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>[Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>. In this configuration, Mg<sup>2+</sup> are for the first time confirmed to be exclusively allocated into the sodium layers during the material synthesis process; and more importantly, as a result of charge compensation, native TM (Mn and Fe) vacancies are successfully generated within the TM layers. With the help of advanced synchrotron‐based X‐ray diffraction techniques, aberration‐corrected high‐resolution electron microscopy and density functional theory (DFT) calculations, we confirm the realization of whole‐voltage‐range oxygen redox from 1.5 to 4.5 V with minimized structural degradation of the P2 host during long‐term cycling. Further analyses reveal that vacancies existing in the TM layer form new configurations of “□–O–□,” “Na–O–□,” and “Mg–O–□” to harvest extra capacity from oxygen redox. An additional bonus of structural stability comes from the Mg<sup>2+</sup> occupying inside the trigonal prismatic Na interlayer, which are seen to sever as “pillars” to buffer the O‐O repulsion force and stabilize the structure along the <i>c</i> axis upon high‐voltage cycling.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0030"><title type="main">Crystal Structure</title><p>A series of P2‐layered Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.4‐</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<i><sub>x</sub></i>]O<sub>2</sub> (□ denotes a vacancy) with different amounts of Mg substitution (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.1, 0.2, denoted as MFM‐0, MFM‐1, and MFM‐2) were prepared by solid‐state reaction. The structure of the synthesized samples was examined by XRD as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>. Typical P2‐type layered structure was confirmed from the diffraction pattern of MFM‐0, which can be fully indexed with the hexagonal <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i> (No. 194) space group without any noticeable impurities. It is also found that P2 structure can be well maintained after Mg introduction. Interestingly, with Mg concentration increasing from <i>x</i> = 0 to 0.2, the (002) diffraction peak shifts continuously to the higher angle range (inset of Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0001">1a</link>) pointing to shrinkage of (002) spacing, which is totally inversed compared with the previous reports on Mg substitution into P2‐type Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Mg<i><sub>x</sub></i>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 0.2), where Mg ions were implanted in TM layer to substitute Ni ions.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0022 #adma202008194-bib-0023"><sup>22,23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It is thus hypothesized that Mg ions are allocated into Na layer instead of into the TM layer as previously reported. This is similar as the (002) peak shift when Mg ions were partially located at Na sites in P2 structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The crystal lattice evolution (Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0001">1b</link>) illustrates that the unit cell shrinks along the <i>c</i>‐axis continuously as the amount of Mg‐ion increases from <i>x</i> = 0 to 0.2. Quantitative calculations of Δ<i>c</i> values derived from XRD profiles are also given in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0001">1b</link>. According to Pauling's definition, Mg possesses stronger electronegativity (1.31 for Mg and 0.93 for Na) and smaller ionic radius (0.72 Å for Mg<sup>2+</sup> and 1.02 Å for Na<sup>+</sup>) than Na, thus displaying more intensive electrostatic attraction with oxygen from the adjacent TM layer and resulting in the contraction of <i>c</i> parameter. In order to resolve the structure with high accuracy, synchrotron‐based powder XRD of MFM‐2 was performed, and corresponding Rietveld refinements of the XRD patterns for MFM‐0, MFM‐1, and MFM‐2 are presented in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0001">1c</link> and Figure S1, Supporting Information. Corresponding structure parameters deduced from refinements are shown in Tables S1–S4, Supporting Information. It should be noted that the cell parameters obtained from the Rietveld refinements agree with those deduced from the raw XRD profiles by <i>Jade</i> for the MFM‐<i>x</i> (<i>x</i> = 0, 1, 2) samples, as seen in Table S5, Supporting Information. The results imply that Mg ions occupy 2d site of Na<i><sub>e</sub></i> in Na layer rather than 2<i>b</i> site of Na<i><sub>f</sub></i> or 2<i>a</i> site of TM ions during Mg substitution. Given the initial chemical composition we designed, vacancies are distributed randomly in the TM layer (2<i>a</i> site). Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0001">1d</link> shows the coordination environment for Na<sup>+</sup> and the vacancy situated upon Na<i><sub>e</sub></i> site viewing along [001] axis. Performance</title><p>The charge–discharge profiles of the as‐prepared MFM‐0, MFM‐1, and MFM‐2 cathodes at 0.1 C (1 C = 150 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>) between 1.5 V and 4.3 V are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> and Figure S3, Supporting Information. MFM‐0 cathode exhibits an initial charge capacity of 112.9 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>, and a discharge capacity of 185.2 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>, which is well consistent with previous work by Rojo's group.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For comparison, the specific capacity of MFM‐1 and MFM‐2 in the first charge–discharge are 106.9/162.8 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> and 96.5/144.1 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>, respectively. The loss of capacity after Mg substitution should be due to the increase of electrochemically inactive Mg and the decrease of active Fe. It is worth noting that the charge curve of MFM‐0 within the voltage ranges of 3.2–3.7 V (circled by green ellipse in Figure S3, Supporting Information) shows a rapid jump, but increase gradually for MFM‐1 and MFM‐2 samples. As Delmas and co‐workers<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have manifested, each TM cation is surrounded by six other TM cations in a triangular lattice. When a large cation (Fe<sup>3+</sup>) is surrounded by a large number of small cation (such as Mn<sup>4+</sup>), the crystal field could be intensified, which makes the iron oxidation process easier. Considering the increase of relative Mn/Fe ratio from 3:2 in MFM‐0 to 3:1 in MFM‐2 after Mg substitution, abundant Mn ions (versus Fe ions) in the material sufficiently facilitate the oxidation of iron and ensure the separated Mn and Fe redox more consecutive. Therefore, MFM‐2 electrode exhibits smoother voltage curve during the first charge than MFM‐0. In addition, the voltage plateau of MFM‐2 above 4.2 V is relatively higher than those of MFM‐0 and MFM‐1 samples, therefore the charge–discharge curves in a wider electrochemical window between 1.5 V and 4.5 V are tested as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2b</link>. A charge capacity of 124.1 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> and a flat plateau are obtained during the first charge for MFM‐2. In the discharge process, a specific capacity of 154 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> is achieved, which is 10 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> higher than the discharge capacity in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2a</link>. This capacity is reversible during the following charge process. The relative large hysteresis of redox potential in the initial cycle is due to the formation of cathode electrolyte interfaces (CEIs) and structure adjusting caused by oxygen redox. It is a common phenomenon appeared in cathode materials with oxygen redox.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0015 #adma202008194-bib-0016"><sup>15,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Typical cyclic voltammogram (CV) curves of MFM‐2 in the first three cycles are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2c</link>. Two apparent redox couples at 2.6/2.3 V, and 4.3/4.0 V are observed in the potential window of 1.5–4.3 V. CV profiles with different scanning rate (<i>v</i>: 0.1–1.0 mV s<sup>–1</sup>) for MFM‐2 between 1.5–4.3 V was also measured (Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2d</link>). It can be seen that the cathodic and anodic peaks expand equably with the increase of scanning rate, while the general shape of the curves basically maintained. Figure S4, Supporting Information is the function of peak current (<i>I</i><sub>p</sub>) depending on square root of scan rates (<i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup>) for the oxidation peak at 2.6 V and reduction peak at 3.8 V. The linear relationship implies the standard diffusion‐controlled behavior of MFM‐2 during charge and discharge. Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2e</link> shows the CV curves of the initial 10 cycles between 1.5 V and 4.5 V. In agreement with the charge curve, an oxidation peak as high as 4.38 V could be observed, which is probably due to the redox of oxygen. After the first cycle, the CV curves in subsequent cycles are well overlapped, suggesting high reversibility of MFM‐2. Figure S5, Supporting Information shows the CV curves of MFM‐0 and MFM‐1 samples. Except for the two main coupled redox waves similar as MFM‐2 sample,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0027 #adma202008194-bib-0028"><sup>27,28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> an additional peak around 3.7 V can be observed in MFM‐0, but vanished for MFM‐1 and MFM‐2. It is known that high‐spin Mn<sup>3+</sup> with the electron configuration of t<sub>2g</sub>3e<sub>g</sub><sup>1</sup> in 3d orbital exhibits strong Jahn–Teller effect due to the single occupancy of a degenerate e<sub>g</sub> orbital.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0028 #adma202008194-bib-0029"><sup>28,29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The partial replacement of low valence Mg<sup>2+</sup> for Fe<sup>3+</sup> can introduce more Mn<sup>4+</sup> and increase the average valance state of Mn ions, which thus suppresses the Jahn–Teller distortion of Mn<sup>3+</sup>. The crystal lattice can also be stabilized by perturbing the charge ordering,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> similar as LiNi<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and LiNi<sub>0.4</sub>Mn<sub>0.4</sub>Co<sub>0.2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode materials with Ni<sup>2+</sup>, Co<sup>3+</sup>, and Mn<sup>4+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, the peak at 3.7 V in MFM‐0 resulted from Jahn–Teller distortion of Mn<sup>3+</sup> is eliminated in MFM‐1 and MFM‐2 by Mg substitution. Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2f</link> illustrates the rate capability of MFM‐2 at 1.5–4.3 V. It can be observed that the specific discharge capacities are 144.1, 122.7, 100.5, 84.6, 65.1, 38.8, and 18.3 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> at the current rates of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 3, 10, and 25 C, respectively. Rate performance at 1.5–4.5 V is shown in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2g</link>. When the current densities increase from 0.1 to 25 C, the discharge capacities of 155.0, 151.0, 131.0, 111.4, 86.1, 54.8, and 27.2 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> can be achieved, respectively. After the current was set back to 0.1 C, the capacities can recover to 142.4 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> (1.5–4.3 V) and 153.3 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> (1.5–4.5 V). These results demonstrate the outstanding rate capability and robust reversibility of MFM‐2 in a wide voltage range. In previous studies, it has been reported that<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0001 #adma202008194-bib-0026 #adma202008194-bib-0032"><sup>1,26,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode suffered from severe capacity fading during cycling, which could be ascribed to the inferior structural stability caused by Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller distortion and the formation of “Z” or “OP4” phase when charging to high voltage. Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2h</link> illustrates the cycle stability of MFM‐0, MFM‐1, and MFM‐2 at 0.2 C between 1.5 V and 4.3 V. After 100 cycles, 92.8% of the initial discharge capacity can be maintained for the MFM‐2 electrode, which is much higher than that of MFM‐0(21.9%) and MFM‐1(44.0%), suggesting that Mg in Na sites can improve capacity retention effectively. To further evaluate the cycle stability of MFM‐2 in a wider voltage window, galvanostatic charge–discharge tests were carried out in the voltage range of 2.0–4.5 V and 1.5–4.5 V at 0.2 C as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0002">2i</link>. After 160 cycles, the relative capacity retention is 70.5% and 71.1%, respectively. The capacity retention within a wide electrochemical window of 1.5–4.5 V is particularly high among cathode materials studied for Na‐ion batteries. As for MFM‐2 sample, the decreasing concentration of Mn<sup>3+</sup> can prevent the disproportionate reaction and benefit for mitigating Mn<sup>2+</sup> dissolution from the cathode bulk into the electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> So that the severe capacity attenuation during long cycling were relieved. Meanwhile, crystal structure evolution is also an influential factor that should be taken into great consideration.</p><figure xml:id="adma202008194-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202008194:adma202008194-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202008194-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Electrochemistry. a,b) Galvanostatic charge–discharge curves of MFM‐2 between 1.5 V and 4.3 V (a) and 4.5 V (b) at 0.1 C. c) CV curves of MFM‐2 in the first three cycles between 1.5 V and 4.3 V (scan rate: 0.1 mV s<sup>–1</sup>). d) CV curves of MFM‐2 with various scan rates from 0.1 to 1.0 mV s<sup>–1</sup> between 1.5 V and 4.3 V. e) CV curves of MFM‐2 in the initial 10 cycles between 1.5 V and 4.5 V (scan rate: 0.1 mV s<sup>–1</sup>). f) Rate capability of MFM‐2 with current rates from 0.1 to 25 C (1.5–4.3 V). g) Rate capability of MFM‐2 from 0.1 to 25 C (1.5–4.5 V). h) Cycle performance of MFM‐0, MFM‐1, and MFM‐2 at 0.2 C between 1.5 V and 4.3 V. i) Cycle performance of MFM‐2 at 0.2 C between 1.5–4.5 V and 2.0–4.5 V.</caption></figure></section><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0050"><title type="main">Structure Evolution</title><p>To gain insights into the crystal structure evolution of MFM‐2 upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/insertion, in situ XRD (Cu Kα, λ = 1.5418 Å) were carried out during the first cycle with results shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>. The peaks marked with black asterisks belong to Al current collector. Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0003">3b</link> shows the contour plots of the evolution in different peaks at the initial cycle. In the charge process, (002) and (004) diffraction peaks shift consecutively to lower angle, whereas (100) and (012) diffraction peaks shift toward higher angle. It indicates that the interlayer distance along the <i>c</i> axis is expanding in a solid solution behavior owing to the increased electrostatic repulsion between adjacent oxygen layers, while the <i>a–b</i> plane is contracting due to the smaller ionic radius of high‐valence transition metal ions formed during charging. In the discharge process, all these peaks move back to their initial states, suggesting highly reversible structure evolution. The lattice parameter and unit‐cell volume changes are displayed in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0003">3c</link>. It can be found that the cell volume decreases during charge, and increases during discharge, which is a typical cell volume “breathing” behavior for layer‐structured cathode materials upon cycling.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, the cell volume change of MFM‐2 before and after desodiation is only ≈0.86%, which is much smaller than that in Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Fe<sub>0.4</sub>O<sub>2</sub><sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and approaches to zero‐strain characteristics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0020 #adma202008194-bib-0033"><sup>20,33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In previous reports of MFM‐0 cathode,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0001 #adma202008194-bib-0032 #adma202008194-bib-0034 #adma202008194-bib-0035"><sup>1,32,34,35</sup></link><sup>]</sup> a highly distorted OP4 or “Z” type structure (S.G. <i>P</i>‐6<i>m</i>2) can be observed in the high voltage region above 4.1 V. This phase has been well illustrated by Komaba's group<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> as a result of the gliding of the TMO<sub>2</sub> layers near the end of the charging process due to the structure instability in high voltage region. Rojo's team<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have found that a greater proportion of Mn to Fe (6:4 in Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Fe<sub>0.4</sub>O<sub>2</sub>) in the crystal lattice could prevent the formation of OP4 or “Z” phase and lead the P2 structure to be stabilized to higher voltage (4.2 V), thus the region above 4.0 V could be sufficiently utilized without incurring losses in capacity during cycling. Despite this, a distorted orthorhombic P'2 accompanied with some unknown structures can still be found in this material after discharge, implying the structure irreversibility. In contrast, MFM‐2 electrode exhibits a rigid solid solution behavior by keeping the high purity P2 phase without the formation of other phases in the wide voltage range from 1.5 to 4.5 V. It suggests that the introduced Mg ions can not only eliminate in‐plane orderings within Na layers, but also prohibit the phase transition from P2 to O2 or OP4 by preventing the gliding of TMO<sub>2</sub> slabs. Mg ions in Na layer can also serve as “pillar” to suppress the structural collapse along the <i>c</i> axis during high voltage charging.</p><figure xml:id="adma202008194-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202008194:adma202008194-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202008194-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>In situ XRD. a) In situ XRD patterns during the first cycling between 1.5 V and 4.5 V (0.1 C). A typical hexagonal configuration can be observed clearly. These results demonstrate the lattice changes of MFM‐2 in atomic scale during charging, which are consistent with the in situ XRD results. Worthy of a special mention is the fact that vacancies in TM layer are invisible because they distribute randomly and are overlapped by the projection of TM atoms. It totally differs from the native vacancies in Mn‐O slabs of the Na<sub>4/7</sub>[Mn<sub>6/7</sub>(□Mn)<sub>1/7</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> sample, which distribute periodically in order and can be observed distinctly.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="adma202008194-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202008194:adma202008194-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202008194-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>TEM observation upon charge. a–c) ABF and d–f) HAADF images of MFM‐2 at various SOCs: a,b,d,e) as‐prepared and c,f) charged to 4.5 V. X‐ray absorption near edge structure (XANES) of Mn and Fe elements are displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>–<link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">f</link> and corresponding energy changes are plotted in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5g</link>. Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5h</link> is the voltage‐time profile and the state points for taking XAS measurement. During the first charge process, Mn K‐edge continuously shifts to higher energy range toward MnO<sub>2</sub> reference, suggesting that the valence of Mn changes from the initial +3.8 to +4. Meanwhile, Fe K‐edge also moves from the initial position to higher energy obviously during the entire procedure, indicating Fe is oxidized from +3 to +3.5 referring to Fe<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> references. In previous studies, several works<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0032"><sup>32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> have revealed that oxygen could contribute to the charge compensation in Mn‐based layered cathode materials, where oxygen ions were partially oxidized during charge in the high voltage region. The spectra can be divided into two regions. Pre‐edge peaks at 529.8 and 532.2 eV are attributed to the transition of O 1s electron to the unoccupied hybridized state of TM 3d and O 2p orbitals just above the Fermi level, which splits into two peaks with different energies of t<sub>2g</sub> and e<sub>g</sub> symmetries. On the other hand, the broad peaks above 535.0 eV correspond to transitions to O 2p‐TM 4sp hybridized states. In Figure S7, Supporting Information, the peak around 534.0 eV is associated with some Na oxides or carbonaceous components that exist on the surface of the active material particles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0044 #adma202008194-bib-0045 #adma202008194-bib-0046"><sup>44–46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5i</link>, it can be seen that the pre‐edge peak changes in the area under the spectra during charge, corresponding to the changes in the density of empty states just above the Fermi level (528–533 eV). Integrated area changes relative to the pristine sample were calculated and shown as the inset of Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5i</link>. It suggests that O 2p electron holes and the average effective charge increase remarkably,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0047"><sup>47</sup></link><sup>]</sup> implying that oxygen contributes to the charge compensation during desodiation. It should be noted that the change of pre‐edge areas can be observed from the very beginning of the charge process and keep propagation during charge, which indicates that oxygen ions participate into the electrochemical reaction in the whole voltage range from the open circuit voltage to 4.5 V. That's quite different from the previous reports that oxygen redox always happens in the high voltage region over 4.0 V in common P2‐layered cathode materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0012 #adma202008194-bib-0016 #adma202008194-bib-0032"><sup>12,16,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> During the discharge process, the integral areas of the pre‐edge peaks decrease back, demonstrating the high reversibility of oxygen redox in MFM‐2. For the TEY mode, the O 2p‐TM 3d interactions become weaker, while the intensity of the peaks corresponding to the CO interaction of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> species at higher energy range is strengthen.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0048"><sup>48</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Considering the surface sensitive feature of the TEY mode (≈5 nm),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0043 #adma202008194-bib-0046"><sup>43,46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It can be deduced that CEIs on the electrode surface are formed after the first cycle. The O K‐edge SXAS spectra confirm that the reversible oxygen redox is involved in the whole voltage range of MFM‐2 during charge and discharge. In previous study,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> DFT calculation results manifest that it is the oxygen around the vacancies that contributes to the charge compensation, similar as the “Li–O–Li” configuration in Li‐excess and Mn‐based cathode materials for LIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It has been reported that native vacancies in triclinic Na<sub>2</sub>Mn<sub>3</sub>O<sub>7</sub> can bring in lone‐paired oxygen 2p orbitals pointing to the vacancy site, thus the total unhybridized oxygen 2p bands would be much narrower in the electrode containing “□–O–Na” or “□–O–□” configuration contrast with the “Li–O–Li/Na” configuration in the conventional layered Li‐ or Na‐excess layered cathodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Most recently, Bruce's team<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0049"><sup>49</sup></link><sup>]</sup> has calculated that more dispersed TM layer vacancies in the structure provided increased stability for high‐voltage (4.1 V) O redox by preserving the degeneracy of the O 2p states, so we speculate that the vacancies in the [Mn<sub>0.6</sub>Fe<sub>0.2</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> slabs can also introduce orphaned electrons for the O 2p orbital and raise its energy above the antibonding orbital t<sub>2g</sub>. Therefore, oxygen in the configurations of “□–O–□,” “Na–O–□,” and “Mg–O–□” can be oxidized more easily during Na‐ion extraction, especially charging to high potential. This should account for the observation that the voltage plateau of MFM‐2 in the first charge can be lifted obviously compared with MFM‐0, and it can maintain better integrity of the crystal structure. Further evidence on oxygen contribution was provided by O 1s X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) shown in Figure S8, Supporting Information. A new component emerges at about 531 eV (the green peak) in the O 1s XPS spectrum of the sample charged to 4.2 V, which is assigned to the existence of O<sup>–</sup>/O<sub>2</sub><sup>2–</sup> (per‐oxo‐like species).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0002 #adma202008194-bib-0003 #adma202008194-bib-0050"><sup>2,3,50</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This new peak is more pronounced as charging to 4.5 V, suggesting the oxidation of O<sup>2−</sup> to O<sup>–</sup>.</p><p>In the discharge process (Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5b</link>,<link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">e</link>,<link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">g</link>), Fe K‐edge shifts back to lower energy range from 4.5 to 3.0 V, then keeps its position constant from 3.0 to 1.5 V. It indicates that the contribution of Fe<sup>3.5+/3+</sup> redox (27.6 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>) happens in the voltage range from 4.5 to 3.0 V during discharge process. While K‐edge energy of Mn decreases continuously throughout the sodiation stage. It can be quantitatively estimated that Mn ions contribute to the charge compensation with Mn<sup>4+/3.5+</sup> redox in the region of 4.5 to 1.5 V, corresponding to 82.8 mAh g<sup>–1</sup> in discharge capacity. Except TM ions, the rest part of capacity would be the participation of oxygen ions (43.6 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>) according to the total discharge capacity of 154 mAh g<sup>–1</sup>. After recharging to 4.5 V (Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5c</link>,<link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">f</link>,<link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">g</link>), K‐edge energies of both Mn and Fe rise nearly to the same energy positions in comparison with the first charge process, demonstrating the satisfactory reversibility of MFM‐2.</p><p>According to the detailed analysis above, charge compensation contributed by different elements during electrochemical cycling is summarized in Figure <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0005">5j</link>. Very interestingly, oxygen ions also participate in the charge compensation in the whole voltage range between 1.5 V and 4.5 V during charge and discharge, thanks to the unique configuration of native TM vacancies in MFM‐2. This is quite different from other Mn‐based P2‐layered cathode materials,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0001 #adma202008194-bib-0012 #adma202008194-bib-0016"><sup>1,12,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> in which oxygen redox can only happen in the high voltage region over 4.0 V. It should be noted that the distinct shift of TM–O peaks is also an indicator of the change of oxygen ions, which sheds light on the contribution of oxygen in charge compensation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202008194-bib-0046"><sup>46</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>In order to examine the kinetic property and polarization of MFM‐2 during cycling, the galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was conducted in the first cycle and the second charge process (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202008194-fig-0006"><b>6</b>a</link>). The resulted Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<sub>0.2</sub>[Fe<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<sub>0.2</sub>]O<sub>2</sub> cathode with native transitional metal vacancies delivers improved cycle stability and rate capability. Nonbonding 2p orbitals of oxygen that pointing to TM vacancies are theoretically responsible for the triggering of reversible oxygen redox reaction in a wide voltage range from 1.5 to 4.5 V. By forming TM vacancies in the pristine P2‐layered cathode, oxygen redox is activated at the very beginning of the charge process. P2 phase is seen to be well maintained in this voltage range even at a high current rate of 10 C. The charge compensation mechanism was disclosed for the MFM‐2, where Mn<sup>3.5+/4.0+</sup> and O<sup>2−/−</sup> redox contribute to the whole voltage range between 1.5 V and 4.5 V, and Fe<sup>3+/3.5+</sup> redox mainly contribute to the high‐voltage region from 3.0 to 4.5 V. Mg ions in Na sites not only function as “pillars” to prevent the collapse of the structure in <i>c</i> direction upon charging, but also suppress the oxygen release effectively at high voltages. These findings provide new opportunities to design cathode materials toward reversible oxygen redox with high structural stability.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0080"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0100" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Material Synthesis</title><p>Na<sub>0.7</sub>Mg<i><sub>x</sub></i>[Fe<sub>0.4−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>Mn<sub>0.6</sub>□<i><sub>x</sub></i>]O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i> = 0, 0.1, 0.2) were synthesized by solid‐state reaction method using commercial Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>, Mn<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>, and MgO as precursors in stoichiometric proportion with an excess of 5 mol% Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>. After ball‐milled at 1400 rpm in ethanol for 2 h, the mixture was dried overnight in an oven. The homogenous precursors were heated at 900 °C for 15 h under an oxygen flow, and then were cooled down to room temperature prior to being transferred to an argon‐filled glovebox in a sealed centrifuge tube. A hard carbon anode was prepared by calcining the degreasing cotton at 1100 °C for 6 h under the nitrogen atmosphere.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrode Preparation and Electrochemical Measurement</title><p>The working electrode was prepared by spreading the slurry (70 wt% active materials, 20 wt% carbon black, 10 wt% poly(vinylidene fluoride) (PVDF, Sigma‐Aldrich, average <i>M</i><sub>w</sub> ≈ 534 000) and an appropriate amount of <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidinone (Sigma‐Aldrich, 99.5%)) onto aluminum foils and then dried at 70 °C under vacuum for 12 h. The electrochemical properties of the fabricated electrodes were evaluated in coin‐type cells assembled in an argon‐filled glove box. In half cells, pure Na foil was used as counter electrode and porous glass fiber (GF/C, Whatman) was used as the separator. The electrolyte was 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> (Sigma‐Aldrich, ≥ 98%) in a nonaqueous solution of ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate (EC: PC, 1:1 in volume) (BASF, 99.98%) with 5 vol% of 4‐fluoro‐1,3‐dioxolan‐2‐one (FEC) (Sigma‐Aldrich, 99%). Galvanostatic charge–discharge measurements were carried out on a Land CT2001A battery test system at 25 °C. The current densities and specific capacities of electrodes were calculated in the basis of the weight of the active materials. Cyclic Voltammetry and other electrochemical tests were performed on CHI660E and Bio‐Logic SP‐300 (EC‐Lab) electrochemical workstations.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202008194-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterization</title><p>The morphology of the synthesized materials was characterized using a field‐emission scanning electron microscope (Cambridge S‐360) equipped with an EDX detector for elements analysis. XPS data was collected on PHI‐5000C ESCA System (Perkin/Elmer) with monochromatic Al‐Kα X‐ray source.
