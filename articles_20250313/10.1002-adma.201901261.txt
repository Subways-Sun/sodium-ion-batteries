However, it remains unexplored to further enlarge the interlayer spacing and reveal the influence of heteroatom doping on carbon nanostructures for developing more efficient SIB anode materials. Here, a series of N‐rich few‐layer graphene (N‐FLG) with tuneable interlayer distance ranging from 0.45 to 0.51 nm is successfully synthesized by annealing graphitic carbon nitride (g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>) under zinc catalysis and selected temperature (<i>T</i> = 700, 800, and 900 °C). More significantly, the correlation between N dopants and interlayer distance of resultant N‐FLG‐T highlights the effect of pyrrolic N on the enlargement of graphene interlayer spacing, due to its stronger electrostatic repulsion. As a consequence, N‐FLG‐800 achieves the optimal properties in terms of interlayer spacing, nitrogen configuration and electronic conductivity. When used as an anode for SIBs, N‐FLG‐800 shows remarkable Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance with ultrahigh rate capability (56.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 40 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and excellent long‐term stability (211.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles), demonstrating the effectiveness of material design.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201901261-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Well‐controlled N‐doped few‐layer graphene (N‐FLG)</b> with tunable interlayer structure, nitrogen content, and nitrogen configuration, is realized through the pyrolysis of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> under zinc catalysis and appropriate temperature. The optimal N‐FLG‐800 features significantly enlarged interplanar spacing and high nitrogen content with pyridinic and pyrrolic configurations, resulting in superior Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance and ultrahigh rate capability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201901261:adma201901261-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201901261-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201901261-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201901261-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have received intensive attention in recent years as a cost‐effective energy‐storage technology to meet the increasing demand for next‐generation energy devices.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0001 #adma201901261-bib-0002 #adma201901261-bib-0003 #adma201901261-bib-0004"/> Apart from their low cost, SIBs hold enormous potential as a platform for powering electric vehicles (EVs) and hybrid electric vehicles (HEVs) given their high power density.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0005"/> The working mechanism of SIBs is very similar to commercial lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs). Therefore, many research efforts have been devoted to mimicking the successful material strategies used for LIBs. This is especially the case regarding suitable electrode materials that enable reversible Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and deintercalation.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0001 #adma201901261-bib-0006"/> In this regard, a series of promising SIB electrode materials has been discovered in the past decade.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0007 #adma201901261-bib-0008 #adma201901261-bib-0009 #adma201901261-bib-0010 #adma201901261-bib-0011 #adma201901261-bib-0012 #adma201901261-bib-0013 #adma201901261-bib-0014"/> In terms of anode materials, however, the standard graphite anodes in LIBs exhibit extremely limited sodium storage properties.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0015 #adma201901261-bib-0016"/> Because Na<sup>+</sup> (1.16 Å) has larger ionic radius than Li<sup>+</sup> (0.90 Å),<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0017"/> which greatly hinders its reversible insertion into and extraction from conventional graphite anodes. Other non‐carbon candidates, including transition metal compounds (e.g., oxides,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0018"/> sulfides,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0019"/> and phosphides<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0020"/>) and alloys (e.g., Sn<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0021"/> and Sb<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0022"/>) usually suffer from poor cycling durability because of their low intrinsic conductivity and significant volume change,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0023"/> making them being far from real application in SIBs. Therefore, it is very urgent to develop efficient anode materials for advancing SIB systems.</p><p>Although traditional graphite fails to provide sufficient interlayer spacing (≈0.34 nm) to host larger Na<sup>+</sup>,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0024 #adma201901261-bib-0025"/> both experimental results and theoretical calculations show that the energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup> insertion into graphite layers can be reduced to a surmountable level once the interlayer distance is increased to 0.37 nm.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0024"/> Accordingly, a rational material design strategy lies in enlarging the carbon interlayer distance to unlock its Na<sup>+</sup> storage capability.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0024 #adma201901261-bib-0026"/> To this end, different carbon materials with expanded interlayer spacing have emerged as improved anodes for SIBs.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0013 #adma201901261-bib-0026 #adma201901261-bib-0027 #adma201901261-bib-0028 #adma201901261-bib-0029"/> For instance, Wen et al. prepared expanded graphite with an enlarged interlayer distance of 0.43 nm by introducing oxygen‐containing functional groups via a two‐step oxidation–reduction treatment of graphite.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0027"/> The as‐prepared expanded graphite exhibited high reversible capacity, but its rate capability needed to be improved. Notably, among the reported carbon materials, heteroatom (e.g., N, P, and S) doping has also been demonstrated as one of the most effective ways to expand the carbon interlayer distance.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0026 #adma201901261-bib-0028 #adma201901261-bib-0029"/> As an example, Hou and coworkers reported large‐area carbon nanosheets doped with phosphorus, which achieved an expanded interlayer spacing of 0.42 nm, allowing for highly reversible Na<sup>+</sup> storage with excellent rate performance and cycling stability.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0028"/> These previous studies provide some empirical foundation for constructing high‐performance carbon anodes for SIBs, yet it remains a great challenge to further enlarge the interlayer distance of carbon materials.</p><p>In addition, to get insightful understanding of the effects of specific heteroatom doping on the interlayer distance of certain carbon nanostructure, it is highly desirable to search for synthetic methodology that can effectively control the amount and configuration of the selected dopant. As a typical 2D carbon material, graphene can serve as an ideal prototype to investigate the relationship between heteroatom doping and interlayer spacing. Particularly, N with different configurations, such as pyridinic, pyrrolic, and graphitic N, can be introduced into graphene framework. In consequence, the comprehensive study of N‐doped graphene with controllable N content and configurations may create new opportunities for solving the aforementioned problems. On the other hand, viewing that Na<sup>+</sup> storage in carbon materials generally involves two mechanisms, namely: i) Na<sup>+</sup> insertion into carbon interlayers and ii) adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup> on the electrode surface and nanovoids, which work in conjunction to contribute to high capacities at various current densities/rates,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0030"/> the deliberate manipulation of N‐rich graphene is beneficial to increase active sites, thereby making full use of the surface capacitive effect toward high rate capability. Meanwhile, it is well known that N doping can tune graphene structure to optimize the physiochemical properties like electronic conductivity and surface hydrophilicity, thus facilitating Na<sup>+</sup> insertion and adsorption.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0026 #adma201901261-bib-0028 #adma201901261-bib-0031 #adma201901261-bib-0032"/> Hence, engineering graphene‐based materials with tuneable structure and composition is highly promising for the exploration of more advanced SIB anodes.</p><p>Herein, we present the synthesis of N‐doped few‐layer graphene (N‐FLG) by annealing graphitic carbon nitride (g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>) as an N‐rich precursor under the assistance of Zn catalyst. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on the fabrication of N‐doped graphene derived from g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> by selecting appropriate catalyst. As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>, melamine with abundant N is used to form g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> precursor, which can be converted to N‐FLG by the pyrolysis treatment at a specified temperature (<i>T</i> = 700, 800, and 900 °C) with the presence of Zn catalyst and subsequent HCl etching (denoted as N‐FLG‐T). It is found that the interlayer distance of resultant N‐FLG‐T can be effectively modulated by varying the calcination temperature. Simultaneously, the synthetic temperature also plays a vital role in controlling the N‐doping levels and configurations. The correlation between N‐doping and interlayer distance highlights the effect of pyrrolic N on the enlargement of graphene interlayer spacing. As a result, the optimized N‐FLG‐800 has the largest interlayer distance of up to 0.51 nm and an N content of 19.3 at.% with the highest pyrrolic N configuration. When applied as SIB anodes, N‐FLG‐800 exhibits overwhelmingly superior Na<sup>+</sup> storage properties with high specific capacity, ultrahigh rate capability, and long cycle life, outperforming the control groups and almost all state‐of‐the‐art carbon‐based anodes for SIBs. This work sheds light on the functions of different N configurations on tailoring carbon nanostructures, which will provide valuable guidance for the development of N‐doped carbon materials with improved Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance toward practical application in SIBs.</p><figure xml:id="adma201901261-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201901261:adma201901261-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201901261-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the synthetic routes for the fabrication of N‐FLG‐T.</caption></figure><p>To understand the nanostructure evolution throughout the synthesis process, the morphology and microstructure of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and N‐FLG‐T were observed with scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). Figure S1a in the Supporting Information is the SEM image of the g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> precursor, which shows it consists of aggregated particles several micrometers in size. The layered and stacked structure of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> can be seen in the magnified SEM image (Figure S1b, Supporting Information), which is further confirmed by the TEM and high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) images (Figure S1c, d, Supporting Information). After pyrolysis of the g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> precursor under Zn catalysis, well‐defined graphene with a lateral size of several micrometers were obtained, evidenced by the flexible large‐area nanosheets shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a. The 2D lamellar structure of N‐FLG‐800 with plenty of wrinkles is also verified in the TEM image (Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0002"/>b). Importantly, 2D N‐FLG is favored as it exposes more surface area, ensuring enough contact between the active material and the electrolyte toward higher surface capacitive contribution. Close observation in the HRTEM image (Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0002"/>c) shows that the as‐derived N‐FLG‐800 has a thickness of about three graphene layers. More significantly, the interplanar spacing of the graphene layers was measured using the DigitalMicrograph software, which shows a sufficiently enlarged interlayer distance of up to 0.51 nm (Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0002"/>d). To the best of our knowledge, this is the largest graphene interlayer spacing among all the heteroatom‐doped graphene materials reported so far.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0026 #adma201901261-bib-0028 #adma201901261-bib-0031 #adma201901261-bib-0032"/> This significantly enhanced interlayer spacing is expected to contribute to reducing the energy barrier for Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation and deintercalation, which can be attributed to the extent of nitrogen doping with specific configurations (discussed later). In addition, there are many defects along the crystal fringes stemming from a discontinuous lattice or dislocations, as marked by the dashed red circles, which can offer abundant active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0033 #adma201901261-bib-0034"/> The bright‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (BF‐STEM) image and corresponding element mappings of N‐FLG‐800 (Figure S2a–c, Supporting Information) suggest the uniform incorporation of N in the graphene nanosheets. Similarly, N‐FLG‐700 and N‐FLG‐900 were also synthesized under Zn catalysis by varying the calcination temperature. Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0002"/>e–g,i–k depicts their SEM, TEM, and HRTEM images. From these images, we can see that both are made up of several graphene layers with similar morphology and flexible 2D structure. As for the microstructures, the most profound difference among all the N‐FLG‐T samples is their interlayer distance, where the graphene interplanar spacing is decreased to 0.45 and 0.49 nm for N‐FLG‐700 and N‐FLG‐900, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0002"/>h, l, and Figure S3, Supporting Information). Again, the BF‐STEM image and corresponding element mappings of N‐FLG‐700 and N‐FLG‐900 (Figure S2d–i, Supporting Information) validate the homogeneous doping of N among the derived carbon framework.</p><figure xml:id="adma201901261-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201901261:adma201901261-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201901261-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) SEM, b) TEM, c) HRTEM images, and d) intensity line profile perpendicular to the graphene layers of N‐FLG‐800. e) SEM, f) TEM, g) HRTEM images, and h) intensity line profile perpendicular to the graphene layers of N‐FLG‐700. i) SEM, j) TEM, k) HRTEM images, and l) intensity line profile perpendicular to the graphene layers of N‐FLG‐900.</caption></figure><p>Interestingly, the same thermal treatment of the g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> precursor as for the preparation of N‐FLG‐800 with Co as the catalyst produces open‐ended N‐doped carbon nanotubes (N‐CNTs) with a diameter of 200–300 nm (Figure S4a, b, Supporting Information). The TEM image of N‐CNTs (Figure S4c, Supporting Information) shows their bamboo‐like hollow structure. From the corresponding HRTEM image (Figure S4d, Supporting Information), it is clear that the wall of the as‐derived N‐CNTs is composed of about 30 graphene layers. The measured distance between the graphene layers is 0.36 nm, larger than the (002) <i>d</i>‐spacing of conventional graphite (0.336 nm), but still smaller than the aforementioned critical value of 0.37 nm. It is worth noting that there are less defects along its edge compared with N‐FLG‐T samples, suggesting the high crystallinity of as‐derived N‐CNTs. The bamboo‐like hollow structure of the as‐obtained N‐CNTs is more distinctly observed in the BF‐STEM image (Figure S4e, Supporting Information). Corresponding element mappings (Figure S4f, g, Supporting Information) indicate the homogeneous distribution of C and N throughout the N‐CNTs. All these results support the successful structure‐controlled preparation of N‐doped graphene and CNTs by employing g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and suitable metal catalysts.</p><p>The crystal structures of all samples were also characterized using powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD). The XRD pattern of the g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> precursor (Figure S5, Supporting Information) shows two peaks at 13.1° and 27.4°, indexed to the (100) and (002) planes of typical g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. However, these characteristic peaks are not present in the XRD patterns of N‐FLG‐T and N‐CNTs (Figures S6 and S7, Supporting Information), implying there was no residual g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> in the pyrolysis products. Instead, N‐CNTs displays a sharp peak around 26.2°, a little lower than the (002) peak (26.6°) for standard graphite (JCPDS No. 26–1079). While N‐FLG‐800 exhibits a very broad peak centered at about 25.4°. This negative shift of the (002) peak reflects an enlarged interplanar distance, agreeing well with the HRTEM results. Likewise, N‐FLG‐700 and N‐FLG‐900 present a similar broad diffraction peak at 25.9° and 25.7°, respectively. It should be noted that many reports ascribe the broadening of the (002) peak in carbon materials to them being amorphous.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0026 #adma201901261-bib-0029"/> Combined with the above structure characterizations, the broadened (002) diffraction peak of the as‐synthesized N‐FLG‐T should be explained from their significantly reduced thickness and enlarged interplanar spacing, correlated to the sharp peak of N‐CNTs with thicker walls and relatively smaller interlayer distance.</p><p>In addition to the enlarged interlayer spacing, crystal imperfection, specific surface area, and nitrogen doping also have significant effect on the Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity, which were analyzed by Raman spectroscopy, Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) technique and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a, Raman spectra of N‐FLG‐T exhibit a D peak at ≈1350 cm<sup>−1</sup> and a G peak at ≈1581 cm<sup>−1</sup>. By contrast, the Raman spectrum of commercial graphite flake (Figure S8, Supporting Information) only shows a sharp G peak centered at 1582 cm<sup>−1</sup>. Accordingly, the appearance of D peak in N‐FLG‐T can be essentially ascribed to the N doping, which results in the disorder of carbon matrix. Given the D peak is attributed to the A<sub>1g</sub> vibration of C<sub>6</sub> rings caused by defects, and the G peak is induced by the E<sub>2g</sub> vibration of the sp<sup>2</sup> carbon atoms, their intensity ratio <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> is widely used to indicate structural imperfection in carbon materials.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0035 #adma201901261-bib-0036"/> Clearly, N‐FLG‐800 shows a relatively higher <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> ratio of 1.26 in comparison with that of N‐FLG‐700 (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> = 1.20) and N‐FLG‐900 (<i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> = 1.17). The increased structural distortion of N‐FLG‐800 is likely due to its heavy N‐doping and defects, in line with the HRTEM observations. Whereas the <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> ratio for N‐CNTs is only 0.58 (Figure S9, Supporting Information), suggesting its high degree of graphitization with less defects, which is in accordance with the XRD and HRTEM results. From the perspective of graphitization, the higher the pyrolysis temperature, the higher the degree of graphitization. Thus, the resultant g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>‐derived products have more g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>‐like properties at low calcination temperature and more graphite‐like properties with increasing pyrolysis temperature. This is further confirmed by their FTIR spectra, which show characteristic vibrational bands related to CN heterocycles (875–1750 cm<sup>−1</sup>) and NH groups (2750–3500 cm<sup>−1</sup>, Figures S10 and S11, Supporting Information) that decrease in intensity in the order of N‐FLG‐700 &gt; N‐FLG‐800 &gt; N‐FLG‐900. Markedly, the FTIR spectrum of N‐CNTs (Figure S12, Supporting Information) shows no these vibration peaks, further proving its good crystallinity.</p><figure xml:id="adma201901261-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201901261:adma201901261-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201901261-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Raman spectra of N‐FLG‐T. N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption isotherms of N‐FLG‐700 (b), N‐FLG‐800 (c), and N‐FLG‐900 (d). The insets in (b–d) show the corresponding BJH pore size distribution plots.</caption></figure><p>N<sub>2</sub> adsorption–desorption analysis of N‐FLG‐T and N‐CNTs was carried out to evaluate their specific surface area and porosity. As depicted in Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0003"/>b–d and Figure S13 (Supporting Information), all isotherms show a typical hysteresis loop belonging to type IV isotherms, indicating mesoporosity. Their BET surface areas are calculated to be 33.30 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for N‐FLG‐700, 81.58 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for N‐FLG‐800, 73.43 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for N‐FLG‐900, and 73.02 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> for N‐CNTs. The relatively large surface area of N‐FLG‐800 is beneficial for providing greater sites for Na<sup>+</sup> absorption. Additionally, the Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) pore size distribution plots all show a similar trend for pore sizes smaller than 10 nm, validating that the materials contain massive nanopores and mesopores. Indeed, a porous structure is advantageous for promoting the adsorption or deposition of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0037 #adma201901261-bib-0038"/> According to the detailed BET analysis (Table S1, Supporting Information), N‐FLG‐800 has the largest monolayer adsorption volume and specific surface area, thereby favoring surface Na<sup>+</sup> storage.</p><p>The survey XPS spectrum of N‐FLG‐800 displays the presence of C, N, O, and Zn signals, and their respective content is 74.79, 19.30, 5.11, and 0.80 at.% (Figure S14, Supporting Information). Survey XPS information of N‐FLG‐700 and N‐FLG‐900 is given in Figures S15 and S16 (Supporting Information), and the nitrogen doping levels of these materials are 35.26 and 14.65 at.%, respectively. While the co‐existence of C, N, O, and Co elements with atomic percentage of 93.33, 2.55, 3.94, and 0.18% has been detected from the survey XPS spectrum of N‐CNTs (Figure S17, Supporting Information). Notably, the C 1s peak of N‐FLG‐T and N‐CNTs (Figures S14–S17, Supporting Information) can be fitted into four peaks at about 284.7, 285.5, 287.4, and 291.1 eV, which correspond to CC, CN, CO, and OC  O species, respectively.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0039 #adma201901261-bib-0040"/> The more pronounced CN peak of N‐FLG‐T than that of N‐CNTs is consistent with their higher N content. The increased N doping level of N‐FLG‐T is likely the result of a zinc nitride intermediate forming from the Zn catalyst, causing further N‐doping in the carbon matrix and preventing the rapid loss of nitrogen source in the form of N<sub>2</sub> gas. The extremely high N content is likely responsible for the significantly enlarged interlayer distance. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a–c presents the N 1s high‐resolution XPS spectra of N‐FLG‐700, N‐FLG‐800, and N‐FLG‐900. Evidently, the N 1s region of all N‐FLG‐T samples can be deconvoluted into three peaks at around 398.4, 399.5, and 400.2 eV, assigning to pyridinic, pyrrolic, and graphitic N, respectively.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0039 #adma201901261-bib-0041"/> It is well known that the nitrogen configuration has a substantial influence on the structure and activity of resulting materials.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0042 #adma201901261-bib-0043"/> With respect to the electron configuration of N in the carbon matrix (Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0004"/>d), pyridinic N with a lone pair occupying an sp<sup>2</sup> orbital and pyrrolic N with a p orbital lone pair are very active for absorbing Na<sup>+</sup> and contribute to increasing surface capacitive capacity. Meanwhile, graphitic N with two single electrons on two p orbitals are very unreactive with Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0043"/> Furthermore, the N content and configuration of all samples are comprehensively compared in Table S2 in the Supporting Information. It is obvious that the N‐doping content of N‐FLG‐T is inversely proportional to the calcination temperature. N‐FLG‐700 achieves the largest N content, while the sum percentage of pyridinic and pyrrolic N is the highest for N‐FLG‐800 up to 72.09%. Except for the contribution to capacitive Na<sup>+</sup> storage, pyridinic and pyrrolic N have a significant impact on the enlargement of graphene interlayer distance. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0004"/>e, unlike the pyridinic N with a lone pair of electrons parallel to the graphene layer, pyrrolic N possessing a lone pair of electrons perpendicular to the graphene plane can expand the interlayer distance to a larger extent,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0043"/> due to the more considerable electrostatic repulsion. For such reason, a higher atomic percentage of pyrrolic N will result in an more enlarged graphene interlayer spacing. As presented in Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0004"/>f, the pyrrolic N content of N‐FLG‐T descends in the order: N‐FLG‐800 (5.63 at.%) &gt; N‐NFL‐900 (0.93 at.%) &gt; N‐FLG‐700 (0.84 at.%), which is positively correlated with the observed (002) <i>d</i>‐spacing order: N‐FLG‐800 (0.51 nm) &gt; N‐NFL‐900 (0.49 nm) &gt; N‐FLG‐700 (0.45 nm). While no such positive correlation has been found between the interlayer distance and the pyridinic or graphitic N doping level for N‐FLG‐T (Figures S18 and S19, Supporting Information). Consequently, it is safe to conclude that the more enlarged interlayer distance of N‐FLG‐800 is attributable to its extremely high N doping in pyrrolic configuration. At the same time, the balance between high N‐doping (to obtain large surface capacity contribution) and good crystallinity (to increase the electronic conductivity) is essential for optimizing the N‐FLG‐T anode materials. Based on all these results, it is anticipated that the optimal structural properties of N‐FLG‐800 result in superior electrochemical performance for SIB application.</p><figure xml:id="adma201901261-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201901261:adma201901261-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201901261-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a–c) N 1s high‐resolution XPS spectra of N‐FLG‐700 (a), N‐FLG‐800 (b), and N‐FLG‐900 (c). d) Electron configurations for pyridinic, pyrrolic and graphitic N. e) Schematic illustration for the effect of pyridinic and pyrrolic N on the enlargement of graphene interlayer distance. f) Correlation between the interlayer distance and the pyrrolic N doping level for N‐FLG‐T.</caption></figure><p>The Na<sup>+</sup> storage properties of as‐prepared N‐FLG‐T and N‐CNTs were investigated by assembling 2032 type coin cells. Shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a, their rate performances were tested by galvanostatic discharge/charge cycling for 10 cycles at current densities ranging from 0.1 to 40 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 2.5–0.01 V. As expected, N‐FLG‐800 exhibits significantly superior rate capability to N‐FLG‐900, N‐FLG‐700, and N‐CNTs. Specifically, N‐FLG‐800 can deliver reversible capacities of 264.3, 258.7, 234.9, 209, 190.1, 148.5, 113.8, 87.3, and 56.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, and 40 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The stabilized discharge/charge profiles of N‐FLG‐800 at each current density are depicted in Figure S20 in the Supporting Information, in which the discharge curves have two obvious regions with different slopes. According to previous reports, the steeper region (2.5–0.3 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) corresponds to the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion process, whereas the shallower region between 0.3 and 0.01 V (versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) is attributed to the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption process.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0027 #adma201901261-bib-0044"/> Surprisingly, when the current density reverts to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, there is a common trend that the reversible capacity is improved. This phenomenon is possibly the result of full penetration of the electrodes during the rate cycling, increasing the active area. Note that N‐CNTs and N‐FLG‐700 show similar but poor rate performance. The Na<sup>+</sup> storage of N‐CNTs is limited by its small interlayer spacing and low N‐doping, while the poor electronic conductivity of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>‐like N‐FLG‐700 prevents fast electron transfer and in turn leads to inefficient Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/adsorption. Due to its largely expanded interplanar distance and balanced N‐doping, N‐FLG‐800 achieves the best rate capability. As highlighted in Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0005"/>b, the impressive rate performance of N‐FLG‐800 outperforms many reported carbon anodes for SIBs,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0024 #adma201901261-bib-0032 #adma201901261-bib-0045 #adma201901261-bib-0046 #adma201901261-bib-0047 #adma201901261-bib-0048 #adma201901261-bib-0049 #adma201901261-bib-0050 #adma201901261-bib-0051"/> and a more comprehensive comparison with other carbonaceous anodes is given in Table S4 in the Supporting Information.</p><figure xml:id="adma201901261-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201901261:adma201901261-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201901261-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Rate capability of N‐CNTs and N‐FLG‐T at various current densities. b) Comparison of as‐synthesized N‐FLG‐800 in this work with some representative carbon anodes in the literature. c) CV curves of N‐FLG‐800 at different scan rates from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, and d) corresponding capacity contribution ratios to the total capacity. e) Long‐term cycling performance of N‐CNTs and N‐FLG‐T at a current density of 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>To gain an in‐depth understanding of the ultrahigh rate capability of N‐FLG‐800, the sodiation/desodiation processes were studied by cyclic voltammetry (CV). Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0005"/>c shows the CV curves of N‐FLG‐800 with scan rates from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. A pair of broad redox peaks during the anodic/cathodic processes explicitly indicate the reversible Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation between graphene layers. Intriguingly, compared with N‐FLG‐800, N‐FLG‐900 manifests similar CV curves (Figure S24c, Supporting Information), but larger potential polarization under the same conditions, implying relatively sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics. Considering that the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics depends on the interlayer distance for carbonaceous anodes, the increased potential polarization of N‐FLG‐900 is likely due to its comparatively smaller interplanar spacing. Generally, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage of carbon materials takes place during a diffusion‐controlled intercalation process (DIP) and a surface‐induced capacitive process (SCP).<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0013 #adma201901261-bib-0030"/> The mixed storage mechanism can be qualitatively appraised using the equation <i>i</i> = aν<sup>b</sup>,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0052"/> where <i>i</i> is the redox peak current, ν is the corresponding scan rate, and <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are constants. Particularly, the <i>b</i>‐value is the index that determines the predominant Na<sup>+</sup> storage process. Normally, <i>b</i> = 0.5 indicates a DIP‐dominant battery mechanism, whereas <i>b</i> = 1.0 suggests SCP‐controlled supercapacitor behavior.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0053 #adma201901261-bib-0054"/> The <i>b</i>‐value can be calculated from the slope of the linear relationship between log(<i>i</i>) and log(<i>v</i>). As displayed in Figure S21 in the Supporting Information, the <i>b</i>‐values of the anodic and cathodic processes for N‐FLG‐800 are 0.853 and 0.868, reflecting that N‐FLG‐800 shows a favorable SCP for fast Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics. Likewise, N‐FLG‐900 displays an anodic <i>b</i>‐value of 0.789 and a cathodic <i>b</i>‐value of 0.798 (Figure S22, Supporting Information), indicating similar Na<sup>+</sup> storage behaviour with N‐FLG‐800 but slightly inferior pseudo‐capacitance. Further, the capacitive contribution to the total Na<sup>+</sup> storage capacity can be quantitatively evaluated by another equation <i>i</i>(<i>V</i>) = <i>k</i><sub>1</sub>ν + <i>k</i><sub>2</sub>ν<sup>1/2</sup>,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0055"/> in which <i>i</i>(<i>V</i>) is the total current at a fixed potential, and <i>k</i><sub>1</sub>ν and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub>ν<sup>1/2</sup> represent the contributions from the SCP and DIP, respectively. The quantitative analysis results of N‐FLG‐800 are presented in Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0005"/>d. As the scan rate increases from 0.1 to 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the SCP contribution ratio for N‐FLG‐800 improves from 36.1% to 86.2% (the separation of capacitive and diffusion currents at 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> is given in Figure S23, Supporting Information). In contrast, N‐FLG‐700 delivers a maximum SCP contribution of only 59.2% at 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, while N‐FLG‐900 is similar to N‐FLG‐800 with a high SCP contribution ratio of 81.9% at 10 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S24, Supporting Information). Given the enhanced SCP contribution, N‐FLG‐800 demonstrates enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> storage kinetics and outstanding rate capability in SIB application.</p><p>The long‐term cycling stability and Coulombic efficiency of N‐FLG‐T and N‐CNTs were estimated by galvanostatic discharge/charge measurement at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> for 2000 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201901261-fig-0005"/>e and Figure S25, Supporting Information). In the first cycle, the discharge and charge capacities of N‐FLG‐800 were 256.7 and 214.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, giving an initial Coulombic efficiency of 83.5%, which is higher than most reported carbon anodes for SIBs.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0024 #adma201901261-bib-0027 #adma201901261-bib-0028 #adma201901261-bib-0029 #adma201901261-bib-0031 #adma201901261-bib-0045 #adma201901261-bib-0046 #adma201901261-bib-0049"/> The Coulombic efficiency of N‐FLG‐800 increases rapidly to 97.2% in the second cycle and exceeds 99% from the third cycle. The irreversible capacity in the initial several cycles is usually attributed to the electrolyte decomposition and the generation of a solid‐electrolyte interfacial (SEI) film.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0056"/> Remarkably, N‐FLG‐800 exhibits high reversible capacities around 210 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 2000 cycles, affording a charge capacity of 211.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with an exceptional retention of 98.6% after 2000 cycles. In comparison, N‐FLG‐900 delivers lower reversible capacity with obvious capacity decay (38.3% attenuation after 2000 cycles). N‐FLG‐700 and N‐CNTs show good durability but a very low reversible capacity of about 33 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> during 2000 cycles. Apart from the greatly enlarged interlayer spacing, we ascribe the remarkable stability of N‐FLG‐800 to its balanced N‐doping and high electronic conductivity. As seen in Figure S26 in the Supporting Information, the Nyquist plots of N‐CNTs and N‐FLG‐T consist of a semicircle in the high‐frequency region and a straight line in the low‐frequency area, representing the charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) and the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics, respectively.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0047 #adma201901261-bib-0048"/> By fitting the electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS, Table S3, Supporting Information), N‐FLG‐800 possesses the smallest <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> (45.8 Ω) and N‐FLG‐900 also has a favorable <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> (58.2 Ω). N‐CNTs and N‐FLG‐700 on the other hand display much higher <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>, mainly originating from low N doping and g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>‐like properties, respectively.</p><p>On the basis of the comprehensive material characterizations and electrochemical investigations, it is reasonable to attribute the superior Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance of N‐FLG‐800 to its unique physiochemical properties from the following aspects. First of all, its larger interlayer distance owing to the multiple N incorporation especially pyrrolic N is capable of facilitating Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction with good reversibility, accounting for the high reversible capacities at various current densities. More importantly, its optimal N‐doping content, with a large proportion of pyridinic and pyrrolic configurations, and defect‐rich mesoporous structure provide greater electrochemical active sites for efficient Na<sup>+</sup> absorption. This is likely responsible for the enhanced surface capacitive effects toward ultrahigh rate capability at high current densities. Additionally, the balance between high N content and sufficient degree of graphitization produces a strong framework with improved electronic conductivity which facilitates exceptional durability for long‐term operation. It is also worth mentioning that as‐synthesized N‐FLG‐800 has a lower specific surface area compared to most carbon materials,<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0026 #adma201901261-bib-0031 #adma201901261-bib-0032 #adma201901261-bib-0033 #adma201901261-bib-0039 #adma201901261-bib-0047 #adma201901261-bib-0048 #adma201901261-bib-0049 #adma201901261-bib-0057"/> which is beneficial for increasing the initial Coulombic efficiency by reducing the formation of an SEI film.<link href="#adma201901261-bib-0057 #adma201901261-bib-0058 #adma201901261-bib-0059"/> In light of these advantageous features, the resulting N‐FLG‐800 achieves significant Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance as an anode for SIBs, which stands out among recently reported anodes for SIBs, including both carbonaceous materials (Table S4, Supporting Information) and transition metal‐related materials (Table S5, Supporting Information).</p><p>In conclusion, N‐doped carbon nanostructures with controlled morphology, structure, and composition have been developed for the first time by the pyrolysis of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> under different catalysts and temperatures. The Zn‐assisted thermal treatment of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> produces 2D N‐FLG‐T with tuneable enlarged interlayer distance ranging from 0.45 to 0.51 nm and high N‐doping levels, whereas 1D N‐CNTs with small interlayer distance of 0.36 nm and low N content can be obtained under Co catalysis. By correlating the interlayer spacing with the N configurations of N‐FLG‐T samples, a new insight into the significant influence of pyrrolic N on the enlargement of graphene interlayer distance is proposed. From this, N‐FLG‐800 was demonstrated to be the optimal architecture. As an anode for SIBs, N‐FLG‐800 exhibits superior Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance with ultrahigh rate capability, surpassing the control groups (e.g., N‐FLG‐700, N‐FLG‐900, and N‐CNTs) and most reported carbon anodes. The as‐developed N‐FLG‐800 is very promising for practical application in SIBs. The identification of the effects of different N configurations in this work creates a new dimension to construct advanced carbon nanomaterials for electrochemical energy conversion technologies.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201901261-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors would like to acknowledge the financial support from the Australian Research Council (ARC) through the Discovery Project programs (DP140104062, DP160104866, DP170104464, DE150101234, and LP160100927) and from the National Natural Science Foundation of China (No. 21576202).</p></section><section xml:id="adma201901261-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma201901261-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma201901261-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma201901261-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>S.‐W.</givenNames> <familyName>Kim</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>D.‐H.</givenNames> <familyName>Seo</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>X.</givenNames> <familyName>Ma</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>G.</givenNames> <familyName>Ceder</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>K.</givenNames> <familyName>Kang</familyName></author>, <journalTitle>Adv.
