However, the sluggish kinetics of sodium ion makes it hard to achieve high‐rate performance, therefore impairing the power density. In this work, a fiber‐in‐tube Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐carbon(C)/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> is designed with fast sodiation kinetics. The dominating capacitance mechanism for high Na‐ion storage performance is due to abundant grain boundaries, three exposed layer interfaces, and carbon wiring. The fiber‐in‐tube hybrid anode shows a high specific capacity (616 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>). A high energy density of 779 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and power density of 7793 W kg<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201900076-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201900076-sec-0001"><p>In the past decades, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have been widely considered as one of the most promising energy storage devices. The increasing development of electric vehicles and smart grids requires superior electrochemical performance of LIBs in terms of energy and power densities. Furthermore, lithium sources are scarce with uneven distribution on the Earth.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0001 #anie201900076-bib-0002 #anie201900076-bib-0003 #anie201900076-bib-0004 #anie201900076-bib-0005"/> Because of this, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are drawing increasing research interest as a competitive alternative to LIBs due to the overwhelming benefits such as low cost, high abundance, and suitable redox potentials. However, the development of SIBs seems not to be viable, which is ascribed to the larger ionic radius of Na<sup>+</sup> as compared with Li<sup>+</sup> (Li<sup>+</sup>: 0.76 Å; Na<sup>+</sup>: 1.02 Å).<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006 #anie201900076-bib-0007 #anie201900076-bib-0008 #anie201900076-bib-0009 #anie201900076-bib-0010"/> The sluggish electrochemical kinetics of sodium ions makes sophisticated demands on the design of electrode materials. The slow reaction dynamics usually results in inferior electrochemical performance, particularly the rate capability, which is a vital factor to be considered in the application of the fast‐charging electronic devices.</p><p>Rate capability relies on the structure and morphology of electrodes, ion diffusion, and electron transport. It is still a great challenge to realize a trade‐off between the high energy density and high power density for battery materials,<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0011 #anie201900076-bib-0012"/> especially, the Na‐free transition‐metal oxide/sulfide (TMO/TMS) anode materials.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0013"/> It has been demonstrated that incorporating TMO/TMS with high‐conductivity materials such as carbon and graphene can ameliorate the rate capability.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0007 #anie201900076-bib-0009 #anie201900076-bib-0014 #anie201900076-bib-0015"/> Recently, considerable attention has been paid to pseudocapacitance‐dominating charge storage to enable high‐rate performance as pseudocapacitance originates from a fast sequence of electrosorption and ion insertion process.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0016 #anie201900076-bib-0017 #anie201900076-bib-0018 #anie201900076-bib-0019"/> Owing to the larger ionic radius of Na, it is more imperative to introduce a pseudocapacitive charge storage mechanism in SIBs than in LIBs to improve the rate performance. To realize the pseudocapacitance, the size and morphology of the material has to be adjusted to boost reaction sites,<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0020 #anie201900076-bib-0021"/> introduce carbonaceous materials to reduce the diffusion energy barrier,<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0022"/> and/or create phase boundaries in a composite to generate more active sites.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006"/> However, it is still a great challenge to obtain TMS electrodes with high energy and power densities for SIBs.</p><p>Herein, we design Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanotubes wired by Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C composite nanofibers to form a fiber‐in‐tube structure (denoted as Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>) as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0001"/> a. In the as‐synthesized structure, the hollow structure effectively buffers the volume change of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> during repeated electrochemical cycling to enable a long cycle life and high rate capability. Moreover, aligning Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C composite nanofibers in the nanotube not only increases the density of active materials in the composite, but provides the conductive pathway for electrons. The experimental and simulation analysis show that the dominating capacitance mechanism for the high Na‐ion storage performance is due to abundant grain boundaries, three exposed layer interfaces, and carbon wiring in the design. As a result, the fiber‐in‐tube Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite shows a high specific capacity of 616 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. At 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, a capacity of about 451 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can still be achieved after 500 cycles. More importantly, a high energy density of 779 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and power density of 7793 W kg<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained simultaneously.</p><figure xml:id="anie201900076-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201900076-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Illustration of high‐rate charge storage of the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite, and the optimized configurations and related adsorption energies of b) a single Na atom and c) eight atoms adsorbed onto Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>An illustration of the synthesis process of the fiber‐in‐tube Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> hybrid is shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S1. ZIF‐67 shell was uniformly grown on PAN/Co(Ac)<sub>2</sub> nanofibers (Supporting Information, Figure S2), which is due to the strong coordination interaction between cobalt ions and 2‐methylimidazole. At the initial stage of the sulfidation process, a thin layer of cobalt sulfide serving as a buffer layer is formed, which plays a vital role in the generation of the hollow structure. Co<sup>2+</sup> diffuses outward more easily than S<sup>2−</sup> moves inward owing to the smaller ionic radius (Co<sup>2+</sup>=70 pm, S<sup>2−</sup>=184 pm). Consequently, the released Co<sup>2+</sup> ions from ZIF‐67 diffuse to the preformed cobalt sulfide layer to further react with S<sup>2−</sup> ions. Hollow cobalt sulfide particles are formed because of the dissolution of inner ZIF‐67 (Supporting Information, Figure S3). The formation of nanovoids between core and shell can also be attributed to the diffusion‐controlled reaction mechanism.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0023 #anie201900076-bib-0024"/> After a heating treatment in nitrogen, the fiber‐in‐tube Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> hybrid can be obtained.</p><p>Detailed structure and composition information of the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composites are shown in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> and the Supporting Information, Figure S4. The resulting film presents one‐dimensional (1D) nanostructure with rough surface as shown by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) image in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> a. The Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C nanofiber aligned in the nanotube composed of hollow Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles can be clearly observed in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> b. The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> c shows that the nanotube wall is composed of many hollow Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles, which can be attributed to a diffusion‐controlled ion exchange reaction during the sulfidation process.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0023 #anie201900076-bib-0025 #anie201900076-bib-0026"/> The magnified TEM image of the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> particle (Supporting Information, Figure S3) reveals its hollow interior. The confined Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C nanofiber is about 80 nm in diameter, which can be observed from the broken Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composites in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> d. The lattice fringes of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> can be unambiguously detected in Figures <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> d (inset) and 2j, where <i>d</i>‐spacings of 0.30 nm and 0.351 nm correspond to (311) and (220) planes of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, respectively. No corresponding lattice fringes of carbon can be observed owing to its amorphous nature. Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> i and the Supporting Information, Figure S5 reveal that the hollow Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticle is composed of many tiny nanocrystallites with diameters ranging from 5 to 12 nm. Abundant grain boundaries can be clearly observed, as highlighted by the red lines, which are beneficial to the pseudocapactive process. The STEM image of the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composites and its corresponding element mappings in Figures <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> e–h verify that the C signal appears in the core part, and Co and S distribute uniformly in the core and shell, showing the fiber‐(Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C)‐in‐tube (Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>) structure. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) pattern in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> k confirms the cubic Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> phase (PDF No. 65‐6801), which matches well with the above HRTEM result. The high‐resolution Co 2p XPS spectrum displays characteristic peaks of Co<sup>3+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup> in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> l.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0009"/> The characteristic peaks of S 2p<sub>2/3</sub> and S 2p<sub>1/3</sub> in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0002"/> m further confirm the presence of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006"/> The high‐resolution C 1s XPS spectra (Supporting Information, Figure S6) can be fitted into four peaks, corresponding to the sp<sup>2</sup>‐hybrizied C, N‐sp<sup>2</sup> C, N‐sp<sup>3</sup> C, and C=O, respectively.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006"/> According to the thermal gravimetric analysis in the Supporting Information, Figure S7, the mass content of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> in the composites is about 80.6 wt %. The prepared hybrid shows a specific surface area of 83.6 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> with porous structures (Supporting Information, Figure S8).</p><figure xml:id="anie201900076-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201900076-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a),b) FESEM images, c),d) TEM images, e)–h) EDX mapping, i),j) HRTEM images, k) XRD pattern, and l),m) Co 2p and S 2p high‐resolution XPS spectra of the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite.</p></caption></figure><p>The electrochemical reaction details can be revealed by the cyclic voltammetric (CV) curves at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> as shown in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0003"/> a. Obviously, there are three peaks in the first cathodic scan at around 1.72, 0.78, and 0.56 V, respectively, which are related to Na<sup>+</sup> insertion into Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, the conversion reaction between Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> and Na to form Co, and the formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI).<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006 #anie201900076-bib-0027 #anie201900076-bib-0028"/> In the anodic scan, the distinct peak at 1.75 V is ascribed to the conversion reaction between Co and Na<sub>2</sub>S to form Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>. The CV curves overlap well from the second scan onward, suggesting good cycling repeatability. The first discharge and charge capacities are 1072 and 634 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0003"/> b). Moreover, the plateaus in the voltage profile correspond to the peaks in the CV curves. There is no significant difference in the subsequent cycles, showing excellent repeatability. Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0003"/> d shows a reversible capacity of 616 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which is better than the other reported cobalt sulfide anodes for SIBs (Supporting Information, Table S1). Moreover, a considerable capacity of 452 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained after 500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Supporting Information, Figure S9). The STEM and EDX mapping reveal the fiber‐in‐tube nanostructure, which can be well maintained after 100 cycles (Supporting Information, Figure S10). In contrast, Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> nanoparticles show a severe capacity decay, such as only maintaining 55 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 150 cycles (Supporting Information, Figures S11–S13). More interestingly, the prepared fiber‐in‐tube composite exhibits excellent rate capability. A surprisingly reversible capacity of 422 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (in 2.5 min) can be retained even when the current density is increased from 0.5 to 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0003"/> c. Based on the comprehensive comparison in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0003"/> f, the fiber‐in‐tube composite shows its great superiority compared with other reported cobalt sulfide‐based anodes for SIBs.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006 #anie201900076-bib-0008 #anie201900076-bib-0010 #anie201900076-bib-0015 #anie201900076-bib-0029 #anie201900076-bib-0030 #anie201900076-bib-0031"/> Furthermore, the synthesized electrode exhibits a specific energy of 779 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and a specific power of 7793 W kg<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0003"/> e), which can #anie201900076-bib-0031"/></p></caption></figure><p>To interpret the high‐rate capability, the electrochemical kinetics of the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> electrode was investigated by a series of CV measurements at scan rates from 0.05 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> a–d) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements (Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> e,f). The CV curves present similar shapes with one anodic and cathodic peaks as the scan rate rises. According to the equation <i>i</i>=<i>aν</i><sup><i>b</i></sup>, the charge‐storage mechanism can be evaluated, where <i>b</i> is an adjustable parameter between 0.5 and 1, determined by the slope of log(<i>ν</i>)−log(<i>i</i>), corresponding to diffusion‐controlled and capacitive‐controlled processes, respectively.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0019 #anie201900076-bib-0036 #anie201900076-bib-0037"/> From Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> b, the <i>b</i>‐values of 0.86 and 0.84 can be quantified for both anodic and cathodic peaks, indicative of capacitive characteristics. The ratio of Na‐ion capacitive contribution at a certain scan rate can be quantified by separating the current response <i>i</i> at a fixed potential <i>V</i> into capacitive‐ and diffusion‐controlled charges, according to the Equation <link href="#anie201900076-disp-0001"/>:<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0006 #anie201900076-bib-0019"/><displayedItem xml:id="anie201900076-disp-0001" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie201900076-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="anie201900076-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201900076-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical kinetics analysis: a) CV curves measured at different scan rates from 0.05 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, b) log <i>i</i> vs. log <i>v</i> plots at oxidation and reduction state, c) CV curve with capacitive‐ and diffusion‐controlled contributions at 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, d) bar chart showing the contribution ratio of capacitive‐ and diffusion‐controlled capacities at different scan rates, e) temperature‐dependent Nyquist plots, and f) reciprocal bulk and interface impedance as a function of reciprocal temperature.</p></caption></figure><p>where, <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> are constants at a given voltage. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> c, a dominant capacitive contribution of 78.6 % can be quantified (light green region) in the CV curve at 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. As the scan rate increases, the contribution from the capacitive process further increases to a maximum value of 89.2 % at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> d. The leading pseudocapacitive charge storage benefits the rate performance of electrodes.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0007"/> The enhanced pseudocapacitance is indicative of faster electron and sodium‐ion transport. To further understand the dynamics, a temperature‐dependent EIS measurement was carried out to investigate the charge transfer resistance and activation energy <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> in Figures <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> e,f. The EIS spectrum at a certain temperature in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> e shows one semicircle followed by a straight line, and the charge transfer resistance decreases as the testing temperature increases. The charge transfer resistance values (Supporting Information, Table S2) at different temperatures can be obtained by fitting the EIS spectra using the equivalent circuit (inset in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> e). The charge transfer activation energy is determined by the Arrhenius plot in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0004"/> f based on Equation <link href="#anie201900076-disp-0002"/>:<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0038"/><displayedItem xml:id="anie201900076-disp-0002" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie201900076-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-math-0002"/></displayedItem></p><p>where <i>σ</i> is the ionic conductivity, <i>T</i> is the absolute temperature, <i>A</i> is a constant, <i>E</i><sub>a</sub> is the apparent activation energy for ion transport, and <i>k</i><sub>B</sub> is the Boltzmann constant. The charge‐transfer activation energy is calculated to be 276 meV, indicating an extraordinarily low energy barrier for charge transfer, which demonstrates a fast transfer of sodium ions through the whole cell. The low charge transfer resistance and activation energy reveal fast sodium uptakes, facilitating the capacitive process and thereby resulting in the superior sodium storage and rate capability for the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite.</p><p>From the above results, the capacitive‐controlled process is of importance to the excellent electrochemical performance, which is further confirmed by density functional theory (DFT) calculations. The sodium storage contributed by the capacitive process is simulated and the adsorption energy (<i>E</i><sub>ad</sub>) based on Equation (3) is evaluated for the interactions between Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> and Na. We carried out a configuration optimization for the system of Na adsorbed on the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> by considering a series of different adsorption sites (Supporting Information, Figure S14). The most favorable adsorption site (site 5, Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0001"/> b) for an isolated Na atom on Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> surface is identified based on its smallest <i>E</i><sub>ad</sub> (−4.583 eV; Supporting Information, Table S3). The condition of the maximum number of adatoms on the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> is also explored. It is found that the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> can accommodate up to 8 Na atoms with the <i>E</i><sub>ad</sub> value of −2.025 eV in Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0001"/> c. These results indicate that Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> can enable a fast Na loading process and there is a strong interaction between the substrate and Na adatom.<link href="#anie201900076-bib-0039"/><displayedItem xml:id="anie201900076-disp-0003" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie201900076-math-0003.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-math-0003"/></displayedItem></p><p>where the <i>E</i><sub>ad</sub> represents the adsorption energy. The <i>E</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie201900076-math-0004.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-math-0004"/> and <i>E</i><inlineGraphic href="graphic/anie201900076-math-0005.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201900076:anie201900076-math-0005"/> are the total energies of the pristine Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> and Na adsorbed Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>, respectively. <i>E</i><sub>Na</sub> represents the energy per Na atom in the bulk form, and n corresponds to the number of Na atoms in the adsorption configurations.</p><p>The experimental and simulation results reveal that the elaborately designed Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite possess a superior sodium‐ion storage capability, mainly due to the following benefits: 1) the unique 1D fiber‐in‐tube structure and hollow structure can not only shorten the transport path, but effectively buffer the volume variation of Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> during cycling; 2) many grain boundaries in Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> and three exposed layer interfaces provide more reaction sites for fast sodium‐ion uptake (Figure <link href="#anie201900076-fig-0001"/> a); 3) carbon wiring in the core is beneficial to the electron transport in the whole electrode.</p><p>In conclusion, the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite presents a high capacity of 616 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. More encouragingly, the electrode displays a high‐energy density of 779 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> and a high‐power density of 7793 W kg<sup>−1</sup>, simultaneously. Electrochemical analysis reveals the pseudocapacitance‐dominated sodium‐ion storage mechanism in the Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub>‐C/Co<sub>9</sub>S<sub>8</sub> composite electrode. This fast kinetics aids the high rate capability. The simulation shows that the grain boundaries and/or exposed interfaces are beneficial to the pseudocapacitive process. This engineering design tactic confers an elevated power density without wakening the energy density. Our findings can pave new ways for developing superior electrodes for SIBs.</p><section xml:id="anie201900076-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Research Grants Council of the Hong Kong Special Administrative Region, China (Project No. PolyU152208/18E and 152064/15E), the Innovation and Technology Fund of Hong Kong, China (Project No. GHX/001/16GD), the Hong Kong Polytechnic University (Project No. 5‐ZJL8), and the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Project No. 11704291).
