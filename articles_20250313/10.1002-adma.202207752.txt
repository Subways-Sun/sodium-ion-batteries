However, the 2D graphene sheet only allows the rapid diffusion of sodium ions along the parallel direction while that of the vertical direction is difficult, limiting the rate capability of graphene‐based electrode materials. To tackle this problem, pore‐forming engineering has been employed to perforate graphene and concurrently achieve the in situ growth of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles. The generation of in‐plane nanohole breaks through the physical barriers of the graphene nanosheets, enabling the fast diffusion of electrolyte ions in the longitudinal direction. In addition, this design limits the aggregation of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles because of the high affinity of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> on graphene. Benefiting from the high conductivity and fast ion transport bestowed by the ingenious architecture, the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/holey graphene exhibits a remarkable rate performance of 519.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> and desirable cycle stability. Conclusions drawn from this investigation are that the transport of sodium inside the graphene‐based composites is crucial for rate performance enhancement and this method is effective in modifying graphene‐based nanomaterials as potential anode materials.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202207752-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Pore‐forming engineering has been employed to simultaneously achieve the perforation of graphene nanosheets and the in situ growth of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles. Benefiting from the fast diffusion of electrolyte ions in the longitudinal direction, Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/holey graphene (Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG) as anode material exhibits remarkable rate performance and excellent cycling performance in sodium‐ion batteries.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202207752:adma202207752-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202207752-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202207752-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Nowadays, with the continuous exhaustion of fossil fuels and the urgent demand for renewable energy resources, great efforts have been made to develop high‐efficiency electrochemical storage systems, such as lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> lithium‐sulfide batteries,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs),<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and supercapacitors.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> LIBs with high energy density and long cycle life have been broadly applied in energy storage devices such as large‐scale smart grids and portable electronic devices. However, ultimately, the limited lithium abundance will inevitably hinder the development of LIBs. In this regard, SIB have been conceived as a promising alternative for LIB owing to the abundance and low cost of sodium resources.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To date, hard carbon anode has been considered as the most promising material for SIBs for its high capacity (≈350 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) and low operational voltage (around 0.3 V). However, the capacity and rate performance are still unsatisfactory.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Consequently, extensive efforts have been poured into exploring novel anode and cathode materials for SIBs. Among them, cobalt chalcogenides have shown great potential as anode materials because of their outstanding theoretical capacity and narrow bandgap.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Particularly, the relatively weaker metal‐selenium bonds make the bond cleavage more favorable in the conversion reaction compared with the metal‐oxygen/sulfur bonds, stemming from the fact that Se possesses a larger ionic radius than O and S.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, cobalt chalcogenides suffer from unsatisfactory performances for sodium ion storage due to poor electronic and ionic together with inevitable volumetric variation during sodiation/desodiation process.</p><p>Graphene‐based nanohybrids have come into the front due to their fascinating physicochemical properties of graphene with 2D nanosheets.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In general, the introduction of graphene into electrode materials endows them with several distinctive architectural merits, such as accelerated electron transfer, robust structural stability, and abundant electrochemical active sites. Cobalt chalcogenides have been incorporated with assorted architectures of graphene (1D nanotube, 2D nanosheets, 3D aerogel, etc.). For instance, well‐dispersedly CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles were anchored on the surface of B,N‐codoped graphene sheets for avoiding the electrochemical Ostwald ripening process, and reversible capacities of 311 and 250 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> were respectively attained.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Another case was graphene nanoscrolls embedded with CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles by a facile liquid nitrogen freezing method, and discharging capabilities of 582 and 438 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 and 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> were respectively realized.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, CoSe<sub>2</sub>/graphene/CNT aerogel was prepared to provide high mechanical strength and a faster conduction pathway among the nanoparticles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, these 1D and 3D structured cobalt‐chalcogenides/graphene nanomaterials are only applicable for SIB electrodes with limited mass loading and poor volumetric energy density, which are not suitable for applications. By contrast, the rate capability of 2D structured nanocomposites, expected to accomplish thick electrodes with high energy density, is not ideal. This is mainly due to the physical barrier of electrolyte ions by stacked graphene sheets whose size range from a few to tens of micrometers.</p><p>Considering the difficulty of electrolyte ions to diffuse across the graphene plane, the perforation of graphene nanosheets is considered as an effective strategy to reduce the longitudinal ion diffusion distance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hybrids composed of holey graphene (HG) combine the features of both 2D nanomaterials and porous architectures. The rich in‐plane nanoholes favor facilitating ion transport along the longitudinal directions, realizing the enhancement of rate capability. Moreover, HG nanosheets can also serve as conductive skeletons to accelerate the electron transfer and maintain the structural stability of electrode materials. It has been demonstrated that partially‐reduced HG oxide, synthesized by H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> treatment and low‐temperature calcination, achieved fast ion diffusion, enlarged interlayer distance, and abundant storage space of Na<sup>+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Besides, reduced HG was used to enwrap hollow FeS<sub>2</sub>@C spheres, exhibiting excellent rate performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, most of the abovementioned HG‐based materials were prepared by employing holey graphene oxides as precursors, which is tedious, time‐consuming and environmentally unfriendly. Therefore, more efforts are urgently needed to explore facile synthesis protocols to obtain HG‐based electrode materials with high energy/power densities.</p><p>Herein, a thermal etching together with an in situ conversion strategy has been employed to synthesize the nanohybrid of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> and HG, where Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles were uniformly anchored next to the holes of HG nanosheets. The HG nanosheets serving as conductive matrices not only accelerated the electron transfer in the electrode materials, but also strengthened the structural robustness. Importantly, the in‐plane nanoholes beside Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles opened up the longitudinal channels of ion diffusion, shortening the distance of electrolyte ions in the electrode materials. Endowed by this unique holey nanostructure, the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG electrode displayed excellent electrochemical performances, including high reversible capacity, remarkable rate performance and ultralong cycle stability. When further combined Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG as anode with commercial Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (NVP) as a cathode in Na‐ion full cells, the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG|NVP full cell presented prominent specific capacity, extraordinary cycling durability, and impressive rate capability.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The synthetic process of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG is illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, involving a simple chemical bath deposition (CBD), thermal etching and selenylation treatment. The graphene oxide (GO) suspension was prepared in a modified Hummer's method. In step I, the nanocomposite of the cobalt precursor and reduced GO (Co‐precursor/rGO) was prepared by a facile CBD method. Due to the existence of abundant functional groups (e.g., –OH, –COOH, etc.), cobalt ions were inclined to adsorb on both sides of GO nanosheets, forming homogeneous active sites on their surfaces. After subsequently adjusting the pH to 8 by the NH<sub>4</sub>OH solution, the suspension was transferred to an oil bath to synthesize the Co‐precursor/rGO. In this process, Co‐precursor nucleated on the GO surfaces by the initial reaction between cobalt ions and functional groups of GO. After slowly adding the NH<sub>4</sub>OH solution, Co‐precursor nanocrystallites were generated according to the heterogeneous nucleation mechanism and continuously grew to tiny Co‐precursor nanoparticles. Meanwhile, the GO was partially reduced to rGO. In step II, cobalt nanoparticles/HG (Co/HG) was obtained by the calcination of Co‐precursor/rGO, involving the reduction of Co‐precursor to Co and the creation of holes in the graphene nanosheets. Co‐precursor was first decomposed into CoO nanoparticles. The redox reaction between CoO and C atoms next to CoO generated Co, achieving the etching of graphene sheets.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> CoO, as the etching reagent, was successfully reduced to Co nanoparticles, while the nanoholes were correspondingly generated adjacent to Co nanoparticles. In step III, Co/HG was converted into Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG by the selenylation treatment under an N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere.</p><figure xml:id="adma202207752-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202207752:adma202207752-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202207752-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the synthetic process of the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG. FE‐SEM images of b,e) Co‐precursor/rGO, c,f) Co/HG and d,g) Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG.</caption></figure><p>The morphology evolution of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG was monitored by the field emission scanning electron microscope (FE‐SEM) and transmission electron microscope (TEM). The GO displayed a relatively thin and wrinkled nanosheet structure (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). After Co‐precursor deposition, it was difficult to observe nanoparticles on the rGO surfaces, as shown in the FE‐SEM images of Co‐precursor/rGO (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0001">1b,e</link>), manifesting the uniform dispersion of tiny Co‐precursor nanoparticles on the rGO nanosheets. After thermal etching treatment, abundant nanoholes adjacent to the Co nanoparticles were distinct, judging from the FE‐SEM images of Co/HG (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0001">1c,f</link>), which originated from the oxidation of C into CO or CO<sub>2</sub>. And large Co nanoparticles were generated by the decomposition of the Co‐precursor via the subsequent reduction reaction. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0001">1d,g</link>, the microstructure of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG was similar to that of Co/HG, indicating that the microstructure of Co/HG was successfully inherited. The atomic force microscopy (AFM) test has been performed to obtain the thickness of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information). Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S2a</link> in the Supporting Information showed the AFM image of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG, and the thickness of a Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticle was about 9.1 nm from Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S2b</link> in the Supporting Information. For comparison, Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO without the thermal etching, no nanoholes were detected in the graphene nanosheets (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S3</link>, Supporting Information). Furthermore, the TEM image of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>) exhibited that Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles were homogenously dispersed on the surface of HG. Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0002">2b</link> manifests that the nanoholes were closely adjacent to the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles. A high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image revealed the lattice spacing of 2.67 Å, corresponding to (111) crystal planes of the monoclinic Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0002">2c,d</link>). The high‐angle annular dark field scanning TEM (HAADF‐STEM) and energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) elemental mappings of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0002">2e</link>) further verified the homogeneous distribution of Co and Se elements in the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG hybrid.</p><figure xml:id="adma202207752-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202207752:adma202207752-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202207752-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) TEM images, c,d) HR‐TEM images, e) HAADF‐STEM image and corresponding EDS mappings of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG.</caption></figure><p>The development of phase structure for Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG is revealed by X‐ray powder diffraction (XRD) (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>). For Co‐precursor/rGO, a typical bump of rGO emerged at 23.5° while that of Co‐precursor did not appear, resulting from the ultrasmall size and low crystallinity of Co‐precursor. After calcination at 800 °C under N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere, three peaks appeared at 44.1°, 51.4°, and 75.6° in the XRD pattern of Co/HG, which was assigned to cubic cobalt nanocrystals (JCPDS No. 01‐15‐0806). The result indicated that the Co‐precursor was completely converted into Co nanoparticles due to the carbothermal reaction arising from the strong reducibility of C atoms at high temperatures. As shown in the XRD pattern of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG, the diffraction peaks accorded with monoclinic Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> (JCPDS No.01‐074‐4767), revealing the successful selenization of Co into Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles. The XRD result of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S4</link>, Supporting Information) also clearly displayed the diffraction peaks of monoclinic Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. The carbon content and thermal stability of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO were assessed by thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information). The weight of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO increased slightly with increasing temperature to 450 °C, which was attributed to the oxidation of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> to CoSeO<sub>4</sub> and SeO<sub>2</sub>. Subsequently, an obvious weight loss occurred in the range of 430–700 °C due to the decomposition of CoSeO<sub>4</sub> to Co<sub>3</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and the evaporation of SeO<sub>2</sub> as well as the conversion of graphene into CO<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The content of graphene in Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO was estimated to be 33.6% and 52.1%, respectively. Moreover, the specific surface area and pore distribution was shown in Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S6a</link> in the Supporting Information. Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO displayed Type IV isotherms with Type H3 hysteresis loops, demonstrating the existence of mesopores. The specific surface area of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG was 83.4 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> inferior to Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO (94.9 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup>), resulting from the reduction of graphene content in the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>‐based composites. The number of macropores in Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG was significantly higher than in Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S6b</link>, Supporting Information), associated with the formation of in‐plane nanoholes. The abundant holes were propitious to enrich active sites for sodium ion storage and promote the penetration of electrolytes in the electrodes.</p><figure xml:id="adma202207752-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202207752:adma202207752-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202207752-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD patterns of Co‐precursor/rGO, Co/HG, and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG. b) Raman spectra of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO. High‐resolution XPS spectra of c) Co 2p and d) Se 3d.</caption></figure><p>Raman spectrum was collected to reveal the effect of pore‐forming engineering on graphene structure. Two peaks, located at 1345 and 1590 cm<sup>−1</sup>, were attributed to the D and G bands of carbon, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0003">3b</link>). D band was the defect band corresponding to the disordered carbon. Whereas G band was the E<sub>2g</sub> mode ascribed to the graphitic carbon. After the creation of nanoholes in the graphene sheets, the <i>I</i><sub>D</sub>/<i>I</i><sub>G</sub> ratio increased from 1.0 to 1.45, indicating an increased amount of defect carbons. The X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) analysis was conducted to study the chemical valence states of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG. Survey spectrum (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S7a</link>, Supporting Information) showed distinct signals of C, O, Se, and Co. The presence of O was due to contact with air. The high‐resolution XPS spectrum of Co 2p (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0003">3c</link>) displayed four peaks at 778.1, 781, 792.6, and 796.2 eV, which were assigned to Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub> for Co<sup>3+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup> and Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub> for Co<sup>3+</sup> and Co<sup>2+</sup>, respectively. Another two peaks at 785.6 and 803.5 eV were the satellite peaks of Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub> and Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub>, respectively. For the spectrum of Se 3d (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0003">3d</link>), two peaks were observed at 54.6 and 55.3 eV, which were indexed to Se 3d<sub>5/2</sub> and Se 3d<sub>3/2</sub>, respectively. Moreover, the peak at 59.4 eV was pointed to the oxidized Se, originating from a small amount of SeO<i><sub>x</sub></i> formed during the selenization process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The XPS spectrum of C 1s (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S7b</link>, Supporting Information) showed that three peaks at 284.8, 286, and 288.6 eV were assigned to sp<sup>2</sup> C, C–O, and CO, respectively.</p><p>The electrochemical performances of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG were estimated by cyclic voltammetry (CV) and galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD) cycling tests. As shown in the first scanning CV curves of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>), a sharp reduction peak at 0.99 V was assigned to the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> to from Na<sub>x</sub>Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> and the formation of the solid electrolyte interface layer (SEI) owing to the decomposition of electrolyte.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Another broad peak at 0.43 V resulted from the reduction of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> to metallic Co. Meanwhile, the oxidation peak observed at 1.75 eV corresponded to the conversion reaction from Co to Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>. From the second cycle, two reduction peaks were observed at 1.23 and 0.8 V, ascribing to the intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> into Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> and the subsequent conversion of Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> into Co and Na<sub>2</sub>Se, respectively. For the oxidation peaks, there was almost no significant change in the subsequence cycles. Moreover, the CV profiles of the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG anode approximately overlapped except for the first cycle, implying its excellent cycle performance. The first four GCD profiles of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0004">4b</link>) showed that Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG delivered an exceptional initial charge capacity (622.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) with an initial Coulombic efficiency (69%). The irreversible capacity in the first cycle was mainly caused by the irreversible decomposition of electrolytes and the formation of the SEI layer.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, GCD curves overlapped in successive cycles, revealing the good stability of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG for sodium ion storage. And all plateaus of the GCD curves were in correspondence with the peaks of the CV profiles. After 100 cycles, Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG achieved a specific capacity of 575.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, much higher than Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO (456.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). The Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG also displayed superior rate performance to Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO at various current densities (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0004">4d,e</link>). Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG delivered a specific capacity of 588.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and maintained a high capacity of 519.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> even at 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>, outperforming Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO (417.8 and 318 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), reflecting the accelerated diffusion of electrolyte ions due to the creation of in‐plane holes in the graphene nanosheets. In addition, a high capacity (590.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) was obtained when the current density recovered to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, revealing the excellent reversibility of electrochemical reaction. The rate capability of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG surpassed most reported traditional metal chalcogenides (e.g., CoSe/C,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanotubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>@N‐CN,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0017"><sup>17b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> CoSe@C/CNTs,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> CoSe<sub>2</sub>@CNTs‐Mxene,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0019"><sup>19a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> NiSe<sub>2</sub>‐rGO‐C,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> NiSe<i><sub>x</sub></i>@C/CNT<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0004">4f</link>), attributed to the exceptional nanostructure with abundant nanoholes and fairly high electronic conductivity.</p><p>The cycle stabilities of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG and Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO were appraised at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202207752-fig-0004">4g</link>). For Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG electrode, the capacity decay was witnessed during the initial 100 cycles, ascribing to the volumetric variation and the aggregation of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles throughout the intercalation and deintercalation of sodium ions at high current densities. Subsequently, the discharge capacity of the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG tended to be stable with a capacity of 472.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles and achieved 464.3 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 1000 cycles, which was 79% of the initial capacity with Coulombic efficiency over 102%. In comparison, although the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO electrode exhibited 444.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 550 cycles, an obvious capacity decay occurred from 550 to 1000 cycles (371.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), resulting from the nanoparticle pulverization and electrical contact loss of active material. These results indicated that the in‐plane nanoholes gave Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG a structural advantage over Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO. The sharp electrochemical fading of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG in the first 50 cycles may be attributed to the formation of unstable SEI and mechanical degradation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202207752-bib-0007 #adma202207752-bib-0018"><sup>7b,18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The CV curves at scan rates from 0.2 to 1.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> were shown in Figure <link href="#adma202207752-supitem-0001">S8</link> in the Supporting Information. First, the HG nanosheets act as conductive networks and robust skeletons to boost the rapid transfer of electrons and strengthen the structural integrity, respectively. Second, the homogeneous dispersion of Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub> nanoparticles on the HG sheets could accommodate the volume variation during the intercalation and deintercalation of sodium ions. Finally, the etched in‐plane nanoholes of graphene sheets provide sufficient nanochannels for ion diffusion along the longitudinal direction, which can increase the contact area of electrode materials with electrolytes and thus facilitate reaction kinetics.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0030"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG nanocomposite was devised by a facile CBD followed by thermal etching and selenylation treatment. It is worth noting that the in‐plane nanoholes in the graphene sheets have been created in situ via the thermal reduction of carbon by Co‐precursor‐derived nanoparticles. The in‐plane nanoholes have opened up the longitudinal channels of ion diffusion, shortening the distance of electrolyte ions in the electrode materials. In comparison with Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/rGO, Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG is capable of faster transport of electrolyte ions to achieve more excellent rate performances of sodium‐ion storage. Therefore, The Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG anode displayed an outstanding rate performance of 519.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 5.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> and a remarkable reversible capacity of 575.2 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. The Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG composite also exhibited prominent cycling stability of 464.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles. When coupled with Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> cathode, the Co<sub>3</sub>Se<sub>4</sub>/HG|NVP full cells delivered a high discharge capacity of 473.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles. Moreover, the full cells possessed good rate performance (227.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>) and extraordinary cycling life (over 500 cycles 446.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 1.0 A g<sup>−1</sup>). It is highly expected that the design strategy can provide enlightenment for engineering graphene‐based electrode materials for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The work was supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (22075217, 21801200, 21871217, and U1905215).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0050" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0060" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202207752-sec-0070" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202207752-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202207752-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202207752-cit-0001">a) <author><givenNames>Y.
