xml:id="adma202110108-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="adma202110108-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Highly efficient electrochemical energy storage technology, such as lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs), would greatly foster the integration with common global targets of carbon‐neutral realization, to further postpone the foreseeable energy crisis in the near future.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0001 #adma202110108-bib-0002 #adma202110108-bib-0003"><sup>1–3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, commercially growing demand for LIBs has triggered the cost surge coming from the limited raw materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0004 #adma202110108-bib-0005 #adma202110108-bib-0006"><sup>4–6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In this regard, cost‐effective sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) with bountiful supply and analogous “rocking chair” working mechanism with LIBs,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0007 #adma202110108-bib-0008 #adma202110108-bib-0009"><sup>7–9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> show promise in becoming one of the favorable alternatives to LIBs, and meet the practical demands of sustainable large‐scale energy storage systems as well.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0010 #adma202110108-bib-0011"><sup>10,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> That is why, SIBs have been extensively studied and flourished over the past decade. However, the existing demerits of poor structural stability, sluggish ion diffusion kinetics, low operating voltage, and unsatisfactory energy/power density of cathode materials remain challenging for the practical application of SIBs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0012 #adma202110108-bib-0013 #adma202110108-bib-0014"><sup>12–14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Toward cathodic hosts for sodium‐ion storage, the commonly available materials mainly include layered transition‐metal oxides (TMOs) and polyanionic compounds (PACs). Layered transition‐metal oxides, Na<i><sub>x</sub></i>/TM/O<sub>2</sub> (TM = Cr, Fe, Co, Mn, Ni, etc.), especially those with a P2 type structure,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> exhibit better cycle stability and rate performance due to the higher sodium‐ion diffusion kinetics, and have been extensively studied.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> At present, through the use of element doping, substitution, and interface modification suppression, the reversibility of layered transition mental oxide materials in the high‐pressure region has been significantly enhanced, and the overall electrochemical performance of the material has been improved.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite the higher theoretical capacity, TMOs usually encounter more problems like poor cycle stability of lattice and low average working voltage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0018 #adma202110108-bib-0019 #adma202110108-bib-0020 #adma202110108-bib-0021 #adma202110108-bib-0022"><sup>18–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Correspondingly, PAC cathodes with impressive structural robustness can significantly prolong the cyclic life of repeating Na<sup>+</sup>‐insertion/‐extraction processes, leaving them as competitive contender in SIBs’ electrodes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0023 #adma202110108-bib-0024"><sup>23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Moreover, tunability of working voltage at higher potential region for PACs shows great potential in large‐scale sodium storage systems, despite the slightly inferior specific capacity values to TMOs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Hence, once the further development in terms of high mean working voltage and rate capability is achieved simultaneously, PAC cathodes will hold a predominate place in large‐scale electrochemical sodium storage realm in the future.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0026 #adma202110108-bib-0027"><sup>26,27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sodium superion conductor structural compound, namely NASICON, is generally deemed as one representative kind of PACs, in which a stable 3D open framework is formed by interlinking PO tetrahedra and MO octahedra (M represents transition metals) through corner sharing, benefiting of rapid Na<sup>+</sup> migration.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0028"><sup>28</sup></link><sup>]</sup> NASICON‐structured materials are widely deployed in electrode materials and solid‐state electrolytes. Wherein NASICON‐type Li<sub>1.3</sub>Al<sub>0.3</sub>Ti<sub>1.7</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub> (LATP) is especially attractive due to its relatively higher room‐temperature ionic conductivity (&gt;1.0 × 10<sup>−4</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>) and better stability in the air and water. What is more, LATP is less expensive compared with commonly used Li<sub>7</sub>La<sub>3</sub>Zr<sub>2</sub>O<sub>12</sub> and Li<sub>10</sub>GeP<sub>2</sub>S<sub>12</sub> fast Li‐ion conductors, which enables it more competitive in mass production and commercial application.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0029"><sup>29</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among diverse NASICON family, Na<sub>3</sub>(VO<sub>1−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>1+2</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i> (0 ≤ <i>x</i> ≤ 1) has been studied extensively owing to its high discharge specific capacity (<i>C</i><sub>s</sub>), and more importantly, the feature of <i>x</i> value (F/O ratio) regulation‐induced adjustable electrochemical properties in terms of working voltage, <i>C</i><sub>s</sub>, and high‐rate capability.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> (NVPF) can deliver the highest mean working voltage of 3.95 V with a theoretical discharging <i>C</i><sub>s</sub> of 128 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, which corresponds to a high material‐based energy density of ≈507 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> in theory, rendering NASICON‐structural NVPF an attractive and promising cathode material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0031 #adma202110108-bib-0032"><sup>31,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nevertheless, as a member of NASICON family, NVPF inevitably endowed intrinsically poor conductive ability for electrons, which presents a huge challenge to its practical application.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0033"><sup>33</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In addition, previously reported NVPF cathodes usually exhibit a low discharge plateau around 3.4 V, further dropping the average operating voltage as well as the corresponding energy density.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0026 #adma202110108-bib-0034"><sup>26,34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In brief, NVPF is still up against two major obstacles, namely poor electronic conductivity and uncontrollable discharging behavior at low voltage region.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0035 #adma202110108-bib-0036 #adma202110108-bib-0037 #adma202110108-bib-0038"><sup>35–38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Building a highly conductive network can certainly improve the electronic conductivity, whereas the excess carbon incorporation will reduce energy density and cost‐effectiveness at the same time. So far, there is still lack of effective strategies to regulate rising mean discharging voltage for fixed crystal lattice with the constant active transition‐metal center, which is beneficial to achieve tunable voltage region, as well as prolonged discharging interval behavior simultaneously. Hence, it is imperative to develop a synergic strategy to elevate the low‐voltage discharging plateau for NVPF cathode with enhanced conductivity, and fully sodium‐ion storages at higher voltage.</p><p>Herein, we pioneeringly introduce the high‐entropy concept into PAC lattice, achieving the regulation and increase of working voltage for the V‐based NVPF lattice. Accordingly, a carbon‐free high‐entropy phosphate cathode of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>1.9</sub>(Ca,Mg,Al,Cr,Mn)<sub>0.1</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> (abbreviated as HE‐NVPF) has been successfully prepared via in situ partial substitution on V atoms. Advantageous high‐entropy effect in HE‐NVPF promotes the realization of obviously elevated mean voltage plateau (from 3.67 to 3.81 V), greatly enhanced Na‐storage capacity and energy density (from 395.78 to 445.42 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> calculated based on the mass of electrode materials), and superior cycling stability, in comparison to pristine NVPF (p‐NVPF, viz., the non‐high‐entropy NVPF) and other fluorophosphate counterparts. It turns out that, the as‐designed HE‐NVPF cathode can suppress the undesirable phase change behavior at low‐voltage plateau (at ≈3.4 V), which usually existed in p‐NVPF due to Na<sup>+</sup> preferential de‐/embedment at Na(2) sites during charging–discharging processes. In other words, benefited from the high‐entropy‐assisted restrain mechanism, Na<sup>+</sup> can be fully stored at the potential window with high‐voltage plateaux. Moreover, density functional theory (DFT) calculation illustrates that the introduced high‐entropy doping can alter the electronic states at around Fermi level, manifesting the enhanced conductivity of HE‐NVPF even without any carbon incorporation in the NVPF materials, hereby further accelerating the Na<sup>+</sup> migration rate owing to the reduced energy barrier. Essentially, the as‐proposed high‐entropy substitution strategy fills the gap on voltage tunability within the ascertain active transition‐metal center in fixed crystal lattice, taking competitiveness in commercial application of NASICON or phosphate‐type SIB cathodes a significant step further.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202110108-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>As proof of concept, Rietveld refinement analysis for X‐ray diffraction (XRD) was performed to investigate the crystal structure and phase purity. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202110108-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information), both p‐NVPF and HE‐NVPF own high crystallinity, and are mainly made up of NASICON‐type NVPF and imperceptible impurities of NaVF<sub>6</sub> and V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0039"><sup>39</sup></link><sup>]</sup> It has been clearly demonstrated that all predominant diffraction peaks are well‐indexed to the tetragonal crystal with the space group of <i>P</i>42/<i>mnm</i> where all six cations are distributed in a random pattern at the 8j site.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0040 #adma202110108-bib-0041"><sup>40,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Tables <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S1–S3</link> (Supporting Information) list the corresponding crystallographic data, atomic coordinates, and occupancies’ information. Additionally, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S2</link> (Supporting Information), the p‐NVPF and HE‐NVPF own high crystallinity and similar diffraction peaks that can be well indexed to the NASICON‐structured NVPF phase (JCPDF card No. 89‐8945). Figure <link href="#adma202110108-fig-0001">1b,c</link> presents the schematic diagram of p‐NVPF and HE‐NVPF lattice, which exhibits a robust 3D open channel framework composed of the connection of HE/VO<sub>4</sub>F<sub>2</sub> octahedrons and PO<sub>4</sub> tetrahedrons by sharing O atoms in the <i>ab</i>‐plane, and the interlinkage of HE/VO<sub>4</sub>F<sub>2</sub> octahedrons via the corner F atoms along the <i>c</i>‐direction. The obtained crystallographic data listed in Table <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S1</link> (Supporting Information) show that the cell parameter <i>c</i> of p‐NVPF is 10.7227 Å, which is obviously larger than 10.698 Å of HE‐NVPF. By contrast, equivalent <i>a</i>/<i>b</i> value of p‐NVPF (9.0325 Å) is slightly less than that (9.0400 Å) of HE‐NVPF.</p><figure xml:id="adma202110108-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202110108:adma202110108-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202110108-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Structural and morphology characterizations of HE‐NVPF. a) XRD Rietveld refinement results of HE‐NVPF. b,c) Schematic illustration for the change of crystal structure from p‐NVPF to HE‐NVPF. d) High‐resolution V 2p XPS spectrum. e) HRTEM image, inset is the corresponding FFT diffraction pattern and TEM image. f) The contrast profiles along the blue lines indicate the interplanar space corresponding. g) TEM–EDS mappings of HE‐NVPF.</caption></figure><p>The unit cell volumes of p‐NVPF and HE‐NVPF are 874.823 and 874.258 Å<sup>3</sup>, respectively, indicating that the high‐entropy substitution induces the desiring crystal lattice variation, which can be gauged by the contraction along the <i>c</i>‐direction, accompanied with the expansion along the <i>a</i>‐ and <i>b</i>‐directions, resulting from the radii of various substituting metal ions that do not have the same size as that of the replaced V<sup>3+</sup>.</p><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S3</link> (Supporting Information), the six elemental signals (C 1s, O 1s, Na 1s, F 1s, P 2s, P 2p, and V 2p peaks) can be observed in the survey X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectrum. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S4</link> (Supporting Information), the signals of five substitutional metallic elements (Mg, Cr, Al, Mn, and Ca) in the high‐resolution XPS of HE‐NVPF can be distinguished obviously, which can be regarded as a proof of the successful cosubstitution of five elements into central V atom in the NVPF lattice structure. Notably, the high‐resolution XPS spectrum of V 2p in Figure <link href="#adma202110108-fig-0001">1d</link> and Figure <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S5</link> (Supporting Information) can be deconvoluted into two intensive peaks, specifically, peak centered at 516.52/516.49 eV belongs to the V 2p<sub>3/2</sub> orbit, while the other at a higher binding energy of 523.58/523.56 eV is assigned to the V 2p<sub>1/2</sub> orbit. Both of them are characteristic peaks of V<sup>3+</sup> species,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0042"><sup>42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> which coincide well with the above XRD tests and the previous reports.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202110108-bib-0034"><sup>34</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Morphological feature of HE‐NVPF and p‐NVPF are analyzed by using scanning electron microscopy (SEM). As shown in Figures <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S6</link> and <link href="#adma202110108-supitem-0001">S7</link> (Supporting Information), taking p‐NVPF as reference, the aggregation of HE‐NVPF particles is greatly reduced, together with smaller particle size and more regular shape, further implying that HE‐NVPF possesses the potential to achieve excellent Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion rate. By virtue of in situ crystal structural characterization, we prove a well‐ameliorated highly reversible phase change behavior when Na ions preferentially desert from the Na(2) site during the charging and discharging process of NVPF‐based cathodes, and more importantly, the detrimental phase change behavior occurring in low‐voltage plateau is completely suppressed and in consequence, the as‐prepared HE‐NVPF only exhibited two high‐voltage plateaux located at 4.2 and 3.6 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na with all the Na ions stored in the high‐voltage region. The as‐proposed mechanism indicates that the high‐entropy stabilization of the host matrix helps to promote the occupation of Na(2) sites greatly, prohibits the phase transition in low‐voltage area, supports long‐term cycle stability, and contributes to higher mean voltage and better rate performance. The intriguing high‐entropy strategy is bound to provide more insights for the development of new cathode materials.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202110108-sec-0040" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors gratefully acknowledge the financial support from the National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant Nos. 52173246, 91963118, and 52102213), the Science Technology Program of Jilin Province (Grant No. 20200201066JC), and the Postdoctoral Science Foundation funded project from Jilin Province (Grant No. 111865008).</p></section><section xml:id="adma202110108-sec-0050" type="conflictOfInterest" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202110108-sec-0060" type="openResearch" numbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202110108-sec-0070" type="dataAvailability" numbered="no"><title type="main">Data Availability Statement</title><p>The data that support the findings of this study are available from the corresponding author upon reasonable request.</p></section></section><bibliography xml:id="adma202110108-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma202110108-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma202110108-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>D.</givenNames> <familyName>Larcher</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>J.
