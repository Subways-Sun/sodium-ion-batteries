Here, highly sodium‐accessible carbon coated nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers have been synthesised via the facile electrospinning technique which can deliver an enhanced capacity of ≈167 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 450 cycles at current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and retain a capacity of ≈71 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the high current rate of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. With the benefits of their porous structure, thin TiO<sub>2</sub> inner walls, and the introduction of conductive carbon, the nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>/C microfibers exhibit high ion accessibility, fast Na ion transport, and fast electron transport, thereby leading to the excellent Na‐storage properties presented here. Nanostructuring is proven to be a fruitful strategy that can alleviate the reliance on materials' intrinsic nature; and the electrospinning technique is versatile and cost‐effective for the fabrication of such an effective nanoporous microfiber structure.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en" xml:id="advs140-abs-0001"><p><b>Nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>‐C microfibers exhibit exceptional electrochemical performance</b> for sodium‐ion batteries. The nanoporous structure with thin inner walls can facilitate ion accessibility and ion transport. The incorporation of carbon can enhance the electronic conductivity. The electrode configuration with nanoporous microfiber construction, therefore, is essential and effective for electrode advancement, especially for transition metal oxides.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs140:advs140-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs140-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="advs140-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs140-sec-0010"><label role="interim">1</label><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>With the aim of powering portable electric devices and electric vehicles, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have attracted tremendous attention over the past two decades.<link href="#advs140-bib-0001 #advs140-bib-0002"/> Intriguingly, current energy demands for large‐scale energy storage have intensified the pursuit of sodium ion batteries (SIBs) because of their low price and the natural abundance of sodium.<link href="#advs140-bib-0003 #advs140-bib-0004 #advs140-bib-0005"/> Many electrode materials for LIBs have correspondingly been investigated for SIBs, based on their chemical similarities.<link href="#advs140-bib-0006"/> In contrast to Li ions (0.76 Å), the larger Na ions (1.02 Å) tend to show sluggish kinetics for electrochemical reactions, which generally results in unsatisfactory battery performance or even complete electrochemical inactivity.<link href="#advs140-bib-0007 #advs140-bib-0008 #advs140-bib-0009 #advs140-bib-0010"/> A common and effective strategy to improve the kinetics is to fabricate nanostructured materials,<link href="#advs140-bib-0011 #advs140-bib-0012"/> which not only can shorten the ions' diffusion paths but also enlarges the electrode/electrolyte contact area. Nanostructured electrodes are also favourable for alleviating mechanical stress and maintaining the structural integrity of the electrode. For instance, expanded graphite delivers a surprisingly high capacity of 284 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> via nanostructural control of inactive graphite (less than 35 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) in SIBs.<link href="#advs140-bib-0013"/> Another functional strategy is to enhance the conductivity of electrodes via doping modification or incorporating conductive materials.<link href="#advs140-bib-0014 #advs140-bib-0015 #advs140-bib-0016"/> Typically, these two strategies can synergistically alleviate the limitations of the materials' intrinsic natures and greatly optimize the sodium‐storage properties of electrode materials.<link href="#advs140-bib-0017"/> Impressively, both strategies have been extensively applied into anode materials design and reach significant achievement, such as nanosized Li<sub>4</sub>Ti<sub>5</sub>O<sub>12</sub>,<link href="#advs140-bib-0018"/> Sn nanoparticles embedded in C,<link href="#advs140-bib-0019"/> yolk‐shell Sn<sub>4</sub>P<sub>3</sub>@C nanospheres,<link href="#advs140-bib-0020"/> yolk‐shell FeS@C nanospheres,<link href="#advs140-bib-0021"/> and phosphorene‐graphene hybrid.<link href="#advs140-bib-0022"/> For instance, in contrast to the steep capacity decline found in commercial MoS<sub>2</sub>, the electrochemical performance in terms of capacity and cycling stability can be significantly enhanced by constructing MoS<sub>2</sub>/graphene nanocomposites.<link href="#advs140-bib-0023"/></p><p>Low‐conductivity anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> was selected to demonstrate the process for accelerating the Na‐reaction kinetics. TiO<sub>2</sub> has been intensively investigated as a potential anode material for SIBs owing to its many merits in terms of its cycling stability, nontoxicity, low cost, and abundance.<link href="#advs140-bib-0024 #advs140-bib-0025 #advs140-bib-0026 #advs140-bib-0027 #advs140-bib-0028 #advs140-bib-0029"/> Amorphous TiO<sub>2</sub> nanotubes grown on a Ti substrate delivered a gradually increasing capacity of up to 120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 50 mAg<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#advs140-bib-0024"/> A thin carbon layer coated on anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> nanorods was proven to effectively enhance the rate capability. In addition, it was proposed that the reaction mechanism involves Na ion insertion to form orthorhombic Na<sub>0.5</sub>TiO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#advs140-bib-0028"/> Most recently, via a special microwave synthesizer, Huang and co‐workers' reported the Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation pseudocapacitance of graphene‐coupled TiO<sub>2</sub>, which realized a specific capacity of 90 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 12 A g<sup>−1</sup>.<link href="#advs140-bib-0027"/> Superb amorphous TiO<sub>2</sub> stands out in LIBs and SIBs due to its intrinsically isotropic nature, which is believed to possess open active diffusion channels, initially facilitating Li and Na‐ion mobility, ionic and electronic diffusion into TiO<sub>2,</sub> leading to high ion accessibility and enhanced power and energy densities.<link href="#advs140-bib-0028 #advs140-bib-0030"/> Based on these productive advances, it is necessary to further develop a simple method to fabricate ideal hierarchical nanostructured TiO<sub>2</sub>‐based composites with low cost and high productivity.</p><p>In this work, we have designed and fabricated hierarchical nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>‐carbon microfibers (NTMFs‐C) with large surface area and enhanced conductivity. The highly nanoporous structure can be formed by a straightforward, general microemulsion electrospinning method with subsequent calcination. This synthesis technique does not require expensive facilities and tedious preparation processes. The obtained NTMF‐C microfibers have continuous 1D geometry with hierarchical nanopores inside, which is able to support fast electron transport and guarantee high ion accessibility. The multilevel nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>‐C microfibers are expected to show long‐term cycling stability and enhanced rate capability with high specific capacity. The electrode delivers a high capacity of ≈167 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 450 cycles at current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and achieves a capacity of ≈71 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, even at the high current rate of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Our results confirm the crucial functions of such a nano‐engineering strategy toward optimization of electrode performance, which will arouse further attention and offer a versatile technique for fabricating active materials, especially regarding those with insufficient intrinsic properties.</p></section><section xml:id="advs140-sec-0020"><label role="interim">2</label><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The synthetic procedures for hierarchical NTMF‐C and nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers (NTMF) are illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs140-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>. The as‐prepared microemulsion was electrospun to form microfibers in a high electric field. Two samples, with and without carbon, could be separately fabricated by simply varying the calcination atmosphere. Under air atmosphere, the random nanosized pores are created from vacancies left by oil droplets and combustion of PVP in the microemulsion. When calcined under Ar atmosphere, the morphology of the nanopores does not only depend on the oil drops, but also on the residual carbon matrix from PVP. NTMF‐C and NTMF, therefore, likely possess distinguishable configurations in their morphologies. This speculation is confirmed by the TEM images in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>.</p><figure xml:id="advs140-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs140:advs140-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs140-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Schematic illustration of the synthetic processes for producing nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub> and TiO<sub>2</sub>‐C microfibers via electrospinning.</caption></figure><figure xml:id="advs140-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs140:advs140-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs140-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>SEM images of a) STMF, b) NTMF, and c) NTMF‐C, with corresponding SEM images at higher magnification (insets). TEM images of d) STMF, e) NTMF, and f) NTMF‐C for single microfibers. g) Elemental mapping of NTMF‐C for C, O, and Ti elements.</caption></figure><p>The scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images in Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>a and the transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image in Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>d reveal that the as‐prepared solid TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers (STMF) possess smooth surfaces and has a cylindrical solid structure. The SEM image at higher magnification (inset of Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>a) confirms that the obtained microfibers of the STMF sample are solid and uniform, and an average diameter of ≈632 nm was obtained (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The NTMF and NTMF‐C obtained by microemulsion electrospinning show similar morphologies, in which there are massive nanopores inside each microfiber (Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>b,c). The high quality nanostructure of the homogeneous nanoporous microfibers can be observed in the SEM inset images. The optical photographs of the NTMF (white) and NTMF‐C (black) microfibers confirm the successful incorporation of carbon (Figure S2, Supporting Information). The morphological differences of all the samples were examined in detail using transmission electron microscopy (TEM) analysis. Unlike the STMF, the NTMF and NTMF‐C samples manifest very high porosity. It is interesting that the NTMF sample shows tube‐like pores (Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>e), while NTMF‐C displays a bubble‐like structure (Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>f), which is mainly ascribed to the confinement effect of the retained carbon matrix. More importantly, NTMF‐C and NTMF show very thin wall thicknesses, which correspond to shortened Na ion transport paths. The energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) elemental mapping confirmed the coexistence and homogeneous dispersion of the C, O, and Ti elements throughout the nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>/C microfibers (Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0002"/>g).</p><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs140-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a,b, it is noteworthy that the bubble‐like NTMF‐C shows a higher average fiber diameter of ≈720 nm and a larger pore diameter of ≈72 nm, which corresponds to ≈630 and ≈60 nm for the tube‐like NTMF (Figure S3, Supporting Information), respectively. The high‐porosity and larger‐diameter NTMF‐C microfibers are believed to contain a larger amount of electrolyte per microfiber, so a larger number of Na ions are available to establish the critical ion concentration for cycling. The carbon ratio (ω<sub>C</sub>) in the composite is estimated to be ≈29 wt% (Figure S4, Supporting Information). Nitrogen sorption analysis of NTMF and NTMF‐C shows type‐IV curves with H4 hysteresis (Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0003"/>c), demonstrating the disordered distribution of nanopores. The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) surface areas were calculated to be 67 and 38 m<sup>2</sup>·g<sup>−1</sup> for NTMF and NTMF‐C, respectively. Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0003"/>d shows the X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns of the STMF, NTMF, and NTMF‐C samples. All XRD patterns could be indexed to a typical TiO<sub>2</sub> anatase phase with space group I41/amd (JCPDS No. 21‐1272). No peaks from other phases can be detected, indicating the high purity of the obtained samples. Compared with the STMF, the NTMF shows lower peak intensity with the absence of some phases, which indicates a lower degree of TiO<sub>2</sub> crystallization. Moreover, the NTMF‐C pattern indicates obvious amorphous properties, only showing broadened (101), (004), and (200) humps, implying small crystals of TiO<sub>2</sub> formed with the aid of the C matrix. The unique nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>‐C microfibers, featuring large nanopores, high conductivity, and thin inner walls, were expected to enhance the availability of ions near the surface and facilitate efficient electron and Na ion transport. This nanostructure, in turn, is expected to yield enhanced capacity, long‐term stability, and excellent rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="advs140-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs140:advs140-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs140-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Fiber diameter distribution, and b) pore diameter distribution of NTMF‐C sample. c) Nitrogen sorption isotherms of NTMF‐C and NTMF. d) XRD patterns of NTMF‐C, NTMF, and STMF.</caption></figure><p>As illustrated in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs140-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a, it is interesting that all three electrodes show self‐improving capacity, which is mostly due to the occurrence of an irreversible phase transition from amorphous TiO<sub>2</sub> to cubic phase. The newly formed cubic phase can exhibit self‐improving capacity via self‐organization based on the electrochemical reactions between TiO<sub>2</sub> and sodium ions.<link href="#advs140-bib-0023"/> The solid TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers (STMF) undergo slow self‐organization over 100 cycles due to their good crystallization, which leads to a slight capacity increase from 41 to 60 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. It can be seen that the nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers (NTMF) complete the self‐improving process after about 50 cycles, which corresponds to a great capacity increase of 67 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (from 40 to 107 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). Afterwards, the NTMF delivers outstanding cycling stability, with a capacity of ≈130 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over 500 cycles (sustainable process). In contrast, the porous NTMF‐C hybrid shows the most rapid self‐improving process and the highest specific capacity. A high and fast self‐improving capacity to ≈150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> can be achieved after 20 cycles, with a sustainable capacity of 167 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over the subsequent 500 cycles. Significantly, the practical capacity of NTMF‐C is estimated to be ≈116 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> based on the following calculation: Q<sub>NTMF‐C</sub> = Q<sub>TiO2</sub> × (1‐ω<sub>C</sub>) + Q<sub>C</sub> × ω<sub>C</sub> = 130 × 0.71 + 82 × 0.29 = 116.08 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Q<sub>TiO2</sub>: the practical capacity of pure NTMF; Q<sub>C</sub>: the practical capacity of nanoporous carbon microfibers, see Figure S5 in the Supporting Information). It is noteworthy that the sustainable capacity (167 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) of NTMF‐C is much higher than the estimated practical capacity, which means the introduction of C accounts for the enhancement of electrochemical activity of TiO<sub>2</sub> with Na<sup>+</sup>. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0004"/>b, with the aid of nanoporosity and carbon incorporation, the specific capacity of NTMF‐C is superior at the various current rates compared with STMF and NTMF‐C. Both the NTMF‐C and the NTMF electrodes show an obvious self‐improving phenomenon when initially cycled at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and can reach a capacity of ≈120 and 150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Clearly, the NTMF‐C electrode shows slower capacity decay when the current rates increase. The retained capacity at 1000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> is 71 and 50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for NTMF‐C and NTMF, corresponding to capacity retention of 47% and 41.6%, respectively. With the current density back to 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, an impressive reversible capacity of ≈180 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> is restored for NTMF‐C electrode, in contrast to ≈150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for NTMF electrode. The STMF, however, delivers negligible capacity at high current rates. In conclusion, NTMF‐C exhibits the best Na‐storage properties in term of reversible capacity and rate capability, indicating the effective functions of the nanoporous structure and C incorporation toward maximizing the electrochemical reactions between TiO<sub>2</sub> and Na<sup>+</sup>. The Na‐storage mechanism of TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers is investigated via charge/discharge curves and ex situ XRD of NTMF‐C electrode after 500 cycles, since the STMF and NTMF show similar charge/discharge profiles to NTMF‐C (Figure S6, Supporting Information). As shown in Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0004"/>c, with TiO<sub>2</sub> phase transition over cycling, the electrode could rapidly reach high and stable capacity with slight‐plateau profiles at both charge and discharge state, which corresponds to the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction coupled with Ti<sup>4+</sup>/<sup>3+</sup> redox reaction, similar to that rutile TiO<sub>2</sub> and nanostructured rutile studied in LIBs.<link href="#advs140-bib-0031 #advs140-bib-0032"/> Moreover, in contrast to the pristine NTMF‐C electrode, four conspicuous peaks occur at 36.05°, 39.1°, 54.3°, and 56.5°, which can be indexed to be rutile TiO<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS No. 21‐1276). These results prove that the amorphous TiO<sub>2</sub> is able to transit into rutile phase after repeated cycles, which is responsible for the enhanced capacity and rate capability.</p><figure xml:id="advs140-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs140:advs140-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs140-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Cycling performance, b) rate capability of NTMF‐C, NTMF, and STMF; c) charge/discharge curves at selected cycles of NTMF‐C, and d) ex situ XRD of NTMF‐C before and after 500 cycles.</caption></figure><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs140-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a, the inferior performance of STMF is mostly due to the limited ion accessibility and sluggish Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion through the bulk fibers. When nanopores are created in the TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers, the obtained NTMF is expected to allow high concentrations of Na<sup>+</sup> ions to be absorbed in the nanopores and efficiently initiate the phase transition reaction. The superior electrochemical performance of NTMF‐C is partially ascribed to the high degree of amorphization, which leads to rapid occurrence of the self‐improving process. On the other hand, the presence of a huge density of nanopores could provide a high amount of active TiO<sub>2</sub> surface, a large TiO<sub>2</sub>/electrolyte contact area, and short diffusion paths for electron and ions. This nanoporous structure, therefore, facilitates the transport of ions to the TiO<sub>2</sub> host. Furthermore, large‐diameter pores can accommodate a large volume of electrolyte, through which abundant Na ions can be used to establish the critical ion concentration that supports electrochemical cycling. Thus, the availability of ions near the TiO<sub>2</sub> host is very much responsible for the observed improvement of the capacity over cycling. In addition, the enhanced conductivity of the electrode can be attributed to the residual C, which guarantees fast electron and sodium ion transportation as well.</p><figure xml:id="advs140-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs140:advs140-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs140-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of ion accessibility for the solid microfibers and nanoporous microfibers during sodiation/desodiation processes. b) Impedance plots for the three anodes after rate capability testing at frequencies from 100 kHz to 10 mHz. c) Fitting lines for the real part of the complex impedance versus ω<sup>−1/2</sup> at 25 °C.</caption></figure><p>In order to confirm the enhanced kinetic processes of the NTMF‐C, electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) of all samples was conducted after rate capability testing at 25 °C. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0005"/>b, the Nyquist plots of the three electrodes were collected from 0.1 mHz to 1 MHz. All the impedance curves show typical semicircles in the high to medium frequency region, which are associated with the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) resistance (<i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub>) and the charge transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>). According to the fitting results, the NTMF‐C electrode has a very low <i>R</i><sub>SEI</sub> of 21.3 Ω, in sharp contrast to 376.2 Ω and 427.8 Ω for the NTMF and STMF electrodes, respectively, indicating that a favorable SEI film was formed in the NTMF‐C electrode. It is believed that <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> is affected by the sodium ion transport and the conductivity of the electrodes. Similarly, the NTMF‐C electrode shows a low <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> (176.6 Ω) as well, which is 530 and 634 Ω for the NTMF and STMF electrodes, respectively. The significantly low <i>R</i><sub>ct</sub> value of the hybrid implies fast kinetic processes, consistent with the superior electrochemical performance in Figure <link href="#advs140-fig-0004"/>. The obtained microfibers demonstrate interesting and outstanding electrochemical performance, undergoing self‐improving and sustainable processes. The TiO<sub>2</sub>‐C electrode delivers a capacity increase of ≈50 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 20 cycles, retaining capacity of ≈167 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 450 cycles at current density of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, and a capacity of ≈71 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at the high current rate of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, with evident self‐improving capacity when cycled back to the initial low rate. The nanoporous structure with thin inner walls is able to realize high ion accessibility and fast ion transport. The incorporation of carbon can guarantee fast ion transport. The enhanced performance confirms the essential functions of the electrode configuration. This unique strategy for nanoporous microfiber construction is universally applicable and easy to implement, and it is expected to be extended to other electrode materials, especially transition metal oxides.</p></section><section xml:id="advs140-sec-0040"><label role="interim">4</label><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><p><i>Preparation of Nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub>‐Carbon Microfibers, Nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub> Microfibers, and Solid TiO<sub>2</sub> Microfibers</i>: 0.5 g polyvinyl pyrrolidone (PVP, Mw ≈ 1 300 000) and 0.25 g hexadecyl trimethyl ammonium bromide (CTAB) were first dissolved in a mixture of 0.5 g acetic acid and 6.5 g ethanol. Then, 3.0 g tetrabutyl titanate (Ti(OBu)<sub>4</sub> was added slowly, followed by stirring for 2 h. Afterward, 1.5 g paraffin oil was further added to form a homogeneous and transparent microemulsion after stirring for another 2 h.<sup>32</sup> The obtained microemulsion was loaded into a 5 mL syringe with a blunt‐ended metallic needle. A syringe pump was used to control the flow rate of the jetting solution (0.6–1 mL h<sup>−1</sup>). The needle was connected to a high voltage power supply, and a stainless steel plate covered by a piece of aluminium foil was used as the collector. The working distance between the needle tip and the collector was 20 cm, and the voltage was 15–25 kV. Finally, the TiO<sub>2</sub>‐carbon microfibers (NTMF‐C) and nanoporous TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers (NTMF) were obtained after calcination at 500 °C in Ar and O<sub>2</sub> atmosphere for 2 h with a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. For comparison, solid TiO<sub>2</sub> microfibers (STMF) were obtained by a similar electrospinning process. The electrospinning solution was prepared as follows. 1 g PVP was dissolved in a mixture of 0.5 g acetic acid and 5 g ethanol, followed by adding 3.0 g Ti(OBu)<sub>4</sub> and stirring for 2 h.</p><p><i>Structural Characterizations</i>: The morphologies of the samples were investigated by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (SEM, JEOL JSM‐7500) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEOL ARM‐200F) operated at an acceleration voltage of 200 kV. The porosity of the NTMF‐C and NTMF samples was measured by nitrogen sorption isotherms at 77 K under relative pressure (<i>P</i>/<i>P</i><sub>0</sub>) from 0.04 to 0.2 with a Micromeritics Tristar 3020 analyzer (USA). Before measurements, the samples were degassed in vacuum at 180 °C for at least 6 h. The BET method was utilized to calculate the surface areas. The crystal structure and phase of products were characterized using powder X‐ray diffraction (XRD; GBC MMA diffractometer) with Cu Kα radiation at a scan rate of 2 min<sup>−1</sup>. The thermal decomposition behavior of the products was monitored by using by using thermogravimetric analysis (TGA, PerkinElmer TG/DTA 6300) from 30 to 900 °C in air with a heating rate of 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>.</p><p><i>Electrochemical Measurements</i>: Measurements of the sodium‐storage properties were conducted on coin‐type half cells assembled in an argon‐filled glove box. The electrode slurry was prepared by mixing 70 wt% active material (NTMF‐C, NTMF, and STMF), 20 wt% Super P, and 10 wt% carboxymethyl cellulose (CMC) in a planetary mixer (KK‐250S). The electrode films were prepared by pasting the slurry on copper foil using a doctor blade in a thickness of 100 μm, followed by drying in a vacuum oven at 80 °C overnight. The working electrodes were obtained by punching the above electrode film into discs 0.96 cm in diameter. Sodium foil was used as both reference and counter electrode. The electrodes were separated by glass fiber separators. The electrolyte was prepared with 1.0 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in 1:1 (volume ratio) ethylene carbonate (EC)/propylene carbonate (PC) with 5 wt% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) additive. The electrochemical performances were investigated on a Land Battery Tester with a cut‐off voltage range from 0. 1 to 2.0 V (vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). Impedance testing was performed using a Biologic VMP‐3 electrochemical workstation.</p></section><section xml:id="advs140-sec-0050" numbered="no" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>The authors appreciate the financial support from the 863 Program (Grant No. 2013AA032203), the National Natural Science Foundation of China (NSFC) (Grant Nos. 21374001, 21134003, 21433012, and 21222309), the 973 Program (Grant No. 2012CB933200), the Program for New Century Excellent Talents in University of China, the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities, and the China Scholarship Council (Grant No. 201406025059).
