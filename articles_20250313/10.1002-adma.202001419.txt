Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Sodium‐ion batteries have gained much attention for their potential application in large‐scale stationary energy storage due to the low cost and abundant sodium sources in the earth. However, the electrochemical performance of sodium‐ion full cells (SIFCs) suffers severely from the irreversible consumption of sodium ions of cathode during the solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) formation of hard carbon anode. Here, a high‐efficiency cathode sodiation compensation reagent, sodium oxalate (Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), which possesses both a high theoretical capacity of 400 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and a capacity utilization as high as 99%, is proposed. The implementation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> as sacrificial sodium species is successfully realized by decreasing its oxidation potential from 4.41 to 3.97 V through tuning conductive additives with different physicochemical features, and the corresponding mechanism of oxidation potential manipulation is analyzed. Electrochemical results show that in the full cell based on a hard carbon anode and a P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Ti<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode with Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> as a sodium reservoir to compensate for sodium loss during SEI formation, the capacity retention is increased from 63% to 85% after 200 cycles and the energy density is improved from 129.2 to 172.6 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>. This work can provide a new avenue for accelerating the development of SIFCs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202001419-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The development of sodium‐ion batteries has been hindered so far by the irreversible consumption of sodium ions of the cathode during the solid electrolyte interphase formation. Therefore, in search of a safe, cost‐effective, and highly efficient cathode sodiation reagent, the feasibility of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> as a sodium reservoir source for enhancing the performance of sodium‐ion batteries is investigated.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202001419-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202001419-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202001419-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>As promising candidates in large‐scale energy storage, sodium‐ion batteries have gained intense attention from either scientific institutes or companies due to the easy availability and abundance of Na resource.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Much progress has been achieved to obtain advanced electrode materials and electrolyte toward sodium‐ion full cells (SIFCs).<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the further development of SIFCs suffers from one critical issue that during the initial charge process the formation of passivating solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) layer on the surface of anode materials (e.g., hard carbon) could irreversibly consume partial sodium ions from the cathode materials. This initial sodium uptake and removing to anode electrode incurs the low Coulombic efficiency and in turn seriously impedes the improvement of capacity, energy density, and cycle stability of SIFCs.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A variety of strategies including benign interface construction (e.g., surface coating to inhibit interfacial reactions<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup>), electrolyte optimization (e.g., introduction of additives to form a stable interface<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup>), and sodium compensation (e.g., addition of sodium powder in anode electrode to compensate for the irreversible loss of sodium ions for SEI formation<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) have been proposed to address these issues. Among them, the sodium compensation strategy is favored more and more because of its simple operation and easily scalable feature.</p><p>Apart from lab‐level pre‐sodiation methods either electrochemistry or direct sodium foil contact electrode,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> there are mainly three ways that are widely accepted for the perspective of further industrialization. One is anode pre‐sodiation method, in which sodium or lithium powder is added to the anode electrode;<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0006 #adma202001419-bib-0007"><sup>6,7</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the second is cathode sodium‐rich method, milling the cathode active material with metallic sodium to obtain a sodium‐rich material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> A typical example is the ball milling of Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> with metallic sodium to change its structure to Na<sub>4</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3</sub> under inert Ar‐filled protection. Compared with the above two methods, the third one, namely cathode self‐sacrificial additive method, has gained much attention from its easy operation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0008 #adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>8,9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> To date, less than ten compounds have been reported for self‐sacrificial sodiation reagents including NaN<sub>3</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>9d,f</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>3</sub>P,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>NiO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>9e</sup></link><sup>]</sup> NaCrO<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>9g</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>9b</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>9c</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and NaNO<sub>2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0009"><sup>9a</sup></link><sup>]</sup> An ideal self‐sacrificial sodiation reagent for SIFCs should possess the following characteristics: environmentally friendly, nontoxic, non‐explosive, nonflammable, high capacity, high capacity utilization, moderate working voltage, low cost, and easy operation. Note that high capacity and high capacity utilization could guarantee the small amount of additives used. However, obtaining a sodiation reagent with all these features is still a huge challenge. For instance, the theoretical capacity of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> can reach 505 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, but its actual capacity is only close to 100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, that is, its capacity utilization is less than 20%. Similarly, the capacity utilizations of Na<sub>2</sub>NiO<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub> are only about 40% and 70%, respectively. In terms of capacity utilization alone, NaCrO<sub>2</sub> is currently the best level, close to about 93%, but its low theoretical capacity (≈250 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>) and toxicity limit its further application. Considering the abovementioned issues, much effort should be paid to explore the safe and high‐performance self‐sacrificial sodiation reagents and hence to promote the development of sodium storage industry.</p><p>Inspired by the structure and reaction characteristics of Na<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub> and Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>4</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, we think that exploring compounds with increased sodium content by reducing carbon content might be an effective way to realize the above ideal. Based on this consideration, we disclosed a green, environmentally friendly, low cost, and highly efficient self‐sacrificial sodiation reagent, sodium oxalate (Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>), and systematically analyzed its sodium compensation feasibility. Electrochemical test results show that the capacity utilization of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is as high as 99% or more (theoretical capacity of 400 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>), and its introduction does not cause any side effects on the electrochemical performance of the cathode. Furthermore, the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> can be significantly reduced from 4.41 to 3.97 V by matching with an appropriate conductive additive. The combined analysis of scanning electron microscopy and electrochemical techniques reveals that when a conductive carbon additive with a high specific surface area is introduced, the as‐formed uniform distribution of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> in cathode and more contact sites with conductive carbon could significantly decrease the charge transfer and polarization resistance and hence decrease the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. And the implementation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> significantly enhances the electrochemical performance of SIFCs in terms of capacity, cycle stability, and energy density, demonstrating its promising application prospects.</p><p>Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> consists of an oxalate anion riveted with two sodium ions (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, left inset). Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) shows that the thermal decomposition temperature of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is close to 500 °C (Figure S1, Supporting Information), suggesting its high thermal stability in battery applications. Commercial Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> has a particle size of the order of tens of micrometers estimated from field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM) (Figure S2, Supporting Information). Given its lower electronic conductivity (as low as 10<sup>−10</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>), reducing particle size is necessary to fully exploit its electrochemical performance. Therefore, it was subjected to particle‐downsizing treatment according to the difference of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> solubility in ethanol and water. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0001">1a</link>, typical X‐ray diffraction (XRD) peaks of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> are clearly observed and no impurity is found after the recrystallization process. Significantly, its particle size was uniformly reduced to less than 10 microns (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0001">1a</link>, right inset). On this basis, the electrochemical properties of the active substance Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> in the voltage window between 2 and 4.6 V were studied using traditional super P carbon black (SP) as conductive additive. At a current density of 0.2 C (18 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> can deliver an initial charge specific capacity of 394.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and a discharge capacity of only 7.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0001">1b</link>). In the subsequent charge and discharge process, substantially no capacity contribution occurs, that is, its initial capacity utilization is as high as 98.6%, indicating that it is feasible as a sodium compensation additive. However, the average working potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> reaches about 4.41 V, which is much higher than its theoretical oxidation potential (1.974 V calculated from the thermodynamic data in Table S1, Supporting Information; Equation (<link href="#adma202001419-disp-0001">1</link>)) and the operation potential of state‐of‐the‐art layered oxide cathode material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup><displayedItem xml:id="adma202001419-disp-0001" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma202001419-math-0001.png"><mrow><mn>2</mn><msup><mrow><mtext>Na</mtext></mrow><mo>+</mo></msup><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mtext> </mtext><mn>2</mn><msup><mtext>e</mtext><mo>−</mo></msup><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mtext> </mtext><mn>2</mn><msub><mrow><mtext>CO</mtext></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub><mo>↔</mo><msub><mrow><mtext>Na</mtext></mrow><mn>2</mn></msub><msub><mtext>C</mtext><mn>2</mn></msub><msub><mtext>O</mtext><mn>4</mn></msub><mtext> </mtext><mi>E</mi><mtext> </mtext><mo>=</mo><mtext> </mtext><mn>1.974</mn><mtext> </mtext><mtext>V</mtext></mrow></math></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="adma202001419-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202001419-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) XRD pattern of recrystallized Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (denoted as rc‐Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, insets are its corresponding crystal structure and FESEM image). b) Electrochemical characteristics of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> in traditional conductive additive SP system at 0.2 C with a potential window of 2–4.6 V.</caption></figure><p>Therefore, reducing this high overpotential has become the key point in promoting its practical application. By optimizing different types of conductive additives, the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is further reduced (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link>). Specifically, when short multi‐walled carbon nanotubes (s‐MWCNTs) are used as conductive additives, the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is reduced to 4.33 V. And when Ketjenblack (KB) and ordered mesoporous carbon (CMK‐3) are used as conductive additives, the corresponding potentials can be significantly reduced to 4.11 and 3.97 V, respectively (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0002">2a</link>, inset). In order to clarify the reduction mechanism of the oxidation potential, the morphology of the corresponding electrodes (Figure S3, Supporting Information) and elemental distributions (Figure S4, Supporting Information) as well as the specific surface area of the conductive additives (Figure S5, Supporting Information) are systematically characterized. Obviously, in the SP system, most Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> particles are piled up (Figure S3a, Supporting Information), and the element distribution of sodium is relatively concentrated (Figure S4a, Supporting Information). In sharp contrast, the particle dispersibility (Figure S3c, Supporting Information) and elemental distribution (Figure S4c, Supporting Information) of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> are very uniform in the KB system. Furthermore, Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> particles disperse much more uniformly in the CMK‐3 system, and less Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> aggregates can be observed on the surface of the electrode, meaning that the Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> particles are uniformly immersed in CMK‐3 particles (Figures S3d and S4d, Supporting Information). The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) results show that as the specific surface area of the conductive additive increases, the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> gradually decreases (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0002">2b</link>). In addition, as the amount of conductive additive increases, the oxidation potential decreases (Figure S6, Supporting Information). For example, when the mass ratio of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to carbon additive is 8, the observed oxidation potential is 4.38 V. When decreasing the ratio to 2, the corresponding oxidation potential can be reduced to 4.15 V, and this modest potential plateau is suitable for conventional carbonate electrolyte of SIFCs. In order to understand the abovementioned attractive results, the SP and KB systems were explored in combination with galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (Figure S7, Supporting Information and Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0002">2c</link>) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0002">2d</link>) techniques. Specifically, the KB system exhibits a smaller polarization potential (0.48 vs 0.85 V) and a smaller charge transfer impedance than the SP system. It can be qualitatively seen that the sodium‐ion diffusion kinetics of the two systems have almost no discernible difference from the low frequency region of the Bode plots (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0002">2d</link>, inset). All of these results further emphasize that charge transfer is a speed control step during the oxidation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> with low electronic conductivity, and the good dispersibility of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and more contact sites with conductive carbon is responsible for the potential reduction, which is consistent with the catalytic mechanism of carbon materials used to improve energy efficiency in Li‐air batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202001419-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> This suggests that the use of doped carbon or non‐carbon‐based catalysts might be an effective strategy to further reduce the oxidation potential of such inert additives. For example, as adding a certain amount of commercial RuO<sub>2</sub> particles (Figure S8a, Supporting Information) to the KB system can further reduce the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to about 3.98 V (Figure S8b, Supporting Information). In order to exclude the possible influence of electrolyte to lowering the oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, a series of control experiments of electrolyte decomposition have also been explored. Whether in a pure electrolyte system (Figure S9a,b, Supporting Information) or in systems with different amounts of conductive additives (Figure S9c, Supporting Information), the decomposition of the electrolyte occurs above 4.6 V, indicating that the used electrolyte is stable within the selected potential window from 2.5 to 4.5 V. Although CMK‐3 can induce more potential drops, we only used KB as a conductive additive in the following experiments, mainly because KB itself has been used as a conductive agent for batteries.</p><figure xml:id="adma202001419-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202001419-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Effect of different conductive additives on electrochemical oxidation potential of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> (inset show the corresponding dQ/dV curves, the ratio of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> to conductive additive is 5.5:3.5 in all systems). b) Relationship between oxidation potential and specific surface area of conductive additives. c) Comparison of polarization overpotential of two electrochemical systems (SP and KB). d) Comparison of the charging impedance (Nyquist plots) of the first charge cycle in the SP and KB systems (inset shows the corresponding Bode plots).</caption></figure><p>To gain insight into the evolution of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> in the electrode, the morphologies of different electrodes before and after the electrochemical cycles were compared. In electrodes with only Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and KB, it is apparent that Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> particles are embedded in the conductive additive before charging (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link>‐<link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">d</link>), and many pores can be seen in either the top view or the cross‐section after cycles (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">3e</link>‐<link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">h</link>), meaning that the oxidation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is accompanied by gas generation. In addition, the surface of the P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Ti<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (NMT, Figure S10, Supporting Information) cathode without Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is smooth (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">3i,j</link>) and there is no significant change in surface roughness after cycle (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">3k,l</link>). When Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is added to the NMT cathode, the surface of the resulting cathode becomes extremely rough (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">3m,n</link>). Considering the larger particle size of the cathode material (Figure S10, Supporting Information, inset), those smaller particles can be attributed to Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. After the cycle, the roughness of the electrode is significantly reduced with the formation of pores (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0003">3o,p</link>), further indicating that only gaseous species are produced from the oxidation process of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="adma202001419-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202001419-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Analysis of electrochemical decomposition characteristics of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive by FESEM. a,b) Surface and c,d) sectional images of (Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + KB) electrode before cycles. e,f) Surface and g,h) sectional images of (Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + KB) electrode after cycles. i–l) Surface images of (NMT + KB) electrode before (i,j) and after (k,l) cycles. m–p) Surface images of (NMT + Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> + KB) electrode before (m,n) and after (o,p) cycles.</caption></figure><p>It should be emphasized that no matter what kinds of additives are intended to serve in the electrode, their addition should not deteriorate the electrochemical performance of the electrode materials. Therefore, the electrochemical performance of the cathode electrode containing Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive in the half‐cell configuration was first investigated. At a current density of 0.2 C, the initial charge specific capacity of the cathode without Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive is 120 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>), while that with 20 wt% Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> added (equivalent to 20.3 vol%, detailed volume percentage calculations are presented in Tables S2 and S3, Supporting Information) reaches 239 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0004">4b</link>). The extra capacity is mainly due to the contribution of oxidation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> at 4.25 V, and there is no significant difference between their subsequent charge–discharge curves (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0004">4c</link>). Fortunately, the addition of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> does not cause any deterioration of the cathode material in terms of cycle stability (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0004">4d</link> and Figure S11, Supporting Information) and rate capability (Figure S12, Supporting Information). For example, the electrodes without Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> can deliver specific capacities of 98 and 70 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 0.5 and 5 C, respectively, while the corresponding discharge capacity can still be maintained at 99 and 74 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after the addition of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>. When the amount of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive is only 10 wt% (equivalent to 10.1 vol%), the initial charge specific capacity is close to 180 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S13a, Supporting Information), which means that every 10 wt% of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> will provide an additional specific capacity of about 60 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> for the entire battery system. It is worth mentioning that when Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is applied to the actual battery system, its oxidation potential is somewhat higher, which is mainly due to the fact that the presence of the cathode material particles will decrease the contact between Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> and the conductive additive to a certain extent. And we further extend the cut‐off potential from 4.4 to 4.5 V for the initial two cycles (Figure S13b, Supporting Information) and then recover to 4.4 V in the subsequent cycles. No obvious deterioration of electrochemical performance is observed. These results indicate the feasibility of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> as a self‐sacrificial sodiation reagent.</p><figure xml:id="adma202001419-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202001419-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Comparison of electrochemical performance of half‐cells without or with (20 wt%) Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive. a,b) Charge–discharge profiles at 0.2 C. c) Differential capacity curves of the first cycle. d) Cycling stability at 0.2 C.</caption></figure><p>In order to evidence the above concept, the performance of SIFCs based on a hard carbon anode and an NMT cathode were investigated. The phase, morphology, and electrochemical properties of the hard carbon anode are shown in Figures S14 and S15, Supporting Information. It has an initial Coulombic efficiency of 76% at a current density of 0.2 C and output a reversible specific capacity of ≈280 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> with good cycle stability. In the full‐cell configuration, the initial discharge specific capacity of the full cell without Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive is only 77 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, and rapidly decreases to 62 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after the second cycle at 0.2 C (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>). In contrast, the cell with Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive (10 wt%) has an initial discharge specific capacity of up to 99 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and remains above 90 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (54.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> based on the total mass of cathode and anode, Figure S16a, Supporting Information) after the second cycle (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0005">5a</link>). After 50 cycles, the capacity retention of the cell with additive is as high as 90%, while that of the cell without additive is only 45% (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0005">5b</link>). Obviously, the full cell containing Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive has a slightly higher and stabler Coulombic efficiency than that without Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive (Figure S17, Supporting Information). When the current density is increased to 1 C, the discharge capacity of the cell with additive can still reach 89 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0005">5c</link>, 51.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> based on the total mass of cathode and anode, Figure S16b, Supporting Information), the corresponding average voltage is 3.33 V (Figure S18, Supporting Information). After 200 cycles, its capacity retention (approaching 85%) is 1.3 times higher than that without additive (66.3%, Figure S19, Supporting Information). Significantly, the introduction of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> not only improves the cycle stability of the cell, but also greatly increases its specific capacity, thus leading to the marked increases of gravimetric energy density of the full cell from 161.5 to 215.9 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> based on the total mass of cathode and anode active materials at 1 C (Figure <link href="#adma202001419-fig-0005">5d</link>). Even considering other components including conductive additive, binder, it can effectively increase the gravimetric energy density from 129.2 to 172.6 W h kg<sup>−1</sup>, the corresponding volumetric energy density can be significantly increased from 129.4 without additive to 173.7 W h L<sup>−1</sup> (Table S4, Supporting Information). To further increase the volumetric energy density, it is worth trying to increase the electrode ratio or the application of multiple conductive additives (Figure S20 and Table S5, Supporting Information, more discussion can be found in the Supporting Information). Compared with the state of the art of sodiation additives, Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> possesses prominent advantages in terms of high capacity, high utilization, safety, environmental friendly, and easy operation (Table S6, Supporting Information). Note that the oxidation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> during charging process would generate gas and leave more or less voids on electrode. Therefore, the amount, adding procedure, and particle size of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> needs to be further optimized. In addition, the adoption of conductive additives with a high specific surface area may incur the high‐voltage instability of the electrolyte. Thus, a series of evaluations on the types of conductive additives or electrolytes should be conducted in actual operation.</p><figure xml:id="adma202001419-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202001419:adma202001419-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202001419-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Comparison of electrochemical performance of full cells with (10 wt%) or without Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> additive. a) Charge–discharge profiles at 0.2 C with a voltage window of 2–4.3 V. b,c) Cycling stability at 0.2 (b) and 1 C (c), respectively. d) Energy density comparison based on the total mass of the anode and cathode active materials or the total mass of the two electrodes at 1 C.</caption></figure><p>In summary, we reported a green, high‐capacity, highly effective cathode sodium compensation additive, that is, Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub>, which was successfully used in SIFCs for the first time to achieve the prominent enhancement in terms of cycle stability, capacity, and energy density. More importantly, the large oxidation overpotential (2.44 V) of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>2</sub>O<sub>4</sub> is significantly reduced by adopting conductive additives with large specific surface area. As a prospect, we believed that this sodium compensation strategy could offer an avenue to motivate the development of high‐performance SIFCs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma202001419-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>Y.‐B.N. and Y.‐J.G. contributed equally to this work.
