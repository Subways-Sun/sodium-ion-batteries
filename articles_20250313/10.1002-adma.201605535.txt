Due to their improved safety, polyanion framework compounds have recently gained attention as potential candidates. With the earth‐abundant element Fe being the redox center, the uniform carbon‐coated Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C composite represents a promising alternative for sodium‐ion batteries. The electrochemical results show that the as‐prepared Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C composite can deliver capacity of ≈100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C (1 C = 120 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), with capacity retention of 92.3% at 0.5 C after 300 cycles. After adding fluoroethylene carbonate additive to the electrolyte, 89.6% of the initial capacity is maintained, even after 1100 cycles at 5 C. The electrochemical mechanism is systematically investigated via both in situ synchrotron X‐ray diffraction and density functional theory calculations. The results show that the sodiation and desodiation are single‐phase‐transition processes with two 1D sodium paths, which facilitates fast ionic diffusion. A small volume change, nearly 100% first‐cycle Coulombic efficiency, and a pseudocapacitance contribution are also demonstrated. This research indicates that this new compound could be a potential competitor for other iron‐based cathode electrodes for application in large‐scale Na rechargeable batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en" xml:id="adma201605535-abs-0001"><p><b>A cheap cathode material for sodium‐ion batteries</b> is synthesized via a facile ball‐milling process. With the earth‐abundant Fe being the redox center, the uniform carbon‐coated Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C composite can deliver ≈100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 3.05 V reversibly with excellent cycling stability up to 1100 cycles with a capacity retention of 89.6%. Investigation of the structural evolution and kinetics is carried out.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605535:adma201605535-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605535-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201605535-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201605535-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>Storing energy from sustainable intermittent resources, such as wind energy and solar energy, has become a significant issue in recent years that needs to be resolved. Although lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have achieved great success in small portable electronics, electric vehicles, and hybrid electric vehicles, there are still many challenges to their real application in large‐scale energy‐storage systems.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0001"/> From cost considerations, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are an attractive alternative to lithium‐ion batteries, especially due to the high abundance of sodium resources.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0002"/> Nevertheless, the low energy density, sluggish sodium kinetics, and poor cycling stability of the current positive materials are the main barriers to the widespread use of SIBs.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0003 #adma201605535-bib-0004"/></p><p>High‐energy and low‐cost cathode materials are the focus of the current research. Generally speaking, there are three major types of cathode materials for sodium‐ion batteries. One is sodium metal oxides (NaMO<sub>2</sub>, including <i>O3</i>‐type and <i>P2</i>‐type, M = Fe, Cr, Mn, Co, Ni, etc.) which have been intensively reported for applications as electrodes recently.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0005"/> Nevertheless, these layered‐structure materials still suffer from poor cycling stability with large volume changes (&gt;10%) and some are even not stable when just contact with moisture.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0004"/> Another promising type is Prussian blue analogues (PBAs), such as Na<sub>1+</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>M<sub>1</sub>M<sub>2</sub>(CN)<sub>6</sub> (<i>x</i> = 1 to 2, M = Fe, Mn, etc.), which have shown good electrochemical properties.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0006"/> The problem for Prussian blue would be the low tap density and temperature instability which would be a potential safety issue, however.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0007"/> Finally, the polyanion‐based compounds have received wider attention recently due to their high structural stability and thermal stability.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0008 #adma201605535-bib-0009"/> The strong covalent bonding of XO (X = P, S, B, Si) can offer great structural stability during long electrochemical cycling, with only small volume changes.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0010"/> This has led to the wide commercial use of olivine lithium iron phosphate, LiFePO<sub>4</sub>, which has iron as the redox center. The applications of polyanion‐based compounds could significantly further reduce energy cost and safety concerns. To date, the search for novel polyanion compounds has concentrated on the iron‐ and vanadium‐based materials, such as Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0011"/> NaFePO<sub>4</sub>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0012"/> Na<sub>2</sub>FeP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0009"/> Na<sub>4</sub>Fe<sub>3</sub>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>),<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0013"/> Na<sub>7</sub>V<sub>4</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>4</sub>PO<sub>4</sub>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0010 #adma201605535-bib-0014"/> Na<sub>2</sub>Fe<sub>2</sub>(SO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>3</sub>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0015 #adma201605535-bib-0016"/> Na<sub>3</sub>V<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>(PO<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>F<sub>3−2</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0017 #adma201605535-bib-0018"/> Na<sub>2</sub>MnP<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0019"/> Na<sub>2</sub>FePO<sub>4</sub>F,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0020"/> and Na<sub>4−</sub><i><sub>α</sub></i>M<sub>2+</sub><i><sub>α</sub></i><sub>/2</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0007"/> Their open frameworks and sturdy structures provide facile sodium‐ion transport and long cycle life for applications in energy storage and conversion.</p><p>Recently, a newly reported iron‐based pyrophosphate sodium host series, Na<sub>4−α</sub>Fe<sub>2+</sub><i><sub>α</sub></i><sub>/2</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> (2/3 ≤ α ≤ 7/8), has aroused our great interest.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0007 #adma201605535-bib-0021 #adma201605535-bib-0022"/> This material has a triclinic structure with the space group <i>P</i>−1, where two FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedra, both symmetrically arranged, and two P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> units constitute the single crown unit. The P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub> group is composed of two PO<sub>4</sub> units with corner sharing, and the whole crystal framework is composed of a corner‐sharing 3D network of [Fe<sub>2</sub>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>22</sub>]<sub>∞</sub> and [Fe<sub>2</sub>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>20</sub>]<sub>∞</sub> infinity group layers parallel to the <i>bc</i> plane, which can provide a 3D framework for reversible sodium de‐/intercalation. Several previously reported studies only focus on the minimum amount of Na in Na<sub>4−</sub><i><sub>α</sub></i>Fe<sub>2+</sub><i><sub>α</sub></i><sub>/2</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> (2/3 ≤α ≤ 7/8), resulting in a formula of Na<sub>3.12</sub>Fe<sub>2.44</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0021 #adma201605535-bib-0022 #adma201605535-bib-0023"/> When α equals 2/3 (the maximum amount of Na possible), the compound's chemical formula can be written as Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>. A schematic illustration is presented in Figure S1 (Supporting Information). The theoretical capacity of Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> can reach 117.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> based on the Fe<sup>2+</sup>/Fe<sup>3+</sup> redox couple, which enables it to be a competitive candidate for the cathode electrode in SIBs.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0007"/> Studies of the cycling stability, kinetics, and reaction mechanisms of isostructural Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> compound are still lacking as yet. Hence, it is urgent and necessary to achieve a deep understanding of both the kinetics and the reaction mechanism for better guidance toward the application of this material.</p><p>Here, we successfully synthesized uniform carbon‐coated Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles by an in situ carbon‐coating method with satisfactory high‐rate capacity and long cycling stability. Based on density functional theory (DFT), first‐principles calculations were performed to investigate the sodium diffusion pathways in Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>, and they revealed that sodium ions diffuse via two 1D pathways.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0024"/> In situ synchrotron X‐ray diffraction (XRD) revealed the single‐phase‐transition process during charge and discharge (i.e., A phase to B phase, with no intermediate C phase), accompanied by the reversible variation of lattice parameters.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0025"/> The results indicate that this iron‐based pyrophosphate Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material is a promising cathode candidate for SIBs.</p><p>The bulk materials with/without carbon coating are denoted as NFP/C and NFP, respectively. Figure S2 (Supporting Information) shows a weight loss of 92.87% for NFP/C, indicating the carbon mass ratio in NFP/C. The Raman spectra in Figure S3b (Supporting Information) also indicate the presence of the carbon‐coating layer of NFP/C with obvious D and G bands located at 1350 and 1600 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0026"/> Note that almost no specific peaks of Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> can be found, which is largely due to the fluorescence effect in Raman spectroscopy.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0027"/> The Cu K<sub>α</sub> XRD patterns (λ = 1.5406 Å) in Figure S3a (Supporting Information) show that both the NFP and NFP/C samples can be indexed to the triclinic <i>P</i>−1 space group (Joint Committee on Powder Diffraction Standards (JCPDS) No. 83‐0225). The higher intensity of NFP/C indicates its greater degree of crystallinity compared with NFP. In order to obtain more detailed information on the crystal structure and phase constitution, synchrotron X‐ray powder diffraction was employed, as displayed in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a,d. The crystal structure was confirmed to be triclinic <i>P</i>−1 for both the NFP and the NFP/C materials. The fitting process was satisfactory, with low reliability‐factors (weighted profile factors) of <i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> = 9.77% and 8.60% (<b>Table</b> <link href="#adma201605535-tbl-0001"><b>1</b></link>), and detailed structural information, including the atomic parameters of both samples, is summarized in Table S1 and S2 (Supporting Information). Tiny amounts (less than 1%) of impurities were detected in both samples (possibly NaFePO<sub>4</sub> or something similar). The data show that the Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> material is a solid solution phase with respect to transition‐metal composition.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0007"/> The morphologies of NFP and NFP/C are very different (Figure S4a,d, Supporting Information). The corresponding Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) and particle‐size distribution results of both samples are shown in Figure S3c,d (Supporting Information). The scanning electron microscopy–energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (SEM‐EDS) results in Figure S5 (Supporting Information) show the homogeneous elemental distributions of Na, Fe, P, O, and C. Also, from the high‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images (Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0001"/>b,e), the lattice fringes of both samples were measured to be 0.53 and 0.56 nm, corresponding to (−101) and (111) reflections and both samples displayed good crystallinity with space group <i>P</i>−1 (selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns from Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0001"/>c,f). In addition, the carbon‐coated material NFP/C shows a very uniform carbon‐coating layer (as illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0001"/>e), and the thickness of the carbon layer was measured to be around 6.0 nm. The uniform carbon layer plays a critical role in the improvement of electrochemical performance, which will be discussed later. The X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy spectra of Fe 2p for both samples are also displayed in Figure S4b,e (Supporting Information), and no obvious differences can be detected except for a tiny binding energy shift, which means that the valence of Fe in both samples is similar and estimated to be +2. The valence of Fe was also measured via the Mössbauer spectra, as shown in Figure S4c,f (Supporting Information). A small amount (less than 10%) of Fe<sup>3+</sup> was found for both samples (Table <link href="#adma201605535-tbl-0001"/>), which can be attributed to the slight oxidation during the measurements.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605535-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605535:adma201605535-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605535-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,d) Rietveld refinement of synchrotron XRD patterns of NFP (a) and NFP/C (d) materials (λ = 0.7741 Å). charge/discharge tests on the Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> electrodes were conducted using Na‐ion coin cells at room temperature. From Figure S6 (Supporting Information), it can be seen that the first cycle and second cycle of the electrodes are almost the same, with a main charge/discharge platform around 3.0 V and a small voltage plateau at about 2.5 V, which means that the electrochemical reaction of the Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> electrode was highly reversible. The NFP/C electrode can deliver a capacity of ≈100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> in the first and second discharge at 0.1 C (1 C = 120 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), about 88% of the given theoretical capacity (117.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>). In <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a, it is found that a satisfactory rate performance of NFP/C electrode can be obtained at 5 and 10 C with capacities of 72.3 and 68.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. After the first cycle, the charge‐transfer resistances (as shown in Figure S7, Supporting Information) were greatly reduced for both electrodes, which is possibly due to the formation of the solid electrolyte interphase layer.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0028"/> What should be specifically pointed out is that the first‐cycle Coulombic efficiency for both samples was above 96%, and the Coulombic efficiency for NFP/C further reached 100.14%. It is well known that the first‐cycle Coulombic efficiency is very important for the application of full‐cell Na‐ion batteries,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0029"/> which is also the main barrier for the anode material.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0026 #adma201605535-bib-0030"/> Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>c shows typical discharge curves at different C‐rates, with no evidence of severe electrochemical polarization. In addition, we compared several types of recently reported polyanionic‐based cathode materials with respect to their voltage plateau, discharge capacity, and energy density at 0.2 C,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0009 #adma201605535-bib-0010 #adma201605535-bib-0011 #adma201605535-bib-0012 #adma201605535-bib-0013 #adma201605535-bib-0019 #adma201605535-bib-0031"/> as presented in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>d. It can be seen that the as‐prepared Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> material (NFP/C) has an energy density of more than 300 W h kg<sup>−1</sup> with a relatively high voltage platform (3.05 V vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) and highly satisfactory cycling stability. The cycling stability at low current density (0.5 C, 60 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) was investigated as well. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>b, it can be seen that both samples displayed satisfactory cycling stability after the 300th cycle (92.1% and 92.3%) and nearly 100% Coulombic efficiency in each cycle. This can be ascribed to the nature of the polyanionic‐based cathode material, especially for the as‐prepared Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material in our work. The HRTEM images (insets in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>b) after the 300th cycle show two obvious lattice fringes, representing the (113) planes (<i>d</i> = 0.32 nm) for NFP and the (011) planes (<i>d</i> = 0.82 nm) for NFP/C, respectively. The carbon layer of NFP/C electrode could still be clearly observed after 300th cycle with a thickness of ≈5 nm. The SAED patterns of both the NFP and the NFP/C electrodes demonstrate the single‐crystal nature of Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> cathode material, even after 300 cycles (Figure S8a,b, Supporting Information). This also can be deduced from Figure S8c,d (Supporting Information) because there was no obvious decrease in the voltage platform with cycling, which means that the energy density also can be maintained during the long cycle life. Figure S9 (Supporting Information) shows the morphology of the electrodes at different times. Both the NFP and the NFP/C electrodes basically retained the same morphologies.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605535-fig-0002"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605535:adma201605535-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605535-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Rate performances of NFP and NFP/C. The inset in (a) is a comparison of the first‐cycle voltage profiles and the Coulombic efficiency of the two samples. b) Cycling stability and corresponding Coulombic efficiency of both NFP and NFP/C electrodes at 0.5 C (60 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) with 5% FEC additive in the electrolyte. The insets in (b) are HRTEM images taken after the 300th cycle for NFP (left) and NFP/C (right) electrodes. c) Voltage profiles at different C‐rates. d) Comparison of reversible capacity, energy density, and average voltage platform (vs Na<sup>+</sup>/Na) of several types of polyanionic‐based cathode materials at 0.2 C. e,f) Schematic diagrams explaining the difference in the rate performances of NFP and NFP/C, respectively. g) High‐rate long cycling stability test and corresponding Coulombic efficiency of NFP/C electrodes at 5 C (0.6 A g<sup>−1</sup>) with/without 5% FEC additive in the electrolyte.</caption></figure><p>The electrolyte was ethylene carbonate/propylene carbonate (EC/PC, 1:1 in volume) with 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub>, which has been proven to be more stable when the cell is charged at or over 4.0 V.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0032"/> The addition of FEC was set at 5% according to a previous study.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0033"/> We chose the carbon‐coated NFP/C sample for the long cycling stability testing due to its better performance, as illustrated above. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>g, the NFP/C material can deliver an initial capacity of 72.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at a discharge rate of 5 C (0.6 A g<sup>−1</sup>), and about 90% of the initial capacity was maintained, even after 1100 cycles. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C cathode material with capacity retention as good as 89.6% after 1100 cycles at 5 C has been reported. During the first 300–400 cycles, there was no obvious difference between the NFP/C electrodes with or without FEC additive. Nevertheless, the discharge capacity of the NFP/C electrode without 5% fluoroethylene carbonate (FEC) additive declined slightly faster after 400 cycles compared to the one with the 5% FEC additive added. In addition, from Figure S10a,b (Supporting Information), it can be seen that the voltage platform around 2.5 V at 5 C was well maintained for the NFP/C electrode with 5% FEC additive, while the one without FEC additive encountered slight decreases at the 500th cycle and the 1100th cycle. We suppose that protective surface films on the NFP/C cathode were formed in the PC‐based electrolyte solution, which was beneficial for the improved passivation and suppression of side reactions between the Na metal and the PC solution containing Na salts, especially for long cycling stability.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0028 #adma201605535-bib-0034"/> This speculation can be further confirmed via the scanning transmission electron microscopy–energy dispersive spectroscopy (STEM‐EDS) technique (Figure S10c,d, Supporting Information). The images of the cycled electrodes and separator also show that there was much less electrode dissolution in the electrolyte with 5% FEC added electrolyte (Figure S10f,h, Supporting Information). No obvious difference in the XRD results can be detected by the two electrodes (see Figure S11, Supporting Information). Nevertheless, the electrochemical polarization and the charge transfer resistance were significantly decreased (Figure S12, Supporting Information). So, based on the results illustrated above, it can be deduced that the FEC additive played an important role in the long cycling stability, with a passivation layer generated from the FEC additive during initial cycles for this polyanionic‐based cathode materials.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0035"/></p><p>The superior electrochemical performance of NFP/C can be explained via Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>e,f. It can be deduced that the uniform amorphous carbon layer dramatically increases the transfer rate of electrons in different layers of the bulk material, and the transfer speed of Na<sup>+</sup> ions can be accelerated simultaneously as well, although the particle sizes are one or several micrometers.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0023"/> Without the carbon coating, the electrons cannot be easily transferred to unconnected particles. Hence, it is very important to choose an appropriate carbon source (such as stearic acid) to obtain a uniform carbon layer and reduce the particle size via the ball‐milling process.</p><p>To obtain a deeper understanding of the charge/discharge process, we performed in situ synchrotron XRD measurements, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>. The structural evolution of the electrode material (NFP/C) during the first cycle was investigated (with the wavelength manually changed to 1.5406 Å for better comparison). Changes in the two peaks ((011) reflection and (−101) reflection) were detected, and the variations are marked by the black dashed lines. The two peaks gradually moved to lower/higher 2θ during Na extraction and returned to their original positions after reinsertion, which can be attributed to the continuous reversible lattice breathing during cycling. The general patterns remained unchanged, indicating topotactic Na extraction from the mother structure, which also gives evidence of the single‐phase‐transition process during cycling.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0013"/> The phase transition of NFP material is also detected and discussed in Figure S13 (Supporting Information). High‐angle annular dark‐field (HAADF) images of the pristine electrode and the electrode after the initial charge and discharge are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0003"/>c–e. The (101), (011), and (011) reflections were observed and measured to be 0.51, 0.82, and 0.83 nm, respectively, which implies that the Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material has a sturdy crystalline nature as a detected solid‐solution phase with respect to the transition‐metal composition. From Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0003"/>f,g, it can be clearly seen that all three lattice parameters have changed reversibly, corresponding to the variation in the detected peaks as well. In particular, the <i>b</i> value gradually decreased, while both the <i>a</i> and the <i>c</i> values continuously increased, and the unit cell volume changed reversibly in the range of 558–570.5 Å<sup>3</sup>. This phenomenon indicates that the pathways for Na<sup>+</sup> ions change continuously during cycling. The increasing <i>a</i> and <i>c</i> values are mainly ascribed to the lengthened bond distance of FeO in the FeO<sub>6</sub> octahedra after the Na extraction. The electron‐cloud density of the [Fe<sub>2</sub>P<sub>4</sub>O<sub>20</sub>]<sub>∞</sub> infinity group layers parallel to the <i>b</i> axis was enhanced owing to the decreased electrostatic repulsion during the Na extraction process, so the <i>b</i> value gradually declined during the discharge stage. It is worth noting that the electrode experienced a tiny volume contraction of about 2.19% (compared to the initial state), which is the smallest volume change according to previous reports.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0004 #adma201605535-bib-0010 #adma201605535-bib-0013"/> Such a small change in a single unit cell offers strong support for the stability of the sodium crystal structure during intercalation/deintercalation so that the excellent cycling stability can be achieved.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605535-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605535:adma201605535-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605535-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) In situ synchrotron XRD patterns of (011) and (−101) reflections (a) with corresponding initial‐cycle voltage curve (b). The wavelength was manually changed to 1.5406 Å for better comparison. c–e) Corresponding HAADF images at different states of charge. f) Changes in the lattice parameters <i>a</i>, <i>b</i>, and <i>c</i>; and g) changes in the unit cell volume.</caption></figure><p>We have further investigated the kinetics characteristics of NFP/C material. The charge‐storage mechanism of the cathode particles always includes nonfaradaic and faradaic processes.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0036 #adma201605535-bib-0037 #adma201605535-bib-0038"/> The latter can be found both in the bulk during the battery process and on the surface where they are regarded as contributing to the pseudocapacitance. The calculated results are shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>a (see Figure S14, Supporting Information, for details of the calculation). It shows that the electrochemical reactions of Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material are composed of both nonfaradaic and faradaic processes, as the <i>b</i> value is in the middle, between 0.5 and 1 for the selected five redox peaks. Since all the processes cannot be completed exactly simultaneously, nonfaradaic reaction is inevitable. The electric double layer mainly dominates the nonfaradaic process and capacitance is the primary manifestation form. The slower Na<sup>+</sup> intercalating process will incur larger contribution of capacitance to the total current. The contributions of nonfaradaic processes of NFP/C and NFP electrodes are calculated to be 29.37% and 28.39%, respectively, based on the area integral method (Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0004"/> and Figure S15c, Supporting Information).<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0036 #adma201605535-bib-0037 #adma201605535-bib-0038"/> The faradaic process enables the cathode material to operate at a fixed high‐voltage potential, and the establishment of pseudocapacitance leads to a fast Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion insertion/desertion process at a high rate with extended cycling life.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0038"/> This can be regarded as one key factor for the superior electrochemical performance illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0002"/>.</p><figure xml:id="adma201605535-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201605535:adma201605535-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201605535-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Cyclic voltammogram with the capacitive contribution to the total current shown via the shaded region (29.37%). b) GITT curves of NFP/C material for the charge and discharge process. The inset is the chemical diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> ions as a function of voltage calculated from the GITT curve. c) Activation energy values based on DFT calculations accompanied by schematic illustrations (d–f) of two Na<sup>+</sup>‐ion diffusion pathways in triclinic <i>P</i>−1 phase Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material.</caption></figure><p>The galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) was carried out to clarify the nature of sodium intercalation/deintercalation of Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C electrode as well as the diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> apart from the cyclic voltammogram method. From Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0004"/>b, it can be seen that both the charge and discharge curves can be ascribed to a single‐phase‐transition process. The whole reaction equation can be written as follows: <displayedItem type="mathematics" numbered="yes" xml:id="adma201605535-disp-0001"><mediaResource href="graphic/adma201605535-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201605535:adma201605535-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><p>The results are consistent with those presented via Nazar and co‐workers,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0007"/> where a single‐phase reaction mechanism was proposed. Based on the method illustrated in Figure S16 (Supporting Information), the diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> can also be calculated at different voltage stages.<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0039"/> The results are displayed in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0004"/>b (inset). It can be seen that the sodium‐ion chemical diffusion coefficients vary from 10<sup>−11</sup> to 10<sup>−13</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> in the voltage range of 2.3–4.0 V, which shows the same order of magnitude as the results obtained from cyclic voltammetry (Figure S14, Supporting Information), and the fast kinetics property would promote its application prospects in high‐power sodium battery systems.</p><p>In addition, the sodium‐diffusion pathways in Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C nanoparticles are shown in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0004"/>d–f based on density functional theory (DFT) calculation. We found that diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> ions preferentially proceeds via two very plausible 1D pathways: pathway 1 is approximately parallel to the <i>bc</i> plane (Na2 → Na3 → Na2) and pathway 2 approximately parallel to the <i>b</i> axis (Na1 → Na3 → Na1). Activation barrier energies for pathway 1 and pathway 2 were calculated to be ≈1.1 and ≈1.7 eV (Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0004"/>c), respectively, which are almost equivalent to a similar previous report on polyanion‐based compounds,<link href="#adma201605535-bib-0016"/> making Na diffusion quite fast at room temperature. It is worth noting that the decreasing <i>b</i> value of the lattice constant (as illustrated in Figure <link href="#adma201605535-fig-0003"/>f) would facilitate Na diffusion as well because both pathways are approximately parallel to the <i>b</i> axis, which means that the diffusion distance would be shortened during the charging process. Moreover, the initial containment of Na in the polyanion‐based composite Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub> can be a great advantage for the wide use of SIBs, and the long cycle life also provides a platform for very stable utilization, which can avoid the potential safety risks from starting with a metallic Na anode.</p><p>In summary, a uniform carbon‐coated Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material was successfully synthesized, and its long cycling stability both for low and high rates have been well characterized. It can deliver capacity of ≈100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at the first and second discharge at 0.1 C, and it features capacity retention of 92.3% at 0.5 C after 300 cycles. Even after 1100 cycles at 5 C, about 89.6% of the initial capacity was maintained. More importantly, almost 100% first‐cycle Coulombic efficiency was achieved, which is quite critical for making full‐cell SIBs. This pyrophosphate composite can be regarded as a safe cathode material for SIBs. This study also highlights the importance of the FEC additive in the electrolyte for long cycling stability. The fast Na diffusion coefficient around 10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> was confirmed via both cyclic voltammetry and the GITT method, and a single‐phase‐transition process was discovered. The contribution of the pseudocapacitance was detected, and it can help improve the high‐rate capability and durable cyclability. Moreover, two 1D Na‐diffusion channels with relatively low activation energy were identified via the DFT calculation, accompanied by a tiny lattice volume change of approximately 2.19% during the electrochemical cycling, which is also a key reason for the superior cycling stability. We believe that this low‐cost, high‐stability pyrophosphate composite Na<sub>3.32</sub>Fe<sub>2.34</sub>(P<sub>2</sub>O<sub>7</sub>)<sub>2</sub>/C material will be one of the most promising cathode candidates for extensive applications in large‐scale Na rechargeable batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201605535-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was supported by the Australian Automotive Cooperative Research Centre and the Malaysian Automotive Institute (AutoCRC/MAI Project Agreement 1‐111: Development of Advanced Electrode and Electrolytes for the LIB), the Australian Renewable Energy Agency (ARENA S4) project, and Australian Research Council Discovery Project DP160102627, and the Young Scientists Fund of National Natural Science Foundation of China (Grant No. 61504055), and the Natural Science Foundation of Hunan Province (Grant No. 2015JJ3110).
