Good control over crystallinity, morphology, and dispersion of the nanohybrid is achieved because of the thermosetting nature of the resin polymer. The effects of the nickel nanoparticle on the composition, crystallographic phase, structure, morphology, tap density, specific surface area, and electrochemical performance as both lithium‐ion and sodium‐ion battery anodes are systematically investigated. It is found that the incorporation of the in situ formed nickel nanoparticles with certain content effectively enhances the electrochemical performance including reversible capacities, cyclic stability and rate performance as both lithium‐ion and sodium‐ion battery anodes. The experimental I‐V profiles at different temperatures and theoretical calculations reveal that the charge carriers are accumulated in the amorphous carbon regions, which act as scattering centers to the carriers and lower the carrier mobility for the composite. With increasing nickel content, the mobility of the charge carriers is significantly increased, while the number of the charge carriers maintains almost constant. The nickel nanoparticles provide extra pathways for the accumulated charge, leading to reduced scatterings among the charge carriers and enhanced charge‐carrier transportation.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>The Matrix Reloaded 2</b>: In situ synthesized super‐small TiO<sub>2</sub> and nickel nanoparticles uniformly embedded in the carbon matrix effectively improve the electrochemical performance as both lithium‐ion and sodium‐ion battery anodes due to a significantly enhanced charge‐carrier mobility by the nickel nanoparticles.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900231:asia201900231-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900231-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta><noteGroup xml:id="asia201900231-ntgp-0001"><note xml:id="asia201900231-note-1001"><p>These authors contributed equally to this work.</p></note></noteGroup></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="asia201900231-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Titanium dioxide has been regarded as a promising anode material due to excellent electrochemical stability, low cost, small volume expansion (&lt;4 %) and safe voltage.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0001"/> Furthermore, TiO<sub>2</sub> is attractive because it exhibits electrochemical activity towards both lithiation and sodiation.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0002"/> However, the intrinsically poor electronic and ionic conductivity of TiO<sub>2</sub> significantly compromise the actual electrochemical performance as both the LIB and SIB anodes. Strategies such as nanostructure construction and bulk composition with electron conductive species including carbonaceous materials and metals have proven to be effective in improving the electrochemical performance.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0001i #asia201900231-bib-0003"/> It is essential to combine both the carbon matrix and metal nanoparticles to composites with TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles, where a synergistic effect can be realized to effectively improve the electrochemical performance.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0004"/> On the one hand, the carbon matrix acts as a dispersion medium to host both the metal and TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles and inhibits the agglomeration of the metal and TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles as well. On the other hand, the well dispersed metal nanoparticles with excellent electron conductivities enhance the electrochemical process around the local TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles. However, it seems that the co‐composition of the carbon and metal nanoparticles with the TiO<sub>2</sub> is much less explored as compared to the TiO<sub>2</sub> composites with only carbon or metal.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0003b #asia201900231-bib-0005"/> Besides, the fundamental mechanisms which are responsible for the performance enhancement of the co‐composition of the carbon and metal nanoparticles are not clearly understood today.</p><p>In recent years, a new synthetic method was developed by our group to achieve facile controllable synthesis of super‐small sized TiO<sub>2</sub> or silicon oxycarbide nanoparticles. Both were in situ synthesized and embedded into the in situ formed carbon matrix.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0006"/> Difunctional dental methacrylate monomers were used as solvents and carbon sources, coupled with a fast and energy saving photo polymerization process.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0006 #asia201900231-bib-0007"/> The precursor of TiO<sub>2</sub> was homogeneously mixed with the resin monomer at a molecular level. Through the photo polymerization process, the solution containing the resin monomer and precursor was instantly converted into a solid form. The precursor molecules were integrated into the polymerized thermosetting network at molecular level by covalent bonding through an alcoholysis between the precursor and hydroxyl group of the resin monomer. Due to the thermosetting structure feature of the polymerized resin, only a very limited melting process happened during the high temperature calcination process under inert atmosphere. As a result, the nucleation, growth, agglomeration, and crystallization of the TiO<sub>2</sub> were significantly inhibited by the in situ formed carbon matrix. A homogenous dispersion of the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles within the carbon matrix was achieved simultaneously. Unusual electrochemical performance including modified discharge/charge voltage profile and excellent cyclic stability were demonstrated by the TiO<sub>2</sub>/C nanohybrid because of the super‐small sized TiO<sub>2</sub> and homogeneous distribution in the carbon matrix.</p><p>Super‐small sized silver nanoparticles were in situ synthesized and incorporated into the silicon/carbon nanohybrid in our previous work.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0008"/> The temperature dependence of the I‐V profile indicated that the charge carrier mobility rather than the charge carrier concentration was enhanced exponentially, leading to accelerated charge carrier transportation and rate capability. Fundamental insight was obtained regarding the mechanism responsible for the performance improvement by the metal nanoparticles. However, it is not clear if the mechanism is universally applicable to other anode materials. Moreover, it is essential to investigate whether the mechanism is working in other metal nanoparticles such as nickel, which are less expensive, rich in resource, and holds more promising for practical applications than silver. Furthermore, the role of the metal nanoparticles size in the fundamental mechanism and corresponding performance improvement is worth studying.</p><p>Based on our previous work, in the present study a co‐composition of carbon and nickel nanoparticles with the super‐small TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles is reported as displayed in Scheme <link href="#asia201900231-fig-5001"/>. The difunctional methacrylate monomers (Bis‐GMA and TEGDMA) are used as carbon source and reaction medium. Titanium tetraisopropoxide (TTIP) is used as the precursor of TiO<sub>2</sub> and Nickel acetate is the precursor of metallic Ni, which is dissolved in acrylic acid (AA) by complexation. Upon photo polymerization and carbonization, super‐small TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles and nickel are in situ formed, assisted by the in situ formed carbon matrix. The spatial dispersion of both the TiO<sub>2</sub> and nickel nanoparticles is well controlled and homogenous throughout the whole carbon matrix. As a result, the nickel species not only enhances the electron conductivity of the TiO<sub>2</sub> based composite, but also modifies the crystallization behavior of the TiO<sub>2</sub>, leading to a comprehensive control over the structure and properties of the TiO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0009"/> The super‐small size feature of the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles helps to stabilize the structure during repeated lithiation/delithiation processes.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0010"/> Consequently, excellent cyclic and rate performance are demonstrated as both the LIB and SIB anodes by the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/carbon nanohybrid.</p><figure xml:id="asia201900231-fig-5001"><title type="figureName">Scheme</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900231:asia201900231-fig-5001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900231-fig-5001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic of the synthesis of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrid using dental resin monomer as solvent and carbon source.</p></caption></figure><section xml:id="asia201900231-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Experimental</title><p>Titanium tetraisopropoxide, nickel acetate tetrahydrate, bisphenol A glycidyl dimethacrylate and triethylene glycol dimethacrylate were purchased from Aladdin Reagent Co., Ltd., China. Acrylic acid was bought from Sinopharm Group Co., Ltd., China. Phenyl bis (2, 4, 6‐trimethyl benzoyl)‐phosphine oxide (I‐819) was obtained from Sigma–Aldrich. All chemicals were used as received without further purification. Standard 2032‐type coin cells were fabricated to evaluate the electrochemical performance of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids.</p></section></section><section xml:id="asia201900231-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussions</title><p>The crystallization behavior of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids with different nickel acetate contents is characterized by XRD (Figure <link href="#asia201900231-fig-0001"/> a). The weak broad peaks in the XRD profile of the nickel free TiO<sub>2</sub>/C sample indicate that poorly crystallized pure anatase titania phase (JCPDS 21‐1272) is formed, which is consistent with our previously reported work.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0006b"/> With introduction of the nickel acetate, the intensity of the diffraction peaks belonging to the anatase phase is significantly weakened. Instead, an additional rutile titania phase (JCPDS 65‐0190) appears. Besides the peaks ascribed to TiO<sub>2</sub>, sharp peaks from cubic phase of nickel are present (JCPDS 04‐0850), indicating the existence of the well crystallized nickel element within the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids. Along with increasing nickel content, the peaks of the anatase titania phase continue to be weakened; while the peaks of the rutile titania phase become stronger. Nevertheless, with the amount of M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> being increased to 2.0, the anatase titania phase reappears. These results suggest that the presence of the nickel ions strongly inhibits the crystallization of the anatase titania phase, while it promotes the formation of the rutile titania phase.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0009a #asia201900231-bib-0009b"/> The nickel ion has a similar ionic radius to the titanium ion (70 pm vs. 74.5 pm). Thus, it is easy to generate crystal lattice defect and oxygen vacancy with incorporation of the nickel ions, which facilitates the phase transformation from the anatase to the rutile titania phase. If the amount of the nickel ion is small, it does not change the anatase lattice significantly, and the phase transformation from the anatase to the rutile phase is limited. With increasing concentration of the nickel ion, significant crystallographic phase change takes place, and the phase transformation is gradually enhanced with continuous increasing amount of nickel ions. However, after the nickel ion concentration reaches a certain level, further increasing the nickel concentration does not enhance the formation of the rutile phase further, and the anatase reappears again instead. The excessive amount of the nickel ions promotes the nucleation and growth of the nickel metal nanoparticles. As a result, the crystal lattice of the TiO<sub>2</sub> is less modified and the phase transformation from the anatase to the rutile phase is partially suppressed. Consequently, the anatase phase is observed again with the high nickel content.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0009c"/> Besides the diffraction peaks from both TiO<sub>2</sub> and nickel metal, a Bragg peak is located at 26.4°, which is caused by the local graphite structure in the samples containing nickel. The formation of the graphite structure is promoted by the catalytic effect of the in situ formed nickel metals during the carbonization process.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0011"/> The average particle sizes of nickel calculated by the Debye–Scherrer equation based on the cubic (111) diffraction peak are 21 nm, 25 nm, 30 nm and 35 nm for M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> having values of 0.25, 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0, respectively. It can be found that the increased content of nickel acetate generates an enlarged particle size. The formation of the nickel nanoparticles involves a multi‐step process. The carbothermal reduction process converts nickel ions into metal in situ, which act as nuclei for the nickel nanoparticles. The growth of the nickel nanoparticles is featured with a thermally induced fusion process, which is influenced by the local nickel species concentration. It is reasonable that high nickel content promotes the growth of the nickel nanoparticles, leading to an increased particle size.</p><figure xml:id="asia201900231-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:18614728:media:asia201900231:asia201900231-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/asia201900231-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>XRD profiles (a) and HRTEM images (b–f) of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids prepared with different mass ratios of nickel acetate over TTIP (M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub>). From (b) to (f) the values are 0, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0. The representative lattices fringes belonging to different phases are indexed and marked by the arrows.</p></caption></figure><p>The TEM images and corresponding SAED patterns (Figure S1) present the morphologies and crystallinities of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids prepared with a systematic increase of M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> from 0 to 2.0. The size of TiO<sub>2</sub> cannot be recognized due to the low magnification in TEM and the size of nickel is not measured due to local aggregation of nickel. The HRTEM images in Figure 1 b–<link href="#asia201900231-fig-0001"/> f further confirm the crystallographic phase of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids. Only the typical lattice fringe spacing corresponding to the (101) crystal plane of the anatase titania phase is found in the nickel free TiO<sub>2</sub>/C sample (Figure <link href="#asia201900231-fig-0001"/> b), suggesting the existence of a pure anatase phase. With M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> being 0.25, both the (101) crystal plane of the anatase phase and (110) crystal plane of the rutile phase of TiO<sub>2</sub> are seen in the HRTEM image. It confirms the existence of both the anatase and rutile phases of TiO<sub>2</sub>, and the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles of the anatase and rutile phases are homogeneously mixed with each other in very local regions within the carbon matrix. When M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> is increased to 0.5, the lattice of the graphite 2H is observed besides the anatase and rutile phases. The existence of the graphite 2H structure is also observed in the HRTEM images of other samples (Figure S2). With M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> being 1.0, only rutile TiO<sub>2</sub> is observed. Even though the HRTEM only reveals the structures within very local area, the absence of the lattice fringes belonging to the anatase phase suggests that the amount of the anatase phase is quite limited compared to the rutile phase. The TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids prepared with M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> of 2.0 exhibit lattice fringes of both, the anatase and rutile phases of TiO<sub>2</sub> and graphite 2H as well in the HRTEM images. Moreover, the average particle size and size distribution of the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles are acquired from the HRTEM images in Figure <link href="#asia201900231-fig-0001"/>. The nickel free sample shows that tiny TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles with an average diameter of 6.2 nm are homogeneously embedded in the carbon matrix. When the value of M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> is increased to 0.25, small particles with a diameter of 4.7 nm are observed. With the value of M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> being further increased to 0.5, the average size of the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles is decreased to 2.1 nm. The average size of the nanoparticles continues to decrease to 1.5 nm with the value of M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> up to 1.0. When the value of M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> is increased to 2.0, the diameter of the nanoparticles is decreased back to 2.7 nm. It can be seen that the size of TiO<sub>2</sub> can be systematically tuned by changing the amount of the nickel ions being incorporated. Specifically, the average sizes of the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles decrease gradually as M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> increases from 0 to 1.0. The increasing concentration of nickel ions (M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> not higher than 1.0) efficiently inhibits the growth of the anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles.<link href="#asia201900231-bib-0012"/> The reversed particle size increase to 2.7 nm with M<sub>Ni/TTIP</sub> of 2.0, which suggests that the excessive amount of the nickel ions cannot effectively inhibit the growth of the anatase TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles. The SAED patterns of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids with increasing nickel content are generally consistent with the XRD results. Thus, in general, the local TEM, SAED, and HRTEM imaging results of all samples agree well with the XRD results having probed larger sample volumes and thus having better statistics. It indicates that the contents of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids are uniform and different species are homogeneously distributed at different length scales within the carbon matrix.</p><p>The STEM image and the corresponding elemental mapping images (Figure S3) indicate that titanium, oxygen and nickel are homogeneously distributed in the carbon matrix in the area of at least 500 nm×500 nm. It exhibits the spatial distribution of the titanium, nickel, oxygen and carbon element, which shows that the titanium species and nickel are homogeneously dispersed in the whole area of the sample. In the blended system, there is an accumulation of charge carriers in the amorphous carbon regions between the graphite flakes due to the imbalance between the band‐like transport in the graphite fakes and the hopping transport in the amorphous carbon regions. These accumulated charges act as scattering centers to the carriers in the graphite flakes and, therefore, lower the carrier mobility for the whole system. The nickel particles embedded in the amorphous carbon regions provide extra pathways for the accumulated charges. As a consequence of the reduced scatterings among the charge carriers, the conduction is improved. The average size of the TiO<sub>2</sub> nanoparticles is controlled to be in the range between 4 nm and 7 nm, and the size of the nickel nanoparticles is around 30 nm. The systematic variation of the nickel content significantly influences the structure and properties of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids including composition, crystallographic phase, structure, morphology, tap density, specific surface area, and electrochemical performance. The TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids with a certain nickel content exhibit a good electrochemical performance as both LIB and SIB anodes in terms of reversible capacities, cyclic stability and rate capabilities. The enhanced electron conductivity induced by the nickel nanoparticles contributes to the improved performance. The fundamental mechanism responsible for the enhanced electrochemical performance originating from the nickel nanoparticles is elaborated by analyzing the I–V profiles of the TiO<sub>2</sub>/Ni/C nanohybrids with different nickel contents at different temperatures. The results confirm that the nickel nanoparticles significantly enhance the charge‐carrier mobility, where the number of the charge carriers is essentially maintained. The nickel nanoparticles within the composite provide extra pathways for the charges accumulated in the amorphous carbon region, which reduces the scattering among the charge carriers and accelerates the charge‐carrier transport, thereby leading to an enhanced electron conductivity and improved electrochemical performance. The work presented herein confirms that the mechanism responsible for the enhanced charge‐carrier mobility is generally applicable to different anode and metal nanoparticles. It is inspiring and instructive to develop new high‐performance lithium/sodium‐ion battery anodes.</p></section><section xml:id="asia201900231-sec-0005" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This research is funded by the National Key R&amp;D Program of China (Grant No. 2016YFB0100100), Natural Science Foundation of China (51702335, 21773279), the Zhejiang Non‐profit Technology Applied Research Program (LGG19B010001), Ningbo Municipal Natural Science Foundation (2018A610084), the CAS‐EU S&amp;T cooperation partner program (174433KYSB20150013), and Key Laboratory of Bio‐based Polymeric Materials of Zhejiang Province.
