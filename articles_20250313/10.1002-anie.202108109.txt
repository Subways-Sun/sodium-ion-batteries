In fact, high voltage is not the reason which causes the irreversible phase transition. There are two internal factors which affect structural evolution: the amount and distribution of Na ions retained in the lattice. Hereon, a series of layered oxides Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>−1/3</sub>Co<sub>4/3−2<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1/3≤<i>x</i>≤2/3) were synthesized. It is observed that different components have different structural evolutions during the charge/discharge processes, and further researches find that the distribution of Na ions in layers is the main factor. By controlling the distribution of Na ions, the phase transition process can be well controlled. As the referential component, P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode with uniform distribution of Na ions is cycled at the voltage window of 1.5–4.5 V, which exhibits a volume change as low as 1.9 %. Such a low strain is beneficial for cycling stability. The current work provides a new and effective route to regulate the structural evolution of the promising P2‐type layered cathode for sodium ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The number and distribution of Na ions retained in lattice are two internal factors which affect structural evolution. Na ions can support the lattice structure like pillars, by regulating the distribution of Na ions, the process of phase transition can be controlled. As a result, the cathode can offer as high capacity as possible without obvious phase transition.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108109:anie202108109-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108109-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>In view of the development of electric vehicles and other energy storage systems, the renewable batteries, such as lithium ion batteries (LIBs) and sodium ion batteries (SIBs), have attracted more and more attention.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0001 #anie202108109-bib-0002 #anie202108109-bib-0003 #anie202108109-bib-0004"><sup>[1–4]</sup></link> LIBs now have been widely used in the range of portable electronics and electrical vehicles, but because of the uneven distribution of lithium,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0005 #anie202108109-bib-0006"><sup>[5, 6]</sup></link> the abundant SIBs are proposed as the complementary technology.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0007 #anie202108109-bib-0008 #anie202108109-bib-0009"><sup>[7–9]</sup></link> For SIBs, capacity and working voltage are two of the important indicators of battery performance. For the cathode materials, these two indicators mean that it is better to make more capacities generated in high voltage range.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0010 #anie202108109-bib-0011 #anie202108109-bib-0012"><sup>[10–12]</sup></link> P2‐type layered oxides (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>, 0.46≤<i>x</i>≤0.83, TM=transition metal) have great potential as cathode materials for SIBs due to their high reversible capacities and stable crystal structure.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0011 #anie202108109-bib-0013"><sup>[11, 13]</sup></link> Recently, more and more researchers are working on P2‐type cathode materials to improve their capacity, voltage and stability.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0014 #anie202108109-bib-0015 #anie202108109-bib-0016 #anie202108109-bib-0017"><sup>[14–17]</sup></link></p><p>For P2‐type cathode materials, the capacity comes from the cationic reaction. But when they are charged into high voltage range (above 4.0 V), anionic redox reaction appears to provide capacity,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0018"><sup>[18]</sup></link> such as P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.72</sub>Mg<sub>0.28</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0019"><sup>[19]</sup></link> P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>7/9</sub>Zn<sub>2/9</sub>O<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0020"><sup>[20]</sup></link> and P2‐ Na<sub>0.72</sub>[Li<sub>0.24</sub>Mn<sub>0.76</sub>]O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0021"><sup>[21]</sup></link> In general, there are three types of ordering in multielement layered oxides: transition metal ordering, charge ordering and Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy ordering.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0022"><sup>[22]</sup></link> By mixing multiple transition metal ions, which have small difference in ionic radii, the structure tends to form disordered arrangement. In charge‐discharge curves, the disordered structure shows smooth curves, which means less phase transition.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0023 #anie202108109-bib-0024"><sup>[23, 24]</sup></link> In addition, during the extraction and insertion processes of Na ions, the lattice structure evolves, accompanied by the volume change.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0025 #anie202108109-bib-0026 #anie202108109-bib-0027"><sup>[25–27]</sup></link> Usually, P2‐type cathode materials can bear slight volume change to maintain good structural stability. So synthesizing multielement materials is an effective method to improve the stability. On the other hand, there is no doubt that, the amount of Na ions which are in lattices is the main factor for phase transitions. When most Na ions are extracted from lattices, P2 phase will translates to O2 phase<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0028 #anie202108109-bib-0029"><sup>[28, 29]</sup></link> or OP4 phase,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0030"><sup>[30]</sup></link> which cause a drastic volume change (the volume change of phase transition from P2 to O2 is about 23.2 %<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0031"><sup>[31]</sup></link>). However, as illustrated by a large number of P2‐layered oxides, in the case of the same amount of Na ions, different P2‐type cathode materials are in different structural states: Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mg<sub>0.1</sub>Ni<sub>0.2</sub>Mn<sub>0.7</sub>O<sub>2</sub> shows P2 and OP4 phases with 0.17 Na ions,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0032"><sup>[32]</sup></link> Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mn<sub>0.67</sub>Ni<sub>0.33</sub>O<sub>2</sub> shows P2 and O2 phases with 0.2 Na ions,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0033"><sup>[33]</sup></link> and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Co<sub>0.5</sub>Mn<sub>0.5</sub>O<sub>2</sub> shows pure P2 phase with 0.2 Na ions.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0034"><sup>[34]</sup></link> So the assumption can be made that, the distribution of Na ions which are remained in lattices is the cause of different structural evolution. And this distribution of Na ions ca be controlled by the distributions of component and charge.</p><p>In this work, Co‐rich Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, Ni‐rich Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (the common formula is Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>−1/3</sub>Co<sub>4/3−2<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1/3≤<i>x</i>≤2/3)) were designed to study the relation between the distribution of remaining Na ions and structural evolution. In situ synchrotron high‐energy X‐ray diffraction (HEXRD) and ex situ XRD tests show that, different components can make different distributions of Na ions, which further lead to different structural evolutions. An optimized component of Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/2) was charged into 4.5 V and retained uniform Na ions in the lattice. At the current density of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, this cathode can maintain a reversible capacity of 150 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, and the unit cell volume change is as small as 1.9 %.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Cathode Characteristics</title><p>With the idea of combing the advantages of Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with uniform distribution of Na ions was obtained. These samples can accord with the chemical formula of Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>−1/3</sub>Co<sub>4/3−2<i>x</i></sub>O2 (1/3≤<i>x</i>≤2/3) (all Na ions can be extracted when all the TM ions change into +4 state). Scanning electron microscope (SEM) was first used to characterize the morphology of carbonate precursors and final products. As shown in Figure S1a,c,e, the precursors of P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub>x−1/3</sub>Co<sub>4/3−2<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1/3≤<i>x</i>≤2/3) are big secondary particles which are made up of many small primary units. After calcining, the primary particles were crystallized with sodium carbonate to form the products (Figure S1b,d,f). In addition, the component analysis was done by the energy dispersive spectrometer (EDS) technology (Figure S2). As shown in Table S1, there is a little difference between observed values and designed values, that is, the content of Co is little higher while that of Ni is little lower than the designed values. Such results may be caused by the different solubility of these carbonates in the hydrothermal reaction.</p><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0001">1 a</link>, for Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=2/3) compound, because the value of Ni is +2, there should be two Na<sup>+</sup> ions nearby to keep the charge in balance. As a result, Na ions are more concentrated than other samples, and in the process of charging, a part of Na ions move out and a lot of vacancies remain, which makes the structure unstable. As for Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/3), there is only one Na<sup>+</sup> ion around Co<sup>3+</sup> ion, which makes Na ions more evenly distributed. When the same number of Na ions move out from layers, Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/3) has less vacancy aggregation and more stable structure than Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=2/3). But for the specific capacity, Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=2/3) certainly does better. So, combining of these two sample to get a compound for high capacity and stable structure is promising. Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0001">1 b</link> shows XRD patterns of the designed Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/3), Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/2) and Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=2/3) cathodes. All these samples are single phase, and Bragg diffraction lines show that these compounds crystallize in a hexagonal layered structure (P2‐type structure) with space group <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub>/<i>mmc</i> (Figure S3a–c). The Rietveld‐refined was done by GSAS package incorporated with the EXPGUI interface,<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0035"><sup>[35]</sup></link> and detailed crystallographic parameters of different samples deduced from the XRD refinement are listed in Figure S3d. The result displays that more Ni ions make all the lattice parameters (<i>a</i>, <i>b</i> and <i>c</i>) increase, as a result, the cell volume becomes larger. Moreover, Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0001">1 c,d</link> show the result of TEM characteristic, and it is obvious that the primary particles are single crystalline. Element mapping shows these transition metal elements are uniformly distributed in the whole particle (Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0001">1 e</link>–h).</p><figure xml:id="anie202108109-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202108109:anie202108109-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202108109-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Synthesis mechanism and characteristic of as‐synthesized samples. a) Distribution of Na ions in different samples. b) XRD patterns of P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub><i>x</i></sub>Ni<sub><i>x</i>−1/3</sub>Co<sub>4/3−2<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (1/3≤<i>x</i>≤2/3). c,d) Selected area electron diffraction (SEAD) pattern (the zone axis is [001]) and high resolution transmission electron microscope (HRTEM) image. e–h) Element mapping of Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/2).</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Performance</title><p>As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0002">2 a</link>, it can be seen that Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=2/3) has the highest first discharge capacity due to the more active element Ni, but it decreases to under 50 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> over 100 cycles. As for Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/3), its stability is the best, with the expense of the lowest discharge capacity. In this system, Ni element plays the role of increasing capacity and Co element can improve structure stability. Due to the synergistic effect of Ni and Co, Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/2) has an excellent overall electrochemical performances (the first discharge capacity is about 156 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>, and it still retains a capacity of 140 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 50 cycles, but just 100 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles), which also occurs at the large current densities (Figure S4). As shown in Figure S5, when the voltage window is 1.5–4.5 V, the surface of cathode particle gradually produces furrows with cycling. However, these furrows disappear when the voltage window is 1.5–4.0 V. So the drastic capacity fading is attributed to the dissolution of transition metals in high voltage. Scanning transmission electron microscopy‐high angle annular dark field (STEM‐HAADF) images are illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 a,b</link>. Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>‐4.5 V (<i>x</i>=1/3) shows a d‐space of 5.5 Å, which corresponds to the XRD peak located at 16.1°. The order of TM ions is a little different from the structure of P2 phase, and there are some slippages in this structure compared to the standard atomic model (Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 c</link>). It means that this structure tends to change towards O2 phase, but Na ions in layers support the structure like pillars. Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 d</link> shows even distribution of Na ions, these pillars keep the large d‐spacing, to avoid excessive volume change. As for Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>‐4.2 V (<i>x</i>=2/3), its structure is much different from Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>Co<sub>2/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub>‐4.5 V (<i>x</i>=1/3), though numbers of Na ions in these two samples are the same. From Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 e</link>, there are several different phases and lattice distortions. Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 f,g</link> show an unique structure with a d‐spacing of 2.2 Å, and it is caused by the structural collapse and form the structure of TM layer‐O layer ‐TM layer.<link href="#anie202108109-bib-0042"><sup>[42]</sup></link> This structure which cannot make Na ions re‐intercalated is one of the main reasons of capacity fade. As for the P2‐phase in this sample, the d‐spacing can keep 5.5 Å, but it is also accompanied by lattice distortion (Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 h,i</link>). From Figure <link href="#anie202108109-fig-0006">6 j</link>, the segregation of Na ions is observed in this sample, and in where there are few Na ions, the content of Ni ions is high. This phenomenon substantiates that, the Na ions around Ni ions have priority to move out and results in vacancy assembly in Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=2/3), further leads to the phase transition from P2 to O2, even the structural collapse. It can be concluded that the distribution of Na ions is an important factor, and the uniform distribution can help to make structure more stable.</p></section></section><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Discussion</title><p>For P2‐type layered oxides, many scholars believe that, P2 phase will transform into O2 phase when it is charged into high voltage. However, this work has proven that, phase transition is not related to voltage. And there are two key factors which control the structural evolution: the amount and distribution of Na ions retained in the lattice. P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Co<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x</i>=1/2) has a uniform distribution of Na ions during the process of charging, and it can still stabilize the structure with 0.17 Na ions. As the result, P2‐O2 phase transition is suppressed with a ultra‐low strain of 1.9 % in volume. In addition, to show the universality of this theory, P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Fe<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>7/12</sub>Ni<sub>1/4</sub>Fe<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>2</sub> were synthesized and charged into 4.4 V. As shown in Figure S19, there is no O2 or OP4 phase appear in these two cathodes. During the process of charging, Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Fe<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> shows P2 and hydrate phases with 0.17 Na ions (Figure S19a–c), while Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>7/12</sub>Ni<sub>1/4</sub>Fe<sub>1/6</sub>O<sub>2</sub> shows only hydrate phase with 0.2 Na ions (Figure S19d–f). It is obvious that, Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>Ni<sub>1/6</sub>Fe<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> exhibits more stable structure with less Na ions. Hence, the distribution of Na ions retained in lattice can tune the structural evolutions, and this strategy can be applied to other P2‐type layered oxide cathodes.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0009" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, a new and effective route of controlled phase transition by regulating distribution of sodium ions for P2‐type layered oxide cathode has been successfully developed. Different components can make different distributions of Na ions, which further lead to different structural evolutions. A uniform distribution of Na ions in the cathode's structure can avoid vacancy segregation when Na ions are de‐intercalated from layers, and suppress drastic structural change. This work provides a new idea and reference component standards to achieve promising cathode materials with high capacity and excellent stability for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202108109-sec-0010" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This research was accomplished with the supported from the National Natural Science Foundation of China (no. 51771076, NSFC51621001), Guangdong “Pearl River Talents Plan” (no. 2017GC010218), R&amp;D Program in Key Areas of Guangdong Province (no. 2020B0101030005), Guangdong Basic and Applied Basic Research Foundation (no. 2020B1515120049), and Science and Technology Innovation Commission under the grant Shenzhen‐Hong Kong Innovation Circle Category D Project (no.
