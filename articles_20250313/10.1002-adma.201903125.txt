However, it usually suffers from the issues of low initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE) and poor rate performance, severely hindering its practical application. Herein, a flexible, self‐supporting, and scalable hard carbon paper (HCP) derived from scalable and renewable tissue is rationally designed and prepared as practical additive‐free anode for room/low‐temperature SIBs with high ICE. In ether electrolyte, such HCP achieves an ICE of up to 91.2% with superior high‐rate capability, ultralong cycle life (e.g., 93% capacity retention over 1000 cycles at 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>) and outstanding low‐temperature performance. Working mechanism analyses reveal that the plateau region is the rate‐determining step for HCP with a lower electrochemical reaction kinetics, which can be significantly improved in ether electrolyte.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma201903125-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>A self‐supporting, flexible, additive‐free and scalable hard carbon paper (HCP)</b> derived from tissue is rationally developed, and it achieves outstanding Na‐storage properties in terms of high initial Coulombic efficiency (91.2%), superior high‐rate capability, ultralong cyclic stability, as well as outstanding low‐<i>T</i> performance in ether electrolyte. More significantly, the Na‐storage and capacity attenuation mechanism of the HCP anode is revealed.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903125-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma201903125-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma201903125-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>In recent years, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have been considered as the most promising energy storage system to replace the market‐dominant lithium‐ion batteries in order to alleviate the shortage of lithium resources.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0001 #adma201903125-bib-0002 #adma201903125-bib-0003 #adma201903125-bib-0004"/> Although SIBs are still at the stage of fundamental study, electrochemical properties of some recently reported cathode materials, such as polyanionic compounds and layered oxides,<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0005 #adma201903125-bib-0006 #adma201903125-bib-0007 #adma201903125-bib-0008"/> are apace approaching the standards of commercial applications, owing to their high initial Coulombic efficiency (ICE), outstanding high‐rate and cycling performance, and low cost. However, in contrast, there is still no anode material that can completely meet the commercial requirements in the actual SIBs, seriously hindering the development and application of SIBs.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0009 #adma201903125-bib-0010"/></p><p>Amongst all of the possible anodes for SIBs, carbonaceous materials exhibit the highest practicability because of their high conductivity, low cost, abundant resources, and environmental friendliness.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0009 #adma201903125-bib-0011 #adma201903125-bib-0012 #adma201903125-bib-0013"/> Presently, hard carbon (HC) anode materials are greatly valued due to the low electrode potential versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na and high cycling stability during the de‐/sodiation processes, making it is the most suitable material for practical application.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0014"/> However, there are still two critical issues that need to be solved before practicability. One is the poor rate performance, and another one is the extremely low ICE.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0015"/> In addition, the low‐temperature performance of HC anode (HCA) is rarely studied although it is really a significant parameter for practical application.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0016 #adma201903125-bib-0017 #adma201903125-bib-0018"/> Previously, studies were mainly focused on enhancing the Na‐storage capacity and rate performance by heteroatom doping and introduction of porous structure and/or defects. Unfortunately, these strategies are basically at the expense of ICE value and reversible Na‐storage capacity from low‐voltage plateau.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0019 #adma201903125-bib-0020 #adma201903125-bib-0021 #adma201903125-bib-0022 #adma201903125-bib-0023"/> There are also some efforts of reducing the defect and porosity in HC materials to increase the ICE of HCA, but they are usually ineffective for the improvement of rate performance.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0024 #adma201903125-bib-0025 #adma201903125-bib-0026"/> High ICE and outstanding rate performance seem to be two irreconcilable contradictions for the HCA in SIBs. Fortunately, some recent studies have proved that rate performance and ICE can be improved simultaneously to a certain extent in ether electrolyte.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0027 #adma201903125-bib-0028"/> Nevertheless, additives including binder and carbon black, which are indispensable in the fabrication procedures for conventional electrodes, will usually cause irreversible capacity loss in the first cycle and hence reduce the ICE.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0024 #adma201903125-bib-0029"/> As a result, there is no report on HCA material that can simultaneously achieve the outstanding comprehensive Na‐storage properties in terms of high ICE of &gt;90 %, excellent high‐rate capability, long cyclic stability, and great low‐temperature (low‐<i>T</i>) performance until now, which is still a big challenge for the carbon anode of SIBs.</p><p>Tissue is one type of typical paper with highly flexible and self‐supporting features,<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0030"/> and the papermaking technology has already become mature a long time ago by using wood pulp as the main raw material,<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0031 #adma201903125-bib-0032"/> suggesting tissue paper may be a promising precursor (cheapness, environmental friendliness, and renewability) for the production of paper‐like HCA. Herein, in order to develop advanced HCA for SIBs, a flexible, self‐supporting, and large‐size HC paper (HCP) composed of self‐interwoven carbon microbelts has been prepared by using commercially available tissue as the scalable precursor via a simple two‐step annealing process. Combined the unique micro/nanostructure with ether electrolyte, the prepared HCP owns high electronic conductivity and ultrafast ionic transport paths to greatly enhance its Na‐storage properties. As a result, HCP realizes high‐rate capability (e.g., delivering the capacity of 338.2 and 170.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 and 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively), ultralong cycling stability (e.g., keeping a reversible capacity of 286.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a very low decay rate of only ≈0.007% per cycle over 1000 cycles at 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), and extremely high ICE of 91.2% in 1 mol L<sup>−1</sup> NaCF<sub>3</sub>SO<sub>3</sub> in diglyme (abbreviated as 1 <sc>m</sc> NaOTf/Diglyme). In addition, such HCP also exhibits outstanding low‐<i>T</i> performance. For example, compared with the capacity delivered at 25 °C under 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity retention at −25 °C is still up to 89.0% that is a very high value for SIBs. More significantly, Na‐storage and capacity attenuation mechanism of HCP anode is revealed by the electrochemical studies of cyclic voltammogram (CV), galvanostatic charge and discharge (GCD), and galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT). It is confirmed that, the plateau region is a rate‐controlled step with a lower electrochemical kinetics in both ether and ester electrolytes. Fortunately, the ether electrolyte can accelerate the kinetics of plateau region, resulting in the highly improved rate performance in ether electrolyte.</p><p>The details for HCP preparation are shown in experimental section in the Supporting Information. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0001"><b>1</b></link>a schematically illustrates its preparation procedure, mainly including a preoxidation process in air followed by the high‐temperature carbonization in argon. After the two‐step annealing, oxygen content in the obtained HCP is very low (10.12 atom%), and only a small amount of CO bonds is reserved (Figure S1, Supporting Information). More surprisingly, such HCP is highly flexible at large size (Figure S2, Supporting Information), and is composed of self‐interweaving 1D microbelts with homogeneous distributions of carbon and oxygen elements (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0001"/>b). The flexibility maintenance of HCP during carbonization should be mainly due to the self‐interweaving structure, which is well inherited from the original tissue (Figure S3, Supporting Information). In comparison to the common nano or porous carbon materials,<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0023 #adma201903125-bib-0033"/> self‐supporting HCP owns smaller specific surface area of ≈46.2 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure S4, Supporting Information), which is highly conducive to achieve a higher ICE.</p><figure xml:id="adma201903125-fig-0001"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903125-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustration of the preparation procedure for HCP and the digital photos of tissue, preoxidation carbonaceous paper (POCP) and HCP showing their flexible and self‐supporting characteristics. b) SEM image of HCP and the corresponding elemental mappings of carbon and oxygen. c) XRD pattern of HCP. Inset: digital photo of one circular HCP electrode at the bending state. d) HRTEM image and Raman spectrum (inset) of the HCP electrode material.</caption></figure><p>In addition, the preoxidation process is significant and indispensable to obtain the effective HCP anode for SIBs. Thereafter, the hard carbon paper prepared at no preoxidation process is abbreviated as HCP‐NP. As shown in Figure S5 (Supporting Information), HCP exhibits the larger area (43.1% vs 31.9%) and higher weight (10.5% vs 17.9%) retentions than HCP‐NP, if both are prepared from one same tissue paper. This demonstrates that, preoxidation process can reduce the shrinkage and increase the residue of carbonaceous material. Furthermore, X‐ray diffraction (XRD) patterns indicate the amorphous nature of both HCP and HCP‐NP materials (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0001"/>c; Figure S6, Supporting Information), while X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Figure S7, Supporting Information) and Fourier Transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR, Figure S8, Supporting Information) spectra show that, both are composed of the same functional groups but the oxygen content in HCP is obviously higher than that in HCP‐NP. High‐resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) images and Raman spectra (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0001"/>d; Figure S9, Supporting Information) demonstrate that preoxidation process promotes the formation of graphitized nanodomain. The higher graphitization degree is also conducive to realize large ICE value due to the less defects and marginal active sites in HCP.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0024"/> In the preoxidation process at a relatively low temperature of 240 °C, the oxygen‐containing atmosphere can retain more oxygen atoms/groups in the carbonaceous precursor of POCP, compared with the direct pyrolysis in inert Ar. As a result, the extra oxygen atoms will occupy some defect sites in the sp<sup>2</sup> C matrix to repair the graphene layers in the subsequent carbonization process at a higher temperature, hence increasing the graphitization degree with higher residual weight in HCP.</p><p>The electrochemical properties of HCP electrode for sodium storage were evaluated in the ether electrolyte of 1 <sc>m</sc> NaOTf/Diglyme. CV curves in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"><b>2</b></link>a present only one pair of redox peaks at very low potential of 0.11/0.03 V versus Na<sup>+</sup>/Na with highly overlapping profiles of initial five cycles, suggesting the outstanding stability of HCP during cycling. This is exactly coincident with the GCD curves (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"/>b). In addition, the HCP more significantly shows outstanding reversibility and high ICE of up to 91.2% that is almost a highest value compared to the HCA reported previously for sodium storage (<b>Table</b> <link href="#adma201903125-tbl-0001"><b>1</b></link>).<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0020 #adma201903125-bib-0021 #adma201903125-bib-0034 #adma201903125-bib-0035 #adma201903125-bib-0036 #adma201903125-bib-0037 #adma201903125-bib-0038 #adma201903125-bib-0039 #adma201903125-bib-0040"/> Such a high ICE value may mainly benefit from the unique structural characteristics of HCP and the usage of ether electrolyte. First, the 1D carbon microbelts are large and complete, resulting in smaller surface area and lesser solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) than the common powder carbon materials as illustrated in Figure S4 in the Supporting Information. Second, preoxidation process promote the formation of more ordered microstructure, effectively reducing the irreversible reaction during the first cycle.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0041"/> Third, the self‐supporting and additive‐free electrode avoids the irreversible capacity loss caused by traditional additives such as binder and carbon black (Figure S10, Supporting Information).<link As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"/>c, it can still deliver a specific capacity of 170.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at a high current density of 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, corresponding to a capacity retention of 50.3% compared to 338.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> delivered at 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (the corresponding area specific capacity is shown in Figure S12 in the Supporting Information). In other words, HCP retain more than half of capacity even current density increases 100 times, which is a fabulous performance compared with previous reports on typical HC materials in traditional ester electrolyte (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"/>d). Moreover, HCP also owns a superior long‐term cycling stability, e.g., exhibiting a high capacity retention up to 93% with specific capacity of 286.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles at 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"/>e). Even at a large current density of 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, it still delivers a specific capacity of 176.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> over 1000 cycles with a low rate of capacity decay of ≈0.025% per cycle. Such an excellent cycling stability at large current density is rarely reported for the HCA previously (Table <link href="#adma201903125-tbl-0001"/>). Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) images and digital photos disclose the favourable preservation of microstructure and morphology of HCP from the excellent electrode integrity after 100 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"/>f; Figure S13, Supporting Information), indicating the outstanding stability of HCP electrode during cycling in ether electrolyte. Figure S14 (Supporting Information) shows the sodium storage performance of HCP electrode in ester‐based electrolyte, which exhibits obviously worse high‐rate capability and cyclic stability compared with HCP in ether electrolyte although the values of specific capacity are almost equivalent at low current density of 20 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in both electrolytes. In addition, the HCP‐NP prepared without a preoxidation process also exhibits relatively inferior sodium storage properties than HCP in the same ether electrolyte (Figure S15, Supporting Information), which may be mainly due to the increase of graphitic nanodomain and graphitization degree in the pre‐oxidation process and hence the raise of plateau capacity (Figure S16, Supporting Information).</p><p>In order to elucidate the sodium storage mechanism of HCP in ether electrolyte, CV tests at varied scan rates were first conducted in half cells. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0003"><b>3</b></link>a, all CV curves exhibit similar shapes with the typical increase of peak current along with scan rate, indicating the excellent reversibility and high‐rate charge/discharge capability of HCP.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0043 #adma201903125-bib-0044"/> Typically, the peak current (<i>i</i>) and scan rate (<i>v</i>) meet the following equations<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0045"/><displayedItem xml:id="adma201903125-disp-0001" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/adma201903125-math-0001.png"><mrow><mi>i</mi><mtext> </mtext><mo>=</mo><mtext> </mtext><mi>a</mi><msup><mi>v</mi><mi>b</mi></msup></mrow></math></displayedItem><displayedItem xml:id="adma201903125-disp-0002" type="mathematics" numbered="yes"><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" display="block" altimg="urn:x-wiley:dummy:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-math-0002" wiley:location="graphic/adma201903125-math-0002.png"><mrow><mi>log</mi><mrow><mfenced><mi>i</mi></mfenced></mrow><mtext> </mtext><mtext> </mtext><mo>=</mo><mtext> </mtext><mtext> </mtext><mi>b</mi><mtext> </mtext><mo>×</mo><mtext> </mtext><mi>log</mi><mrow><mfenced><mi>v</mi></mfenced></mrow><mtext> </mtext><mo>+</mo><mtext> </mtext><mtext>log</mtext><mrow><mfenced><mi>a</mi></mfenced></mrow></mrow></math></displayedItem>where <i>a</i> and <i>b</i> are the adjustable parameters. If <i>b</i> value is close to 1, the electrochemical process is surface‐controlled. As <i>b</i> is close to 0.5, the electrochemical reaction mainly relies on a diffusion‐controlled process, such as sodium ions insert into the graphene interlayers.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0046"/> Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0003"/>b shows the log(<i>i</i>) versus log(<i>v</i>) plots and fitting results for two pairs of representative peaks <i>P</i>/<i>P′</i> and <i>S</i>/<i>S</i>′. It is calculated that the <i>b</i> values of <i>P</i>/<i>P</i>′ and <i>S</i>/<i>S</i>′ peaks are 0.42/0.44 and 0.92/1.10, which are close to 0.5 and 1, respectively. As a result, it can be rationally deduced that, the plateau regions at the low potential range of below 0.2 V in GCD curves (corresponding to the range of <i>P</i>/<i>P</i>′ peaks in CV) belong to a slow sodium de‐/intercalation process from/into the graphene interlayers in the graphitic nanodomain. In contrast, the high‐potential slope regions in GCD curves are a fast adsorption/desorption process at the surface‐active sites including defects. Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0003"/>c schematically illustrates such an “intercalation‐adsorption” mechanism in a charging (desodiation) process, which is consistent with the sodium storage mechanism in ester electrolyte (Figure S17, Supporting Information) as well as in the previously reported ones.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0026 #adma201903125-bib-0041"/> Furthermore, the capacities of plateau and slope regions in the charging curves at different cycles are counted to reveal the origin of capacity decay of HCP. Taking cycling at 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup> as an example (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0003"/>d), the capacity attenuation is mainly derived from the rapider reduction of plateau capacity than the slope capacity especially after 300 cycles in ether electrolyte (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0003"/>e). This may be due to the gradual damage or failure of the graphitic nanodomain in HCP during cycling, resulting in the irreversible capacity loss. Such a mechanism of intercalation‐decisive capacity decay is also existed in the ester electrolyte, as shown in Figure S18 (Supporting Information), although there is a much faster capacity attenuation from the plateau region than that in the ether electrolyte.</p><figure xml:id="adma201903125-fig-0003"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903125-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Analyses for the electrochemical data of HCP electrode in ether electrolyte: a) CV curves at varied scan rates from 0.1 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, b) plots of log(<i>i</i>) versus log(<i>v</i>) and the corresponding linear fitting, c) schematic illustration of sodium storage mechanism, d) charge curves at different cycles at 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, e) statistical results of plateau and slope capacity in (d), f) charge curves at varied current densities from 20 to 2000 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, and g) statistical results of plateau and slope capacity in inset (f).</caption></figure><p>Moreover, analyses for the variations of plateau and slope capacity at different current densities are used to reveal the main reason that affects the rate performance of HCP. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0003"/>f, the plateau capacity decreases sharply and linearly, while the slope capacity keeps almost unchanged, as current density is larger than 600 mA g<sup>−1</sup> in ether electrolyte. In the ester electrolyte as shown in Figure S19 (Supporting Information)<sub>,</sub> the similar tendency of capacity reduction is even occurred at a much lower current density of 60 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. These results suggest that, the outstanding high‐rate capability of HCP electrode is benefitted from the incredible retention of surface‐controlled capacity contribution along with the increase of current density. GITT tests are further used to calculate the apparent Na diffusion coefficient (<i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub>) in both electrolytes (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0004"><b>4</b></link>) and to analyze the HCP electrode kinetics in the whole charging/discharging processes. Figure S20 (Supporting Information) shows the detailed calculation method of <i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub> by taking a single GITT titration as an example. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0004"/>, the average <i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub> in the slope region is higher than that in the plateau region in both charging and discharging processes and also in both ester and ether electrolytes, further confirming the electrochemical adsorption/intercalation reactions in the slope/plateau regions as proposed above because of the relatively fast/slow kinetics, respectively. As a result, the plateaux are a rate‐determining step affecting the rate performance of HCP in both electrolytes compared to the slopes. In addition, the average <i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub> of plateau region in ether electrolyte is significantly higher than that in ester electrolyte, although the <i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub> values of slope region are almost the same in two electrolytes, suggesting it is the plateau‐region kinetics that is accelerated greatly by ether electrolyte rather than the slope‐region one, and hence resulting in much improved high‐rate capability in ether electrolyte. In addition, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0002"/>a and Figure S11c (Supporting Information), the voltage polarization of HCP in ether electrolyte is obviously smaller compared to the ester electrolyte, further indicating the faster sodium storage kinetics of HCP in the ether electrolyte.</p><figure xml:id="adma201903125-fig-0004"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903125-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a,b) GITT test results of the HCP electrodes in ether (a) and ester (b) electrolytes at 25 °C.</caption></figure><p>In addition to the outstanding room‐temperature performance, advanced low‐<i>T</i> sodium‐storage properties are also significant for the practical applications. However, there are still only a few public reports on the low‐<i>T</i> performance of hard carbon anode for SIBs in previous works. Considering the superior high‐rate capability and excellent retention of high <i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub> at low temperature of −25 °C of the presently developed HCP (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0005"><b>5</b></link>a), it can be rationally predicted that the HCP could be also an advanced anode with excellent low‐<i>T</i> performance for SIBs. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0005"/>b, it indeed exhibits high retention of sodium storage capacity along with the decrease of temperature. For example, at 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, the reversible capacity retentions are ≈97.3%,94.7%, 92.8%, 90.6%, and 89.0% at 15, 5, −5, −15, and −25 °C, respectively, compared to the reversible capacity of 337.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> delivered at 25 °C (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0005"/>c). Such an exciting and impressive low‐<i>T</i> performance is even much better than most of anode materials of the metal‐containing compounds recently reported for SIBs.<link href="#adma201903125-bib-0017 #adma201903125-bib-0018 #adma201903125-bib-0044 #adma201903125-bib-0047"/> Moreover, the HCP anode can still deliver a capacity of 217.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a high capacity retention of about 81% over 1000 cycles at a low temperature of −15 °C at 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#adma201903125-fig-0005"/>d), indicating its excellent low‐<i>T</i> long‐term cycle life. Such a superior low‐<i>T</i> performance combined with the abovementioned Na‐storage properties further clears the obstacles for the practical application of HCP.</p><figure xml:id="adma201903125-fig-0005"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma201903125:adma201903125-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma201903125-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>Temperature‐related sodium storage data of HCP in ether electrolyte: a) <i>D</i><sub>Na,app</sub> values calculated from GITT tests at a low temperature of −25 °C; b) temperature‐dependent capacity retention at various current densities; c) GCD curves of 50 mA g<sup>−1</sup> at the temperature range from −25 to 25 °C; and d) long‐term cyclic stability at −15 °C and 500 mA g<sup>−1</sup>.</caption></figure><p>In summary, a flexible and self‐supporting HCP derived from tissue is rationally developed for practical application in room/low‐<i>T</i> SIBs. No need for a traditional coating process for the carbon anode material, HCP achieves the comprehensively outstanding electrochemical properties in terms of high ICE (91.2%), superior high‐rate capability, ultralong cyclic stability as well as outstanding low‐<i>T</i> performance in ether electrolyte. More importantly, it is revealed that the sodium storage mechanism of HCP in the ether electrolyte is the “adsorption–intercalation” process which is same as the one in the ester electrolyte. And the capacity attenuation mainly comes from the plateau region corresponding to a certain degree of damage or failure of the graphitic‐like nanodomain. In addition, the GITT results suggest that the plateau region is a rate‐determining step with a lower electrochemical reaction kinetics in both electrolytes, and ether electrolyte can accelerate the kinetics in plateau region rather than slope region, resulting in the much better rate performance. In view of the advantages of superb room/low‐<i>T</i> Na‐storage performance, flexible self‐supporting structure, facile preparation method, low cost, environmental friendliness, the developed HCP exhibits a great potential for practical application as the anode material for SIBs. In addition, this research has provided a new strategy for the practical application of HCA and will promote the commercial development of room/low‐<i>T</i> and flexible SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="adma201903125-sec-0020" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by the National Natural Science Foundation of China (51602048), and the Fundamental Research Funds for the Central Universities (2412019ZD010).</p></section><section xml:id="adma201903125-sec-0030" type="conflictOfInterest"><title type="main">Conflict of Interest</title><p>The authors declare no conflict of interest.</p></section><bibliography xml:id="adma201903125-bibl-0001" style="numbered"><bib xml:id="adma201903125-bib-0001"><citation type="journal" xml:id="adma201903125-cit-0001"><author><givenNames>L.</givenNames> <familyName>Li</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>Y.</givenNames> <familyName>Zheng</familyName></author>, <author><givenNames>S.
