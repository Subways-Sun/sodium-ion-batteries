However, intrinsic g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> exhibits poor electronic conductivity, low reversible Na‐storage capacity, and insufficient cyclability. DFT calculations suggest that this could be due to a large Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion barrier in the innate g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> nanosheet. A facile one‐pot heating of a mixture of low‐cost urea and asphalt is strategically applied to yield stacked multilayer C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composites with improved Na‐storage capacity (about 2 times higher than that of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>, up to 254 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), rate capability, and cyclability. A C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> sodium‐ion full cell (in which sodium rhodizonate dibasic is used as the positive electrode) demonstrates high Coulombic efficiency (ca. 99.8 %) and a negligible capacity fading over 14 000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p><b>Flatland composites</b>: Low‐cost carbon/graphitic carbon nitride (C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>) composites can be used as the negative electrode for a long‐life sodium‐ion battery.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201905803:anie201905803-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201905803-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie201905803-sec-0001"><p>Electric vehicles are one of the most promising ways of reducing carbon emissions from the transportation sector. Although the electric vehicle market is growing, the cost of lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) is one of the major hurdles standing in the way of widespread use of electric cars. To greatly reduce the cost of a battery and make it commercially viable, battery materials should be chosen to be low‐cost, abundant, easy‐processable, and non‐toxic.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0001"/> Towards a long‐life rocking‐chair battery system, these materials should be able to undergo reversible intercalation with minimal volume changes during operation.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0002"/> There is keen interest in developing alternative rocking‐chair batteries,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0003"/> for example, sodium‐ion batteries (NIBs), owing to the abundance of sodium relative to lithium.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0004"/> However, one of the biggest challenges facing NIBs is the negative electrode.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0004c #anie201905803-bib-0005"/> Although graphite electrodes are attractive for LIBs due to their low cost,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0006"/> these materials are thermodynamically unstable with high Na content and therefore suffer from very low Na storage (&lt;35 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0004a"/></p><p>Recently reported carbon‐based negative electrodes<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0004b #anie201905803-bib-0005"/> (for example, hard carbon with an intrinsically disordered microstructure,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0007"/> heteroatom‐doped carbon,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0008"/> slope‐dominated carbon,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0009"/> nitrogen‐rich graphene,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0010"/> and 3D amorphous carbon<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0011"/>) demonstrate impressive reversible Na‐storage capacity (for example, 150–750 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>; Supporting Information, Table S1) and cyclic performance (for example, 100–3400 cycles; Supporting Information, Table S1). However, most of them are synthesized at high temperatures in the range 800–1400 °C.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0007 #anie201905803-bib-0009 #anie201905803-bib-0010 #anie201905803-bib-0011"/> Furthermore, developing carbon negative electrodes with high reversible Na‐capacity mainly from the sloping region (which alleviates the issues of Na plating and polarization) still remains unsatisfactory.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0007 #anie201905803-bib-0009"/> Layered two‐dimensional graphene‐like graphitic carbon nitride (g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>) nanosheets are an obvious candidate owing to easy scalability (via simple polymerization or polycondensation in the temperature range 400–600 °C),<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012"/> low cost,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012 #anie201905803-bib-0013"/> chemical stability in different environments (for example, acid, base, and organic solvents)<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0014"/> and potentially high rate capability.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0007 #anie201905803-bib-0015"/> Theoretical studies by pioneers<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012 #anie201905803-bib-0016"/> show that g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> has a high Li‐storage capacity up to 524 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, which could indicate a similarly promising application with Na.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0016d"/> However, g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> exhibits a poor electronic conductivity,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0015"/> low reversible Na‐storage capacity (for example, 10 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>)<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0016d"/> and insufficient cyclability caused by the irreversible intercalation reaction.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0016b"/> To improve its electronic conductivity and cyclability, g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> should be modified to deliver a high density of pyridinic terminal bonds and a low density of quaternary graphitic nitrogen species.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0016a"/>–<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0016c"/> By applying these findings and density functional theory (DFT) calculation, Adekoya et al. developed a high‐Li‐storage g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> fiber with abundant Li‐storage edges and pores, and a remarkable specific capacity of about 182 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> was achieved with a half‐cell device.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012"/> To yield a low‐cost and long‐life NIB, we seek to develop a high‐performance g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> based electrode material.</p><p>We show using DFT calculation that a rather large Na diffusion barrier of about 2.2 eV in a g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> sheet with a path from one adsorption site to another (Supporting Information, Figure S1). Liu et al. reported the diffusion barrier for Li<sup>+</sup> ions in graphite is in the range of 0.218–0.4 eV.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0017"/> To accelerate the ion diffusion inside the active matrix and improve its electronic conductivity, we hypothesize that coupling electrically conductive amorphous carbon with g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> to form a composite would lead to a lower Na<sup>+</sup> ion diffusion barrier and could be a feasible strategy for a high performance NIB anode. To prepare a carbon/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composite, a facile one‐pot heating of a mixture of urea and asphalt under N<sub>2</sub> atmosphere is used (as illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0001"/>). In a typical formation process, the thermal condensation of urea creates a layered carbon nitride template, which binds the as‐formed aromatic carbon intermediates to its surface by means of donor–acceptor interactions and finally confines their condensation in a cooperative process to the interlayer gaps of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> at 600 °C.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0018"/> Owing to the appreciable flow behavior of the melted asphalt at high temperature, good adhesion between the two phases (that is, urea and asphalt) can be achieved.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0019"/> We believe this carbon addition significantly improves the electronic conductivity of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub><link href="#anie201905803-bib-0020"/> and enhances the ion diffusion between the adsorption sites. We demonstrate that the mass ratio of urea to asphalt (or precursors) can be improved with our best ratio of 1:0.2 in terms of electrochemical performance (Supporting Information, Figure S2).</p><figure xml:id="anie201905803-fig-0001"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201905803:anie201905803-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201905803-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Illustration of the preparation of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. This material can be used as a host for sodium ions.</p></caption></figure><p>To understand the crystalline nature of the as‐prepared materials, we use X‐ray diffraction (XRD) spectroscopy where our results are shown in Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0002"/> A. The g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> control system shows a typical peak at about 27° (<i>d</i>‐spacing=0.331 nm) which can be assigned to the interlayer‐stacking of aromatic groups.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012"/> The other characteristic peak of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> at about 13° (derived from the in‐plane ordering of tri‐s‐triazine motifs) is absent or weak, which could be attributed to the destroyed stacking structure with decreased planar size of the layers.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012 #anie201905803-bib-0021"/> This result fits well with the references.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012"/> Similarly, C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> shows a broader peak for the stacking of aromatic groups, but it shifts to about 25° (<i>d</i>‐spacing=0.358 nm). This indicates the coexistence of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and turbostratic carbon,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0020"/> which could introduce a unique stacked multilayer structure. Compared to the counterparts, the Raman spectrum of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> shows the D peak (at about 1340 cm<sup>−1</sup>) shifts toward higher wavenumbers (or blue‐shift), suggesting the π–π stacking interactions between turbostratic carbon and electron‐withdrawing g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> nanosheets (Supporting Information, Figure S3).<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0022"/> We further use X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) measurements to study the chemical compositions of the as‐prepared materials. Both spectra (that is, g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>) consist of the following peaks located at about 285, 400 and 532 eV, and can be assigned to C 1s, N 1s, and O 1s (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0002"/> B). The trace amount of oxygen could be attributed to the precursor (that is, urea) and/or the solvent (that is, ethanol) used during synthesis.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012"/> By integrating the peak area, C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> shows an atomic C/N ratio of about 11 while 0.82 for g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. We note that such a high C/N ratio for g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> has been reported and can be attributed to the complete condensation.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0023"/> Detailed element concentration of these as‐prepared materials is shown in the Supporting Information, Table S2. The results indicate that the ratio of amorphous carbon to g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> in the C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composite is about 83:15. We further show high‐resolution XPS spectra of the C 1s and N 1s regions for both g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> (Figures <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0002"/> C and D). Four peaks centered at binding energies of 284.8, 286.2, 288.1, and 289.0 eV are obtained after deconvolution of the C 1s spectrum. The four peaks are attributed to the adventitious sp<sup>2</sup> C−C carbon species, sp<sup>3</sup> hybridized carbon atoms in C−O, sp<sup>2</sup> hybridized carbon atoms in N‐C‐N, and those attached through the −NH<sub>2</sub> group, respectively.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012 #anie201905803-bib-0018b #anie201905803-bib-0024"/> In line with the surface chemistries, less nitrogen‐related bonds is observed for C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. The N 1s spectrum of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> is deconvoluted into three peaks at 398.6, 399.5, and 401.1 eV (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0002"/> C). These three peaks are assigned to sp<sup>2</sup>‐hybridized nitrogen (pyridine‐N) in triazine rings (C−N=C), tertiary nitrogen bonded to carbon atoms (pyrrolic‐N) in the form of N‐C<sub>3</sub>, and graphitic‐N in the form of C‐N‐H, respectively.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0025"/> For the N 1s spectrum of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>, only two peaks can be resolved, that is, pyridine‐N and graphitic‐N (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0002"/> D). Interestingly, we find the content of pyridine‐N is slightly higher than graphitic‐N, which might lead to improved Li/Na storage according to previous works.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0012 #anie201905803-bib-0016d"/></p><figure xml:id="anie201905803-fig-0002"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201905803:anie201905803-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201905803-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>A) XRD spectra of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. Insets are the photographs of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> (yellow) and C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> powders (black), respectively. B) XPS surface spectra of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. High‐resolution XPS spectra of the C) C 1s and D) N 1s regions for g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>To characterize the morphology of the as‐prepared materials, we use scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). We reveal using SEM that a sheet‐like structure of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> is formed (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0003"/> A). This finding further confirms the stacking structure of g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> is destroyed (as discussed in the XRD section). Similarly, C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> also exhibits a noticeable sheet‐like structure (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0003"/> B). To have direct visible proof of the existence of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composite, we characterized the microstructure using TEM. We illustrate an isolated pieces of the as‐prepared g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> which display a two‐dimensional sheet‐like structure with a lateral scale of several micrometers (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0003"/> C). Interestingly, we observe a stacked multilayer structure for C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composites (Figures <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0003"/> D–F). More TEM images of multiple composite samples are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S4. Based on the energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) elemental analysis in TEM‐high angular annular dark field (TEM‐HAADF) mode (Supporting Information, Figure S5), we suspect that the stacking structure of the composite is formed by multiple carbon‐based dark layers attached to the sheet‐like isolated g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> pieces via donor–acceptor interactions<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0018"/> (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0003"/> F). This observation correlates with the previous works<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0018"/> suggesting that polymeric planes of carbon nitrides are developed even with the presence of asphalt or as‐formed aromatic carbon intermediates. We believe such a stacked multilayer structure of the C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composite is stable (Note: 30 min ultrasonication was used to prepare these TEM samples). Apart from improving the electronic conductivity, the stacked multilayer structure of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composites can further introduce additional Na storage (as confirmed by the latter section).</p><figure xml:id="anie201905803-fig-0003"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201905803:anie201905803-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201905803-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>SEM images of the as‐prepared A) g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and B) C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. TEM images of C) g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> and D)–F) C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>.</p></caption></figure><p>The electrochemical performance of the as‐prepared C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> is first evaluated with a half‐cell device, in which Na metal is used as the counter electrode. To understand the Na‐storage mechanism, we conduct cyclic voltammetry (CV) and g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> is also included for comparison. In both cyclic voltammograms (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0004"/> A), the reversible peaks at very low voltage values (vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>), that is, at about 0.02 V in reduction and about 0.1 V in oxidation, could be due to the intercalation/deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions into the nanopores of the carbon materials.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0026"/> However, most Na‐storage capacity of these electrode materials stems from the potential window between 1.2 and 0.3 V, which may be assigned to the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion between the interlayers.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0026"/> Interestingly, C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> shows about two times higher Na‐storage capacity in the region between 1.2 and 0.3 V. This finding suggests that the stacked multilayer structure of the C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composite leads to higher Na storage (note that all specific current densities and capacities are based on the total mass of the negative active materials). In line with the CV results, the C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na half cell demonstrates excellent C‐rate performance. Specifically, the cell shows 254, 220, 186, 170, 159, and 151 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0004"/> B). In contrast, inferior rate performance is observed for the g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na half cell, indicating the improved performance for the composite is due to the carbon addition. By studying the electrochemical performance of the asphalt‐derived carbon (Supporting Information, Figure S6), the improved performance of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> is mainly due to its unique stacked multilayer structure. Specifically, the average highest Na capacity of the asphalt‐derived carbon is about 150 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Supporting Information, Figure S6, this is consistent with a previous work<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0027"/> in which the pristine asphalt‐derived carbon showed an average Na capacity of ca. 140 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>) while 110 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> for g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0004"/> B). Therefore, 1 g of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> can only deliver a capacity of about 141 mAh based on the ratio of amorphous carbon to g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> (that is, 83:15). In fact, the as‐prepared C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> can show a higher capacity of about 260 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. This means additional Na storage (about 120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) is introduced by the interlayers of the stacked multilayer C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> composites (as confirmed by CV study, Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0004"/> A). The characterization of the as‐prepared Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> is shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S8. The CV of this full cell displays one pair of well‐defined redox peaks located at 1.2–2.5 V, which directly indicates the operating voltage for this type of Na full cell (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0005"/> B). The response current increases rapidly during the forward scan in the region between 2.8 to 3 V and is due to the formation of Na<sub><i>x</i>≤2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> with higher oxidation state.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0034b"/> To avoid any irreversible side reactions at the positive side (Supporting Information, Figures S8 and S9), we operate this full cell with a cutoff voltage window between 0.01 to 3.0 V. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0005"/> C, this C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na full cell delivers good C‐rate performance. Specifically, the cell shows 172, 148, 120, and 96 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0005"/> C). We demonstrate high Coulombic efficiency (CE, ca. 99.8 %), an average energy efficiency (EE) of about 75 %, and illustrate that the discharge capacity remains at about 120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 14 000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie201905803-fig-0005"/> D). Remarkably, this finding discloses a negligible capacity fading for this C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na full cell over a long‐term operation period (about 6 months). Specific charge/discharge curves are shown in the Supporting Information, Figure S10. We note a gradual increase of discharge capacity upon cycling to 3000 cycles and subsequently stabilize at about 120 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>. This gradual increment in deliverable capacity could be ascribed to the activation of the electrode and/or system. Such long cycling performance is most likely ascribed to the superior structural stability of the electrode materials. The NIBs with C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> demonstrate a very high capacity among the g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> based negative electrode materials (Supporting Information, Table S4). Furthermore, they show a very impressive cyclability among NIBs with carbon‐based electrode materials (Supporting Information, Table S1). Herein we report for the first time an ultralong cycle‐life NIB with low‐cost and scalable materials. We note that higher energy density of rocking‐chair batteries can be achieved by operating them in larger voltage windows, and some reported systems showed impressive performance with a large voltage window (for example, up to 4 V; Supporting Information, Table S1). Trials have been done to explore the potential of this C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na full cell (Supporting Information, Figure S11). Compared to the case with a 0.01–3 V cut‐off, lower average CE (ca. 98.5 %) and relatively higher capacity are observed when the cut‐off voltage range is 0.01–3.5 V (Supporting Information, Figure S11A,B). Serious electrolyte decomposition and extremely low initial CE (44 %) are found when extending to a 4 V cut‐off (Supporting Information, Figure S11C). Although the capacity fading over cycles is serious, the as‐prepared C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> shows a maximum capacity up to about 700 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (Supporting Information, Figure S11C). The low CE observed in these trials could be mainly due to the decomposition of electrolyte because the electrolyte used for this full cell (that is, 0.8 <sc>m</sc> sodium perchlorate in the binary solvents of ethylene carbonate and diethylene carbonate) starts to oxidize beyond 3.5 V vs. Na/Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0035"/> Furthermore, irreversible phase transformation of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> under high voltage operation should be solved to achieve higher performance. Further improvement in the electrochemical stability and performance could be achieved by the choices of electrolyte solvents,<link href="#anie201905803-bib-0034b"/> additives (for example, fluoroethylene carbonate) and electrode preparation methods (for example, roll‐pressing).</p><figure xml:id="anie201905803-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201905803:anie201905803-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201905803-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>A) Configuration of a C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na full cell consisting of a positive electrode of Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> and a negative electrode of C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>. D) Cycling performance of a C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> Na full cell at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>.</p></caption></figure><p>In summary, we synthesized low‐cost carbon/graphitic carbon nitride (C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub>) composites as the negative electrode for a long‐life sodium‐ion battery. Compared to the pristine g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> counterpart, approximately two times higher Na‐storage capacity was achieved for this composite. We also demonstrated that C/g‐C<sub>3</sub>N<sub>4</sub> can be combined with Na<sub>2</sub>C<sub>6</sub>O<sub>6</sub> to create a full cell with high CE and a stable cycling (&gt;14 000 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>).
