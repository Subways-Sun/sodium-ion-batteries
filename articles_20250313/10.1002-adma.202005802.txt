Herein, starting from well‐organized WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods, an ingenious design of a one‐step method is proposed to prepare a bimetallic sulfide composite with a coaxial carbon coating layer, simply enabled by ZIF‐8 introduction. Rich sulfur vacancies and WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterojunctions can be simultaneously developed, that significantly improve ionic and electronic diffusion kinetics. In addition, a homogeneous carbon protective layer around the surface of the composite guarantees an outstanding structural stability, a reversible capacity of 170.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at a high rate of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. A great potential in practical application is also exhibited, where a full cell based on the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C anode and the P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Ni<sub>1/3</sub>Mn<sub>1/3</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode can maintain a reversible capacity of 89.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 500 cycles at 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Moreover, the underlying electrochemical Na storage mechanisms are illustrated in detail by theoretical calculations, electrochemical kinetic analysis, and operando X‐ray diffraction characterization.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="adma202005802-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>A new‐type W<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite with special sulfide vacancies and heterostructures is successfully synthesized using a facile one‐step metal−organic frameworks (MOFs)‐engaged method, which shows excellent electrochemical performances when applied in sodium‐ion batteries. The rich sulfur vacancies and WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterojunctions can significantly improve the ion‐ and electron‐diffusion kinetics. In addition, a homogeneous carbon protective layer around the surface of the composite guarantees an outstanding structural stability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202005802:adma202005802-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202005802-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body xml:id="adma202005802-body-0001" sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="adma202005802-sec-0010" type="opening"><p>The renewable energy sources with intermittent nature call for fast development of electrical energy storage (EES) devices for practical applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Over the past decades, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have pervaded our daily lives, ranging from portable electronics to large‐scale EES systems.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the cost of rare lithium resources involving electrical grid and large‐scale storage purposes have raised widespread concerns. In this regard, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) are highly promising to meet these demands due to that sodium is practically inexhaustible and easily accessible around the globe.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the higher standard electrochemical potential of Na<sup>+</sup>/Na (−2.71 V versus SHE) than that of Li<sup>+</sup>/Li (−3.04 versus SHE) and the larger ion radius of Na<sup>+</sup> compared with Li<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å versus 0.76 Å) mean that SIBs possess a lower energy density, and most conventional electrode materials of LIBs are not suitable for SIBs. Hence, it is of great significance to explore advanced electrode materials that could provide satisfactory specific capacities and rapid ion diffusion kinetics. So far, the development of the cathode materials for SIBs has progressed rapidly, including layered oxides<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and polyanionic compounds.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As for the anodes, although hard carbon as a hotspot has been widely studied due to its high capacity and lower voltage platform,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> the random adsorption sites and irregular channels for Na<sup>+</sup> migration lead to a relatively poor sodium‐ion diffusion.</p><p>2D transition metal chalcogenides (TMCs) have been broadly reported as a kind of promising electrode materials for both LIBs and SIBs due to their open framework and unique electrochemical properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0007 #adma202005802-bib-0008"><sup>7,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among them, WS<sub>2</sub> as a typical 2D TMCs has a much larger interlayer spacing of 0.62 nm and weaker van der Waals interaction, which enables fast reversible Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion and avoids terrible volume expansion during Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation/deintercalation processes.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, the terrible issue of pure WS<sub>2</sub> anode applied in SIBs is its low intrinsic electronic conductivity, significantly limiting the specific capacity, and rate performance.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Generally, the electrochemical properties of materials are strongly dependent on the conductivity of electrode materials as well as the diffusion rate of Na<sup>+</sup>. Thus, the scrupulous design and rational controllable synthesis of WS<sub>2</sub> anode with excellent transport kinetics to present long cycle life and superior rate performance are highly desirable. For example: i) preparing nano‐scaled powders to shorten the diffusion pathway for ions like 1D materials (nanowires,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0007 #adma202005802-bib-0008 #adma202005802-bib-0011"><sup>7,8,11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> nanorods<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and nanofiber<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0013 #adma202005802-bib-0014"><sup>13,14</sup></link><sup>]</sup>); and ii) mixing with conductively carbonaceous materials (graphene,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> carbon nanotube,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0009 #adma202005802-bib-0016"><sup>9,16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and doped carbon<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0017"><sup>17</sup></link><sup>]</sup>) to improve electron conductivity.</p><p>From abovementioned two perspectives, most of the studies mainly focus on the morphology control and modification of electrode materials to elevate the diffusion kinetics. However, there is, as yet, little research on how to aim at regulating internal crystal structure of the materials to further accelerate ion transport rate. As we all know, the ion transport in a material is in two forms: interstitial diffusion and vacancy diffusion. For interstitial diffusion, metal sulfides already have an advantage due to its inherent layered open framework. Therefore, constructing moderate vacancies in crystal structure to offer effective vacancy diffusion could be expected to considerably improve the ion transport in metal sulfides.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0018"><sup>18</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In recent years, the introduction of oxygen vacancies (<i>V</i><sub>O</sub>) into metal oxides has drawn much attention due to that these intrinsic defects could significantly enhance the electrochemical performance in the following aspects: a) <i>V</i><sub>O</sub> can induce excess electrons around the specific metal atoms, which could be treated as centers for negative charge to attract Na<sup>+</sup> and promote fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics; b) <i>V</i><sub>O</sub> can act as an electronic charge carrier to greatly improve the electrical conductivity; c) <i>V</i><sub>O</sub> can offer extra active sites for redox reactions to increase the capacitance behavior. Inspired by this, this work attempts to construct sulfur vacancies (<i>V</i><sub>S</sub>) in metal sulfide, which can induce localized electron centers to expedite ionic/electronic diffusion kinetics and show enhanced electrochemical properties.</p><p>In addition, building electrodes with heterostructure presents an excellent potential in the field of optoelectronics devices and high‐speed electronics owing to that it could providing unprecedented effects at the interfaces.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0019"><sup>19</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Two nanocrystal couples with different bandgaps could encouragingly stimulate the built‐in electric field effect, which allows for fast charge transport and satisfactory reaction kinetics.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0020"><sup>20</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Guo et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0021"><sup>21</sup></link><sup>]</sup> designed SnS/SnO<sub>2</sub> heterostructures with internal electric fields that shows an enhanced charge‐transfer capability. Li et al.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0022"><sup>22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> prepared an ordered nanotube arrays of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub> simply enabled by surface sulfurization. The heterostructure of oxide and sulfide could spontaneously develop a built‐in electric field, which significantly reduces the activation energy and accelerates charge transport. Furthermore, Ou's group successfully fabricated a series of heterogeneous anode materials with interior self‐built‐in electric‐field induced by phase boundaries which can enhance the reaction kinetics and boost the charge transfer, including hierarchical Bi<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>/MoS<sub>2</sub>,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Sb<sub>2</sub>S<sub>3</sub>@FeS<sub>2</sub> hollow nanorods,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> (SnCo)S<sub>2</sub> nanocubes,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and so on.</p><p>Herein, we report an ingenious method to synthesize a bimetallic sulfide/carbon composite with special sulfur vacancies and heterostructures, which render fast electrochemical kinetics and superior reversible capacities when used as anode for SIBs. This method can be called “kill three birds with one stone”, mainly assisted by metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) during synthesis process. MOFs have gained enormous attention in term of the synthesis for porous carbon and TMCs/carbon hybrids, because of perfect combination of metal ions and organic ligands into regular crystal structure.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among various MOFs, the zeolitic imidazolate framework‐8 (ZIF‐8) as a template or precursor can be easily transformed into homogeneous transition metal sulfide with N‐doped carbon after pyrolysis under inert atmosphere.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0027"><sup>27</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Therefore, introducing ZIF‐8 to pure WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods with subsequent calcination is a feasible method to prepare our target product.</p><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, uniform ZIF‐8 nanoparticle layer should be grown in situ on the surface of bulk WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods. After calcination, an even carbon protective layer is easily formed on the WS<sub>2</sub> surface. Moreover, since the electronegativity of the metal Zn is stronger than that of metal W, which means it is easier for Zn to combine with S, it would induce the generation of WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterostructures and simultaneously the formation of rich sulfur vacancies in bulk WS<sub>2</sub>. In virtue of the integration of this coaxial nanostructure and multicomponent architecture, this unique design concept endows the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite with multiple advantages: i) the carbon coating layer allows for quick electron migration and excellent electronic conductivity, while for suppression of volume expansion during cycling that can guarantee the structural stability of the composite; ii) the formed WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterostructures could boost extra charge transfer to enhance the reaction kinetics benefited by the build‐in electric field effect; iii) <i>V</i><sub>S</sub> produced in WS<sub>2</sub> crystal can not only provide more reaction active sites for better Na storage, but also induce excess electrons around W metal atoms, which is regarded as centers for negative charge to boost fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion rates. Consequently, the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite exhibits remarkable cycle durability and outstanding rate performance when applied as anode for SIBs. Furthermore, the density functional theory (DFT) calculations help us in depth understand the mechanism for the enhanced electronic conductivity and the fast ion transportation in the WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS@C composite in terms of the co‐construction of sulfur vacancies and heterointerface. Our work sheds a light on the synergistic effect of the multicomponent composite on the excellent Na storage kinetics, which is expected that this smart synthetic strategy and rational interface engineering could be extended to the development for other advanced energy storage materials.</p><figure xml:id="adma202005802-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202005802:adma202005802-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202005802-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic diagram of synthetic route for coaxial anode material WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C nanorods. b−e) SEM images of the WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods (b), the WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods with a ZIF‐8 coating layer (c), the WS<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub>/ZnS@C nanorods (d), and a zoom‐in of a WS<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub>/ZnS@C nanorod (e).</caption></figure><p>Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of the pristine WS<sub>2</sub> nanorods are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0001">1b</link>, where the uniform diameter of 150−200 nm and length of about 5 µm are evident. From the SEM image (Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0001">1c</link>) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image (Figure S1, Supporting Information) of WS<sub>2</sub>@MOF, we can clearly observe that there is a close‐grained and uniform MOFs nanoparticles coating layer (confirmed by X‐ray diffraction pattern in Figure S2, Supporting Information) on the surface of sample WS<sub>2</sub> (WS), and the length of WS<sub>2</sub>@MOF precursor is reduced to around 3−4 µm, may due to fracture of WS<sub>2</sub> during fabrication processes. After calcination, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0001">1d,e</link>, the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C (WZSC) retains its nanorod morphology.</p><p>The TEM image of the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite presented in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> shows a nanorod with a typical coaxial morphology that is wrapped by an even conductive carbon layer derived from ZIF‐8, which is in line with the SEM images. The outer carbon layer can not only elevate the electronic conductivity, but also provide a buffer layer for sample WZSC nanorods to effectively alleviate the stress caused by volume change during cycling and improve the structural stability of WZSC. To explore the structure of the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite, we adopt high resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) and selected area electron diffraction (SAED) to further analyze this sample. In Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2b</link>, the lattice distance of 6.18 Å and amorphous region correspond to the (002) plane of WS<sub>2</sub> and carbon phase, respectively. From different regions of this nanorod with the lattice distance of 2.3 Å exhibited in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2c</link>, is indexed to the (101) plane of ZnS, demonstrating the as‐prepared nanorods are indeed comprised by WS<sub>2</sub>, ZnS, and C phases. This conclusion is further confirmed by the SAED pattern (Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2d</link>), showing the clear diffraction signals of WS<sub>2</sub> corresponding to (002), (100), and (110) planes (marked with blue lines) and that of ZnS corresponding to (100), (101), and (110) planes (marked with orange lines). Also, the energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDX) was used to understand the distribution of C, S, Zn, and W within the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite (Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2e</link> and Figure S3, Supporting Information), one can see that these four elements are uniformly distributed within nanorods, with some Zn‐riched areas observed, demonstrating the tight junction of WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS two phases and the well formation of heterostructures. The accurate contents of W, Zn, and S in the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite were confirmed by inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry. After the introduction of ZIF‐8 and calcination, apart from the diffraction peaks for WS<sub>2</sub>, a set of new peaks can be detected, which corresponds to wurtzite structure ZnS (PDF card No. 36‐1456) with the space group Pmc. Thus, we can just observe WS<sub>2</sub> peaks in the spectrum for sample WZSC and without obvious ZnS peak. Besides, the defected D band and E<sub>2g</sub> vibration of sp<sub>2</sub>‐hybridized G band of carbonaceous materials can be clear seen at 1343 and 1593 cm<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, which confirm the presence of carbon layer in sample WZSC.</p><p>X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) characterizations are performed to determine the surface components of the WS and WZSC. For sample WS, both of W and S were detected, and W4f and S2p regions are shown in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2h,i</link>. In the W4f region, a pair of peaks were observed at 35.1 and 33.0 eV, corresponding to the W4f5/2 and W4f7/2 characteristic peaks of the W−S bond. Besides, the peaks at ≈38.6 and 36.0 eV, corresponding to the characteristic peaks of the W−O bond, is due to the unavoidable oxidation of superficial WS<sub>2</sub> in air. Analogously, two pairs of peaks were observed in the S2p region, corresponding to the S species in S−W and S−O forms, respectively. The rich N could potentially improve the electrical conductivity of the carbon within the composite,<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0030"><sup>30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and meanwhile the N‐doped sites will facilitate Na storage.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0031"><sup>31</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Compared to sample WS, the contents of W and S in the WZSC's external surface decrease greatly. Moreover, in the W4f region of WZSC (Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2l</link>), the W4f5/2 and W4f7/2 characteristic peaks move to lower binding energy values of 34.9 to 32.6 eV, comparing to the W4f peaks of WS in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0002">2h</link>, which directly proves the formation of sulfur vacancies in WS<sub>2</sub> phase for sample WZSC.</p><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a</link> exhibits the cycle stabilities and Coulombic efficiencies of the WS<sub>2</sub> and the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite over a voltage range of 0.01−3.0 V at the current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Meanwhile, the introduction of ZnS phase could build the stable chemical bonds among heterojunction, effectively improves the structural stability. In addition, carbon coating layer, in the other way, works as a protective buffer to suppress volume change. As expected, the coaxial structure of WZSC nanorods can still be maintained after 500 cycles (Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0003">3b,d</link>), yet the original morphology of sample WS can hardly be found in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0003">3c</link>. Outstanding long‐term cycling performance is obtained for sample WZSC, a reversible capacity of 170.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a stable Coulombic efficiency for 5000 cycles (<b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>). The tested electrode was prepared by directly mixing WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C powder with poly tetra fluoroethylene (binder) and acetylene black to form self‐supported electrode slice. The cell was cycled at a current density of 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The results are shown in Figure S8a, Supporting Information, color transformed from black to orange demonstrates the strengthening intensity. The peaks at around 38.6°, 41.2°, and 44.1° are assigned to the BeO material derived from Be window.<sup>[</sup><link href="#adma202005802-bib-0023 #adma202005802-bib-0024"><sup>23,24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> According to the voltage platforms shown in Figure S9, Supporting Information, we divided the initial charge/discharge curves for the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C anode into four parts. In Figure S10a, Supporting Information, the d spacings of 3.76 and 2.31 Å are obviously observed, which are consistent with the (111) and (220) planes of Na<sub>2</sub>S. Meanwhile, the d spacings of 2.47 and 2.03 Å can be indexed to the (002) and (200) crystal planes of metallic zinc and tungsten, respectively, demonstrating a conversion reaction between WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS composite and inserted Na<sup>+</sup>. Compared with pristine structure, although with ZnS amorphization, the unambiguous phase boundary between WS<sub>2</sub> and ZnS maintains well, suggesting the built‐in electric field effect originated from heterostructure could still work in the following cycles.</p><p>To demonstrate the vital role of constructed sulfur vacancies and heterostructures in improving the electrochemical performance of WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C anode for sodium storage, DFT calculations were performed. In order to simplify the simulation, perfect WS<sub>2</sub> with no defect was first used to construct the heterostructure and to study the impact of heterostructure on the charge distribution with the composite. Crystal structure of WS<sub>2</sub>(001)/ZnS(001) heterojunction is constructed, as shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a–c</link>, and the charge density difference is calculated. The calculated results reveal imbalanced charge distribution in the WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterostructure, and significant charge transfer occurs from ZnS to WS<sub>2</sub>. Consequently, the bandgap of WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterostructure is greatly changed. As shown in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0005">5d</link>, the bandgaps of bulk‐phase WS<sub>2</sub> and ZnS are 1.569 and 2.034 eV, respectively, which are similar to the results in Material Project (ZnS mp‐10281: 2.048, WS<sub>2</sub> mp‐224: 1.597). As shown in Figure S11, Supporting Information, the electronic energy bandgap of WS<sub>2</sub> with the sulfur vacancy was reduced to 0.98 eV. Above results indicate the enhanced electronic conductivity after the introduction of ZnS into WS<sub>2</sub> structure, via the formation of WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterojunctions and the sulfur vacancies in WS<sub>2</sub> simultaneously.</p><figure xml:id="adma202005802-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:09359648:media:adma202005802:adma202005802-fig-0005" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/adma202005802-fig-0005-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a−c) Crystal structure and charge density difference of ZnS (001)/WS<sub>2</sub> (001) heterojunction from side (a), top (b), and bottom (c) views. The minimum energy paths in these two structures were identified, as shown in Figure <link href="#adma202005802-fig-0005">5f,g</link>, respectively. The calculated energy barrier for Na‐ion diffusion in WS<sub>2</sub> with the sulfur vacancy is 0.22 eV, which is much lower than that in WS<sub>2</sub> without the sulfur vacancy (0.33 eV). These results strongly show that the sulfur vacancy in WS<sub>2</sub> structure could effectively decrease the diffusion barrier of Na, thus to accelerate Na‐ion diffusion, which presents the internal mechanism of WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C anode with superior rate performance. Ultimately, owing to these two synergetic effects caused by the heterostructure and the sulfur vacancy, the electronic and ionic conductivity of WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C are enhanced simultaneously, which leads to a great electrochemical performance of WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C as the SIB anode.</p><p>In summary, a new‐type WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite is successfully synthesized using a facile one‐step MOFs‐engaged method. With rich sulfur vacancies, special heterostructure and a highly homogeneous carbon coating layer, the WS<sub>2−</sub><i><sub>x</sub></i>/ZnS@C composite shows excellent electrochemical performances when applied in SIBs. Outstanding long‐term cycling performance is obtained from this composite, a reversible capacity of 170.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at a high current density of 5 A g<sup>−1</sup>. Electrochemical kinetic analysis uncovers that the dynamic behavior mainly depends on intercalation pseudocapacitive mechanism with a high capacitive contribution up to 70.88% for as‐prepared electrode at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. In situ XRD characterization and ex situ HRTEM images reveal the Na storage mechanism is based on conversion reaction of WS<sub>2</sub> and ZnS. Besides, DFT calculations resonantly prove that the significant effects of sulfur vacancies and heterojunctions on the improvement for ions/electrons diffusion kinetics. Overall, we determine that the co‐construction of sulfur vacancies and WS<sub>2</sub>/ZnS heterojunctions can effectively promote the electrochemical performance of WS<sub>2</sub> as an anode for SIBs.
