Sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted enormous attention owing to the wide distribution, high natural abundance and potentially low cost of sodium compared to lithium.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0002"/> Given that SIBs have great potential at electrical grid systems,<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0003"/> cost, elemental abundance and specific capacity should be the primary considerations for a qualified cathode material. Manganese is an earth‐abundant, low‐cost, and low‐toxic transition metal. Among various cathodes that have been investigated, layered sodium transition metal oxides (Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>TMO<sub>2</sub>), especially Mn‐based materials, are considered highly of the potential to meet the above requirements and thus present a great promise for the application of SIBs.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0004"/></p><p>The Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> layered oxides, have been firstly studied as positive materials of SIBs over 30 years ago<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0005"/> while their phase variations<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0006"/> had been discussed much earlier. Based on the MnO<sub>2</sub>‐slab stacking patterns along the <i>c</i>‐axis, the layered Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> can be grouped into two polymorphs, namely the O3‐type NaMnO<sub>2</sub> (ABC oxygen stacking) and the P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> (ABBA oxygen stacking) structures. It was reported that O3‐type Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> exhibits poor rate capability and fast capacity degradation because of their unfavored Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion paths and complicated phase‐transition processes.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0004a #anie201911698-bib-0007"/> In contrast, the distorted P2′‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> phase<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0008"/> was reported to deliver higher capacities than the undistorted P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0009"/> And unlike LiMnO<sub>2</sub>, Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> is hard to transforms from layered phases to spinel structures.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0007b #anie201911698-bib-0010"/> Even so, the electrochemical performances of P2/P2′‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> are still far from expectations due to the strong Jahn–Teller effect associated with Mn<sup>3+</sup>/Mn<sup>4+</sup> redox couple. The presence of Jahn–Teller effect causes P2–P2′ phase transformation thus lowers Na<sup>+</sup> mobility and further leads to stresses, structural defects and amorphization during repeated cycling.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0007a #anie201911698-bib-0011"/> Besides, the multiple structure transformations induced by oxygen layer glides were also reported to have detrimental effects on the Na<sup>+</sup> transport and electrochemical reversibility.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0007a #anie201911698-bib-0012"/></p><p>A great number of studies have focused on the substitution of the Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller center by various transition metals in P2/P2′‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0013"/> One of the most successful examples should be Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mg<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0014"/> Magnesium is an earth abundant element and very effective to smooth the charge–discharge curves, reduce polarization and improve capacity retention of P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0014a"/> Grey's group showed that Mg‐substitution is able to moderate the structural changes and delay the high‐voltage phase transition in P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, by characterizations of solid‐state <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectroscopy, neutron diffraction and X‐ray diffraction (XRD).<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0014b"/> Improved electrochemical performances were also observed in Li‐doped P2‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>(Li<sub><i>y</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>y</i></sub>)O<sub>2</sub><link href="#anie201911698-bib-0015"/> and Cu‐doped P2/P2′‐Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>(Cu<sub><i>y</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>y</i></sub>)O<sub>2</sub>.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0013 #anie201911698-bib-0016"/> Aluminum is the most abundant metal elements in the earth. Its abundance in the continental crust (8.23 %) is much higher than Fe (5.63 %) and nearly 4‐times higher than Mg (2.33 %).<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0004b #anie201911698-bib-0017"/> Once the Al<sup>3+</sup> are introduced into Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, the Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller centers will be reduced. Moreover, the oxygen and Al atoms in the TM layers form the stronger chemical bonds of 501.9±10 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup> than Mn−O bonds (362±25 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>) and Mg−O bonds (358.2±7.2 kJ mol<sup>−1</sup>).<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0018"/> The stronger Al−O chemical bonds in Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> will work through the Na‐O‐Al‐O‐Na interactions and finally create a broader spacing of the Na<sup>+</sup> layers in of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> than in both Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Mg<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> materials. A larger Na<sup>+</sup> layer‐spacing will enhance the Na<sup>+</sup> hopping and leads to a faster Na<sup>+</sup> mobility. Therefore, Al‐doping is a suitable strategy to improve the electrochemical performances and to stabilize the structure of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>. Recently, Al‐doped P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> materials were reported by Wu et al.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0019"/> and Passerini et al.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0020"/> Their results indicate that Al‐doping could improve the electrochemical performances of P2‐type Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> materials. However, the hydrated and orthorhombic impurities in their prepared materials certainly influence the electrochemical behaviors of targeted electrode materials.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0004a"/> Moreover, as well as other layered sodium transition metal oxides systems,<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0021"/> the in‐depth comprehension of the Al‐doping mechanisms remains poorly understood.</p><p>Herein, we present a systematic investigation on Al‐doped P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x=</i>0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2) series as cathodes for SIBs. With the increase of Al content, the structures transform from P2′ + P2 (<i>x=</i>0 and 0.05) to the undistorted P2 (<i>x=</i>0.1 and 0.2) phase, due to the gradual reduction of Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller centers. The Al substitution samples exhibit smoother charge‐discharge profiles and the optimized Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compound exhibits exceptional electrochemical performances. It delivers high specific capacity of 193 and 175 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.05 C within 1.5–4.3 and 2.0–4.0 V, respectively; outstanding rate capability with over 50 % capacity retention even the current increased 100 times, and long cycling stability exceeding 200 cycles with 85 % capacity retention at 480 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The further improvement of Al contents leads to better capacity retentions but lower specific capacities. Furthermore, the structures of powder materials, phase transitions during electrode preparation and electrochemical cycling are investigated in‐depth by the combination of long‐range (powder and synchrotron XRD) and short‐range (ss‐NMR) structures characterization techniques. The results reveal that Al‐doping could increase Na<sup>+</sup> layers spacing, stabilize the structure during the electrode preparation procedures and prevent the multiple biphasic structure transformations at both charge and discharge, leading to excellent electrochemical performances. The high specific capacity, remarkable rate capability, high cycling stability of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> suggest that Al‐doped P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> is promising for large scale energy storage systems.</p></section><section xml:id="anie201911698-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="anie201911698-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Characterization of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub></title><p>A series of Al‐doped P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (<i>x=</i>0, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2 and 0.3) were prepared by sol‐gel method with a quenching process. As the Al content raised to 5 mol %, the refinement results in Figure S3 b confirm that the amount of hexagonal phase is increased to 73.9 wt %. With the further increase of Al content, pure P2 phase material is produced. This trend indicates that the distorted Mn<sup>3+</sup> Jahn–Teller centers are reduced by substituting Mn<sup>3+</sup> with Al<sup>3+</sup>. However, the observation of MnAlO<sub>4</sub> and NaAlO<sub>2</sub> impurities in the XRD pattern of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.3</sub>Mn<sub>0.7</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (Figure S2) suggests that the substitution limit of Al in P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> is lower than 30 mol %, at least in the sol‐gel method. Therefore, the following research is mainly focused on the Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> phases with <i>x=</i>0, 0.05, 0.1, and 0.2. The expansion of c lattice parameter is caused by the increase of repulsive electrostatic interactions of O<sup>2−</sup> with the extraction of Na<sup>+</sup>. During the discharge process, P2 and P2′ phases are observed in 0.23 &lt; <i>x</i>&lt;0.60 and 0.74 &lt; <i>x</i>&lt;0.96, respectively. In the mixed two‐phase stage (⑥),the (110) peak of P2 phase disappears and that of P2′ phase emerges gradually indicating a two‐phase reaction mechanism of P2–P2′ transformation in the discharge process of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> electrode.</p><figure xml:id="anie201911698-fig-0004"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201911698:anie201911698-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201911698-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>In situ synchrotron XRD during the initial charge–discharge cycle of a) Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and b) Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The cycling curves are shown at the left of each pattern. The electrode phases are shown at the right of each pattern.</p></caption></figure><p>Unlike the complexity of structural evolutions of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, the operando XRD patterns of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode in Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0004"/> b present much more moderate structure transition. As can be seen, the P2‐phase is maintained during the whole charge process and the discharge processes ranging from 0.32 &lt; <i>x</i>&lt;0.78. With the further insertion of Na<sup>+</sup>, the (002) peak splits into two peaks, indicating the coexistence of P2 (<i>P63/mmc</i>) and P2′ (<i>Cmcm</i>) phases at the end of discharge, investigated in detail by ex situ <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectroscopic characterization below. The evolution of the lattice parameters of Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode during the first cycle is calculated and plotted in Figure S13. The lattice parameter <i>a</i> shows a monotonous decrease in the charge process and then increases upon Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation, due to the contraction and expansion of MnO<sub>6</sub> octahedron upon the oxidation and reduction of Mn<sup>3+/4+</sup> redox couples, respectively. The parameter <i>c</i> first increases till <i>x=</i>0.44 in Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> due to the increasing electrostatic repulsion of the adjacent oxygen layers upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction. Then, the contraction in lattice parameter <i>c</i> is observed with the further removal of Na<sup>+</sup> because of the slight glides of Mn(Al)‐O<sub>2</sub> slabs, as a result of alleviating the repulsion force between neighboring oxygen ions. During discharge process, the change of <i>c</i> lattice parameters reverse directions and the lattice parameters of P2′ phase have similar tendency to that of P2 phase. The relative volume change of Na<sub><i>x</i></sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is only 3.0 % during discharge, which is beneficial to its cycling performance.</p></section><section xml:id="anie201911698-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Local Structure Evolution upon Na<sup>+</sup>‐(De)intercalation</title><p>To further explore the mechanisms of the improvement of electrochemical performance for Al<sup>3+</sup>‐doping, ex situ NMR spectroscopy was performed to characterize the local structure evolutions of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrodes during charge and discharge processes. As mentioned above, the NMR signals located at around 250–600, 650–900, 900–1400, and 1400–1850 ppm correspond to the hydrated, P2′, P2, and <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> phases, respectively. The hydrated signal is prone to appear in undoped material at high voltage suggests that layered sodium transition metal oxides with lower sodium content are more vulnerable to water and Al‐doping could be beneficial to suppresses the insertion of H<sub>2</sub>O in Na<sup>+</sup> layers. The ex situ <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectra of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> in the first and second charge/discharge cycles are presented in Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0005"/> a. In the first charge process, the intensity of the P2 phase signal at 1190 ppm decreases, while the intensity of <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> phase signal at 1620 ppm increases and moves towards the lower field gradually, which corresponds to the oxidation of Mn<sup>3+</sup>. In the first discharge process, the <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectra are almost reversed to that in the charge process as the Na content is lower than 0.68 (<i>x</i>&lt;0.68), demonstrating a reversible electrochemical process. With the further intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup>, a new sharp Mn<sup>3+</sup>‐rich signal (corresponding to P2′ phase) at 825 ppm is observed and its intensity becomes stronger due to the increasing of Mn<sup>3+</sup> sites. Simultaneously, the intensities of the NMR signals corresponding to the <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> and P2 phases decrease. Only P2′ signal is observed as the material is fully discharged (<i>x</i>≥0.87). In the second charge process, the P2′ phase at 825 ppm gradually decrease in intensity and totally disappear as <i>x</i> is lowered to 0.55. Meanwhile, the <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> and P2 phase signals recover when <i>x</i>≤0.76 and become weaker as <i>x</i>≤0.47. Moreover, the glides of oxygen layers at low Na content lead to many local Na<sup>+</sup> environments or stacking faults, which finally result in a broader signal at around 1100 ppm, as shown in Figure S14.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0014b"/> In contrast to Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>, the <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> phase signal is absent in the <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectrum of pristine Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode in Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0005"/> b. More importantly, the P2′‐phase at 650–900 ppm emerges at <i>x=</i>0.68 in the first discharge process and disappears at <i>x=</i>0.55 in the second charge process for Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub>. While for Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode, the P2′ phase can only be observed as <i>x=</i>0.97. The above NMR results confirm that 10 % Al substitution leads to milder and more uniform local structure evolutions during cycling, leading to less structural distortion especially at the highest and lowest Na contents.</p><figure xml:id="anie201911698-fig-0005"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201911698:anie201911698-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201911698-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Ex situ <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectra during the initial charge–discharge cycles of a) Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and b) Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub>. The cycling curves are shown at the left of each spectrum. Na content are represented by <i>x</i> and shown at the right of each spectrum. Yellow=<i>C</i>2/<i>c</i>, pink=P2, green=P2′ and blue colored correspond hydrated phases.</p></caption></figure><p>From the above NMR and XRD results, the structure evolutions upon Na<sup>+</sup> extraction/intercalation of the Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrodes are summarized and illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0006"/>. The Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> electrode is dominated by severe biphasic transitions during the Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation, involving the evolution of <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> structures and P2–P2′ transformation during Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation (Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0006"/> a). Although NMR results of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> electrode is consistent with the XRD patterns, slight discrepancy occurs at the first discharge process. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0005"/> a, the <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> phase emerges at 0.45&lt;<i>x</i>&lt;0.87 in the initial discharge process, while no significant phase change is observed in the corresponding XRD patterns. We speculate that this phenomenon is caused by the smaller size of these <i>C</i>2/<i>c</i> phase than the detection limit of XRD, since the NMR and XRD characterizations reflects the local and long‐range structures, respectively. For Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode, both NMR and XRD results exhibit more moderate structural evolution than Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> electrode (Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0006"/> a,b). It is important to point out that the synchrotron in situ XRD is insufficient to verify the P2–P2′ phase transition at the end of initial discharge, because the split of (002) peak could be resulted from the two P2 phases with different Na contents. While this structure transformation is clearly confirmed by the ex situ NMR spectra, in which both P2 and P2′ phases can be clearly observed in <i>x=</i>0.81 and 0.97 at the end of initial discharge. The electrochemical signature is more sensitive to the local structures. The structural evolution results correlate well with the shape of the electrochemical charge–discharge curves. Moreover, the galvanostatic intermittent titration (GITT) technique was employed to understand the influence of complex phase transformations on the diffusion coefficient of Na<sup>+</sup> (D<sub>Na+</sub>). The GITT profiles and D<sub>Na+</sub> results are plotted in Figure S15 as a function of the Na content <i>x</i>. Despite the apparent difference of geomean values of D<sub>Na+</sub> between Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> (3.32×10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> for charging, 3.76×10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup> for discharging) and Na<sub>0.9</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> (charge: 9.28×10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>, discharge: 6.01×10<sup>−11</sup> cm<sup>2</sup> s<sup>−1</sup>) electrodes, the evolution characteristics of D<sub>Na+</sub> versus <i>x</i> are significantly different. For the Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> electrode, the ①‐⑧ stages (in Figure S15a) are marked according to the phase evolution results according to both ex situ NMR and in situ XRD characterizations (Figure <link href="#anie201911698-fig-0006"/>). The calculated diverging minima located at <i>x=</i>0.55, 0.47, 0.32 during charge and <i>x=</i>0.44, 0.59 and 0.77 during discharge coincides well with the phase boundaries, which suggests that these transitions between two structures lower the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients significantly by 1–3 orders of magnitude. For Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode, the D<sub>Na+</sub> values increase firstly at the beginning of the initial charge process but decrease at that of the following cycles (Figure S15). The similar phenomenon is also observed in Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> electrode (Figure S15 and Figure S16). Indicating that the prepared electrodes undergo an activation process at the start of the first cycle. After activation, the D<sub>Na+</sub> versus <i>x</i> profile exhibits downward sloping shape during both charge and discharge processes, in good accordance to the one‐phase characteristics during charge and the mild P2–P2′ phase transformation during discharge. The GITT results suggests that biphasic phase transition of Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> hinders the diffusion of Na<sup>+</sup> and leads to poor rate capability as well as large voltage hysteresis. Moreover, repeated phase transformation leads to various structural defects and accumulated amorphization, results in inferior cycling stability.<link href="#anie201911698-bib-0007a"/> The above problems are significantly mitigated in Al<sup>3+</sup>‐substituted Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrode because of its quasi‐single‐phase behavior upon Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation.</p><figure xml:id="anie201911698-fig-0006"><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie201911698:anie201911698-fig-0006"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie201911698-fig-0006-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Schematic illustration of structure evolutions of a) Na<sub>0.67</sub>MnO<sub>2</sub> and b) Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> electrodes.</p></caption></figure></section></section><section xml:id="anie201911698-sec-0008" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>In summary, the cost‐effective P2‐Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub><i>x</i></sub>Mn<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>O<sub>2</sub> (0≤<i>x</i>≤0.3) materials have been developed by a sol‐gel and post‐quenching approach. In contrast to Mn−O and Mg−O bonds, the stronger Al−O bonds lead to larger sodium spacing thus facilitate Na<sup>+</sup> mobility and stabilize the structure at electrode‐preparing processes. Ex situ <sup>23</sup>Na NMR spectroscopy and Synchrotron in situ XRD results are in excellent agreement, providing evidence in the advantages of the delayed Jahn–Teller effect of the P2–P2′ phase transition and the suppressed biphasic structural transformations during Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation as Al is introduced into the transition metal layers. The enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion coefficients, stable frameworks, and delayed structural evolutions ensure excellent rate capability as well as outstanding cycling performance. As a result, without any other modifications, the Na<sub>0.67</sub>Al<sub>0.1</sub>Mn<sub>0.9</sub>O<sub>2</sub> compound delivers a high capacity of 175 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 12 mA g<sup>−1</sup> within 2.0–4.0 V; When the discharge current densities increased by 100 and 400 fold, the capacities reach 96 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (144 s discharging time) and 52 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> (11.2 kW kg<sup>−1</sup>, discharging in 39 s), respectively, which are exceptional rate and power capabilities. This work presents an effective strategy and provides in‐depth insights into the advanced high‐power and sustainable layered cathodes based on earth abundant elements for sodium‐ion batteries.</p></section><section xml:id="anie201911698-sec-0009" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by National Key Research and Development Program of China (grant no. 2018YFB0905400, 2016YFB0901502, 2018YFB0905402), National Natural Science Foundation of China (grant no. 21233004, 21428303, 21621091, 21603231, 21761132030, 21875196, 21935009).
