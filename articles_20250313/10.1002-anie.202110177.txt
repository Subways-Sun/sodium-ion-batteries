Due to the highly continuous N‐doped carbon framework and a spring‐buffering graphitized carbon layer around the FeP nanoparticle, a sodium‐ion battery with the FeP@NC composite exhibits an ultra‐stable cycling performance at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 82.0 % in 10 000 cycles. Also, particle refinement leads to a capacity increase during cycling. The FeP nanoparticles go through a refining–recombination process during the first cycle and present a global refining trend after dozens of cycles, which results in a gradually increase in graphitization degree and interface magnetization, and further provides more active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage and contributes to a rising capacity with cycling. The capacity ascending phenomenon can also extend to lithium‐ion batteries.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>Refinement of FeP nanoparticles can result in a gradual increase in the graphitization degree and interface magnetization. Furthermore, it provides more active sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage and contributes to a rising capacity during cycling.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110177:anie202110177-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110177-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="no"><section xml:id="anie202110177-sec-0001"><p>As one of the most important energy storage devices, lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) have been widely used in portable electronic devices and electrical vehicles (EVs). Nevertheless, the development of LIBs is mainly impeded by the high cost and resource limitation.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> Owing to the attractive features of abundant sodium source and low cost,<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0002"><sup>[2]</sup></link> sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted wide attention recently.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0006"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Among all kinds of anode materials, carbon materials have been extensively studied as anodes of SIBs, exhibiting good reversible capacity and cycle life.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0009"><sup>[4]</sup></link> However, their energy density is relatively low due to the limitation of their theoretical capacity.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0005 #anie202110177-bib-0013"><sup>[2c, 5]</sup></link> Comparatively, the rapid insertion/extraction of sodium ions in the host material is more difficult because of the larger radius of sodium ions, and more serious volume changes will occur during repeated insertion/extraction of sodium ions.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0016"><sup>[6]</sup></link> As a result, the cycling performance of carbon‐based anodes such as hard carbon and soft carbon, at high rate in particular, is not so satisfied presently.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0017"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Therefore, high performance electrode with more Na<sup>+</sup> storage and high stability is highly important.</p><p>One of the effective ways to achieve high specific capacity is mixing metal compounds with carbon‐based materials. Among multitudinous negative materials, FeP has become a good candidate for their high theoretical capacity (926 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>), abundant resources and environmental friendliness.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0022"><sup>[8]</sup></link> However, large volume change (≈200 %) and poor diffusion kinetics during the electrochemical processes of pure FeP lead to poor rate performance and serious capacity degradation during cycling.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0025"><sup>[9]</sup></link> The approaches to overcome the drawbacks mainly include increasing the diffusion coefficient or shorting the diffusion pathway for sodium ions.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0029"><sup>[10]</sup></link> In general, fabrication of FeP particles with nano size can effectively reduce the diffusion length and building high‐speed conducting paths through appropriate nanostructure design can enhance the charge transfer kinetics and structural stability.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0032"><sup>[11]</sup></link> Recently, fundamental researches on appropriate structural design to improve the electrochemical performance of FeP‐based compositions with carbon compound have been performed, such as FeP@C nanotube arrays,<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0033"><sup>[12]</sup></link> FeP@C nanoboxes,<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0034"><sup>[13]</sup></link> FeP@NPC nanofiber films,<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0026"><sup>[9a]</sup></link> and FeP@CNs composites,<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0035"><sup>[14]</sup></link> etc., whereas, progress is still hindered by the dissatisfied cycle stability, resulting from the volume expansion during repeated electrochemical processes.</p><p>In this work, a coral‐like FeP composite with FeP nanoparticles anchored and dispersed on a nitrogen‐doped three‐dimensional carbon framework (FeP@NC) was synthesized by in situ phosphating/carbonization of polymer precursors. The FeP nanoparticles are uniformly distributed on a nanoscale nitrogen‐doped carbon substrate, which plays a critical role on the graphitized degree of the composite by catalyzing the amorphous carbon around FeP to some layered graphitized carbon. Accordingly, the layered graphitized carbon can provide more embedding space and location for Na<sup>+</sup> storage and meanwhile inhibit the volume expansion based on a spring‐buffering function, by combing with the highly continuous conductive N‐doped carbon framework, the FeP@NC composite can achieve a reversible capacity of 90.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 10 000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 82.0 % for SIBs and 179.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 5000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> with a capacity retention of 90.3 % for LIBs, which has overwhelmed mostly reported FeP‐based composites. More importantly, the refinement of the FeP nanoparticles during cycling can not only increase the adsorption ability of the nanoparticles to the surrounding carbon, leading to an increase in the graphitization degree, but also give rise to magnetism variation, which jointly contribute to the increase in capacity during long cycling, offering a new insight to understand the phenomenon of increased cycle capacity.</p><p>The FeP@NC composites were prepared by phosphating/carbonization of precursors, as described in Figure S1. The resulted samples with different content of dopamine were deonated as 6h‐FeP@NC‐1, 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5 and 6h‐FeP@NC‐0, respectively, with a calcination time of 6 hours. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) of 6h‐FeP@NC samples (Figure S2) shows all the diffraction peaks, which can be indexed to FeP (JCPDS: 65‐2595). According to the thermogravimetric (Figure S3) and ICP analysis (Table S1), the atomic ratio of P and Fe of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1, 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5, 6h‐FeP@NC‐0 is 0.975, 0.974, 1.034, respectively, and the carbon content in these composites is also similar, corresponding to ≈48.8 wt %, ≈50.6 wt %, ≈51.1 wt %, respectively. And the Fe, P, C and N elements on the surface for 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 was confirmed by X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) in Figure S4 and Figure S5. The field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM), transmission electron microscope (TEM) and scanning TEM (STEM) results demonstrate that the FeP nanoparticles are uniformly distributed in the nanoscale carbon substrate and connected each other by the carbon framework (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0001">1 a</link>–g, Figure S6), presenting like a coral shape. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) pattern in inset image of Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0001">1 g</link> indicates a good crystallinity of FeP nanoparticles. Based on the reduced charge transfer path and highly conductive N‐doping carbon network, the coral‐like FeP@NC composites are expected to present a greatly improved charge transfer kinetics. Interestingly, the high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0001">1 h</link>) shows that the (002) plane of graphite, corresponding to the graphitized carbon layer near each FeP nanoparticle, which can be attributed to the formation of graphitization of amorphous carbon catalyzed by the nanosized FeP particles during the carbonization process.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0036"><sup>[15]</sup></link> In Raman spectra (Figure S7), the two peaks at 1354 and 1584 cm<sup>−1</sup> can be assigned to the D and G band of carbon materials, respectively.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0038 #anie202110177-bib-0040"><sup>[15b, 16]</sup></link> The I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub> ratios of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1, 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5 and 6h‐FeP@NC‐0 are 0.72, 0.75 and 0.76, respectively, indicating the high graphitization of prepared samples. The high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) image clearly indicates the crystal and atomic arrangement of FeP nanoparticles (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0001">1 i</link>), and the average particle size of FeP nanoparticles is ≈12 nm (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0001">1 j</link>). The STEM energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping images suggest that the C, P, Fe and N elements of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite are uniformly distributed in C skeleton (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0001">1 k</link>, Figure S8).</p><figure xml:id="anie202110177-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110177:anie202110177-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110177-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) FESEM image and b, c) TEM image of 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5 composite. d) FESEM image and e, f) TEM image of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite. g) STEM image and the inset of image corresponding FFT image of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite. h) HRTEM image of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite and i) HAADF‐STEM image matched with the corresponding molecular model of FeP. j) SAXS pattern of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite (inset: relation curve of the diameters of the FeP). k) STEM‐EDS mapping images of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite.</p></caption></figure><p>The electrochemical performance of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite in SIB coin half cells was tested (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 a</link>–g) with the voltage window of 0.01–3.0 V. The cyclic voltammogram (CV) curves are presented in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 a</link> at a scan rate of 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. The prominent peak at 0.79 V in the first cathodic scan can be attributed to the transformation from FeP to Na<sub>3</sub>P and Fe. The other sharp reduction peak at 0.39 V is associated with the formation of solid electrolyte interphase (SEI) film.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0023"><sup>[8a]</sup></link> The sharp redox peaks at 0.01–0.2 V can be ascribed to the reversible co‐intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> and solvent molecules into the graphitized carbon layer.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0041"><sup>[17]</sup></link> We also demonstrate the capacity contribution made by graphitized carbon in Figure S9,S10. The capacity contributed by graphitized carbon is higher than that by N‐doped amorphous carbon in the composite. The broad oxidation peak at 1.86 V can be assigned to the gradually transformation from Na<sub>3</sub>P and Fe to FeP during the first anodic scan. In the successive cathodic scan, the reduction peak shifts to a higher potential (≈1.23 V) due to the electrochemical activation of FeP.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0022"><sup>[8]</sup></link> No obvious plateaus can be observed in the charge‐discharge profiles of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 b</link>, while the curves are apparently distinguished from 2<sup>nd</sup> to 200<sup>th</sup> cycle at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, which is related to the capacity increase phenomenon during cycling in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 c</link> and d. As can be seen, the reversible capacity of the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode is 296.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and the capacity rises to 465 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 200 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 c</link>). The capacity decay phenomenon in the initial dozens of cycles can be attributed to the incomplete redox reaction.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0044"><sup>[18]</sup></link> When cycling at a current density of 1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, the capacity keeps increasing during the first 600 cycles, and a specific capacity of 297 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be maintained after 1000 cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 d</link>). The 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5 and 6h‐FeP@NC‐0 electrodes also display similar capacity increment at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> with the cycling processes. Thanks to the improved charge transfer kinetics of the coral‐like FeP@NC carbon framework, the 6h‐FeP@NC composites demonstrated excellent rate capability and outstanding structural stability. Especially for the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1, the high reversible capacity of 269.7, 266.9, 261.8, 253.9, 243.8, 222.2, 202.2, and 176.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> can be obtained at 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10, and 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 e</link>). Upon reverted to 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>, it can restore to a higher capacity of 266.8 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>. Comparatively, the 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5 and 6h‐FeP@NC‐0 electrodes show a relatively low capacity retention at high current (Table S2), which can be primarily ascribed to slower charge transfer kinetics resulting from lower N content and smaller surface area in the framework for the two samples (Table S4, Figure S11). The galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT) test (Figure S12) also verified the fastest Na ions dynamics of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1. Apart from extraordinary rate performance, 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode also has extremely high‐stability and ultralong cycle‐life. It retains 145.1 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 2000 cycles at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> and no significant capacity decay, which is higher than 6h‐FeP@NC‐0.5 electrode (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 f</link>). Impressively, the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode still delivers a high reversible capacity of 90.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 10 000 cycles even at a high current of 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, giving a capacity retention of 82.0 % (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 g</link>). The stable cycle performance can be attributed to the relatively regular structure of the graphitized carbon layer, as illustration of the inserted images in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 g</link>, which is similar to the function of a spring and is more conducive to buffering volume expansion. In contrast, the interlaced connection of amorphous carbon can easily cause structural damage during cycling.</p><figure xml:id="anie202110177-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110177:anie202110177-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110177-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electrochemical evaluation of 6h‐FeP@NC composite for SIBs (a–g) and LIBs (h): a) The CV curves of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 at 0.1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Charge‐discharge profiles of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. c, d) Cycle performance at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> and 1 A g<sup>−1</sup> of 6h‐FeP@NC composites, respectively. e) Rate performance of 6h‐FeP@NC composites. f, g) Long cycling performance at different current density. h) Cycle performance at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> of 6h‐FeP@NC composites.</p></caption></figure><p>Meanwhile, the superior electrochemical performance of the as‐prepared FeP@NC composites was confirmed by the storage properties of LIBs (Figure S13,14, Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 h</link>). As seen in Figure S14a and Table S3, the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode demonstrated discharge capacities of 704.6, 604.9, 541.1, 484.8, 434.3, 362.2, 305.9, and 238.4 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5, 10 and 20 A g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. In addition to its prominent rate capability, the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode exhibits a discharge capacity of 269.6 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 5 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 2500 cycles, a capacity retention of 90.3 % at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup> over 5000 cycles (Figure S14b), and a high reversible capacity of 649.7 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> after 300 cycles at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0002">2 h</link>), suggesting outstanding cycling stability of the FeP@NC composites. To the best of our knowledge, the long‐term cycle life is unprecedented in FeP‐based anode materials for SIBs and LIBs (Table S5). Interestingly, a capacity ascending phenomenon can be observed for nearly all the cycling tests, and the increment is more obvious for a lower current operation.</p><p>To get a deep understanding of the electrochemical reaction process and the interesting capacity variation during repeated discharge/charge cycles, in situ and ex situ XRD were implemented. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 a</link> and b, the phase of FeP peaks located at 32.8°, 34.6° and 37.2° and the (020) and (203) crystal planes of FeP periodically disappear and reappear during the sodiation/desodiation progresses, which can correspond to the conversion reaction mechanism of FeP. Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 c</link> further demonstrates the transformation mechanism of FeP. During the first charging process, the peak of Na<sub>3</sub>P gradually disappears, and finally converted to FeP. In addition, the ex situ selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns also confirmed this process (Figure S15). Meanwhile, a stable SEI film with constant‐thickness can be observed during the whole discharge/charge process (Figure S16), which is favourable for achieving a stable cycling performance.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0047"><sup>[19]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202110177-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110177:anie202110177-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110177-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a and b) In situ XRD patterns of the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 anode with the corresponding galvanostatic charge/discharge curves at a rate of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup> in the 2<i>θ</i> of 31–38° and 57–61°, respectively. c) Ex situ XRD, d) Ex situ Raman and e) Ex situ FTIR patterns of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 anode during the first cycle at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. f) Ex situ Raman spectra of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 anode after different discharge/charge cycles at a current density of 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. g) Discharge profiles and h) CV curves of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite within 0.01–0.4 V. i) The contribution ratio of capacitance and diffusion control charge at different scan rates.</p></caption></figure><p>Further, ex situ Raman (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 d</link>) and ex situ Fourier transform infrared (FTIR) (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 e</link>) were used to capture the surface variation of the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 during electrochemical processes by detecting the graphitization degree of carbon materials. The I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub> ratio decreased from 0.79 to 0.55 during the sodiation process, demonstrating the enhanced graphitization degree,<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0016 #anie202110177-bib-0048"><sup>[6, 20]</sup></link> which may be ascribed to more graphitized carbon layers. While the I<sub>D</sub>/I<sub>G</sub> rose to 0.80 during the desodiation process and it was close to the original value, indicating some special effect of the FeP nanoparticles is varying for the surrounding amorphous carbon during the cycling. In ex situ FTIR spectra, the C−C bond moves to the high band in the process of discharging and moves back to the low band during the charging process, indicating the enhanced C−C bond energy during discharging and weakened bond energy during charging process. This phenomenon can be attributed to the sp<sup>2</sup> and sp<sup>3</sup> hybridized carbon atoms in amorphous carbon, while sp<sup>2</sup> hybridized carbon atoms in graphitized carbon.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0049"><sup>[21]</sup></link> And the bond energy of graphitized carbon is higher than that of amorphous carbon, so it moves to the high wavenumber when discharging, and moves to the low wave band when charging. This further predicts that part of the amorphous carbon is reversibly transformed into graphitized carbon owing to the different effect of the FeP nanoparticles on the graphitization of amorphous carbon during a discharge/charge process, which as a result causes the increased graphitization degree during discharging, while decreased graphitization degree during charging.</p><p>Meanwhile, the Raman test on 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode after different cycles (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 f</link>) was performed to investigate the relationship between the capacity increment and the graphitization degree of the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite. It can be seen, the graphitization degree of carbon materials in 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite continues to increase as the cycle proceeding, indicating the increase in graphitized layers. As reported, the ether‐class electrolytes can reversibly realize the co‐intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> and solvent molecules into graphite.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0050"><sup>[22]</sup></link> Figure S17 and Figure S18 demonstrated the co‐intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> and solvent molecules into 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 anode. In addition, the ex situ FTIR patterns further verifies the increase of graphite degree during cycling (Figure S19). Moreover, Figure S20 shows the diffusion coefficient <i>D</i><math xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML" altimg="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110177:anie202110177-math-0001" wiley:location="graphic/anie202110177-math-0001.png"><msub><mrow/><mrow><mi>Na</mi><msup><mrow/><mo>+</mo></msup></mrow></msub></math> of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite continues to increase as the cycle. Therefore, the increase in graphitized layers will be conducive to the co‐insertion of more Na<sup>+</sup> and solvent molecules, and thus leads to the capacity enhancement after numerous cycles. After several discharge/charge cycles, the discharge capacity of the low‐voltage section increases (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 g</link>) and the co‐intercalation section at 0.01 V is more obvious (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 h</link>). To clarify the different presentation of capacity increment at low or high current, the dynamic behavior (CV test) of the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 electrode at different scan rates was evaluated, as displayed in Figure S21. As illustrated in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 i</link>, the proportion of capacitance contributed by the capacitive process gradually increases with the scan rate, which clearly illustrates the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite has fast ion transfer kinetics. Whereas, the proportion of diffusion control gradually decreases either for SIBs or LIBs (Figure S22), indicating it is not conducive to the co‐intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> and solvent molecules between graphite layers at high currents, so the capacity increment gradually decreases and becomes weak with the current increasing.</p><p>To further visually clarify the phase transition, microstructure and morphology variation during the electrochemical process, in situ TEM was performed to record a complete sodiation/desodiation process of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 a</link>). Here, the sodiation process is demonstrated to be a gradual reaction from the outside to the inside (Video S1), and Na<sup>+</sup> start sodiation gradually along the edge from proximal to distal (Video S2 and Figure S23).<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0054"><sup>[23]</sup></link> In a whole, a suppressed volume expansion (23 %) and extraction (17 %) during one cycle was recorded (Figure S24), which verified the superior structural stability of the as prepared 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 a</link>, the corresponding SAED patterns (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 b</link>) with the clear diffraction rings of FeP nanoparticles can be observed in the original sample. In addition, when Na<sup>+</sup> was introduced into 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 for a certain time, some new diffraction patterns of Fe and Na<sub>3</sub>P, corresponding to the (110), (103), (004) and (114) planes, respectively, can be observed, which is good consistent with the results of in situ/ex situ XRD and ex situ HRTEM. Since the in situ electrochemical reaction is a little different from the practical electrochemical reaction process, an insufficient sodiation at the end of the discharge can be found, and the product of FeP can be detected as well. Correspondingly, during the desodiation, a three‐phase state was presented as well at the end of charge process (Video S2). The diffraction rings of Fe and Na<sub>3</sub>P become misty and the (011) plane of FeP is getting clear, which can be related to the conversion of Na<sub>3</sub>P and Fe to FeP. More impressively, we can clearly observe that smaller nanoparticles in the edge area after the sodiation process (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 c</link>), and the refined nanoparticles are potential to bring an improved graphitization degree during the sodiation process. Whereas, the small nanoparticles are recombined into relatively larger nanoparticles after completely desodiation (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 c</link>), which decreases the graphitization of amorphous carbon. These results are in accordance with the results of ex situ Raman and ex situ FTIR (Figure 3 d and <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 e</link>). Nevertheless, the recombined nanoparticles are still smaller than the state before discharge, as shown in Figure S25. As a result, the overall nanoparticles will continue to be refined during long cycling (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 d</link> and S26), contributing to an increase in developing graphitized carbon layer and an increase in graphitization degree of the composite during repeated cycling (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0003">3 f</link>), as illustration in Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 e</link> of the entire particle refinement diagram.</p><figure xml:id="anie202110177-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202110177:anie202110177-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202110177-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>a) Sequential images from of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 nano pillar in situ test and b) corresponding SAED patterns for 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 at different states. c) The real‐time images of in situ TEM measurements during sodiation/desodiation processes and the inset of images corresponding the enlarged view of the local area. d) Ex situ TEM images of the 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 anode with 2<sup>nd</sup> cycle and 90<sup>st</sup> cycle at 0.1 A g<sup>−1</sup>. e) Schematic diagram of FeP nanoparticles and graphitized carbon layer changes during charge/discharge processes. Side view, vertical view and the corresponding adsorbed energies of the optimized geometries for C adsorbed on the (211) plane of FeP: f) the status before sodiation (FeP without defects) and g) particle refining during sodiation (FeP with defects). Here, Fe, P and C are shown as blue, green and brown spheres.</p></caption></figure><p>In order to get a deep understanding of the above phenomenon, the surface chemical bond between the graphitized carbon layer and FeP nanoparticles (here, we consider the initial state of FeP particle is defect‐free, while the crystal is deficient after the FeP particle refining during sodiation) was further investigated by DFT calculations. The optimized calculation results show that the adsorbed energies of C‐FeP (initial state) is −1.65 eV (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 f</link>), and C‐FeP with defects (after FeP particle refined) is −2.38 eV (Figure <link href="#anie202110177-fig-0004">4 g</link>). Remarkably, the adsorption energy of FeP with defects on the carbon layer is obviously higher than that of FeP without defects on the carbon layer, indicating that the unavoidable defects in the particle refining process are more conducive to the formation of the graphitized carbon layer after particle refining. Figure S27 further verified the relationship between adsorption energy and graphitization degree by employing FeP and Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. As the adsorption energy of FeP nanoparticles on the carbon layer is relatively higher, the degree of graphitization presents higher than that of Fe<sub>2</sub>O<sub>3</sub>. Thus, the graphitization degree can be enhanced during sodiation with the FeP particles refining, and consequently, more graphitized carbon layers can be formed and the capacity increase resulting from the co‐intercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> and solvent molecules occurs during long‐time charge‐discharge processes. Meanwhile, the magnetism of 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite has been increased after a certain number of cycles in the same voltage range (the detailed information in Figure S28 and S29), which can generate more extra storage capacity.<link href="#anie202110177-bib-0055"><sup>[24]</sup></link></p><p>Besides, we also demonstrated that other sensitive effects, such as particle size, FeP content in the composite, on the capacity increment in Figure S30–S35 (with detailed analysis in SI). These results demonstrated that the smaller the nanoparticles, the more obvious the capacity increment, and the higher the content of the nanoparticle with the more obvious the increase in capacity. Consequently, the refinement of the FeP nanoparticles during cycling plays an important role in enhancing the graphitization degree based on the adsorption effect of the nano‐sized FeP particles, and meanwhile increasing the interface magnetization, which jointly provides more extra embedding sites for Na<sup>+</sup>/Li<sup>+</sup> storage, making contribution to the capacity increment during cycling.</p><p>In summary, a coral‐like 6h‐FeP@NC‐1 composite was designed and fabricated with high‐rate and long‐cycle performance for sodium/lithium storage. It can maintain a capacity retention of 82.0 % for SIBs after 10 000 cycles at 10 A g<sup>−1</sup>, and 90.3 % for LIBs after 5000 cycles. Significantly, the mechanism of the capacity increase upon cycling is clarified in detail. The results indicated that the refining of FeP nanoparticles can induce the generation of more graphitized carbon due to the enhanced absorption ability during cycles, which can provide more extra embedding sites for Na<sup>+</sup> storage, meanwhile result in additional capacitance owing to the increase of interface magnetization. And the capacity ascending phenomenon has been observed for use of other similar materials and can also extend to LIBs. In general, this work not only provides a new idea for the development of high‐performance and high‐stability Na<sup>+</sup> and Li<sup>+</sup> anode materials, but also provides a new insight to understand the phenomenon of increased cycling capacity of similar metal compound materials.</p><section xml:id="anie202110177-sec-0002" type="acknowledgments"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>C.P.W. and J.T.Y. contributed equally to this work.
