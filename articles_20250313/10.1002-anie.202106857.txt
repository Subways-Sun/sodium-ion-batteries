Herein, we design and create Co vacancies at the interface of atomically thin CoSe<sub>2</sub>/graphene heterostructure and obtain Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/graphene heterostructure electrode materials that facilitate significant Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation pseudocapacitance. Density functional theory (DFT) calculation suggests that the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy is dramatically increased, and the Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier is remarkably reduced due to the introduction of Co vacancy. The optimized electrode delivers a superior capacity of 673.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C, excellent rate capability of 576.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 C and ultra‐long life up to 2000 cycles. Kinetics analysis indicates that the enhanced Na<sup>+</sup> storage is mainly attributed to the intercalation pseudocapacitance induced by Co vacancies. This work suggests that the creation of cation vacancy could bestow heterostructured electrode materials with pseudocapacitive Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation for high‐capacity and high‐rate energy storage.</p></abstract><abstract type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>The Co vacancies (V<sub>Co</sub>) at the interface of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/graphene (GE) afford strong adsorption of Na<sup>+</sup> and a low Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion energy barrier to facilitate rapid intercalation/deintercalation of Na<sup>+</sup> ions giving remarkable pseudocapacitance. The as‐prepared Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE‐based sodium ion batteries deliver high specific/rate capacity performance and exceptional cycling performance.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-toc-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106857-toc-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0001" numbered="no"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Lithium‐ion batteries (LIBs) dominate the market of energy storage devices in the past two decades due to their high energy and power densities, long life and safety.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0001"><sup>[1]</sup></link> However, the high cost and scarce resource of lithium impede their further development. As a valid alternative, sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) have attracted increasing attention in recent years since they are more environmentally friendly and resource‐abundant.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0002"><sup>[2]</sup></link> However, even though SIBs share a similar working principle with LIBs, the traditional electrodes that are effective in LIBs may not be suitable for SIBs.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0003"><sup>[3]</sup></link> Since the radius of Na<sup>+</sup> (1.02 Å) is 1.34 times larger than that of Li<sup>+</sup> (0.76 Å), the insertion of Na<sup>+</sup> normally results in sluggish kinetics and severe capacity fading.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0004"><sup>[4]</sup></link> Therefore, developing advanced electrode materials capable of reversibly accommodating Na<sup>+</sup> is highly desirable to alleviate the aforementioned issue.</p><p>Finding the right materials is the first step to build up efficient SIB electrodes. Chalcogenides are typically more reversible than oxides due to the relatively weaker metal‐sulfur/selenium (M‐S/Se) compared with metal‐oxygen (M‐O) bond, resulting in more kinetically favorable and higher first‐cycle efficiency.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0008"><sup>[5]</sup></link> Moreover, carbonaceous materials like graphene are commonly incorporated to enhance the electrical conductivity and suppress the volume expansion.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0009"><sup>[6]</sup></link> Furthermore, morphology engineering plays a vital role in promoting the reversible storage of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0012"><sup>[7]</sup></link> Especially, atomically thin metal chalcogenides possess high specific surface areas and thus could generate large interfacial areas when coupled with graphene, which dramatically shortens the ion diffusion pathway and creates highly accessible channels at the interface, facilitating surface redox pseudocapacitance and interfacial intercalation pseudocapacitance, respectively.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0008 #anie202106857-bib-0015"><sup>[5, 8]</sup></link> The intercalation pseudocapacitance undergoes a faradaic storage mechanism, involving the ion intercalation/deintercalation on the same timescale as redox pseudocapacitance.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0018"><sup>[9]</sup></link></p><p>A further step to fully unlock the power of atomically thin heterostructured electrodes is electronic structure engineering, which is important yet always underrated.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0019"><sup>[10]</sup></link> For the electrode of a single component, rational defect creation has been demonstrated to not only promote ion diffusion and charge transfer but also provide more active sites for reversible energy storage.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0023"><sup>[11]</sup></link> The role of the defects at the hetero‐interface, however, has rarely been reported. Considering the intercalation pseudocapacitive behavior at the hetero‐interface, we believe that cation vacancies should be a good choice since their presence could efficiently tune the adsorption energy and diffusion barrier of Na<sup>+</sup>.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0026"><sup>[12]</sup></link> Especially, the presence of graphene (GE) in the heterostructure will facilitate the charge transfer process and enhance the role of cation vacancies at the interface.</p><p>Herein, as an example, we prepared atomically thin CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanobelt/graphene (CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE) heterostructure as an anode for SIB. Via a theory‐guided approach, we induced and incorporated Co vacancies and consequently obtained Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. Density functional theory (DFT) suggests that the Co vacancies could increase the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy and decrease Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion energy barrier, which dramatically enhance Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance. Such a theoretical estimation is confirmed by the remarkable specific capacity of 673.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C (1 C ≈494.36 mA g<sup>−1</sup>), excellent rate capability of 576.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 2.0 C and ultralong life up to 2000 cycles. The kinetic study on CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE concludes that the high‐capacity and high‐rate Na<sup>+</sup> storage are mainly contributed by the significant Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation pseudocapacitance at the Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE hetero‐interface, which is consistent with the theoretical prediction.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0002" numbered="no"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0003" numbered="no"><title type="main">Theoretical study of the roles of Co vacancies</title><p>To date, there have been very few studies regarding the effect of cation vacancies at the hetero‐interface on Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance because of their high formation energy. Due to the lack of information on this topic, we will need to evaluate the role of Co vacancy theoretically in order to assure efficient reaction kinetic. DFT calculations were first performed to understand the role of Co vacancies at Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE interface in determining the Na<sup>+</sup> storage from an atomistic viewpoint. Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0001">1 a</link> and <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0001">1 b</link> show the optimized structural models of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE, respectively. The most stable Na adsorption positions at the interfaces of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0001">1 c</link> and Figure S1a) and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0001">1 d</link> and Figure S1b) were then identified and the calculated adsorption energies are 4.19 and 4.57 eV, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0001">1 e</link>). Therefore, the presence of Co vacancy at the interface leads to enhanced interaction between Na atom and heterostructure, which is beneficial for Na uptake.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0027"><sup>[13]</sup></link> The diffusion properties of Na at the hetero‐interface were then investigated by the nudged elastic band method and the optimal diffusion paths for CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE were provided in Figure 1 f&amp;g and 1 h&amp;i, respectively. The diffusion energy barrier associated with this process for Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is ≈1.7 eV, which is much lower than that of ≈3.7 eV for CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0001">1 j</link>), indicating the dramatically enhanced diffusion kinetics and fast charge/discharge processes. Overall, our theoretical study suggests that the Co vacancies at the interface could not only create more active sites but also facilitate ion migration, enabling high capacity and high rate Na<sup>+</sup> storage for Co<sub>2−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE.</p><figure xml:id="anie202106857-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-fig-0001"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106857-fig-0001-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Theoretical study of the role of Co vacancies at the atomically thin interface in promoting the Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance. a),b) Top views of the optimized CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE structures with green Se, blue Co, and brown C. c),d) Side views of the most stable Na (yellow) adsorption positions at the interfaces of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. e) Na adsorption energies. f)–i) Top and side views of Na diffusion pathways at the interfaces of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. j) Na diffusion energy barriers.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0004" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials synthesis and structural characterization</title><p>To experimentally validate the proposed theory, atomically thin Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> nanobelt/GE heterostructure was prepared with the synthetic procedure shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0002">2 a</link>. Firstly, the CoSe<sub>2</sub> nuclei generated from Co and Se sources were coordinated with diethylenetriamine (DETA) under hydrothermal conditions. Then, atomically thin CoSe<sub>2</sub> nanobelts were assembled on the surface of graphene under the direction of DETA, forming DETA/CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE layered intermediates. After that, the obtained suspension was subjected to ultrasonication to separate DETA from CoSe<sub>2</sub>. In this process, low‐power ultrasonication leads to the formation of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, while high‐power ultrasonication enables DETA to drag Co atoms out of the lattice,<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0028"><sup>[14]</sup></link> resulting in the formation of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE.</p><figure xml:id="anie202106857-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-fig-0002"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106857-fig-0002-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Synthesis and structural characterization of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. a) Schematic illustration of the synthetic procedure. b) STEM image of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE with inset showing its growth on graphene. c) Elemental maps of Co, O, Se, and C in Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. d) SAED pattern of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. e) HAADF‐STEM image of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE. f) HAADF‐STEM image of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. The intensity profiles along the selected regions suggest the intact crystal structure of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and the presence of Co vacancy in Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE.</p></caption></figure><p>Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0002">2 b</link> shows the high‐angle annular dark‐field scanning transmission electron microscopy (HAADF‐STEM) image of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. Clearly, the Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> possesses an ultrathin nanobelt structure and is grown on the surface of graphene (inset of Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0002">2 b</link>), indicating the strong coupling effect between them. The thickness of the heterostructure is ≈4.6 nm according to the cross‐sectional STEM image (Figure S2). Energy‐dispersive X‐ray spectroscopy (EDS) maps show that the Co, Se and C are homogeneously distributed throughout the heterostructure (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0002">2 c</link>), and the selected area electron diffraction (SAED) patterns reveal that the Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is composed of cubic CoSe<sub>2</sub>, orthorhombic CoSe<sub>2</sub> and graphene phases (some unspecified dots are probably attributed to the CoO<sub>x</sub> impurities due to the limited solubility of Na<sub>2</sub>SeO<sub>3</sub> in H<sub>2</sub>O‐DETA solvent). The intact orthorhombic crystal of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and the created Co vacancies in Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE are readily visible via high‐resolution STEM (HR‐STEM) images (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0002">2 e,f</link>). In addition, the intensity profile along the selected region also suggests the missed Co atom in Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE.</p><p>The phase structures of different electrodes were characterized by X‐ray diffraction (Figure S3). The peaks are indexed to cubic CoSe<sub>2</sub> (JCPDS 09‐0234), orthorhombic (JCPDS 53‐0449) CoSe<sub>2</sub> and CoO<sub>x</sub> impurities.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0029"><sup>[15]</sup></link> This was further confirmed by the Raman spectra (Figure S4). The peak located at 159 cm<sup>−1</sup> is assigned to the stretching mode of Se‐Se in CoSe<sub>2</sub>,<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0033"><sup>[16]</sup></link> while that at 204 cm<sup>−1</sup> corresponds to the F<sub>2g</sub> mode of CoO<sub><i>x</i></sub>. In addition, there are two strong peaks in CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE located at 1350 and 1582 cm<sup>−1</sup>, which are attributed to the D band and G band of graphene,<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0036"><sup>[17]</sup></link> respectively.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0005" numbered="no"><title type="main">Understanding the electronic structure induced by interface Co vacancies</title><p>The electronic structures of different electrodes were then studied via X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS) and X‐ray absorption fine structure spectroscopy (XAFS). The XPS survey spectra show the existence of Co, Se, C and O elements (Figure S5), which is consistent with the XRD and Raman results. The high‐resolution Co 2p spectra were fitted by multiple Gaussian functions, representing the Co‐Se, Co‐O and satellites, respectively (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0003">3 a</link>). Notably, the Co‐Se peaks of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE shift to higher binding energies compared with those of CoSe<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0003">3 b</link>), indicating the electron transfer from Co to Se induced by Co vacancies. Similarly, the positive shifts of Co L<sub>2,3</sub> edges of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE from the EEL spectra were also detected (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0003">3 c</link>), originating from the changed electronic environment around Co vacancies.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0037"><sup>[18]</sup></link> The vacancy‐tuned electronic structure was further analyzed by XAFS at the Co K edge. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0003">3 d</link>, the near‐edge structure at Co K edge of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE shows a slight positive shift compared to CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, suggesting a higher oxidation state due to Co vacancies.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0038"><sup>[19]</sup></link> Moreover, the Fourier transform of Co K‐edge extended X‐ray absorption fine structure of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE shows a main peak at <i>R</i>=2.03 Å, representing the bond distance of the nearest Co‐Se coordination. In comparison, the peak intensity of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE decreases, attributing to the coordination missing induced by Co vacancies. In addition, the main peak of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE shifts to 1.87 Å, which reflects the shortened Co−Se bond length due to the higher oxidation state of Co.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0028"><sup>[14]</sup></link></p><figure xml:id="anie202106857-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-fig-0003"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106857-fig-0003-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Electronic structure modulated by Co vacancies. a) Co 2p XPS spectra of different electrodes. b) Co‐Se binding energy positions of different electrodes. c) Co L<sub>2.3</sub>‐edge EEL spectra. d) Co K‐edge X‐ray absorption near‐edge structures of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE. e) Fourier transforms of Co K‐edge extended X‐ray absorption fine structures of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE and Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE.</p></caption></figure></section><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0006" numbered="no"><title type="main">Evaluation of Na<sup>+</sup> storage performance</title><p>Having proven the existence of Co vacancies at the interface and understood their role in tuning the electronic structure, we proceed to study their effect on Na<sup>+</sup> storage properties in Na half cells. The cyclic voltammetry (CV) of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE was first conducted at a scan rate of 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> between 0.01 and 3.0 V (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0004">4 a</link>). Clearly, the cathodic scans show three reduction peaks at around 1.40, 1.09 and 0.54 V, which can be assigned to the intercalation, transition and conversion reactions between Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and Na<sup>+</sup>:<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0041"><sup>[20]</sup></link><displayedItem xml:id="anie202106857-disp-0001" numbered="no" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie202106857-math-0001.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-math-0001"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="anie202106857-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-fig-0004"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106857-fig-0004-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Sodium storage performance of different electrodes. a) CV curves of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE at 0.2 mV s<sup>−1</sup> for three cycles. b) Discharge–charge voltage profiles of different electrodes at 0.2 C at the 20<sup>th</sup> cycle. c) Rate performance evaluated at stepwise current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 C. d) Cycle performance of different electrodes at 0.2 C. e) Long‐term cycling performance of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE at 1.0 C; pink trace specific capacity, black trace Coulombic efficiency.</p></caption></figure><p>In anodic scans, only a strong oxidation peak appears at about 1.93 V, representing the desodiation process and the formation of CoSe<sub>2</sub>:<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0042"><sup>[21]</sup></link><displayedItem xml:id="anie202106857-disp-0002" numbered="no" type="mathematics"><mediaResource href="graphic/anie202106857-math-0002.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-math-0002"/></displayedItem></p><p>Notably, the reduction and oxidation peaks for the three cycles are well overlapped, implying the good reversibility and stability of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE during the Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction process. The complex Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE was confirmed by the discharge/charge voltage profiles (Figure S6) and the proposed storage mechanism was verified by the HRSTEM images of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE at fully discharged and charged states (Figure S7). Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0004">4 b</link> shows the 20<sup>th</sup> discharge/charge profiles of different electrodes at 0.2 C. Clearly, the discharge capacities under 0.5 V follow a decreasing order of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE (100.6) &gt; CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE (59.7) &gt; Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> (39.8) &gt; CoSe<sub>2</sub> (36.3 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>). Meanwhile, the Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE has the lowest charge platform among all the electrodes. The high low‐voltage capacity and low charge platform of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE could achieve high energy and power densities in full‐cell applications.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0043"><sup>[22]</sup></link></p><p>The rate performance and corresponding discharge/charge curves of different electrodes were evaluated at stepwise current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.5, 1.0, and 2.0 C (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0004">4 c</link> and Figure S8). The Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE delivers average discharge capacities of 673.6, 658.1, 637.1, 610.5 and 576.5 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, higher than 411.5, 401.0, 390.5, 372.0 and 342.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, respectively. They exhibit better rate capabilities than Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively, and therefore both hetero‐interface and Co vacancies are essential for the enhanced performance. Moreover, when the current density returns to 0.1 C, the capacity of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE recovers to 655.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, indicating the remarkable reversibility and cyclability. Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0004">4 d</link> demonstrates the cycling performance at a current density of 0.2 C. The initial discharge capacities of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE, CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>, CoSe<sub>2</sub> and GE are 1155.9, 691.7, 563.6, 466.2 and 55.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively, and the corresponding Coulombic efficiencies (CEs) are 57.8 %, 58.1 %, 60.2 %, 46.2 % and 55.6 %, respectively. The relatively low CEs are due to the formed SEI layers on electrode surfaces.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0044"><sup>[23]</sup></link></p><p>After 70 cycles, the capacities of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE, CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>, CoSe<sub>2</sub> and GE maintain at 626.2, 340.4, 232.2, 176.3 and 9.7 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. Obviously, the capacity retention (compared with that of the 2<sup>nd</sup> cycle) of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is 91.0 %, much higher than 80.9 %, 77.2 %, 64.9 % of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub>, respectively, further suggesting the high reversibility induced by Co vacancies at the interface.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0026"><sup>[12]</sup></link> To verify the importance of Co vacancies at the interface in improving the Na<sup>+</sup> storage property, long‐term cycling performance at a high current density of 1.0 C was assessed. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0004">4 e</link>, the Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE electrode exhibits an ultra‐long life up to 2000 cycles and could still deliver a high reversible capacity of 323.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with a high CE of 99.4 %. It is noteworthy that the Na<sup>+</sup> storage property of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is among the best when compared to previously reported CoSe<sub>2</sub>‐based electrodes (Figure S1).</p><p>To understand the Na<sup>+</sup> storage mechanism, the reaction kinetics of different electrodes were analyzed by performing CV at varied scan rates from 0.2 to 100 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 a</link> shows that both anodic and cathodic peaks become stronger and broader and the separation between them gradually increases with increasing scan rate, indicating an enhanced polarization at high scan rates. In respect of the CV measurements, a relationship exists between the peak current (<i>i</i>) and the scan rate (<i>v</i>) [Eq. <link href="#anie202106857-disp-0003">1</link>]:<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0045"><sup>[24]</sup></link><displayedItem xml:id="anie202106857-disp-0003" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><label>1</label><mediaResource href="graphic/anie202106857-math-0003.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-math-0003"/></displayedItem></p><figure xml:id="anie202106857-fig-0005"><label>5</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource alt="image" href="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-fig-0005"/><mediaResource alt="magnified image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/anie202106857-fig-0005-m.png"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption><p>Reaction kinetics study of the Na<sup>+</sup> storage behavior. a) CV curves of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE at varied scan rates from 0.2 to 100 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. b) Log(<i>i</i><sub>peak</sub>) versus log(<i>v</i>). c) Normalized capacity versus scan rate<sup>−1/2</sup>. d) Capacitive current separated from total current of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup> and calculated capacitive contribution. e) Capacity contribution ratios at 0.2, 0.5, 1, 2, 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup>. f) Nyquist plots at fully discharged states.</p></caption></figure><p>By plotting log(<i>i</i>) against log(<i>v</i>), the value of <i>b</i> can be obtained (Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 b</link>). When the <i>b</i>‐value approaches 0.5, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage is controlled by semi‐infinite linear diffusion. By contrast, when it is close to 1.0, the Na<sup>+</sup> storage is dominated by a capacitive mechanism with fast reaction kinetics.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0046"><sup>[25]</sup></link> Take Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE as an example, the <i>b</i>‐value in the range of 50–100 mV s<sup>−1</sup> was calculated to be 0.74, implying a diffusion‐controlled discharge/charge process, whereas that in the range of 0.2–20 mV s<sup>−1</sup> was calculated to be 0.87, implying the capacitance‐dominated electrochemical process. For CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub>, the capacitance‐dominated storage behaviors locate in the ranges of 0.2–10, 0.2–2.0 and 0.2–1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. The relationship between normalized capacity and scan rate could be established. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 c</link>, the capacity of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is independent of the scan rate in the range of 0.2–20 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, while those of CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE, Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub> and CoSe<sub>2</sub> are in the ranges of 0.2–10, 0.2–2.0 and 0.2–1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, respectively. As a result, the capacity of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is more independent of the scan rate than other electrodes due to its dominated capacitive behavior,<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0047"><sup>[26]</sup></link> which is consistent with the result in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 b</link>.</p><p>To quantitatively determine the capacitive and diffusion‐controlled contributions, the total current density (<i>i</i>) at a fixed potential can be separated into two parts [Eq. <link href="#anie202106857-disp-0004">2</link>]:<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0048"><sup>[27]</sup></link><displayedItem xml:id="anie202106857-disp-0004" numbered="yes" type="mathematics"><label>2</label><mediaResource href="graphic/anie202106857-math-0004.png" alt="urn:x-wiley:14337851:media:anie202106857:anie202106857-math-0004"/></displayedItem></p><p>where <i>k</i><sub>1</sub> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub> are parameters and <i>k</i><sub>1</sub><i>v</i> and <i>k</i><sub>2</sub><i>v</i><sup>1/2</sup> represent the capacitive and diffusion‐controlled contributions,<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0049"><sup>[28]</sup></link> respectively. Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 d</link> shows a typical example of the separated capacitive contribution of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE at 1 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, the ratio of which is estimated to be 90.1 %. The capacitive contribution ratios of different electrodes at scan rates from 0.2 to 5 mV s<sup>−1</sup> were also calculated and provided in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 e</link>. Obviously, they increase with increasing scan rate, suggesting that the diffusion‐controlled reaction is suppressed under high scan rates. In addition, the capacitive contribution in Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE is the largest among all the electrodes, indicating the enhanced capacitive storage behavior induced by Co vacancies at the interface.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0050"><sup>[29]</sup></link> The electrochemical impedance spectroscopy was performed to understand the reaction kinetics of different electrodes. As shown in Figure <link href="#anie202106857-fig-0005">5 f</link>, based on the radius of the semi‐circles in the Nyquist plots, Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE has the lowest charge transfer resistance, which agrees well with the CV kinetic analysis.</p><p>It is well known that the faradaic pseudocapacitance is several orders of magnitude higher than the electric double‐layer capacitance,<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0051"><sup>[30]</sup></link> and thus the capacitive contribution reported here originates mainly from a pseudocapacitive behavior. For atomically thin heterostructures, the pseudocapacitive storage is composed of surface redox pseudocapacitance and interfacial intercalation pseudocapacitance.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0048 #anie202106857-bib-0052"><sup>[27, 31]</sup></link> The creation of Co vacancies on the surface of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE will not lead to the obvious change in the surface redox pseudocapacitance due to the conversion storage mechanism.<link href="#anie202106857-bib-0053"><sup>[32]</sup></link> Therefore, the high‐capacity and high‐rate storage property of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE compared to CoSe<sub>2</sub>/GE is mainly attributable to the boosted intercalation pseudocapacitance. According to the experimental results and theoretical calculations, the high Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption capacity and fast Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion kinetics induced by Co vacancies at Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE interface should be the main reason of the greatly enhanced intercalation pseudocapacitance.</p></section></section><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0007" numbered="no"><title type="main">Conclusion</title><p>To summary, through a theory‐guided approach, we discover a remarkable Na<sup>+</sup> intercalation pseudocapacitive energy storage mechanism at the interface of Co<sub>1−<i>x</i></sub>Se<sub>2</sub>/GE with Co vacancies. The Co vacancies enhance Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption energy and reduce Na<sup>+</sup> diffusion barrier, resulting in high storage capacity and fast electrode kinetics. As a result, the as‐prepared electrode delivers high specific capacity, excellent rate capability and ultralong cycle life, significantly outperforming reported CoSe<sub>2</sub>‐based electrodes. This work could open up a new way for the design of high‐energy and high‐power electrodes by stimulating the pseudocapacitive storage behavior via rational defect engineering.</p></section><section xml:id="anie202106857-sec-0008" type="acknowledgments" numbered="no"><title type="main">Acknowledgements</title><p>This work was financially supported by an Australian Research Council (ARC) Discovery Project (DP210103266) and an ARC Discovery Early Career Researcher Award (DE210101102).
