Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract type="main" xml:lang="en"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Manganese dioxide (MnO<sub>2</sub>) is considered as a strong candidate in the field of new‐generation electronic equipment. Herein, Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub> has excellent electrochemical properties in tests as the cathode electrode of sodium‐ion batteries and potassium‐ion batteries. The rate performance remains at 50.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup> for sodium‐ion batteries. X‐ray diffraction (XRD) is utilized to evaluate the crystal structure transition from Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> to NaMnO<sub>2</sub> with discharge to 1 V, proving that Co‐doping does indeed facilitate the acceleration of ion transport and support layer spacing to stabilize the structure of MnO<sub>2</sub>. Subsequently, highly conductive (0.0848 S cm<sup>−1</sup>) gel‐type supercapacitors are prepared by combining Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, potassium hydroxide (KOH), and poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA) together. Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> provides capacitive behavior and strengthens the hydrogen bonds between molecules. KOH acts as an ion crosslinker to enhance hydrogen bond and as electrolyte to transport ions. 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA has superb mechanical endurance, appreciable electrical conductivity, and ideal capacitive behavior. The quasi‐solid‐state supercapacitor demonstrates stabilized longevity (86.5% at 0.2 mA cm<sup>−3</sup> after 500 cycles), which can greatly promote the integration of flexible energy storage fabric devices.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs3448-abs-0001" type="graphical" xml:lang="en"><p>This paper reports the Na‐ion and K‐ion storage mechanism of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and the mechanical property with capacitance of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA has the largest tensile strength and compressive resistance. The high volume capacitances and energy density of quasi‐solid‐state supercapacitors provide direction for the next generation of wearable device applications.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3448:advs3448-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3448-gra-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Among various active materials used in sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs), potassium‐ion batteries (PIBs), and supercapacitors, manganese dioxide (MnO<sub>2</sub>) is a promising candidate due to its impressive theoretical specific capacity/capacitance, wide working window, and eco‐friendliness.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Whereas, MnO<sub>2</sub>‐based materials exhibit serious performance degradation, which is affected by their low electronic conductivity (≈10<sup>−5</sup> to 10<sup>−6</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>), slow diffusion rate of ions, narrow tunnel size, and dramatic structural change during the charge/discharge process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0002"><sup>2</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Doping heteroatoms, on the one hand, can optimize the electronic structure of MnO<sub>2</sub>, thereby shortening ion transfer routes and improving its constitutive conductivity; on the other hand, can play a role in extending and supporting layer spacing to prevent structural collapse.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0003"><sup>3</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Furthermore, MnO<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode materials with high electrical conductivity can be used to prepare flexible supercapacitors, which have tremendous potential in next generation wearable device applications due to its simple structural strategy, fast charge–discharge time, and long circular life.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0004"><sup>4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The key to construct a stretchable MnO<sub>2</sub>‐based supercapacitor is to combine an MnO<sub>2</sub>‐based electrode material with the elastomer material successfully. Hydrogels have been known for malleability and flexibility among various kinds of elastomer substrates.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0005"><sup>5</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA), a kind of polymer, can be used to manufacture a nontoxic, biodegradable, and deformable matrix. However, there are many problems needed to be solved urgently, such as poor mechanics properties, and inferior ionic conductivity (10<sup>−5</sup>–10<sup>−8</sup> S cm<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0006"><sup>6</sup></link><sup>]</sup>Actually, doping inorganic substances, like borax and FeCl<sub>3</sub>, can not only improve the conductivity, but also cross‐link the PVA to form a polymer network and then improve the mechanical properties.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0007"><sup>7</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Herein, we report the Na‐ion and K‐ion storage mechanism of Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and the mechanical property with capacitance of Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. Minutely, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> has a capacity of 71.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles in SIBs. A series of tests has been used to elucidate the electrochemical kinetics. The X‐ray diffraction (XRD) reveals that the characteristic peak of NaMnO<sub>2</sub> appears. 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA has the largest tensile strength of 4.25 MPa and compressive resistance of 4.7 MPa as the strain increases to 70%. The volume capacitances of quasi‐solid‐state supercapacitors are 6.86, 6.44, 4.2, 3.1, and 1.8 mF cm<sup>−3</sup> at current densities of 0.1, 0.2, 0.3, 0.5, and 1 mA cm<sup>−3</sup>.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0030"><title type="main">Phase, Component, and Morphological Characterizations</title><p><b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link> demonstrates a schematic illustration that Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub> was synthesized by a one‐step hydrothermal process. In order to explore the crystal structure and composition of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) spectra were performed. Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1b</link> indicates the XRD patterns of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> and MnO<sub>2</sub> nanorods. All diffraction peaks of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> agree well with MnO<sub>2</sub> (PDF#01‐0799) without impurity peak. Compared with pure MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> shows a characteristic peak of (110) with a higher intensity and sharper peak shape, which confirms that Co not only supports and widens the layer spacing but also contributes to the exposure of a (110) crystal plane. Subsequently, XPS is used to explore the chemical compositions and valence states of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>. The existence of Co can be proved by the appearance of Co 2p peaks in a full spectrum of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> (Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1c</link>). As illustrated in the Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1d</link>, the two spin orbitals of Mn 2p are grouped into three parts, standing for Mn<sup>4+</sup>, Mn<sup>3+</sup>, and Mn<sup>2+</sup>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0008"><sup>8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The high‐resolution spectrum of Co 2p in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1e</link> exhibits two characteristic peaks of 801.6 and 780.7 eV, corresponding to the binding energies of Co 2p<sub>1/2</sub> and Co 2p<sub>3/2</sub>.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0009"><sup>9</sup></link><sup>]</sup> In the Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1f</link> of O 1s spectrum, the peaks of 533.6, 531.4, and 529.5 eV are parallel to hydrous metallic oxides (Mn/Co─O─H), residual water molecule (HOH), and metallic oxides (Mn/CoOMn/Co), respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0010"><sup>10</sup></link><sup>]</sup> These facts confirm that the element of Co is successfully doped into the crystal lattice of MnO<sub>2</sub>.</p><figure xml:id="advs3448-fig-0001"><label>1</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3448:advs3448-fig-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3448-fig-0001-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Schematic illustrating the fabrication process. b,c) XRD and XPS survey spectra of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> and MnO<sub>2</sub>. d–f) High‐resolution spectra of Mn 2p, Co 2p, and O 1s of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>. g) Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherm, h) high‐magnification SEM images, i,j) TEM images, and k) EDS mapping images of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>.</caption></figure><p>The morphology characterization can be authenticated by Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET), scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and transmission electron microscopy (TEM). The surface area of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> turns out to be 11.183 m<sup>2</sup> g<sup>−1</sup> in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1g</link>. In fact, the large specific surface area of the material can enhance the area for the electrolyte to penetrate. The low‐ and high‐magnification SEM images (Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information, and Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1h</link>) of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> nanorods show urchin‐like clusters with diameters of 2–10 um. Low‐magnification TEM image in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1i</link> further reveals that the diameter of nanorods is ≈70 nm, the enlarged part (Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1j</link>) of which demonstrates evident lattice fringe of 0.353 nm, responding to the (110) plane of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>. The energy‐dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) images (Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0001">1k</link>) show that Mn, O, and Co elements are evenly dispersed on each nanorod, displaying that Co is uniformly distributed in the MnO<sub>2</sub> matrix.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0040"><title type="main">Sodium/Potassium Storage Performance</title><p>The electrochemical performances of Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub> cathode were analyzed by 2032 cells with the range of 1.0–3.8 V for SIBs. All electrodes in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002"><b>2</b>a</link> are pre‐activated by 5 cycles at 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup> and then cycle at a constant current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> has a discharge capacity of 71.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles with a coulombic efficiency (CE) approaching 100%, whereas, Co<sub>0.1</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.3</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and MnO<sub>2</sub> show poor stability (50.5, 51.5, and 17.0 mAh g<sup>−1</sup>) throughout the charge–discharge cycles. The fact indicates that the doping of Co can significantly improve the electrochemical performance of MnO<sub>2</sub>‐based materials and Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> possesses the best reversible capacity and structural stability than any other material.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0011"><sup>11</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2b</link> documents the rate data of 132.5, 98.5, 81.8, 71.7, 66.5, and 50.2 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> with current densities of 10, 20, 50, 75, 100, and 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The performance remains at 71.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> when the current density backs to 10 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2c</link> exhibits charge/discharge curves of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> at different current densities. The similar curves prove the splendid reversibility and stability of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> once again. The performance of this work is better than that of other materials, the comparison diagram is shown in Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S2</link>, Supporting Information.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0012"><sup>12</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><figure xml:id="advs3448-fig-0002"><label>2</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3448:advs3448-fig-0002" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3448-fig-0002-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Cycling performance of different materials at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, b) rate performance, and c) charge/discharge curves of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> at different current densities for SIBs. d) Percentage of pseudocapacitive contribution at different scan rates, e) XRD pattern of electrodes at different states, f,h) SEM, g,i) TEM images of the electrode at 1 and 3.8 V for SIBs, respectively. j) Cycling performance of different materials at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>, k) rate performance, and l) charge/discharge curves of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> at different current densities for PIBs.</caption></figure><p>To interpret the prominent electrochemical properties, the cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves (0.1 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>) have been attained to elucidate the electrochemical kinetics. As shown in Figures <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S3</link> and <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S4,</link> Supporting Information, the calculated slope values of specific peak currents are greater than 0.5, implying that the capacity is attributed to a combination of a diffusion and pseudocapacitive process.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0013"><sup>13</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The pseudocapacitive contribution ratios in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2d</link> increase from 52.9% to 90.1% with a scan speed from 0.1 to 1.0 mV s<sup>−1</sup>, further verifying that the material has an excellent fast charging/discharging ability. In addition, Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S5</link>, Supporting Information, shows the Nyquist diagram of the impedance spectrum of electrodes with Co<sub>0.1</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.3</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and undoped MnO<sub>2</sub>, as the active material. The values of charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) for Co<sub>0.1</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.3</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and undoped MnO<sub>2</sub> electrodes are 2645, 1571, 2586, and 4749 Ω, respectively. The smaller charge‐transfer resistance (<i>R</i><sub>ct</sub>) in the high frequency region indicates that doping with Co enhances sodium intercalation near the electrode/electrolyte interface. The real part (<i>Z</i><sub>Re</sub>) in the low‐frequency range is linear against the square root of the angular frequency (<i>ω</i><sup>−1/2</sup>, <i>ω</i> = 2<i>π</i>f) in Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S6</link>, Supporting Information. The slope (Warburg coefficient) of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> is the lowest, indicating its fast sodium ions diffusivity, which in turn illustrates the best rate performance. Just as we predicted, the doping of Co reorganizes the electronic distribution of MnO<sub>2</sub>, promoting the Na<sup>+</sup> adsorption and the reaction kinetics.</p><p>In addition to investigating the kinetic properties of SIBs, the redox peak of the CV diagram (Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S7</link>, Supporting Information) and the ex‐situ XRD (Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2e</link>) can be used to explore the crystal structure transition of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> electrodes at different states after 10 cycles. It can be seen that the characteristic peaks of NaMnO<sub>2</sub> (PDF#25‐0845) appear and the characteristic peaks of MnO<sub>2</sub> gradually decrease with discharge to 1 V. The SEM in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2f</link> and TEM in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2g</link> further confirm the structural stability and presence of NaMnO<sub>2</sub>. The peaks of MnO<sub>2</sub> will reappear when charging to 3.8 V, as shown in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2h,i</link>.</p><p>In fact, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> not only has an excellent performance in SIBs, but also has outstanding results in PIBs. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2j</link>, the capacity still maintains at 65.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles at 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The rate performance in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2k</link> manifests that Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> cathode can stay at 47.6 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> at 200 mA g<sup>−1</sup>. The CV curves in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0002">2i</link> are analogous at different current densities, which also proves the stability and reversibility of the material. As previously speculated, Co‐doping does help to speed up ion transport and support layer spacing to stabilize the structure of MnO<sub>2</sub>.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0050"><title type="main">Performance of Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA Fiber Hydrogels</title><p>As is well‐known, supercapacitor is a new type of energy storage device with the characteristics of the energy storage mechanism of electrochemical cells and fast charge/discharge. Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, with stable structure and fast transmission speed, could also be used to make supercapacitors. Flexible supercapacitors can be prepared by combining elastic material with manganese dioxide. Poly(vinyl alcohol) (PVA, thickener) is a water‐soluble polymer, containing a mass of hydroxyl groups. KOH, as crosslinking agents, can enhance the mechanical properties of PVA by increasing the extent of intermolecular cross‐linking and the extent of network entanglement.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0014"><sup>14</sup></link><sup>]</sup> As shown in Figures <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S9–S11</link>, Supporting Information, the stress, fracture energy, and elongation at break increase with the increase of KOH content, which phenomenon is caused by the augment of hydrogen bond. Hydrogen bond (OH groups) can be verified by the Fourier transfer infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) spectra in Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S12</link>, Supporting Information. The peaks of <i>V</i><sub>O‐H</sub> shift from 3282 to 3255 cm<sup>−1</sup> of 1 <sc>m</sc>, 2 <sc>m</sc>, and 2.5 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA, indicating the hydrogen bonds are gradually increasing.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0015"><sup>15</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The pictures of these four materials (Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S13</link>, Supporting Information) can also prove the above point. The hydrogel gradually becomes transparent and strong as KOH increases. However, the elastic modulus does not conform to this law. The hydrogen bond is too strong to be stretched and has inferior rebound resilience, resulting in that the elastic modulus of 2.5 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA is weaker than that of 2 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA. Therefore, 2 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA is used for the next test.</p><p>Similarly, the mechanical properties of 2 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA can be adjusted readily by doping different masses of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>. <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003"><b>3</b>a–c</link> compare the tensile properties of 1 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, 2 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. 10 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA is too crosslinked to form a flexible material to participate in the test. The largest tensile strength reaches 4.25 MPa of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, which is 26 times more than that of pure PVA. 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA with the diameter of 3 mm can pull up to 500 g of weight in the illustration of Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3a</link>. Not only that, elastic modulus, fracture energy, and elongation at break are also the largest with the value of 0.97 MPa, 213 kJ m<sup>−2</sup>, and 506%, demonstrating 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA has the most excellent tensile properties.</p><figure xml:id="advs3448-fig-0003"><label>3</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3448:advs3448-fig-0003" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3448-fig-0003-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Tensile stress–strain curves, b) elastic modulus and stress, c) fracture energy and elongation at break of 1 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, 2 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, and 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. d) Compression properties, e) cyclic compression performance at 30% strain, f) maximum stress and energy loss of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. g) FT‐IR spectra for 2500–4000 cm<sup>−1</sup>, h) Nyquist plots of 1 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, 2 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, and 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. i–k) The circuit experiment of PVA, 1 <sc>m</sc> KOH, 2 <sc>m</sc>, 2.5 <sc>m</sc> KOH, 1 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, 2 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, and 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA.</caption></figure><p>In fact, the compressibility of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA is impressive. It can be seen in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3d</link> that the stress increases to 4.7 MPa as the strain increases to 70%, and the curves overlap well at the strain of 10–30%, indicating the material has outstanding compressive resistance. In order to verify cyclic compression stability, 100 compression cycles were performed on 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3e</link>. The curves of 25th, 50th, 75th, and 100th cycles are roughly coincident. Subsequently, Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3f</link> reports that the calculated data of maximum stress and energy loss are 247 kPa and 24% at the first compression, and then tend to be stable after several cycles. The key to excellent mechanical properties of materials is the degree of cross‐linking between molecules, known as hydrogen bonds. The peaks of the hydrogen bond in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3g</link> shift from 3249 to 3227 cm<sup>−1</sup>, indicating that the hydrogen bond is strengthened with the increase of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>. In conclusion, it shows that 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA has excellent mechanical durability, which provides a guarantee for application in flexible supercapacitors.</p><p>It is also crucial that the material has admirable electrical conductivity. In order to verify that, the electrochemical workstation was used to test the resistance of the material in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3h</link> and Figures <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S15</link> and <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S16</link>, Supporting Information, and we calculated the electroconductibility value (Table <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information). The results in Table <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S1</link>, Supporting Information, show that both the addition of KOH and Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> can promote the conductivity of PVA, and it will increase with the increase of KOH and Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>. The conductivity is the highest (0.0848 S cm<sup>−1</sup>), when the content of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> is 5 wt% and KOH is 2 <sc>m</sc>. Moreover, it is obvious from the circuit experiment (Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0003">3i–k</link>) that the brightest light‐emitting diode (LED), as a conductive connector, is lit by 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, indicating that its conductivity is the highest. And even more interesting, the LED can be bright even when the 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA gel fiber is bent, stretched 100 times, and knotted. This shows that the conductive network of the material is stable and is not disturbed by external forces. Moreover, the comparison of performance between the Co<sub>0.1</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, and Co<sub>0.3</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> in hydrogel supercapacitors has been carried out in Figure <link href="#advs3448-supitem-0001">S17</link>, Supporting Information, and the results show that Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> still performs well in hydrogel supercapacitors.</p><p>As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3448-fig-0004"><b>4</b>a</link>, the 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA can be prepared in a variety of shapes, and the prepared fibers can be bent and folded, which demonstrates the plasticity of the material and can be further used to make flexible supercapacitors. In order to select the material with the best electrochemical properties, a three‐electrode system was applied in 1 <sc>m</sc> KOH solution to evaluate the electrochemical behavior of three different yarn electrodes (Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0004">4b</link>). 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA has the largest galvanostatic charge/discharge (GCD) curves area, that is, has the highest specific capacitance. The reason is that on the one hand, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> owns the excellent capacitive performance, and on the other hand, it has the highest content of Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> in 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA fiber. Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0004">4c</link> shows the rectangular CV curves ranging from 5 to 100 mV s<sup>−1</sup> of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, revealing ideal capacitance characteristics. In addition, a 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA electrode still maintains a rectangle for all CV curves at low rates, indicating that the fiber has fast electronic transfer.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs3448-bib-0016"><sup>16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The GCD curves of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA at different current densities in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0004">4d</link> is triangular‐like and has excellent symmetry, revealing ideal capacitance behavior. As shown in Figure <link href="#advs3448-fig-0004">4f</link>, the capacitance retention is 80.5% over 500 cycles, revealing excellent electrochemical stability.</p><figure xml:id="advs3448-fig-0004"><label>4</label><title type="figureName">Figure</title><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs3448:advs3448-fig-0004" alt="image"/><mediaResource mimeType="image/png" href="graphic/advs3448-fig-0004-m.png" rendition="webHiRes" alt="image"/></mediaResourceGroup><caption>a) Flexible display of materials. b) GCD curves of 1 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, 2 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, and 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. c) CV curves at scan rates ranging from 5 to 100 mV s<sup>−1</sup> of d) GCD curves at different current densities of e) volume capacitances calculated based on the GCD curves and volume energy density versus power density of f) cycle life at 0.2 mA cm<sup>−3</sup> of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, the inset is the first ten and the last ten GCD curves.</caption></figure><p>Subsequently, a pair of 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA gel fibers were encapsulated parallelly in 1 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA acting as electrolyte and separator to evaluate the electrochemical properties of a quasi‐solid‐state asymmetric supercapacitor in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs3448-fig-0005"><b>5</b>a</link>. Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> exhibits a splendid capacity at a current density of 100 mA g<sup>−1</sup> (71.8 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles for SIBs, 65.9 mAh g<sup>−1</sup> after 100 cycles for PIBs). Afterwards, 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> combining with 2 <sc>m</sc> KOH‐PVA has excellent mechanical stability and conductivity to be used in flexible electronic devices. Quasi‐solid‐state supercapacitors possess superior energy densities and a stable life of 86.5%, which provides direction for the next generation of wearable device applications.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0070"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0080" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0090" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials</title><p>Manganese sulfate, monohydrate (MnSO<sub>4</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O, Macklin, 99.9%), ammonium persulfate ((NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>8</sub>, Macklin, 99.0%), cobalt nitrate, hexahydrate (Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O, Macklin, 99%), poly(vinyl alcohol) 1799 (PVA, Macklin, alcoholysis degree: ≈98–99%), and potassium hydroxide (KOH, Macklin, 90%), all reagents were used directly without purification.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0110" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub></title><p>0.01 mol MnSO<sub>4</sub>·H<sub>2</sub>O, 0.01 mol (NH<sub>4</sub>)<sub>2</sub>S<sub>2</sub>O<sub>8</sub>, and the proper amount of Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O (the molar ratios of Mn:Co are 10:1, 5:1, and 10:3) were dissolved in deionized water and then stirred 10 min at 20 ℃ to form a black solution. Next, the solution was transferred to a Teflon‐lined autoclave at 180 ℃ for 24 h. Finally, the Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub> was gathered by filtration and washing it several times, followed by a calcining process at 300 ℃ for 2 h. According to the different content of Co(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·6H<sub>2</sub>O, the materials were named Co<sub>0.1</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, Co<sub>0.3</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> in turn.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0120" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Co‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA Fiber Hydrogels</title><p>Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub> with different mass fractions (1, 2, and 5 wt%) were dispersed in 20 mL deionized water, in which PVA powders were added at 90 ℃ and stirred for 2 h (12%, w/v%). Next, different concentrations of KOH (1, 2, and 2.5 <sc>m</sc>) were added with intense agitation to form a viscous liquid. Finally, the liquid was injected into a silicone tube with a diameter of 3 mm, followed by three cycles of freeze‐thawing (−18 ℃ for 5 h and at 25 ℃ for 2 h), called 1 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, 2 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA, and 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>@KOH/PVA. In addition, PVA hydrogels (12 wt%) with different concentrations of KOH (1, 2, and 2.5 M) were prepared, named 1 M KOH/PVA, 2 M KOH/PVA, and 2.5 M KOH/PVA, successively.</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0130" numbered="no"><title type="main">Preparation of Fiber Supercapacitor</title><p>Two fibers (2 <sc>m</sc> KOH, 5 wt% Co<sub>0.2</sub>‐MnO<sub>2</sub>, diameter of 1 mm) were paralleled together and then coated with PVA electrolyte (PVA 12 wt%, 1 <sc>m</sc> KOH) to form fiber hydrogel supercapacitors (diameter of 3 mm).</p></section><section xml:id="advs3448-sec-0140" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Tests</title><p>Electrochemical performance of SIBs/PIBs was tested by CR2032 cells with Na/K metal foil as the counter electrode and glass fiber (GF/F) as separator.
