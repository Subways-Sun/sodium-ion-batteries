Information</caption></supportingInfoItem></supportingInformation><abstractGroup><abstract xml:lang="en" type="main"><title type="main">Abstract</title><p>Iron/manganese‐based layered transition metal oxides have risen to prominence as prospective cathodes for sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) owing to their abundant resources and high theoretical specific capacities, yet they still suffer from rapid capacity fading. Herein, a dual‐strategy is developed to boost the Na‐storage performance of the Fe/Mn‐based layered oxide cathode by copper (Cu) doping and nanoengineering. The P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode material synthesized by electrospinning exhibits the pearl necklace‐like hierarchical nanostructures assembled by nanograins with sizes of 50–150 nm. The synergistic effects of Cu doping and nanotechnology enable high Na<sup>+</sup> coefficients and low ionic migration energy barrier, as well as highly reversible structure evolution and Cu/Fe/Mn valence variation upon repeated sodium insertion/extraction; thus, the P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nano‐necklaces yield fabulous rate capability (125.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C with 56.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C) and excellent cyclic stability (≈79% capacity retention after 300 cycles). Additionally, a promising energy density of 177.4 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup> is demonstrated in a prototype soft‐package Na‐ion full battery constructed by the tailored nano‐necklaces cathode and hard carbon anode. This work symbolizes a step forward in the development of Fe/Mn‐based layered oxides as high‐performance cathodes for SIBs.</p></abstract><abstract xml:id="advs2021-abs-0001" xml:lang="en" type="graphical"><p>Pearl necklace‐like hierarchical nanostructures of a P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode are synthesized by electrospinning and evaluated in sodium‐ion batteries. The synergistic effects of Cu doping and nanoengineering enable high Na<sup>+</sup> coefficients and low ionic migration energy barrier, as well as highly reversible structure evolution and Cu/Fe/Mn valence variation upon repeated sodium insertion/extraction, rendering fabulous rate capability and excellent cyclic stability.<blockFixed type="graphic"><mediaResourceGroup><mediaResource href="urn:x-wiley:21983844:media:advs2021:advs2021-gra-0001" alt="image"/><mediaResource href="graphic/advs2021-gra-0001-m.png" alt="image" rendition="webHiRes" mimeType="image/png"/></mediaResourceGroup></blockFixed></p></abstract></abstractGroup></contentMeta></header><body sectionsNumbered="yes"><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0010"><title type="main">Introduction</title><p>Realizing a secure and sustainable energy supply sets a high standard for large‐scale energy storage systems, which requires the systematic utilization of renewable energy sources and thus facilitates the development of rechargeable batteries.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0001"><sup>1</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Recently, researches on sodium‐ion batteries (SIBs) continuously surge owing to the inexhaustible supply and wide distribution of sodium resources compared with lithium, and the similar electrochemistry to that of lithium‐ion batteries. These advantages pave the way for SIBs to be a forthcoming candidate for grid‐scale energy storage applications.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0002 #advs2021-bib-0003 #advs2021-bib-0004"><sup>2–4</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Nonetheless, the larger size in conjunction with the heavier mass of Na<sup>+</sup> versus Li<sup>+</sup> commonly leads to undesirable sluggish reaction kinetics and even severe structural collapse of Na‐storage electrode materials, hindering this technology to reach a commercial prosperity.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0005 #advs2021-bib-0006"><sup>5,6</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Given that cathode is the crucial element dominating the cost and electrochemical performance of batteries, great efforts should be devoted to exploring advanced cathode materials with high redox potential, high capacity, and rapid sodium‐ion mobility.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0007 #advs2021-bib-0008"><sup>7,8</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Among the various reported cathode candidates, such as transition metal oxides (TMOs),<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0009 #advs2021-bib-0010 #advs2021-bib-0011 #advs2021-bib-0012 #advs2021-bib-0013 #advs2021-bib-0014 #advs2021-bib-0015 #advs2021-bib-0016"><sup>9–16</sup></link><sup>]</sup> polyanion‐type compounds,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0017 #advs2021-bib-0018 #advs2021-bib-0019 #advs2021-bib-0020 #advs2021-bib-0021 #advs2021-bib-0022"><sup>17–22</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Prussian blue analogs,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0023"><sup>23</sup></link><sup>]</sup> and organic salts,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0024"><sup>24</sup></link><sup>]</sup> layered TMOs have gained considerable attention on account of their high theoretical capacities and special 2D structural merits.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0009 #advs2021-bib-0013 #advs2021-bib-0025"><sup>9,13,25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Typically, the layered TMOs can be mainly divided into two polymorphs: P2 type (P: prismatic) and O3 type (O: octahedral) in accordance with the coordination environment of Na ions, where the “2” or “3” means the number of transition metal layers in a single cell unit.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0026"><sup>26</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The P2‐type oxides featuring large prismatic residing sites and facile diffusion paths for sodium ions generally exhibit better electrochemical properties than the O3‐type materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0027 #advs2021-bib-0028 #advs2021-bib-0029 #advs2021-bib-0030"><sup>27–30</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For instance, P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> is recognized to be an appealing cathode for SIBs in view of its abundance and low cost in raw materials, environmental benignity, and remarkable theoretical specific capacity (175 mA h g<sup>−1</sup>).<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0028 #advs2021-bib-0031 #advs2021-bib-0032"><sup>28,31,32</sup></link><sup>]</sup> However, deteriorated cycling performance is usually observed for this material due to the Jahn–Teller lattice distortions caused by Mn<sup>3+</sup> and the phase transitions at high voltage during Na<sup>+</sup> (de)intercalation.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0033 #advs2021-bib-0034 #advs2021-bib-0035 #advs2021-bib-0036"><sup>33–36</sup></link><sup>]</sup></p><p>Aiming at addressing the aforementioned issues facing the layered Na‐Fe‐Mn‐O system, a variety of strategies have been attempted to achieve better electrochemical performance, such as metallic cations (Ti<sup>4+</sup>, Cu<sup>2+</sup>, Co<sup>3+</sup>, etc.) doping to stabilize the P2 structure,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0025 #advs2021-bib-0035 #advs2021-bib-0037 #advs2021-bib-0038 #advs2021-bib-0039 #advs2021-bib-0040 #advs2021-bib-0041 #advs2021-bib-0042"><sup>25,35,37–42</sup></link><sup>]</sup> surface engineering to retard the detrimental side reactions,<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0043 #advs2021-bib-0044"><sup>43,44</sup></link><sup>]</sup> or nanosizing to shorten the ionic diffusion paths.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> For example, Rojo's group successfully synthesized a Ti‐substituted P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Mn<sub>0.8</sub>Fe<sub>0.1</sub>Ti<sub>0.1</sub>O<sub>2</sub> material, which turned out to proceed with a solid solution reaction rather than the P2‐O2 phase transition during the charge/discharge process and showed a long cycling life of 87% capacity retention after 300 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0025"><sup>25</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Hu and co‐workers fabricated a free‐standing electrode of P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with graphene flakes, in which the 2D graphene effectively improved the reaction activity of the oxide particles by forming a 3D conductive network, delivering a fascinating discharge capacity of 156 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C with a capacity retention of 61% after 40 cycles.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0043"><sup>43</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Guo's team focused on the morphology design and the tailored P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nanofibers enabled an optimized rate performance of 142 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> and 52 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 1 C and 15 C, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0045"><sup>45</sup></link><sup>]</sup> Despite inspiring progresses have been made, in terms of practical applications, it is still not competent to well combine good cyclic stability with superior rate capability in P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> only through a single modification route. Considering that cations doping can stabilize the crystal structure and nanoengineering can facilitate the reaction kinetics, the incorporation of the aforementioned two strategies may offer an opportunity to simultaneously boost the fast and stable sodium‐ion storage in a Fe/Mn‐based layered oxide cathode. However, to the best of our knowledge, such a dual‐strategy has scarcely been adopted which still remains a big challenge to date.</p><p>In the light of the foregoing, we herein successfully synthesized the pearl necklace‐like hierarchical nanostructures of a novel P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode through electrospinning and fully dug out the effects of Cu<sup>2+</sup> doping on electrochemical properties. Such a distinctive nano‐necklace architecture assembled by secondary nanograins (50–150 nm, the smallest size ever reached for Fe/Mn‐based layered oxides) can expose rich active sites readily accessible to electrolyte, shorten the ionic diffusion distance, and alleviate the nanoparticles from aggregation upon repeated Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction. Meanwhile, the Cu substitution of Fe is proved to accelerate the Na<sup>+</sup> transportation and restrain the Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy ordering during cycling. Consequently, outstanding rate capability (125.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C and 56.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C) and excellent cycling stability (≈79% capacity retention after 300 cycles) between 2.0 and 4.0 V are simultaneously achieved. The reaction mechanism behind the superb performance regarding structure evolution and valence variation of Cu/Fe/Mn is monitored by in operando X‐ray diffraction (XRD) and ex situ X‐ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS). Moreover, through galvanostatic intermittent titration technique (GITT), cyclic voltammetry (CV), electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS), and density functional theory (DFT) computations, the electrode process kinetics concerning Na‐ion diffusivity and Na<sup>+</sup> migration energy barrier are systematically investigated. In addition, a soft‐package sodium‐ion full battery employing the as‐prepared P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode and the hard carbon anode is well established, exhibiting bright practical prospects.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0020"><title type="main">Results and Discussion</title><p>The target P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nano‐necklaces were successfully synthesized via a facile electrospinning method followed by annealing processes. As shown in <b>Figure</b> <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001"><b>1</b>a</link>, the XRD Rietveld refinement of the as‐prepared Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> reveals that most of the diffraction peaks can be indexed to a hexagonal P2‐structure with a space group <i>P</i>6<sub>3</sub><i>/mmc</i> (JCPDS No. 54–0894), meanwhile, a good agreement between the experimental and the calculated data is obtained (<i>R</i><sub>wp</sub> = 8.55%). Due to the limited solubility of Cu<sup>2+</sup> in the P2‐structure, the presence of minor CuO impurity (3.5%) is confirmed. This phenomenon has also been reported before in other copper‐doped P2‐phase materials.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0040 #advs2021-bib-0046"><sup>40,46</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The lattice parameters, which are refined to be <i>a</i> = <i>b</i> = 2.9146 Å, <i>c</i> = 11.2104 Å, and <i>V</i> = 82.478 Å<sup>3</sup>, turn out to be bigger than those of the standard P2‐structured material resulting from the larger ionic radius of Cu<sup>2+</sup> (0.73 Å) than that of Fe<sup>3+</sup> (0.645 Å). This indicates that the copper ions have been successfully incorporated into the TMO<sub>2</sub> layers, providing more interstitial spaces for Na<sup>+</sup> ions diffusion.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0040 #advs2021-bib-0041"><sup>40,41</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The detailed structure information regarding lattice parameters, thermal factors, agreement factors, atomic sites, and occupancies determined by the Rietveld refined XRD is exhibited in Table S1 (Supporting Information). The P2‐type crystalline structure acquired from the fitted results is plotted in Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1b</link>. Along the <i>c</i>‐axis, oxygen slabs are stacked in an ABBAAB sequence and sodium ions are located at two different prismatic sites, namely, Na1 and Na2 sharing faces and edges with MO<sub>6</sub> octahedra, respectively.<sup>[</sup><link href="#advs2021-bib-0038"><sup>38</sup></link><sup>]</sup> The Cu/Fe/Mn ions occupy the octahedral sites of TMO<sub>2</sub> layers in a random distribution. Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1c</link> clearly displays the scanning electron microscopy (SEM) image of the electrospun precursor sample, presenting smooth and continuous nanofibers that weave into a 3D network. After a thermal treatment at 850 °C for 6 h, the continuous 1D nanostructures are inherited and still maintain an average diameter of around 200 nm (Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1d</link>); myriads of irregular nanograins string tightly together to constitute the pearl necklace‐like hierarchitectures. When viewed at a lower magnification, the as‐spun nanofibers and the annealed nano‐necklaces both demonstrate a wide‐range and uniform production (Figure S1, Supporting Information). The microstructure is further scrutinized by transmission electron microscopy (TEM) image (Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1e</link>) and high‐magnification SEM image (Figure S2 in the Supporting Information), disclosing that the sizes of the secondary nanograins mostly range from 50 to 150 nm. To the best of our knowledge, these are the smallest particles ever reported for the Fe/Mn‐based layered oxides. The high‐resolution TEM (HRTEM) image is subsequently presented in Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1f</link>, intuitively corroborating the layered structure of P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> with the (002) interplanar distance of 0.56 nm, as is also validated in the fast Fourier transform (FFT) image (inset of Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1f</link>). The selected‐area electron diffraction (SAED) pattern (Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1g</link>) featuring concentric rings suggests the polycrystalline nature of the Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nanograins. The narrower lattice fringes with an interplanar spacing of 0.23 nm can be well attributed to the (102) plane (Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1h</link>). Meanwhile, the high‐angle annular dark field (HAADF) TEM image (Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1i</link>) also exhibits the (102) plane and the bright dots depict the positions of transition metal (Cu/Fe/Mn) atoms. A homogenous distribution of elements in the P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nano‐necklaces such as Na, Cu, Fe, Mn, and O is verified by energy dispersive spectroscopy (EDS) mapping (Figure <link href="#advs2021-fig-0001">1j</link>). Besides, in order to gain the ideal structure and morphology, the calcination temperature and time have been adjusted reasonably (Figures S3 and S4 in the Supporting Information). It is found that a mixture of P3 + P2 phases is obtained at a low temperature of 750 °C. On the one hand, the unique 1D nanostructures assembled by small nanograins could facilitate the accessibility to electrolyte, offer facile pathways for electrons/Na<sup>+</sup> ions transportation, and maintain the structural integrity upon repetitive Na<sup>+</sup> insertion/extraction. On the other hand, the Cu substitution of Fe created larger interstitial spaces in the oxide crystal through which sodium ions could rapidly diffuse, and efficaciously mitigated the Na<sup>+</sup>/vacancy ordering during cycling. Thus, an exceptional rate capability (125.4 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 0.1 C in comparison with 56.5 mA h g<sup>−1</sup> at 20 C) and a fascinating cyclability (≈79% capacity retention after 300 cycles) were simultaneously attained. In operando XRD and ex situ XPS were employed to track the highly reversible structure evolution and Cu/Fe/Mn valence change upon charge/discharge, and the positive effects of Cu doping on the reaction kinetics were further confirmed by GITT, CV, EIS, and DFT computations. More intriguingly, the prototype Na‐ion full battery constructed by the P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nano‐necklaces cathode and hard carbon anode delivered a distinguished energy density of 177.4 Wh kg<sup>−1</sup>, demonstrating a great promise for practical applications. We believe that the combination of metallic cation doping with hierarchical nanoengineering provides a new and ideal platform for enhancing the electrochemical performance of the layered oxide cathode materials for SIBs.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0040"><title type="main">Experimental Section</title><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0050" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0060" numbered="no"><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0070" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Synthesis</title><p>The Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> nano‐necklaces were fabricated via a feasible electrospinning method with subsequent heat treatments. In order to prepare the precursor solution, 4.4 g of polyvinylpyrrolidone (average Mw = 13 00 000, Aladdin) was first dissolved in 30 mL of deionized water under magnetic stirring at room temperature. After complete dissolution, 4 mL of CH<sub>3</sub>COOH (99.5%, Aladdin) was added to create an acidic environment. Then, 3.6 mmol of Mn(CH<sub>3</sub>COO)<sub>2</sub>·4H<sub>2</sub>O (99.9%, Aldrich), 5.985 mmol (5% excess) of NaNO<sub>3</sub> (99.9%, Aladdin), 1.65 mmol of Cu(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>2</sub>·3H<sub>2</sub>O (99.9%, Aladdin), and 2.25 mmol of Fe(NO<sub>3</sub>)<sub>3</sub>·9H<sub>2</sub>O (99.9%, Aladdin) were successively put into the above solution with fully mixing.</p><p>After overnight stirring, electrospinning was carried out by loading the as‐prepared fluid into a plastic syringe equipped with a 21‐gauge blunt‐tip needle. The solution was electrospun by a high voltage of 17 kV applied between the needle tip and the aluminium foil collector, with a distance of 15 cm, at a feeding rate of 15 µL min<sup>−1</sup>. Consequently, an ≈150 µm thick membrane could be obtained by electrospinning at 35 °C for 36 h.</p><p>After electrospinning, the as‐spun membrane was peeled off from the Al foil collector and thermally treated in a following step‐wise process: 350 °C for 2 h (heating rate: 1 °C min<sup>−1</sup>), 500 °C for 2 h (heating rate: 2 °C min<sup>−1</sup>), and 850 °C for 6 h (heating rate: 3 °C min<sup>−1</sup>) in ambient air. Adopting slow heating rates in a step‐wise calcination process was to protect the pearl necklace‐like nanostructure from collapse. After careful calculation, the cost for synthesizing 100 mg of the target nano‐necklace materials was around 4.59 USD. This implied that the present nanoengineering technique was relatively cost‐effective, and was suitable for large‐scale production by adopting the multi‐nozzle equipment. Besides, the final calcination temperature (750, 800, or 850 °C) and time (2, 6, or 10 h) were modulated to optimize the synthesis conditions. In order to figure out the effects of Cu substitution, P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.20</sub>Fe<sub>0.32</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub>, and P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.24</sub>Fe<sub>0.28</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> samples were also prepared through electrospinning and annealing procedures under the similar conditions to the abovementioned P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> sample used, except for changing the metal salts dosage ratio according to the stoichiometry. The bulk Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was synthesized by a sol–gel method using the same metal salts dosage as that in the preparation of the nano‐necklaces sample, with extra addition of the complexing agent (citric acid). The annealing processes included 400 °C for 4 h and then 850 °C for 6 h in air (heating rate: 5 °C min<sup>−1</sup>).</p></section><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0080" numbered="no"><title type="main">Materials Characterizations</title><p>Crystallographic properties of the annealed products were determined by XRD (Rigaku D/Max‐2500) with filtered Cu K<i>α</i> radiation (<i>λ</i> = 1.5418 Å) in the 2<i>θ</i> range of 10°–90°. Morphological and microstructural studies were scrutinized by field‐emission scanning electron microscopy (FE‐SEM, JEOL JSM7500F) and TEM (TECNAI G2 20). Elemental analyses were ascertained by SEM‐ and TEM‐energy dispersive X‐ray spectrometer (EDS) mapping. Nitrogen adsorption–desorption isotherms obtained on a Micromeritics ASAP 2460 instrument (MicroActive) at 77 K were applied to evaluate the porosity and specific surface area. Raman spectra were gathered on a confocal Raman microscope (Thermo‐Fisher Scientific) using an argon‐ion laser (<i>λ</i> = 532 nm) in ambient air. The chemical content was ascertained by ICP atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP‐AES, Varian 725‐ES). X‐ray photoelectron spectrometry (XPS, Perkin Elmer PHI 1600 ESCA) was applied to investigate the valence states of the elements during cycling.</p><p>When it comes to in operando XRD characterizations, a particular mold battery was assembled employing beryllium foil as the testing window to allow X‐ray passage and carbon paper as the current collector, thus monitoring in situ reaction during cycling. Each scan was recorded in 0.02° incremental steps between 2<i>θ</i> = 12° and 42°. The charge/discharge test was conducted at 0.1 C.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0090" numbered="no"><title type="main">Electrochemical Measurements</title><p>All electrochemical properties were examined in CR2032 coin‐type cells assembled in a glove box (Mikrouna Universal 2440/750) filled with high‐purity argon. Electrodes were fabricated by casting slurries, the composition of which was 75 wt% active materials, 15 wt% acetylene black, and 10 wt% PVdF dissolved in <i>N</i>‐methyl‐2‐pyrrolidone, onto Al foil, followed by drying at 80 °C in vacuum overnight. Then the electrodes were cut into suitable‐size slices with a mass loading of about 2.5 mg cm<sup>−2</sup>. The counter and reference electrode of sodium metal disk was put face‐to‐face with the working electrode sandwiched by a glass fiber separator in the coin cells. 1 <sc>m</sc> NaClO<sub>4</sub> in propylene carbonate solution with the addition of 5 vol% fluoroethylene carbonate was employed as the electrolyte. Besides, the assembled cells were placed for 6 h before tests to make sure that the electrolyte soaked into the separators and electrodes completely. The coin cells were galvanostatically charged/discharged on a LAND battery‐test instrument (CT2001A) in a voltage interval of 2.0–4.0 V (vs Na/Na<sup>+</sup>). The tests of cyclic voltammetry within the same voltage region at various scan rates and EIS with the ac perturbation signal of 5.0 mV over the frequency from 100 kHz to 100 mHz were used to study the electrode process kinetics, which were both conducted using a CHI660C electrochemical workstation (Shanghai Chenhua, China). The EIS spectra were recorded at open circuit voltage. Prior to the post‐mortem examinations of the cycled active materials using XRD, XPS, or SEM, the electrodes were taken out from the cells and washed with dimethyl carbonate repeatedly in an argon‐filled glove box.</p><p>To construct the full cells, the pre‐sodiated hard carbon was adopted as the anode and well matched with the P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> cathode. The active mass loading ratio of cathode to anode was set at 1.7:1 to balance the capacity. Finally, the full cell was measured within a voltage window of 1.6–3.8 V.</p></section><section xml:id="advs2021-sec-0100" numbered="no"><title type="main">Computational Details</title><p>The ionic/electronic conductivity of P2‐Na<sub>0.76</sub>Cu<sub>0.22</sub>Fe<sub>0.30</sub>Mn<sub>0.48</sub>O<sub>2</sub> and P2‐Na<sub>2/3</sub>Fe<sub>1/2</sub>Mn<sub>1/2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> was evaluated by ab initio computations based on the DFT via Vienna ab‐initio simulation package (VASP).
